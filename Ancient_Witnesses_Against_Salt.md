# Ancient Witnesses Against Salt

## *An Index to the Eight-Tradition Quote-Book on Chloride*

### Why This Collection Exists

Modern medicine congratulates itself for discovering, around 1906, that chloride burden drives the edema and collapse of Bright's disease. But the traditions that watched the human body with the closest attention had made the same finding long before — often in fewer words, and without the instruments. They recorded it as dietary rule, as monastic discipline, as scripture, as prophecy, as epic verse, as political economy.

This collection is organized by tradition. Each file collects, in the original languages where available, the testimony of one cultural lineage. Sanskrit is given in IAST transliteration; Greek in polytonic or transliterated form; Hebrew in academic transliteration alongside the Masoretic consonantal text; Classical Chinese in characters with pinyin. Citations name book and chapter so the curious can verify.

### Method — How to Find More

The old-world record on salt is not scattered. It is located, and locating it is a matter of knowing the technical terms and the text families.

- **The terms.** In Sanskrit sources, the relevant terms are *lavaṇa* (salt as the salty taste or as the substance generally), *saindhava* (rock salt from the Sindh), *sauvarcala* (black salt), *audbhida* (earth-derived salt), *romaka* (a North Indian rock salt), *sāmudra* (sea salt — the common chloride ingestant), and *kṣāra* (alkaline salt). A single search for *lavaṇa* across the Āyurvedic corpus returns the *rasa-karma*, *rasa-atiyoga*, and *dravya-guṇa* passages. In Greek sources, *hals/hales* (ἅλς / ἅλες). In Hebrew, *melaḥ* (מֶלַח). In Classical Chinese, *xián* (鹹) for the salty taste and *yán* (鹽) for salt as substance.

- **The text families.** The Āyurvedic *trikanda* — *Caraka*, *Suśruta*, *Vāgbhaṭa's Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya* — treats salt in three overlapping registers: taste-quality, substance-class, and disease-etiology. The haṭha-yoga corpus treats it exclusively as *apathya* in the *mitāhāra* chapters. The tantric corpus treats it as a direct obstructor of the body-alchemy. The Dharmaśāstra treats it under the *aluṇa-vrata* and the *śrāddha* rules. The *Bhagavad Gītā* treats it as a defining quality of rājasic food. Greek sources are in Homer, Plutarch's *Moralia*, and the doxographical tradition on Pythagoras. Hebrew sources are spread across narrative, prophetic, and wisdom books. Chinese sources are in the *Huangdi Neijing Suwen* and *Lingshu* with elaborations in Sun Simiao and the Jin-Yuan masters.

- **The method.** Find the term; find the text family; read the context. The quotes below are the ones that surface first. The corpus is larger than any single essay can hold.

### The Convergence

Across the Āyurvedic, yogic, tantric, and dharmic traditions of India; across Homer, Plutarch, and the Pythagoreans in Greece; across Genesis, Judges, Deuteronomy, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, and the Psalms in the Hebrew Bible; across the *Huangdi Neijing* and its successors in China; and across the Natural Hygiene tradition in nineteenth-century America — the verdict on salt is one and the same:

- It inflames appetite while producing thirst.
- It hardens tissue, greys the hair, loosens the teeth, and dulls the senses.
- It causes hemorrhage, anemia, and the wasting syndromes of chronic disease.
- It desolates the land on which it falls.
- It disturbs the clarity that priesthood, yogic discipline, tantric practice, and household austerity alike require.
- It produces the rigidity that scripture calls the curse and physicians call old age.
- It marks the land from which the saltless people have, by prophecy, escaped.
- It produces, in every civilization that has industrialized its production, the dependency that empire and industry have used to bind populations to tribute.

No tradition that examined salt closely declared it a nutrient. Every tradition that examined it closely declared it a stimulant, an irritant, a curse, or a political instrument — and most often all four at once. Modern biochemistry, which confirmed each of these findings in sequence across the past two centuries, discovered nothing that the old world had not already recorded.

The convergence is the evidence.

### What the Old World Saw

What the old world saw, the new world is only now admitting. The witnesses have always been unanimous. This collection preserves their testimony in the original languages, so that the reader can go to the sources and read for himself.
