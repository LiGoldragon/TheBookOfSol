# "Fruits From India Are Different"

## On the Deflection That Protects the Fruit Myth From Its Own Scriptures

When the classical texts are cited — when the Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā names
anna, not phala, as the yogin's food; when the Caraka Saṃhitā classifies
fruit after fruit as heavy, distending, doṣa-aggravating; when the Bhagavad
Gītā requires sāttvic food to be snigdha and sthira, neither of which fruit
is — the modern fruitarian has a ready escape:

"Those texts are talking about Indian fruits. Tropical fruits are different.
The fruits I eat — mangoes from Mexico, bananas from Ecuador, papayas from
Thailand — are not the fruits Caraka was warning about."

This deflection fails on two grounds, and both are fatal.

### First: Yoga Is Not an Indian Science

The assumption that the classical texts describe a local cuisine adapted to
Indian conditions treats yoga as a regional practice — a product of the
subcontinent's climate, agriculture, and genetics. This is the standard
academic framing, and it is wrong.

There was a prior civilization. Its sciences were not national. They were
planetary.

The oldest memories of humanity converge on the same starting point:
long-lived, powerful, deeply knowledgeable ancestors whose understanding of
physiology, purity, and the subtle energies of the body made longevity natural.
India remembered. Egypt remembered. The Toltec seers remembered. Genesis
records lifespans of nine hundred years without embarrassment. The Atharva-veda
asks to "obtain the long life that our ancestors attained." The Ṛg-veda
describes sages who "found the hidden light" and "set it down in the hidden
place."

After the collapse of that age — recorded as flood, fire, and upheaval across
every surviving tradition — the knowledge scattered. It survived in fragments,
carried by lineages that fled to wherever geography offered protection from the
chaos that followed. India preserved the largest written corpus because the
Himalayas — the greatest mountain range on earth — shielded its guardians from
the waves of destruction, invasion, and cultural erasure that consumed
knowledge-keepers elsewhere. Egypt preserved its fragment in stone and
priesthood. The Toltecs preserved theirs in oral lineage and direct practice.
Each carried a piece of the same original science.

The Sanskrit scriptures are not Indian inventions. They are the most intact
written remnants of a worldwide civilization's wisdom, preserved behind a wall
of rock that happened to be tall enough.

To say "those texts are about Indian fruits" is to misunderstand what the texts
are. They are not regional dietary guidelines. They are the surviving medical
science of a civilization that predates India as a political or cultural
entity. Their observations on fruit apply to fruit — not to Indian fruit, not
to subtropical fruit, not to the fruit that happened to grow near the ashram.
To fruit. The physiology they describe is human physiology, not South Asian
physiology. The doṣas they name are universal forces, not local conditions.
Vāta rises in a fruitarian in California exactly as it rises in a fruitarian
in Kerala.

### Second: All Fruits Do the Same Thing

Even if we set aside the question of origin and treat the texts as purely
empirical — as clinical observations about what fruit does to a living body —
the deflection still collapses, because all fruits, regardless of their
latitude, produce the same physiological pattern when consumed in excess.

The Caraka Saṃhitā does not warn against fruit because of something peculiar
to the Indian subcontinent's cultivars. It warns because of what fruit *is*:
light, cold, dispersing, and sweet. These are qualities intrinsic to the
category. A mango from Ratnagiri and a mango from Oaxaca are both guru, both
sweet, both kapha-aggravating. A banana from Tamil Nadu and a banana from
Honduras are both heavy, both cold, both distending. A papaya from Varanasi
and a papaya from Chiang Mai are both dispersing, both pitta-provoking when
sour, both kapha-provoking when ripe. The latitude changed. The qualities did
not.

This is because the qualities the Caraka Saṃhitā describes — guru (heavy),
laghu (light), śīta (cold), uṣṇa (hot), snigdha (unctuous), rūkṣa (rough) —
are not cultural categories. They are descriptions of how substance interacts
with living tissue. A fruit that is sweet, cold, and heavy in Varanasi is
sweet, cold, and heavy in Tulum. The organism that processes it does not
change its doṣic response because the fruit crossed an ocean.

The arc of the exclusive fruit diet is the same everywhere it has been tried:

Initial clearing — lightness, sharpness, relief from accumulated burden. The
body, freed from chloride, cooked protein, and kapha congestion, responds with
gratitude. This phase is real, and it is universal.

Then dispersion — internal fluids thin, ojas depletes, vāta rises. Anxiety,
insomnia, cold extremities, constipation, drying of the colon, racing thoughts.
The organism, having expelled its burdens, begins to consume its own reserves.
This phase is also real, also universal, and occurs whether the fruit was grown
in Maharashtra, Costa Rica, or the Philippines.

Steve Jobs ate fruit in California. His pancreas failed. Ashton Kutcher ate
fruit in Los Angeles. His pancreas rebelled within weeks. A man in Europe ate
primarily apples for two years. His mind dissolved. None of them were eating
Indian fruit. All of them experienced the same depletion, because the
depletion is not caused by the origin of the fruit. It is caused by fruit.

### The Deflection Exposed

"Fruits from India are different" is not an argument. It is a dodge. It
protects the fruit myth by narrowing the scope of the warning to a geography,
so that the practitioner can continue eating unlimited fruit while claiming
that the ancient physicians were talking about someone else's fruit.

They were not. They were talking about fruit. And the body they were
describing is the same body — yours, theirs, everyone's. The doṣas do not
check passports. Vāta does not pause at the border. Agni does not ask where
the mango was grown before deciding whether to struggle with it.

The science is universal because the civilization that produced it was
universal. And the physiology is universal because physiology does not vary by
postal code. Both roads lead to the same conclusion:

Fruit clears. Fruit does not sustain. This is true in India. It is true
everywhere else. And the texts that say so are not regional cookbooks. They
are the surviving medical science of the world's oldest known civilization,
preserved behind the Himalayas because that is where the mountains were tall
enough to keep the knowledge alive.
