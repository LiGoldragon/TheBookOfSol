**Salt Is Not Vegan**

Veganism is grounded in a single ethical claim: the refusal to participate in animal abuse. It is not fundamentally a list of permitted ingredients, nor a rule about animal-derived substances, but a principle about harm. Everything else is downstream of that premise.

A human is an animal. This is a factual statement, not an ideological one. Any ethical framework that claims to oppose animal abuse while excluding one animal species by default introduces an arbitrary exception that weakens the principle itself. Once humans are included within the category “animal,” the scope of vegan ethics necessarily includes the human body.

Ingesting sodium chloride constitutes abuse of the animal ingesting it. Sodium chloride imposes physiological harm through well-documented toxic and degenerative effects on circulation, renal load, tissue hydration, and vascular integrity. Harm that is imposed knowingly and repeatedly qualifies as abuse, regardless of cultural normalization.

Self-directed harm does not lose its ethical character because the abuser and the abused are the same organism. If harming an animal is abuse, then harming oneself—when one is an animal—does not become ethically neutral by technicality. The ethical property attaches to the act and its effect on an animal body, not to whether another party is involved.

Therefore, sodium chloride ingestion is animal abuse when the animal is human. A vegan ethic that excludes this case must either deny that humans are animals, deny that sodium chloride is harmful, or redefine veganism so narrowly that it no longer means opposition to animal abuse but instead functions as a symbolic dietary label.

Under a definition of veganism that is logically consistent and ethically universal, salt is not vegan.
