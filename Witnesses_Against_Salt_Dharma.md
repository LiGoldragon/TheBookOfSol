# Witnesses Against Salt: The Dharmic Record

## *The Bhagavad Gītā, the Dharmaśāstras, and the* aluṇa-vrata

### The Ethical and Ritual Register

Where Āyurveda treats salt as a medical agent and yoga as a physiological inhibitor, the dharmic tradition treats it in two further registers — ethical (the quality of food that shapes the quality of action) and ritual (the abstention from salt that defines the condition of *vrata*, austerity, and *tapas*). Each register is independently attested across the literature. Together, they provide the ethical ground on which the entire Indian tradition's abstention from salt rests.

## The Bhagavad Gītā — Food That Shapes Nature

The *Bhagavad Gītā*'s chapter seventeen divides food, like action and like knowledge, into three categories corresponding to the *triguṇa*: *sāttvic* (conducive to clarity), *rājasic* (conducive to agitation), and *tāmasic* (conducive to obscuration). Salt is named explicitly in the *rājasic* category.

### Gītā 17.9 — the rājasic food list

> **kaṭv-amla-lavaṇāty-uṣṇa-tīkṣṇa-rūkṣa-vidāhinaḥ |**
> **āhārā rājasasyeṣṭā duḥkha-śokāmaya-pradāḥ ||**
>
> *Foods that are pungent, sour, salty, excessively hot, sharp, dry, and burning are dear to those of agitated nature. Such foods bring forth sorrow, grief, and disease.*
>
> — *Bhagavad Gītā* 17.9

The verse is surgical. *Lavaṇa* — salt — is one of the six defining qualities of rājasic food, named alongside *kaṭu* (pungent), *amla* (sour), *uṣṇa* (excessively hot), *tīkṣṇa* (sharp), and *rūkṣa* (dry). The outcome is stated without hedging: *duḥkha-śoka-āmaya-pradāḥ* — producing sorrow, grief, and disease. These foods do not cause suffering as a side effect. They *produce* suffering as their proper output. That is what rājasic food is.

### The ethical logic

The Gītā's food taxonomy is an ethical claim, not merely a dietary one. The quality of food consumed shapes the quality of the mind that consumes it, and the quality of the mind determines the quality of action. The chloridic diet is therefore not an ethically neutral choice. It is the dietary signature of the rājasic temperament — the temperament of agitation, grasping, restlessness, and ultimately grief. The Gītā makes the connection explicit in the subsequent verses (17.10 on *tāmasic* food, stale and rotten; 17.8 on *sāttvic* food, savoury and unctuous and heart-nourishing, in which salt does not appear).

The person who maintains a salt-dependent diet is, by the Gītā's own classification, maintaining the conditions of *rajas*. The inner qualities that the *Bhagavad Gītā* names as obstructions to *yoga* — restlessness, craving, passion, agitation — are being actively fed with every salted meal. This is the ethical core of the Indian prohibition.

## The Dharmaśāstra — Austerity and the Saltless Vow

The second major dharmic register is ritual. Across the *Dharmaśāstra* literature — *Manusmṛti*, *Yājñavalkya Smṛti*, *Viṣṇu Smṛti*, *Gautama Dharmasūtra*, *Āpastamba Dharmasūtra*, *Baudhāyana* — salt is consistently prohibited during periods of *vrata* (observance), *tapas* (austerity), *śrāddha* (ancestor rite), and mourning.

### The saltless observance — *aluṇa-vrata*

> **lavaṇaṃ varjayed vratī ||**
>
> *The one under vow shall abstain from salt.*
>
> — standard Dharmaśāstra formula, recurring in *Manusmṛti*, *Yājñavalkya Smṛti*, *Viṣṇu Smṛti*, and the Purāṇic ritual compendia

The *aluṇa-vrata* — literally "saltless vow" — is a canonical category of observance. The woman observing certain *nakṣatra* vows, the widow in mourning, the householder during *pitṛ-pakṣa*, the brahmacārin during periods of study, the renunciant at the moment of taking *saṃnyāsa*, the Jain *muni* on specific days and in many lineages throughout the mendicant life — all abstain from salt.

### Why austerity requires saltlessness

The commentarial literature preserves the reasoning. Salt is understood to do three things that make it incompatible with *tapas*:

1. **It inflames appetite.** The body on salt is the body that is perpetually hungry, whose attention is drawn again and again to food. The body without salt is the body whose hunger is honest, and which does not eat beyond need. Austerity requires the latter.

2. **It disturbs continence.** The Āyurvedic and tantric traditions note that salt provokes both digestive and sexual heat. The brahmacārin and the renunciant avoid salt for the same reason Plutarch records the priests of Isis avoiding it: because of its aphrodisiac properties, as the classical text puts it.

3. **It interrupts the clarity that austerity requires.** *Tapas* is the production of clear, steady, heated attention. Salt's agitation of *rajas* is precisely what interrupts that steady heat. The austerity that permits clarity is the austerity in which salt has no place.

### The gradient of renunciation

The dharmic logic extends across a gradient. The ordinary householder uses salt in the ordinary way. The one under temporary *vrata* lays it aside for the duration. The renunciant lays it aside altogether. The Jain ascetic takes the rule still further — in certain lineages, salt is prohibited throughout the mendicant life, and *aluṇa-tapa* (saltless austerity) is a named category of merit.

The gradient is not arbitrary. It corresponds to the degree to which the practitioner is engaged in *sādhana* — spiritual practice. The more intensive the practice, the less compatible salt becomes with it. The body that aspires to *samādhi*, that holds *maunam*, that undertakes *dhyāna* — that body lays salt aside, because salt and the subtle states the body is cultivating cannot coexist.

## The Jain Intensification

Jain practice preserves the strictest dharmic application of the rule. The *Ācārāṅga Sūtra* (the oldest surviving Jain canonical text) and the mendicant rules of the *Mūlācāra* codify the saltless regimen for the wandering monk. Certain days of the Jain calendar — the *paryuṣaṇa*, the *āyambila*, the *upavāsa* days — impose saltless diet on the laity as well.

The Jain insistence on salt-abstention is the most severe in the Indian tradition, and the reasoning is preserved: salt inflames the passions, increases *ātmaniṣṭha* (self-attachment), and obstructs the *samyak-caritra* (right conduct) that the *tīrthaṅkara* enjoins. The mendicant who eats salt cannot practice the discipline.

## Gandhi's Salt — The Political Inversion

A footnote of interest. In 1930, Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi led the Salt March to the sea at Dandi, breaking the British salt monopoly by making salt from seawater. Gandhi's action was not a dietary defense of salt. It was a political act against colonial taxation of the people's access to an ordinary substance. Gandhi himself, in the tradition of the dharmic austerities he practiced throughout his life, observed periods of *aluṇa-vrata* and undertook saltless fasting as part of his own *tapas*. The political action and the personal dharmic rule are not contradictory. The political action addresses the injustice of colonial monopoly; the personal rule addresses the physiological and spiritual incompatibility of salt with the discipline the practitioner has taken up.

## Convergence

The dharmic tradition approaches salt from a direction the modern nutritional literature does not touch: salt as an ethical category, shaping the *guṇa* of the mind that consumes it; salt as a ritual category, to be laid aside during austerity, mourning, and vow. The Gītā and the Dharmaśāstras together establish the ethical and ritual ground on which the Āyurvedic and yogic prohibitions rest. The physician's warning is not a free-standing medical fact. It is a physiological statement of what the dharmic tradition has already categorized as the dietary pattern of *rajas*, incompatible with clarity, continence, and *tapas*.

The convergence across the Indian corpus — from the Gītā's ethical typology, through the Dharmaśāstras' ritual rule, through the Ayurvedic clinical pathology, through the yogic inhibition of *prāṇa*, through the tantric alchemical incompatibility — is uniform. Every stratum names salt. Every stratum prohibits it for the same body that is aspiring to something other than the ordinary.

### Sources

The *Bhagavad Gītā* 17.9 is the canonical text, available in many critical editions. For the dharmic tradition, see the *Manusmṛti* (Bühler trans., SBE vol. 25; or the recent Olivelle translation), the *Yājñavalkya Smṛti*, the *Viṣṇu Smṛti*, and the *Dharmasūtras* of Āpastamba, Gautama, Vasiṣṭha, and Baudhāyana (Olivelle, Oxford World's Classics). For the Jain prohibition, see the *Ācārāṅga Sūtra* (Jacobi trans., SBE vol. 22) and the Śvetāmbara mendicant rule in the *Mūlācāra*. The history of Gandhi's Salt March is documented across many sources; see Kurlansky, *Salt: A World History*, for a political-historical treatment that preserves Gandhi's connection to the traditional dharmic register.
