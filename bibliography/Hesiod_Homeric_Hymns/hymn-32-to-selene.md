# Homeric Hymn XXXII — To Selene

Translation: Hugh G. Evelyn-White, *Hesiod, the Homeric Hymns, and Homerica* (Loeb / Heinemann, 1920), pp. 459–461.

> "And next, sweet voiced Muses, daughters of Zeus, well-skilled in song, tell of the long-winged Moon."\
> — *Homeric Hymn* 32.1–2, trans. Evelyn-White

> "From her immortal head a radiance is shown from heaven and embraces earth; and great is the beauty that ariseth from her shining light. The air, unlit before, glows with the light of her golden crown, and her rays beam clear, whensoever bright Selene having bathed her lovely body in the waters of Ocean, and donned her far-gleaming raiment, and yoked her strong-necked, shining team, drives on her long-maned horses at full speed, at eventime in the mid-month: then her great orbit is full and then her beams shine brightest as she increases. So she is a sure token and a sign to mortal men."\
> — *Homeric Hymn* 32.3–13, trans. Evelyn-White

> "Once the Son of Cronos was joined with her in love; and she conceived and bare a daughter Pandia, exceeding lovely amongst the deathless gods."\
> — *Homeric Hymn* 32.14–16, trans. Evelyn-White

> "Hail, white-armed goddess, bright Selene, mild, bright-tressed queen! And now I will leave you and sing the glories of men half divine, whose deeds minstrels, the servants of the Muses, celebrate with lovely lips."\
> — *Homeric Hymn* 32.17–20, trans. Evelyn-White

## Notes for The Book of Sol

- **She does not pierce — she embraces.** Where Helios (Hymn 31) "pierces," "beams," "dazzles," Selene's light "embraces earth" and "ariseth from her shining light." Not emission outward but diffusion around. The verbs are receptive and enveloping. This is the lunar signature in archaic Greek: light *as surround*, not *as arrow*.
- **Bathes in Ocean before shining.** Before she drives her team, Selene "bathes her lovely body in the waters of Ocean." Her brightness is preceded by immersion in the primordial water — wet preparation, not dry kindling. The moon's relation to water is not metaphor; it is ritual sequence.
- **Full orbit at mid-month.** The hymn fixes the moon's peak beauty at the middle of the month — *eventime in the mid-month* — not at any fixed daily hour like the sun. Luna's time is counted in cycles (fortnights, months), not in positions on a daily arc. This is her temporal signature: she *is* the calendar. "A sure token and a sign to mortal men" (*τέκμωρ δὲ βροτοῖς καὶ σῆμα*) — the moon *is* the measure.
- **Pandia, the all-bright daughter.** Selene by Zeus bears Pandia, "the all-shining," whose name means "utterly-bright-one." Lunar generativity — the moon does not merely reflect Sol; she bears offspring with the sky-father. She is cosmically productive, not derivative.
- **Crown of gold, silver limbs.** "White-armed goddess" (*θεὰ λευκώλενε*) + "golden crown" — the moon is described with silver for her body and gold for her light-ornament. The gold is her *inherited* illumination (from Helios through the cosmic family); the silver is her *own* substance.
- **Long-winged.** *Τανυσίπτερον* — "long-winged" — is an epithet normally applied to birds. Applied to Selene it either figures her passage "like a bird through the air" or means "far-flying." Evelyn-White's note: "cp. Hesiod, *Works and Days* 210 as applied to Selene it may merely indicate her passage, like a bird, through the air, or mean 'far-flying.'" The moon as winged traveller — not charioteer alone but also bird.
