# Hesiod, Theogony — the birth of Helios, Selene, and Eos

Translation: Hugh G. Evelyn-White, *Hesiod, the Homeric Hymns, and Homerica* (Loeb / Heinemann, 1920), p. 105. Standard Theogony line-numbering places this passage at 371–374.

> "And Theia was subject in love to Hyperion and bare great Helius (Sun) and clear Selene (Moon) and Eos (Dawn) who shines upon all that are on earth, and upon the deathless Gods who live in the wide heaven."\
> — Hesiod, *Theogony* 371–374, trans. Evelyn-White

## Context (immediately preceding and following)

The passage sits within the second generation of Titans. Theia and Hyperion are both children of Gaia (Earth) and Ouranos (Heaven). Their union produces the three luminaries. The verse immediately before catalogues the Oceanides, the verse immediately after recounts Eurybia's union with Crius producing Astraeus (Starry), Pallas, and Perses.

## Notes for The Book of Sol

- **The oldest Greek genealogy of Luna.** Hesiod's *Theogony* (c. 700 BCE) is the earliest systematic Greek cosmogony. By placing Sol, Luna, and Dawn as siblings born of *Theia* (the goddess of the divine light-quality, θεῖα = "divine") and *Hyperion* ("the one who goes above"), the earliest stratum of Greek tradition refuses the hierarchy Sol > Luna. They are *co-descended*. Luna is not Sol's dependent; they are children of a shared parent-principle of divinely-moving-above-ness.
- **"Clear Selene."** *Λαμπράν τε Σελήνην* — "and clear Selene." The epithet is *lamprán* (bright, clear, shining). Luna in Hesiod is not dim or reflected — she is luminous in her own description. Bright and clear, like Helios but with the feminine adjective-ending signaling her distinct gender. The archaic Greek imagination did not yet contain the later (post-Aristarchus) physics of a non-luminous reflecting moon; Selene simply *shines*.
- **Eos is their sister.** The triple — Sun, Moon, Dawn — forms one genealogical cluster. The Dawn is neither subordinate to Sun nor Moon, but their third. Where *The Book of Sol* currently works a solar/lunar binary, Hesiod hints at a triadic structure: the morning/twilight threshold (Eos) as the third luminary-principle. Worth noting for the 360-phases-of-Sol framework which already has a sunrise/sunset register.
- **Shines upon mortals *and* immortals.** Eos — and by implication the whole cluster of luminaries — shines upon *both* those on earth *and* upon "the deathless Gods who live in the wide heaven." Luna's light is not only for mortals; it reaches the gods too. The lunar principle has a double audience in archaic Greek cosmology, reinforcing Plutarch's later reading of Luna as threshold between terrestrial and celestial.
