# Plutarch — De facie quae in orbe lunae apparet

Translation: Harold Cherniss (prose) and W. C. Helmbold (verse), *Plutarch: Moralia, Volume XII*, Loeb Classical Library 406 (Harvard / Heinemann, 1957).

Stephanus numbers (e.g. 935C, 943A, 944C) are the canonical pagination for Plutarch's *Moralia*, preserved in every edition.

## I — The moon is a holy earth, not a fire (935C–D)

> "It is in fact not incredible or wonderful that the moon, if she has nothing corrupted or slimy in her but garners pure light from heaven and is filled with warmth, which is fire not glowing or raging but moist and harmless and in its natural state, has got open regions of marvellous beauty and mountains flaming bright and has zones of royal purple with gold and silver not scattered in her depths but bursting forth in abundance on the plains or openly visible on the smooth heights."\
> — Plutarch, *De facie* 935C, trans. Cherniss

> "The honourable repute of the moon is surely not impaired nor is her divinity because she is held by men to be a (celestial and) holy earth rather than, as the Stoics say, a fire turbid and dreggish."\
> — Plutarch, *De facie* 935D, trans. Cherniss

## II — The moon as the earth-like middle body (944B–C)

> "It is no such thing, however; but just as our earth contains gulfs that are deep and extensive, one here pouring in towards us through the Pillars of Heracles and outside the Caspian and the Red Sea with its gulfs, so those features are depths and hollows of the moon. The largest of them is called 'Hecate's Recess,' where the souls suffer and exact penalties for whatever they have endured or committed after having already become daemons."\
> — Plutarch, *De facie* 944B–C, trans. Cherniss

## III — The tripartition: earth gives body, moon gives soul, sun gives mind (943A)

> "Most people rightly hold man to be composite but wrongly hold him to be composed of only two parts. The reason is that they suppose mind to be somehow part of soul, thus erring no less than those who believe soul to be part of body, for in the same degree as soul is superior to body so is mind better and more divine than soul. The result of soul and body commingled is the irrational or the affective factor, whereas of mind and soul the conjunction produces reason; and of these the former is source of pleasure and pain, the latter of virtue and vice."\
> — Plutarch, *De facie* 943A, trans. Cherniss

> "In the composition of these three factors earth furnishes the body, the moon the soul, and the sun furnishes mind to man for the purpose of his generation — even as it furnishes light to the moon herself."\
> — Plutarch, *De facie* 943A, trans. Cherniss

## IV — The two deaths: Demeter's earth, Persephone's moon (943B–C)

> "As to the death we die, one death reduces man from three factors to two and another reduces him from two to one; and the former takes place in the earth that belongs to Demeter (wherefore 'to make an end' is called 'to render one's life to her' and Athenians used in olden times to call the dead 'Demetrians'), the latter in the moon that belongs to Phersephone, and associated with the former is Hermes the terrestrial, with the latter Hermes the celestial."\
> — Plutarch, *De facie* 943B, trans. Cherniss

> "While the goddess here dissociates the soul from the body swiftly and violently, Phersephone gently and by slow degrees detaches the mind from the soul and has therefore been called 'single-born' because the best part of man is 'born single' when separated off by her."\
> — Plutarch, *De facie* 943B, trans. Cherniss

## V — The interlunar passage of souls between earth and moon (943C–D)

> "When the soul has been separated from the body and must wander in the region between earth and moon for a period not all of equal length, but the unjust and licentious suffer penalties for their offences, whereas the good must in the gentlest part of the air, which they call 'the meadows of Hades,' pass a certain set time sufficient to purge and blow away the pollutions contracted from the body as from an evil stench."\
> — Plutarch, *De facie* 943C, trans. Cherniss (paraphrased following the textual restoration)

## VI — The moon's two hemispheres: Elysium above, Persephone's house below (944C)

> "For the souls pass through them [the moon's hollows], now to the side of the moon that faces heaven, now back again to the side that faces earth. The side of the moon toward heaven is named 'Elysian plain,' the side here is called 'house of counter-terrestrial Persephone.'"\
> — Plutarch, *De facie* 944C, trans. Cherniss

## VII — Daemons do not stay on the moon: they descend (944D)

> "The daemons do not always remain upon the moon; they descend here to take charge of oracles, they attend and participate in the highest of the mystic rituals, they act as warders against misdeeds and chastisers of them, and they flash forth as saviours both in war and at sea. For whatever they do in these matters not fairly but in wrath or for an unjust favour or out of envy they are penalized, for they are cast out upon earth again and confined in human bodies."\
> — Plutarch, *De facie* 944D, trans. Cherniss

## Notes for The Book of Sol

- **The moon is the soul-place.** The single most important doctrine for a Luna chapter that honours Luna's own nature: in Plutarch's reading, Luna is not Sol's mirror or Sol's negative; she is the *middle term*, the place where soul lives between incarnations. Sol contributes mind (νοῦς), Earth contributes body (σῶμα), Moon contributes soul (ψυχή). Luna is the middle substance. The human being is literally a Sun-Moon-Earth composite and disassembles back into those three.
- **Two deaths, not one.** The first death is at Demeter's earth: body separates from soul. The second death is at Persephone's moon: soul separates from mind. The moon is the site of the more gradual, more gentle, more elective dissolution — "gently and by slow degrees." This is Luna's signature as a threshold rather than an endpoint.
- **Hemisphere toward heaven = Elysium; hemisphere toward earth = Persephone's house.** Luna is explicitly two-faced cosmologically. One side is the blessed plain of the liberated, the other is the house of the descending one. This is the ancient root of later traditions about the moon's "dark side" as chthonic.
- **Holy earth, not a fire.** Plutarch's polemic is against the Stoic doctrine that the moon is "a fire turbid and dreggish." His counter-claim: she is "a celestial and holy earth" with regions, mountains, plains, metallic veins. Luna has substance, weight, locus — she is not an image or a reflection, she is a world.
- **Her warmth is moist fire, not raging fire.** Plutarch's signature Luna adjective-set: "fire not glowing or raging but moist and harmless and in its natural state." Sol burns; Luna is warm-moist. This maps directly onto the Book of Sol's lunar-pillar doctrine of ghee (moist, cohesive, nourishing) as the lunar physical medium — and is the Greek cosmological parallel to the Vedic doctrine of Soma-rasa.
- **Daemons descend from the moon.** The lunar inhabitants do not stay. They come down as oracle-guardians, as ritual-partakers, as saviours in war and at sea. Luna is not isolation but *circulation*. She sends her population down and receives them back. Parallels the pitṛyāna/devayāna doctrine of the Upaniṣads (souls descend as rain and return to the moon through ritual).
- **Hecate's Recess.** The largest hollow on the moon is the place where "souls suffer and exact penalties for whatever they have endured or committed after having already become daemons." Luna is not only Elysium — she is also the site of the reckoning. The two faces again.
- **Luna as "counter-earth" (ἀντίχθων).** Plutarch's phrase *Phersephone antichthonos* — Persephone who is *counter-earth* — places Luna as the mirror-below-heaven of the earth-below-heaven. The moon is another earth, reversed. Useful Luna-identification: not Sol's complement, but Earth's uncanny double.
- **Cross-reference.** The Vedic/Upaniṣadic parallel is astonishing: Chāndogya 5.10 has souls traveling to the moon after death and being consumed by the gods as Soma; Kauṣītaki 1 has the moon as the gatekeeper who asks "who are you?" and releases those who answer correctly. Plutarch's moon-as-soul-threshold and the Upaniṣadic moon-as-soul-gate are separate cultural elaborations of the same ancient cosmological intuition about Luna.
