# Ṛgveda — Soma, the Moon, and the Solar–Lunar Pair

Translation: Ralph T. H. Griffith, *The Hymns of the Ṛgveda* (Chowkhamba / Motilal, 1889–92; complete reprint).

The Ṛgveda's lunar material is concentrated in three places: (1) Maṇḍala 9 in its entirety, dedicated to Soma Pavamāna — the self-purifying Soma-god identical with the moon; (2) Maṇḍala 10, Hymn 85 ("Sūryā's Bridal"), the wedding of the sun's daughter Sūryā to Soma the Moon-bridegroom; (3) Maṇḍala 10, Hymn 90 (Puruṣa Sūkta), line 13, in which the Moon is born from Puruṣa's mind and the Sun from his eye.

## I — The Puruṣa-Sūkta: Moon from mind, Sun from eye (RV 10.90.13)

> "The Moon was gendered from his mind, and from his eye the Sun had birth; Indra and Agni from his mouth were born, and Vāyu from his breath."\
> — *Ṛgveda* 10.90.13, trans. Griffith

*candramā manaso jātaś cakṣoḥ sūryo ajāyata — mukhād indraś ca agniś ca prāṇād vāyur ajāyata*

This stanza is the earliest Vedic statement of the cosmic body's organology. Each luminary is born from a distinct organ of the primordial Person (Puruṣa) whose dismemberment becomes the universe. **The Moon is born from the mind (manas).** **The Sun is born from the eye (cakṣus).** Mind and eye are not the same faculty — the eye *sees*, the mind *moves and shapes thought* — and so the two luminaries, though cosmologically paired, are not the same principle. The Moon is inner (mental, interior-illuminating, reflective-in-consciousness) while the Sun is outer (visual, outward-illuminating, object-making). The Puruṣa-Sūkta encodes at the origin of Vedic cosmology the distinction later developed by the Upaniṣads into the doctrine of manas (moon-faculty) and cakṣus-buddhi (sun-faculty).

## II — Sūryā's Bridal: Soma the Moon wedding the Sun's Daughter (RV 10.85)

> "Truth is the base that bears the earth; by Sūrya are the heavens sustained. By Law the Ādityas stand secure, and Soma holds his place in heaven."\
> — *Ṛgveda* 10.85.1, trans. Griffith

> "By Soma are the Ādityas strong, by Soma mighty is the earth. Thus Soma in the midst of all these constellations hath his place."\
> — *Ṛgveda* 10.85.2, trans. Griffith

> "One thinks, when they have brayed the plant, that he hath drunk the Soma's juice; of him whom Brahmans truly know as Soma no one ever tastes."\
> — *Ṛgveda* 10.85.3, trans. Griffith

> "Soma, secured by sheltering rules, guarded by hymns in Bṛhatī, Thou standest listening to the stones: none tastes of thee who dwells on earth."\
> — *Ṛgveda* 10.85.4, trans. Griffith

> "When they begin to drink thee, then, O God, thou swellest out again. Vāyu is Soma's guardian God. The Moon is that which shapes the years."\
> — *Ṛgveda* 10.85.5, trans. Griffith

> "Soma was he who wooed the maid: the groomsmen were both Aśvins, when the Sun-God Savitar bestowed his willing Sūryā on her Lord."\
> — *Ṛgveda* 10.85.9, trans. Griffith

> "Her spirit was the bridal car; the covering thereof was heaven: Bright were both Steers that drew it, when Sūryā approached her husband's home."\
> — *Ṛgveda* 10.85.10, trans. Griffith

## III — The Ninth Maṇḍala is the Soma-Maṇḍala

Griffith's editorial headnote at *Ṛgveda* 9.1:

> "Nearly all the hymns of this Book are addressed to the deified Soma juice, or to Soma, or Indu, the Moon, who as containing the celestial nectar, the drink of the Gods, is identified with the Soma-plant and its exhilarating juice. As the Moon-God pours down his ambrosial rain through the sieve of heaven, he is addressed and worshipped as Pavamāna (Self-Purifying), represented by the Soma juice as it undergoes purification by flowing through the wool which is used as a filter or strainer."\
> — Griffith, editorial headnote to *Ṛgveda* 9.1

The opening verses of the Soma-Maṇḍala:

> "In sweetest and most gladdening stream flow pure, O Soma, on the way, pressed out for Indra, for his drink."\
> — *Ṛgveda* 9.1.1, trans. Griffith

> "By means of this eternal fleece may Sūrya's Daughter purify thy Soma that is foaming forth."\
> — *Ṛgveda* 9.1.6, trans. Griffith

## Notes for The Book of Sol

- **The triple identity Plant=Juice=Moon=God.** The single most important Vedic doctrine for a Luna chapter: Soma is simultaneously (a) a physical plant, (b) the juice pressed from that plant in the sacrifice, (c) the Moon in the sky, and (d) a deity. There is no separation. When the Soma-plant is ritually pressed, what is being pressed *is the Moon*. When the Moon waxes and wanes, the celestial Soma is being filled and drained. The body of the Moon and the body of the plant and the body of the juice and the body of the god are one substance under different aspects. Griffith's headnote makes this explicit.
- **"The Moon is that which shapes the years" (10.85.5).** The lunar signature is time — cyclical, periodic, month-and-year-giving. Sūrya gives the day; the Moon gives the month, and through the thirteen-month lunar year, shapes even the solar year. The Greek counterpart (Homeric Hymn 32: "a sure token and a sign to mortal men," *tekmōr kai sēma brotois*) is the same intuition in Greek. Luna = measure, Sol = light. Both are time-giving but on different scales.
- **The Moon as food of the gods that no earth-dweller tastes (10.85.3–4).** "One thinks, when they have brayed the plant, that he hath drunk the Soma's juice; of him whom Brahmans truly know as Soma no one ever tastes." Luna is the inner drink — what is physically drunk is only the outer analogue. The real Soma is unavailable to earth-bound consumption. This is the scriptural root of the Upaniṣadic doctrine (CU 5.10, BU 6.2) that the dead pass into the moon and become Soma for the gods. It is also the archaic backing for the project's ghee-as-lunar-substance doctrine: ghee is the terrestrial shadow of an un-terrestrial nectar.
- **Waxing as refilling after divine consumption (10.85.5).** "When they begin to drink thee, then, O God, thou swellest out again." The moon's cyclical waxing is the *replenishment mechanism* after the gods drink. The moon is literally the open cup the gods drain by fortnightly draughts — but it refills because the soma-substance is inexhaustible. This is the earliest attested Vedic rationale for lunar periodicity. Luna is not merely changing — she is being *poured out and refilled*.
- **Soma wooing Sūryā (10.85.9).** The marriage of the Moon (Soma) to the Sun's daughter (Sūryā) is the Vedic counterpart of the Hesiodic sibling-structure (Theia and Hyperion beget Helios, Selene, Eos) and the Plutarchian three-way composition (earth body + moon soul + sun mind). The two luminaries are linked at the origin — but in the Vedic case, as *bride and groom*, not as siblings. Luna is wedded to Sol's daughter; the solar–lunar union is a marriage at the base of the cosmos. The Aśvins (dawn-twins) are the groomsmen; Savitar (the impelling Sun) is the father who bestows the bride.
- **Soma as the keystone of heaven (10.85.1–2).** "By Law the Ādityas stand secure, and Soma holds his place in heaven. By Soma are the Ādityas strong, by Soma mighty is the earth." The Ādityas (solar beings — Varuṇa, Mitra, Aryaman, etc.) are *made strong by Soma*. The earth itself is made mighty by Soma. The Moon is not Sol's satellite in Vedic astronomy; he is the strengthener of solar beings and the foundation of earth's might. Luna feeds Sol's court, not the other way around.
- **Pavamāna — the Self-Purifying one.** The Moon-Soma is the god who *purifies himself*. The filtering through wool (the sacrificial strainer) is his essential action. Not purity received from elsewhere but purity self-generated through flowing. Useful for Luna's Yang-register: she is the one who is always washing herself, always filtering, always rising clarified from her own motion.
- **Sūrya's Daughter's eternal fleece (9.1.6).** The strainer through which Soma is filtered is called "Sūrya's Daughter's eternal fleece" — Sūryā-duhitā's woollen sieve. The Sun's daughter (the same Sūryā who weds Soma in 10.85) is the *filter* through which the lunar substance is made pure. Solar female agency *purifies* lunar male substance — a gender/cosmic inversion of the usual alignment.
