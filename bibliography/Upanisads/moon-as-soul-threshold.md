# The Upaniṣads — the Moon as soul-threshold, the two paths, Pañcāgni-vidyā

Translations quoted below are from Patrick Olivelle, *The Early Upaniṣads* (Oxford 1998) unless noted. Other editions on the shelf: Hume, Radhakrishnan, Gambhirananda, Deussen.

The Upaniṣads inherit and transform the Vedic moon-Soma doctrine. Where the Ṛgveda had Soma as the inexhaustible cup that the gods drink and refill, the early prose Upaniṣads extend this into a cosmology of rebirth: the souls of the dead go to the moon, become Soma, are drunk by the gods, fall as rain, enter plants, are eaten by men, and are born again through women. This is the *Pañcāgni-vidyā* ("doctrine of the five fires") and its corollary doctrine of the *two paths* — the path of the gods (devayāna) that does not return and the path of the fathers (pitṛyāna) that cycles through the moon.

## I — Chāndogya Upaniṣad 5.3–10 and Bṛhadāraṇyaka 6.2 — the Pañcāgni and the two paths

The doctrine appears in almost identical form in two early prose Upaniṣads: Chāndogya 5.3–10 and Bṛhadāraṇyaka 6.2. The king Pravāhaṇa Jaivali asks Śvetaketu five questions, which Śvetaketu's father Uddālaka Āruṇi must then go to the king to learn:

1. Where do people go when they depart from this world?
2. How do they return?
3. Where do the two paths — of the gods and of the fathers — diverge?
4. Why does the further world never fill up?
5. In the fifth oblation, why do waters speak with a human voice?

The answer is the Pañcāgni-vidyā — the doctrine that the cosmos is a sequence of five fires, each consuming the previous one's offering, producing the next:

- **Fire 1 — the far world (dyuloka):** its fuel is the sun, its smoke the rays, its flame the day, its coals the moon, its sparks the stars. The gods offer faith (*śraddhā*) into this fire. From that offering arises **King Soma** — the moon.
- **Fire 2 — the rain-god (Parjanya):** its fuel is the year, its smoke the clouds, its flame the lightning, its coals the thunderbolt, its sparks the hail. The gods offer Soma (from fire 1) into this fire. From that offering arises **rain**.
- **Fire 3 — this world (pṛthivī):** its fuel is the earth, its smoke the fire, its flame the night, its coals the moon, its sparks the regions. The gods offer rain into this fire. From that offering arises **food**.
- **Fire 4 — man (puruṣa):** its fuel is the open mouth, its smoke the breath, its flame the speech, its coals the eye, its sparks the ear. The gods offer food into this fire. From that offering arises **semen**.
- **Fire 5 — woman (strī):** its fuel is the vulva, its smoke the hair, its flame the vagina, its coals the penetration, its sparks the pleasure. The gods offer semen into this fire. From that offering arises **the embryo**.

The soul travels this circuit. In death it ascends to the moon, where it *becomes* Soma — food of the gods. After the gods have consumed it (the waning), it is poured out as rain, becomes a plant, is eaten, becomes semen, and is reborn through woman. The moon is the hinge of the cycle.

Olivelle's editorial note at CU 5.10:

> "Rain, the dead people who were in the moon enter plants, which are eaten by a man..."\
> — Olivelle, *Early Upaniṣads*, notes

The iconic verse-summary of this doctrine is preserved in Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad 2.1.5 (which compresses the entire pañcāgni cycle into a single quatrain):

> "From him comes the fire whose firewood is the sun;\
> From the moon comes rain; plants grow upon earth;\
> And in the woman the man spills his seed—\
> From the Person have issued many creatures."\
> — *Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad* 2.1.5, trans. Olivelle

## II — Kauṣītaki Upaniṣad 1 — the Moon as gatekeeper, the "Who are you?" test

In Kauṣītaki 1.2 the doctrine takes its most dramatic form. Every soul that departs from this world arrives first at the moon. The moon is the *closed door*. It asks the arriving soul: *ko 'si — "Who are you?"*

Olivelle's editorial summary:

> "The closed door represented by the moon (KsU 1.2)… door to the heavenly world… The semen… I am you: on these somewhat difficult verses, see the comments of Bodewitz 1973, 54–61. In the JB (1.18), which was probably the original context of these verses, it is the seasons that lead the dead man to immortality, which explains why the verses are addressed to the seasons and not to the moon (see also KsU 1.6). Likewise, at SB 2.6.4.9 the seasons escort a man who has performed the Caturmāsya (seasonal) sacrifices. The fifteen parts refer to the digits of the moon (see BU 1.5.14 n.), while the comment 'who is born' may refer to the fact that the moon is being born continuously, although the words *prasuta* ('born') and *prasuta* ('pressed,' i.e., Soma) are often connected in these texts… All these epithets refer to the moon, from whom the semen (in the form of rain) is gathered."\
> — Olivelle, *Early Upaniṣads*, notes to KsU 1.2

The soul that answers correctly — identifying itself as Soma, as the fifteen-parted moon, as the rain-begetter — passes through the lunar door and proceeds to the world of Brahman, to the halls *Vijara*, *Ilya*, *Salajya*, *Aparājita*, *Vibhu*, *Vicakṣaṇa*, and *Amitaujas* — "ageless," "speech-or-earth," "invincible," "pervading," "far-shining," "unlimited power." The soul that cannot answer correctly is returned through the lunar door to rain, plants, food, woman, and rebirth.

## III — The sun-and-moon bracket in the upper Upaniṣads

At the highest pitch of upaniṣadic discourse, the luminaries appear as the *limit beyond which no image reaches*:

> "There the sun does not shine, nor the moon and stars; there lightning does not shine, of this common fire need we speak! Him alone, as he shines, do all things reflect; this whole world radiates with his light."\
> — *Kaṭha Upaniṣad* 2.2.15 (= Muṇḍaka 2.2.10 and Śvetāśvatara 6.14), trans. Olivelle

The Sun and Moon together mark the threshold of the visible. What is beyond them is imageless brahman. Within them is the whole cosmos of light.

## IV — The sun as eye, the moon as mind (the Upaniṣadic refrain)

The Puruṣa-Sūkta correspondence (moon=mind, sun=eye) becomes the standard Upaniṣadic formula:

> "His head is the fire, his eyes the sun and moon;\
> His ears are the quarters; his speech the Vedas disclosed;\
> His breath is the wind, his heart the universe;\
> and with his feet he is, indeed,\
> the inmost self of every being."\
> — *Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad* 2.1.4, trans. Olivelle

Note that Muṇḍaka 2.1.4 says *both* sun and moon are the cosmic person's eyes, departing slightly from the Puruṣa-Sūkta's strict moon-from-mind / sun-from-eye split. The later tradition reads this as two eyes of the one Puruṣa: the sun-eye sees the day, the moon-eye sees the night. The mind-connection of the moon is retained elsewhere (BU 1.5.14 n. in Olivelle: fifteen digits of the moon = functions of mind).

## Notes for The Book of Sol

- **The moon is the cosmos's threshold, not its destination.** Every soul that leaves this world comes first to the moon. The moon is *the door*. Elysium, pitṛloka, brahmaloka — whatever the further world is called — is reached only *through* the moon. This is the structural claim that is shared between the Upaniṣadic pitṛyāna doctrine and Plutarch's *De facie*: Luna is *where soul pauses between incarnations*, the threshold between mortal and immortal.
- **Become Soma to enter, or return to rain.** The moon's test is not arbitrary. What the soul must *become* in order to pass through the moon is Soma — the inexhaustible substance. Those who have not made themselves Soma-shaped are turned back into the rain-plant-food-semen-embryo cycle. The moon selects what is amṛta (deathless) and drains what is mṛta (mortal). The waxing-and-waning of the moon in the sky is the visible sign of this cosmological selection-and-return.
- **The Moon drains and refills.** The Vedic motif of the moon being drunk by the gods and swelling out again (RV 10.85.5) is taken up in the Upaniṣads as the cosmology of rebirth. The souls of the dead *are* what the gods drink. The moon's fortnightly cycle is the rhythm of divine consumption and replenishment. The phases of the moon are the pulse of the rebirth cycle.
- **Mind and the lunar digits.** BU 1.5.14 makes the standard equation: the sixteen kalās of the moon correspond to the sixteen functions/parts of the person. Manas (mind) is the fifteenth; the sixteenth is the immortal part that does not diminish. Luna is the cosmic diagram of the person's own waxing-and-waning parts. This is the later tantric sixteen-kalās-of-the-candra-maṇḍala at the sahasrāra (see Avalon).
- **Who are you?** The Kauṣītaki test — *ko 'si* — is the starting point for a Luna chapter that honours Luna's own nature. She is not a light or a signal; she is a *gatekeeper who asks a question*. Her verb is interrogation, not illumination. She accepts what answers rightly and returns what cannot.
- **Sun and moon as the bracket of the imageable.** The Kaṭha/Muṇḍaka/Śvetāśvatara refrain — "There the sun does not shine, nor the moon" — treats the two luminaries as a single bracketing pair. What is *within* their light is the cosmos of images; what is *beyond* their light is brahman. For this project's Sol/Luna framework, this is the Upaniṣadic authorization to treat Sol and Luna as the *twin pillars* of the imageable world rather than as master and servant, without flattening Luna into Sol's shadow.
- **Cross-reference to Plutarch.** The Upaniṣadic pitṛyāna and Plutarch's *De facie* share the structural intuition that **the moon is the place of soul-transit between incarnations**. They differ in direction: in the Upaniṣadic version souls come to the moon, become Soma, are drunk, return as rain; in Plutarch's version souls come to the moon, undergo a second death in which mind returns to the sun, and are reborn. Both agree that Luna is the hinge. This is arguably the oldest intercultural cosmological convergence attested in the project's source-texts.
