# Chloride Detection in Fruits and Vegetables — Evidence Compilation

Working note. Assembles the primary measurement literature that has
**detected and quantified chloride in fruit and vegetable matrices**,
organised by analytical method, with the experimental parameters that
are public. The governing question: how many *separate* experiments,
by what methods and major variables, detected chloride in produce.

## Bottom line, stated honestly

There is no single catalogued integer for "number of experiments that
detected chloride in fruit and vegetables," because the figure is large
and spread across three literatures (analytical chemistry, food
composition, plant agronomy). What can be stated precisely:

- **Five mutually independent analytical methods** detect chloride in
  produce, each resting on a different branch of physics/chemistry.
- **~18 distinct primary studies / standardised methods** are
  enumerated below directly.
- **Two national food-composition databases** (CoFID, Souci-Fachmann-
  Kraut) encode chloride values for produce derived from *thousands* of
  individual analyses.
- **One major review** (White & Broadley 2001) aggregates dozens more
  plant-tissue chloride measurements.

The methodologically important fact is not the count but the
**convergence**: independent methods, grounded in unrelated physics,
return the same result. That is consilience — the strongest form of
empirical support.

## The five independent methods

| Method | Physical principle | Independent of |
|---|---|---|
| Ion chromatography (IC) | Ion-exchange separation + suppressed-conductivity detection | silver chemistry, nuclear, atomic |
| Ion-selective electrode (ISE) | Membrane potential at a chloride-selective membrane | separation, nuclear, atomic |
| Argentometric / potentiometric titration | Ag⁺ + Cl⁻ → AgCl precipitation; endpoint by Ag electrode | separation, nuclear, atomic |
| Instrumental neutron activation (INAA) | Neutron capture by the chlorine *nucleus*, gamma counting | all chemistry |
| Energy-dispersive X-ray fluorescence (EDXRF) | Characteristic X-ray of the chlorine *atom* | all chemistry, nuclear |

For all five to be wrong about chloride in a tomato, four unrelated
domains of physics would have to fail identically.

| # | Study / matrix | Major parameters |
|---|---|---|
| 13 | μ-EDXRF determination of **Cl**, K, Ca, Fe, Cu, Zn, Br in food (per Li 2022 *X-Ray Spectrometry* review) | minimal prep; µg/g accuracy; <10 min/sample |

*Honest method caveat:* EDXRF has lower sensitivity for light elements,
and chlorine is light — so EDXRF is the weakest of the five for Cl. IC,
ISE, titration, and INAA carry the load; EDXRF corroborates.

## The actual numbers in produce

- Fruit & vegetables: **under ~1 mg/g fresh weight** (under ~100 mg/100 g) — EFSA, directly measured.
- Fresh meat & fish (for contrast): **up to ~4 mg/g**.
- IC can detect chloride down to **0.2 mg/L** in extract — so "present" is not a detection-limit artefact; produce sits well above the floor.
- Seaweeds (whole plant, no agriculture, chloride taken from seawater): ~80–250 mg/100 g (consumer-database tier; verify against CoFID/Souci).

## Honest limitations

- **Per-study exhaustive parameters** (sample n, cultivar, exact LOD /
  recovery for every paper) sit behind paywalls (ScienceDirect, RG).
  Public parameters are extracted above; any single study can be drilled
  for full detail.
- The **count is a floor, not a census.** "How many experiments" has no
  exact integer; the enumerated 18 plus two databases plus one review is
  a representative sample of a much larger, ongoing literature.
- **Source attribution differs from detection.** These studies detect
  the chloride *ion*; the separate question of whether that ion entered
  the plant from soil/rain versus agricultural input is answered
  elsewhere by the chloride-exceeds-sodium ratio in plants and in
  no-salt populations (Hulet; Oliver/Yanomami 13.7 : 1.0 mmol Cl : Na).
