# NaCl Toxicity — The Trial Record

The companion to [The Chloride Indictment](./The_Chloride_Indictment.md). Where the *Indictment* assembles the case in prose, this article catalogs the controlled-experiment evidence in compact form, trial by trial. The compound under test is chloride of sodium (NaCl), the same compound delivered as ordinary table salt, sea salt, rock salt, "Himalayan" salt, *fleur de sel*, kosher salt, and every other branded crystal at 97–99% NaCl by mass.

## The Question

The trials answer one question: when chloride of sodium is ingested, what happens? They answer it across four methodologies — human metabolic-ward trials, community randomized trials, animal ingestion models, and ion-substitution experiments that isolate which component of the compound does the damage.

## Human Trials

**DASH-Sodium (Sacks et al., *NEJM*, 2001).** Inpatient crossover, three graded levels of chloride of sodium intake, identical baseline diet otherwise. Blood pressure fell stepwise as chloride intake decreased. Every reduction produced measurable benefit; no threshold of innocuous dose emerged.

**Trials of Hypertension Prevention I & II (Cook et al., *BMJ*, 2007).** Long-term randomized intervention reducing chloride of sodium intake. The reduced-intake arms showed lower blood pressure throughout the trial; long-term follow-up showed fewer cardiovascular events. Benefit scaled with the duration of the reduction.

**Salt Substitute and Stroke Study — SSaSS (Neal et al., *NEJM*, 2021).** 20,995 participants across 600 villages in China. Intervention villages received a reduced-chloride substitute (partly KCl); control villages received ordinary chloride of sodium. Result: 14% fewer strokes, 13% fewer major cardiovascular events. The only systematic difference between arms was the quantity of chloride ingested.

## Animal Trials

**Dahl Salt-Sensitive Rats (Dahl, *J Exp Med*, 1962).** Two strains bred for differential salt response, fed chow loaded with chloride of sodium or stripped of it. The salt-sensitive strain developed severe hypertension, renal injury, and early death; the salt-resistant strain was largely spared. The agent was the compound; the variation was only in the body's capacity to compensate. The Dahl model has been replicated in every generation since.

## Substitution Trials — Isolating the Chloride Anion

These are the load-bearing experiments. They give equivalent sodium loads paired with two different anions, and ask which anion drives the injury.

**Kurtz and Morris, human (*J Clin Invest*, 1983).** Healthy volunteers given controlled diets with capsules of chloride of sodium or capsules of sodium bicarbonate, equal sodium content. Chloride of sodium raised blood pressure and increased urinary calcium excretion. Bicarbonate did not. When the cation is delivered without chloride, it ceases to be the same agent.

**Schmidlin et al., rat (*Hypertension*, 2007).** Stroke-prone hypertensive rats given equivalent sodium loads as either chloride of sodium or sodium bicarbonate. Hypertension and vascular damage followed the chloride arm only; the bicarbonate-loaded animals were spared.

**Luft et al. (*Kidney Int*, 1982).** Rat model with sodium delivered as chloride versus bicarbonate or citrate. Hypertension and renal injury tracked the chloride-bearing compound, not sodium in abstraction.

The substitution trials settle which component does the damage. Chloride is the agent; sodium is the cation it travels on. The vocabulary of "sodium intake" misdirects: it foregrounds the buffered cation and hides the reactive anion that drives the injury. The trials measure the anion; the language should follow the trials.

## A Population With Near-Zero Intake

**Yanomami (Oliver et al., *Circulation*, 1975).** A people of the upper Amazon with no added chloride of sodium in their diet. Urinary chloride excretion approximately one millimole per day — roughly two orders of magnitude below modern Western intake. Blood pressure low across the lifespan; hypertension absent as a disease. The population is the closest natural approximation to a zero-intake control arm. The outcomes favor the zero arm.

## Mechanism

The toxic action of chloride of sodium runs along four documented pathways (Cappuccio et al., *J Hypertens*, 2000, and the converging literature):

- **Volume.** Chloride expands the extracellular fluid compartment and suppresses renin activity, driving blood pressure upward.
- **Renal.** Chronic chloride load produces glomerular strain, proteinuria, and renal hypertrophy.
- **Vascular.** Chloride drives endothelial dysfunction and progressive arterial stiffening.
- **Calcium.** Chloride ingestion increases urinary calcium excretion, contributing to bone demineralization over time.

## Dose–Response

The relationship is monotonic across every methodology. Metabolic-ward trials show stepwise rise in blood pressure with stepwise increase in chloride intake. Community RCTs show measurable cardiovascular benefit from even modest reductions. Animal models show direct, graded relationships between chloride load and severity of organ injury. No safe upper bound has been demonstrated; from the data that exist, the least-harm endpoint is zero.

## Synthesis

Across more than a century of experimentation, in four methodologies, on multiple species and continents, the conclusion is one and the same. Ingestion of chloride of sodium elevates blood pressure, injures kidneys, and raises cardiovascular risk. These effects do not occur to the same extent with other salts of sodium. Chloride of sodium is therefore a cardiovascular and renal toxicant with a clear dose–response relationship. Every added gram of intake increases measurable risk; reduction or replacement consistently improves outcomes.
