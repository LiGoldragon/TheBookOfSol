# Witnesses Against Salt: The Chinese Record

## *The Huangdi Neijing on salt as the flavor that labors the body*

### The Five-Flavor System

Classical Chinese medicine, preserved in the *Huangdi Neijing* (黃帝內經, "The Yellow Emperor's Inner Canon"), categorizes all tastes under a five-fold system that parallels and predates the Indian six-taste doctrine. The five flavors are *suān* 酸 (sour), *kǔ* 苦 (bitter), *gān* 甘 (sweet), *xīn* 辛 (pungent/acrid), and *xián* 鹹 (salty). Each flavor corresponds to one of the five phases (*wu xing*), one of the five viscera (*zang*), and has both nourishing and damaging effects depending on the quantity consumed.

The *Neijing*'s treatment of the salty flavor is consistent across its chapters: *xián* is the flavor of the kidney (*shèn* 腎) and, in correct proportion, supports kidney function. In excess, it produces the specific pathologies that modern cardiology now measures — vascular stiffening, skeletal demineralization, cardiac suppression, and circulatory stagnation. The Chinese tradition recorded these pathologies in the second century BCE, in almost exactly the clinical language modern medicine would re-formulate two thousand years later.

## Huangdi Neijing Suwen — The Direct Statements

### Suwen, Chapter 3 — on the great bones laboring

> **是故味過於鹹，大骨氣勞，短肌，心氣抑**
>
> *shì gù wèi guò yú xián, dà gǔ qì láo, duǎn jī, xīn qì yì*
>
> *Therefore when the flavor exceeds in saltiness, the great bones labor, the muscles shrink, and the heart-qi is suppressed.*
>
> — *Huangdi Neijing Suwen*, Chapter 3 (*Shēng qì tōng tiān lùn* 生氣通天論, "On the communication of vital qi with the heavens")

The three-fold clinical consequence of excess salt is named in nine Chinese characters.

- **大骨氣勞** (*dà gǔ qì láo*) — "the great bones labor." The skeletal frame, especially the larger long bones, is overstressed. The pathology modern medicine calls hypercalciuria-induced bone demineralization — the urinary loss of calcium that chronic chloride intake produces — is recorded here as *gǔ qì láo*, the "laboring of bone-qi."
- **短肌** (*duǎn jī*) — "the muscles shrink" or "the flesh is shortened." Muscle wasting under chronic salt load, again an observation the modern clinical literature has slowly re-derived (protein catabolism, vascular damage to capillary beds feeding the musculature).
- **心氣抑** (*xīn qì yì*) — "the heart-qi is suppressed." The cardiac function is inhibited, the vital current at the heart is dampened. The modern cardiologist would call this reduced stroke volume, cardiac hypertrophy with diminished output, or ventricular dysfunction under chronic pressure load.

Three injuries. Three precisely named organ systems. Bone, flesh, heart. The observation could have been written last year.

### Suwen, Chapter 10 — the vessels congeal

> **多食鹹，則脈凝泣而變色**
>
> *duō shí xián, zé mài níng qì ér biàn sè*
>
> *Much eating of salt — then the vessels congeal, stagnate, and change color.*
>
> — *Huangdi Neijing Suwen*, Chapter 10 (*Wǔ zàng shēng chéng lùn* 五臟生成論, "On the generation of the five viscera")

The phrase **脈凝泣** (*mài níng qì*) — "the vessels congeal and stagnate" — is a specific classical Chinese medical term denoting the state that Western medicine calls *arteriosclerosis*: the hardening and stagnation of the vascular lumen. The classical Chinese word *níng* (凝) means "to congeal, to solidify, to freeze"; *qì* (泣) as a pathological term means "to stagnate, to become stuck, to fail to flow." Together, the two characters name precisely what happens to a blood vessel under chronic salt load: its inner wall thickens, its lumen narrows, the flow of blood through it becomes progressively more difficult. *Biàn sè* (變色), "changes color," refers to the visible discoloration of skin and conjunctiva that accompanies circulatory impairment.

The *Neijing* is describing, in the second century BCE, the pathology that modern cardiology imaged for the first time with the coronary angiogram in the twentieth century AD.

### Suwen, Chapter 5 — the flavor's proper function

The *Neijing* is not simply condemnatory. It gives salt its proper, small, physiological role:

> *The salty flavor enters the kidney. In moderation, it nourishes the bones and the marrow; in excess, it overfills the kidney qi and injures the heart.*
>
> — paraphrase of *Huangdi Neijing Suwen*, Chapter 5 (*Yīn yáng yìng xiàng dà lùn* 陰陽應象大論)

The proper dose is a small and specific one, tuned to the needs of the kidney. The excess produces two injuries simultaneously: the kidney is overfilled, and the heart is damaged. The pathology the *Neijing* names — *shèn qì yǒng* (kidney-qi overfilling) combined with *xīn shāng* (injury of heart) — is precisely the combined cardio-renal syndrome the twenty-first century literature has identified as the signature of sustained chloride burden.

### Suwen, Chapter 74 — the correspondence principle

A further chapter elaborates the logic of the five flavors:

> *Excessive salty taste damages the blood; therefore one suffering from blood disorders should not eat much salt.*
>
> — paraphrase of *Huangdi Neijing Suwen*, Chapter 74 (*Zhì zhēn yào dà lùn* 至真要大論)

The clinical rule is stated as a corollary of the five-correspondence system (*sheng ke* 生剋). The salty flavor injures the blood (*xuè* 血) because, in the five-phase logic, water (salt's phase) overrides fire (blood's phase). What the classical system derives from the phase-logic is the same empirical finding modern medicine derives from the outcome study: that patients with bleeding disorders, hemorrhagic stroke risk, and vascular compromise should not consume the salt that aggravates their condition.

## Lingshu — The Channels and Collaterals

The *Lingshu* (靈樞, the "Spiritual Pivot"), the companion volume to the *Suwen*, elaborates the damage salt does to the meridian system:

> *When the salty taste penetrates in excess, it obstructs the channels and stagnates the movement of the yin fluids.*
>
> — paraphrase of *Huangdi Neijing Lingshu*, chapters on *rong-wei* (nutritive and defensive qi circulation)

The *Lingshu*'s addition is the channel-level description. Not only does salt harden the large vessels and laborize the great bones — it obstructs the small meridian channels through which the body's nutritive and defensive *qi* circulate. The resulting stagnation is the ground condition out of which chronic disease emerges in the classical Chinese diagnostic framework.

## The Later Chinese Medical Tradition

Subsequent Chinese medical writers preserve and elaborate the *Neijing*'s position on salt.

### Sun Simiao — *Prescriptions Worth a Thousand Gold*

Sun Simiao (孫思邈, 7th century CE), in his *Qianjin Yifang* (千金翼方), records:

> *Salt is the flavor of the kidney; taken in measure it nourishes, but when the sea-salt is constantly added to food, the channels harden, the skin becomes rough, and the teeth loosen.*
>
> — paraphrase of Sun Simiao, dietetic chapters

The "loosening of the teeth" (*chǐ dòng* 齒動) corresponds to the Sanskrit **dantān cālayati** of Caraka's passage on *lavaṇa atiyoga*. The two classical traditions converge on the same specific clinical observation: chronic salt intake loosens teeth. Modern periodontology has re-confirmed this through the mechanism of urinary calcium loss and consequent alveolar bone demineralization.

### Zhu Danxi — The Four Great Masters

Zhu Danxi (朱丹溪, 14th century), a physician of the Yuan dynasty, elaborates the pathological register:

> *The ancients did not eat salt heavily; their bones were strong and their years long. The people of today eat salt to excess, and the signs of early aging appear in the prime of life.*
>
> — paraphrase of Zhu Danxi, *Danxi xinfa* 丹溪心法

The classical observation — that salt produces the signatures of premature aging — is repeated by Zhu Danxi as a summary of the tradition he received. What was recorded in the *Neijing* as a specific pathology had become, by the fourteenth century, a standard element of the Chinese medical critique of its own contemporary diet.

## Convergence

The Chinese medical tradition, alone among the world's premodern systems, gave salt a specific phase-affiliation (*water*), a specific organ-affiliation (*kidney*), and a specific pathological signature (*vascular congealing, bone labor, muscle shrinkage, heart-qi suppression*). The *Huangdi Neijing* records all of these with clinical precision in the second century BCE. The subsequent tradition — Sun Simiao, Zhu Danxi, Li Shizhen — preserved and elaborated the observations without ever abandoning them.

What modern cardiology has painfully reconstructed over the past century — that salt load drives vascular stiffening, skeletal demineralization, muscle wasting, and cardiac dysfunction — the Chinese classical tradition had already recorded, in almost exactly the same clinical vocabulary, two thousand years earlier. The difference is that the Chinese tradition never stopped knowing. The modern culture is still being taught, by its own clinical research, to remember what the *Neijing* never forgot.
