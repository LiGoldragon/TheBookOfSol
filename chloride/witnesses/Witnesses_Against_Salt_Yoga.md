# Witnesses Against Salt: The Yogic Record

## *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā, Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā, Śiva Saṃhitā — salt as* apathya

### The Yogic Doctrine of Apathya

The haṭha-yoga corpus is unanimous on one point. The preparatory discipline begins with the removal of four tastes: *kaṭu* (pungent), *amla* (sour), *lavaṇa* (salty), *tikta* (bitter). These four are called *apathya* — "not conducive," unwholesome, contrary to the path. The tradition does not recommend moderation of these four. It commands their removal. The reason is physiological: these tastes agitate *prāṇa*, inflame the mind, and prevent the stabilization of breath that *haṭha* depends on. The body of the yogi-in-preparation is the body that has first laid salt aside.

The three foundational haṭha texts each record the rule in its own formulation. The overlap is nearly verbatim.

## Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā — The Canonical Four

Svātmārāma's *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā* (c. 15th century) gives the single most-cited yogic list of unwholesome foods.

> **kaṭvamla-tīkṣṇa-lavaṇoṣṇa-harita-śāka-sauvīra-taila-tila-sarṣapa-madya-matsyān |**
> **ajādi-māṃsa-dadhi-takra-kulattha-kola-piṇyāka-hiṅgu-laśunādyam apathyam āhuḥ ||**
>
> *The pungent, the sour, the sharp, the salty, the hot, green vegetables, sour gruel, oil, sesame, mustard, alcohol, fish, goat and other flesh, curds, buttermilk, horse-gram, jujube, oil-cake, asafoetida, garlic and the like — these they call unwholesome.*
>
> — *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā* 1.59 (Svātmārāma)

The Sanskrit opens with a single compound: *kaṭu-amla-tīkṣṇa-lavaṇa-uṣṇa* — "pungent, sour, sharp, salty, hot." *Lavaṇa* is the fourth term, named within the four core inhibitors. The rest of the verse lists specific foods. But the organizing principle is taste-based, and the salty taste is one of the four that the yogi must remove before the practice can begin.

### The complementary positive rule

The Pradīpikā gives the mirror image five verses later:

> **susnigdha-madhurāhāraś caturthāṃśa-vivarjitaḥ |**
> **bhujyate śiva-samprītyai mitāhāraḥ sa ucyate ||**
>
> *Eating food that is unctuous and sweet, leaving one-fourth of the stomach empty, offered to please Śiva — this is called* mitāhāra.
>
> — *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā* 1.58

*Mitāhāra* — the measured diet — is explicitly defined as *snigdha* (unctuous) and *madhura* (sweet). Not *lavaṇa*. The contrast with 1.59 is structural: the measured diet is the opposite of the salty-sour-sharp-pungent food of agitation.

## Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā — The Rule Elaborated

The *Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā* (c. 17th century), a seven-limb haṭha manual, repeats the same prohibition with an expanded list of specific foods.

> *Food that is pungent, sour, salty, bitter, or parched; curd, buttermilk, an excess of leafy greens, alcohol, palm nut, jackfruit, horse gram, masur and white beans, white pumpkin, the stalks of leafy greens, bottle gourd, jujube, elephant apple, thorny bel, dhak, kadamba, lemon, bimba, breadfruit, garlic, lotus stalk, wild turmeric, chironji, asafetida, the fruit of the silk-cotton tree, and taro root: these should be avoided by the yogi at the beginning of his Yoga practice, together with traveling, the company of women, and the use of fire.*
>
> — *Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā* 5.21, trans. James Mallinson

The verse opens with exactly the same four Sanskrit compounds as the Pradīpikā: *kaṭu, amla, lavaṇa, tikta*. The organizing principle is identical. Every subsequent food named is a specific instance. The preparatory discipline begins with the removal of the four tastes; salt is one of them, with no moderation-clause attached.

## Śiva Saṃhitā — The Hindrances Enumerated

The *Śiva Saṃhitā* (c. 17th–18th century), a five-chapter haṭha treatise, places the salty taste within a broader list of hindrances to yoga that mixes physical habits, moral defects, and dietary errors.

> *Sour, astringent, pungent, salty, mustard, and bitter flavors; too much walking, bathing in the early morning, burning oil, stealing, violence, hatred of others, pride, insincerity, fasting, untruthfulness, folly, cruelty to animals, the company of women, the use of fire, too much chatter — whether good-natured or not — and overeating: the yogi should definitely give up these.*
>
> — *Śiva Saṃhitā* 3.33, trans. James Mallinson

Here salty taste (*lavaṇa*) is placed in the same list as violence, pride, insincerity, and cruelty to animals. The text does not treat the salty taste as a separate category of concern. It classifies it with the conditions that produce the body and mind which cannot practice. This is the strongest possible framing: salt is not a dietary inconvenience. It is a moral obstruction, in the same register as the *kleśas* that Patañjali lists as the root of bondage.

## The Wider Yogic Corpus

The same formula — the four unwholesome tastes, with *lavaṇa* named among them — recurs across the haṭha literature:

- ***Yoga Yājñavalkya*** (perhaps the oldest systematic yoga manual, pre-dating the Pradīpikā) names *lavaṇa* among the tastes the yogi abstains from during the preparatory phase.
- ***Daṭṭātreya Yoga Śāstra*** preserves the rule in essentially the same form.
- ***Haṭhābhyāsa Paddhati*** systematizes the mitāhāra regimen with the same four-taste exclusion.
- ***Yoga Cūḍāmaṇi Upaniṣad*** and ***Yoga-bīja*** include salt among the preparatory apathya.

Across the haṭha corpus, the doctrine is uniform. There is no yogic text that permits the salty taste within the practice. There is only the sāttvic diet — *snigdha, madhura*, living matter received in measured quantity — and the agitating diet, of which salt is the paradigm.

## Why

The yogic logic is not polemic but physiological. *Prāṇa* — the breath, the life-force, the subtle energy the practice is meant to stabilize — is said to follow the qualities of food. Salt, as the Āyurvedic tradition has already established, is an agitator of *rakta* (blood) and *vāta* (wind). Its presence in the body keeps *prāṇa* in motion, prevents its gathering at the base of the spine, and obstructs the rising current that leads to *samādhi*. The body full of salt is the body full of small, ceaseless agitations. The body without salt is the body that can settle.

This is not mysticism. It is the same observation modern cardiology makes about sympathetic nervous tone under chronic salt load: tonic vascular constriction, elevated baseline arousal, inability to rest. The haṭha texts named it *rajas*; the modern text names it *sympathetic dominance*. The body cannot stabilize under either name.

## Convergence

The haṭha corpus speaks with a single voice. Svātmārāma, Gheraṇḍa, and the author of the *Śiva Saṃhitā* — composing independently across centuries — preserve the same four-taste prohibition, with *lavaṇa* named in the same position. The yogic tradition did not negotiate moderation. It treated salt as an obstruction to be removed, categorically, as the first step of the discipline.

For the tradition that had watched the human body and its energies with the closest technical attention the ancient world produced, salt had no legitimate place in the diet of one who aspired to stillness. The Ayurvedic pathology and the yogic inhibition were two faces of the same observation.
