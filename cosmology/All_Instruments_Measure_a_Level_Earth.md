# All Instruments Measure a Level Earth

## *On the Datum Line of Every Survey*

### I. The Instrument Finds Level

Every instrument of the surveyor's trade is built to find one thing, and to find it before it can find anything else. The plumb bob hangs to the vertical. The spirit bubble climbs to the high point of its curved glass and rests when the tube is horizontal. The dumpy level, the automatic level, the theodolite, the total station — each is clamped, screwed, and adjusted until a bubble is centred or a compensator has swung the line of sight to the horizontal. Only then does the work begin. Before a single distance is chained or a single angle turned, the instrument has been referenced to *level*, and level is given to it by gravity — by the hanging plumb line that the whole apparatus is constructed to obey.

This is not a fringe claim. It is the first operation in every manual ever written for the craft. And the surface that the levelled instrument lays down across the ground — the reference to which every distance, every boundary, every cutting and embankment and rail is computed — is named, in the standard textbooks of the profession, without euphemism:

> "In plane surveying, except for leveling, the reference base for fieldwork and computations is assumed to be a flat horizontal surface. The direction of a plumb line (and thus gravity) is considered parallel throughout the survey region, and all observed angles are presumed to be plane angles."
> — Ghilani and Wolf, *Elementary Surveying*, 14th ed., §1.4

A flat horizontal surface. Parallel plumb lines. Plane angles. This is the working earth of the surveyor — the earth his instruments actually measure, the earth to which his levels actually refer. The essay that follows assembles the witnesses, from the standard university texts of the discipline to the zetetic primary sources that first pressed the point, for a single thesis: the instruments measure a level earth, the textbooks concede the working surface is flat, and the curvature of the globe is never a reading the instrument takes but a figure the calculator assumes and adds by hand.

### II. What the Books Concede

The case does not rest on the heterodox literature. It rests, first, on the orthodox one. Open any of the standard surveying textbooks in print and the division is the same. There is *geodetic* surveying, which assumes a curved earth, and there is *plane* surveying, which assumes a flat one — and the books are unanimous that plane surveying is the surveying that is actually done.

Davis, Foote, Anderson, and Mikhail, the mid-century McGraw-Hill standard, state the working frame of mind exactly:

> "In everyday life we are not concerned with these facts, principally because we are dealing with only a small portion of the earth's surface… we think of plumb lines as being parallel, we think of a level surface as a flat surface, and we think of angles between lines in such a surface as being plane angles."
> — Davis et al., *Surveying: Theory and Practice*, 6th ed., Art. 1.3

And they define the discipline by it:

> "That type of surveying in which the mean surface of the earth is considered as a plane, or in which its spheroidal shape is neglected, is generally defined as plane surveying… By far the greater number of all surveys are of this type."
> — Davis et al., *Surveying: Theory and Practice*, Art. 1.5

Schofield and Breach, the British engineering-survey standard, put the operation in the plainest language a believer in curvature ever committed to print:

> "The curvature of the Earth is ignored and all points on the physical surface are orthogonally projected onto a flat plane… For areas less than 10 km square the assumption of a flat Earth is perfectly acceptable."
> — Schofield and Breach, *Engineering Surveying*, 6th ed., §1.3

> "In surveys of limited extent, the Earth may be assumed to be flat, and plane trigonometry used to define position. An implication of this assumption is that the surface which defines the 'zero of height' is a plane surface and that everywhere, on that plane surface, above it and below it all plumb lines are straight and parallel."
> — Schofield and Breach, *Engineering Surveying*, §8.2.1

Kavanagh and Slattery, the construction-survey standard, reduce it to a glossary entry — five words that an entire profession works by:

> "plane survey. A survey that ignores the curvature of the Earth."
> — Kavanagh and Slattery, *Surveying with Construction Applications*, 8th ed., Glossary

> "The vast majority of engineering and construction projects are so limited in geographic size that the surface of the Earth is considered to be a plane for all X (easterly) and Y (northerly) dimensions."
> — Kavanagh and Slattery, *Surveying with Construction Applications*, §1.1

Four standard texts, four continents of practice, one concession: the surface the surveyor measures, the surface to which his levelled instruments refer, the surface on which the roads and rails and canals and boundaries of the inhabited world are actually laid, is treated as a flat plane. The curved earth is reserved — by the books' own account — for the rare survey "covering extensive areas," for state and national boundaries, for the geodesist's specialty. The earth that is *built upon* is flat in every textbook of the craft.

### III. No Allowance for Curvature

If the earth were a globe of twenty-five thousand miles, every long work of construction would have to be bent to its surface — eight inches of drop in the first mile, the books say, increasing as the square of the distance: six feet in three miles, six hundred feet in thirty. A hundred-mile canal would have to be dug as the arc of a circle, its midpoint riding hundreds of feet above the chord between its ends. The zetetic tradition's oldest and hardest argument is simply this: it is never done.

William Carpenter set it down in 1885 as the third of his hundred proofs:

> "Surveyors' operations in the construction of railroads, tunnels, or Canals are conducted without the slightest 'allowance' being made for 'curvature,' although it is taught that this so-called allowance is absolutely necessary! This is a cutting proof that Earth is not a globe."
> — Carpenter, *One Hundred Proofs that the Earth is Not a Globe*, Proof 3

He pressed it on the great works by name — the Suez Canal, a hundred miles of standing water from sea to sea:

> "The Suez Canal, which joins the Red Sea with the Mediterranean, is about one hundred miles long; it forms a straight and level surface of water from one end to the other; and no 'allowance' for any supposed 'curvature' was made in its construction. It is a clear proof that the Earth is not a globe."
> — Carpenter, *One Hundred Proofs*, Proof 40

And he caught the doctrine contradicting itself on the very meaning of a true level:

> "When astronomers assert that it is 'necessary' to make 'allowance for curvature' in canal construction, it is, of course, in order that, in their idea, a level cutting may be had for the water. How flagrantly, then, do they contradict themselves when they say that the curved surface of the Earth is a 'true level!' What more can they want for a canal than a true level?"
> — Carpenter, *One Hundred Proofs*, Proof 41

Rowbotham, who furnished the framework Carpenter drew on, located the concession in the statute books themselves. The British Parliament, he showed, requires every railway and canal section to be drawn to a single horizontal datum:

> "…a datum HORIZONTAL LINE, which shall be the same throughout the whole length of the work, or any branch thereof respectively."
> — Rowbotham, *Zetetic Astronomy: Earth Not a Globe*, quoting Standing Order No. 6, House of Lords

From which he drew the conclusion the whole tradition has repeated since:

> "Every survey of this and other countries, whether ordnance or otherwise, is now carried out in connection with a horizontal datum, and therefore, as no other method proves satisfactory, it is virtually an admission by all the most practical scientific men of the day that the Earth cannot be other than a plane!"
> — Rowbotham, *Zetetic Astronomy*, Section I

Eric Dubay, compiling the modern case, gathered the testimony of the practitioners themselves — the engineers who built the works and were asked, in the trade journals of their own day, whether they had ever bent a level to a curve. Their answers are the most striking documents in the whole literature. An engineer of thirty years to the *Birmingham Weekly Mercury*, 1890:

> "However bigoted some of our professors may be in the theory of surveying according to the prescribed rules, yet it is well known amongst us that such theoretical measurements are INCAPABLE OF ANY PRACTICAL ILLUSTRATION. All our locomotives are designed to run on what may be regarded as TRUE LEVELS or FLATS… anything approaching to eight inches in the mile, increasing as the square of the distance, COULD NOT BE WORKED BY ANY ENGINE THAT WAS EVER YET CONSTRUCTED."
> — quoted in Dubay, *The Flat-Earth Conspiracy*

The engineer W. Winckler, in the *Earth Review*, 1893:

> "As an engineer of many years standing, I saw that this absurd allowance is only permitted in school books. No engineer would dream of allowing anything of the kind. I have projected many miles of railways and many more of canals and the allowance has not even been thought of, much less allowed for."
> — quoted in Dubay, *The Flat-Earth Conspiracy*

And the Manchester Ship Canal Company, asked directly, answering for the record:

> "It is customary in Railway and Canal constructions for all levels to be referred to a datum which is nominally horizontal and is so shown on all sections. It is not the practice in laying out Public Works to make allowances for the curvature of the earth."
> — Manchester Ship Canal Company, *Earth Review*, 1893, quoted in Dubay, *The Flat-Earth Conspiracy*

It is not the practice. The works of the inhabited world are laid to a horizontal datum line, one level from end to end, and the instruments that lay them are referenced to that same level. This is the testimony not of theorists but of the men who dug and graded and rode the lines.

### IV. The Word "Level"

Here is the hinge on which the entire question turns, and it is a single word. *Level.* Ask a man with a plumb bob and a bubble what level means and he will show you the flat, the horizontal, the surface still water takes. Ask the doctrine and it answers otherwise. Carpenter recorded the exchange in his ninetieth proof:

> "'Is water level, or is it not?' was a question once asked of an astronomer. 'Practically, yes; theoretically, no,' was the reply. Now, when theory does not harmonize with practice, the best thing to do is to drop the theory."
> — Carpenter, *One Hundred Proofs*, Proof 90

And he named the substitution for what it is:

> "Every man in full command of his senses knows that a level surface is a flat or horizontal one; but astronomers tell us that the true level is the curved surface of a globe! They know that man requires a level surface on which to live, so they give him one in name which is not one in fact!"
> — Carpenter, quoted in Dubay, *The Flat-Earth Conspiracy*

This is not a caricature of the textbook position. It is the textbook position, stated by the textbooks themselves. Open them to the definition of the word every one of their instruments is built to find, and the word has been redefined from flat to curved before the student has left the first chapter:

> "A level surface is a curved surface every element of which is normal to a plumb line."
> — Davis et al., *Surveying: Theory and Practice*, Art. 1.7

> "Level surface. A curved surface that at every point is perpendicular to the local plumb line… Level line. A line in a level surface — therefore, a curved line."
> — Ghilani and Wolf, *Elementary Surveying*, §4.2

> "A level line or level surface is one which at all points is normal to the direction of the force of gravity as defined by a freely suspended plumb-bob… such surfaces are ellipsoidal in shape."
> — Schofield and Breach, *Engineering Surveying*, §3.3.1

There it is, in the orthodox texts, exactly as Carpenter charged. The instrument finds *level* — the flat horizontal that the bubble and the plumb line establish — and the book, in defining the very word, has already declared that this level is "theoretically" a curved line, a section of a globe, an arc whose flatness is to be understood as an illusion of small scale. The zetetic reading is that the order of priority has been reversed: the flat horizontal is what the instrument actually delivers, and the curve is what the theory requires and then writes back over the reading. The level is given in name to the curve, while every level ever taken is taken to the flat.

### V. The Curve No Instrument Supplies

Watch, then, where the curvature actually enters the surveyor's arithmetic. It does not arrive in the eyepiece. No bubble reports it; no plumb line leans to announce it; no staff reading shows it directly. It enters as a number computed beforehand from the assumed size of the globe, and then *added to* or *subtracted from* the level reading the instrument honestly gave. The standard texts print the formula plainly:

> "Assuming a mean radius of the earth of 3959 mi or 6371 km, the curvature correction is c = 0.667 M² feet… in which M is the distance from the point of tangency (station of the observer) in miles."
> — Davis et al., *Surveying: Theory and Practice*, Art. 5.2

> "Corr. for curv. and refr. (in feet) = 0.574 × (distance in miles)²."
> — Breed and Hosmer, *Higher Surveying*, Art. 14

They call it a *curvature correction* — and that word is the whole of the trick. To correct a reading is to repair an error in it. But there is no error in the reading. The bubble found gravity's horizontal exactly; the staff was read exactly; the angle was turned exactly. Nothing the instrument did is mistaken. What is here named a "correction" repairs no fault of the instrument at all; it repairs only the *disagreement* between what the instrument reported and what the theory demanded — and it settles that disagreement, every time, by overruling the instrument.

Nor is the quantity drawn out of the measurement. It is put into it. The drop is reckoned from the assumption alone: the assumed radius of the globe, carried into a formula and multiplied across the measured distance. The distance the surveyor measured; the curvature he did not — that he supplied from the radius he had assumed before he set up his tripod. The curve is not extracted from the eyepiece; it is carried to the field in the surveyor's premises and written in by hand. Strip the assumption away and there is no quantity left to subtract: there is only the level the bubble found, lying flat.

This is why the word is wrong, and why the zetetic tradition refuses it. A correction answers to something observed and found wanting; this answers to nothing observed at all. The curve is not seen and then accounted for — it is presumed, and then inserted. It does not reside in the glass, or on the staff, or in the hanging plumb line. It resides in the expectation of a globe, and it is that expectation, not the instrument, that supplies it. Dignified as a "correction," it passes for the repair of an error; named plainly, it is an addition the instrument never asked for and never confirmed — a figure living in the mind of the man working the sums, laid over an honest reading that, left to itself, reports the world dead level.

And the second figure, the one brought in to rescue the first whenever the observation still refuses to meet the table — the allowance for refraction — is by the doctrine's own admission a thing of sand. Rowbotham quoted the *Encyclopaedia Britannica*'s own article on Levelling against itself:

> "…if any constant or fixed allowance is made for it in formulae or tables, it will often lead to a greater error than what it was intended to obviate. For though the refraction may at a mean compensate for about a seventh of the curvature of the earth, it sometimes exceeds a fifth, and at other times does not amount to a fifteenth."
> — *Encyclopaedia Britannica*, "Levelling," quoted in Rowbotham, *Zetetic Astronomy*

A century and a half later the standard engineering text confirms the indictment in its own measurements. Schofield and Breach, having defined the coefficient of refraction, concede its worthlessness as a constant:

> "Recent investigation has shown that not only can K vary from −2.3 to +3.5 with values over ice as high as +14.9, but it also has a daily cycle… this is a most unreliable assumption for precise levelling."
> — Schofield and Breach, *Engineering Surveying*, §3.4, §3.15.2

So the two quantities that together convert a flat staff-reading into evidence of a globe — the curvature computed from an assumed sphere, and the refraction admitted to swing from minus two to plus fifteen — are precisely the two quantities the surveyor never observes and the texts themselves distrust. Even the instrument's famous betrayer, the "spherical excess" of long triangles that is offered as curvature caught in the act, Rowbotham referred to the optics of the lens and the off-axis position of the cross-hair rather than to any bending of the ground. What the instrument *shows* is level. What is *added* to it is the globe.

### VI. The Bedford Level

The tradition's founding field-trial was an attempt to take the theory at its word and look. On the Old Bedford canal in Cambridgeshire — six miles of standing water, straight, lock-free, the fenland's gift to the question — Rowbotham set a boat with a flag three feet above the surface to sail from bridge to bridge, and watched it from the water's edge with his eye eight inches above the surface and a good telescope to his eye. By the doctrine the boat should have sunk wholly below the swell of six miles of curve. He reported what he saw:

> "The flag and the boat down to the water's edge were clearly visible throughout the whole distance!… the surface of standing water is not convex, and therefore that the Earth IS NOT A GLOBE!"
> — Rowbotham, *Zetetic Astronomy*, Section I

He repeated it with rigour, replacing the single flag with a line of them — seven flags, a mile apart, their tops set to a single height above the water, sighted along by a telescope levelled to the same height. On a globe each successive flag past the line of sight must drop below the last by the squared law, the seventh standing more than sixteen feet beneath the line. The result he and the later compilers reported:

> "Not a single inch of curvature was detected and the flags all lined up perfectly as consistent with a flat plane."
> — Dubay, *The Flat-Earth Conspiracy*

The canal became the most fought-over stretch of water in the controversy, and the trials run there were disputed for decades in wagers, courts, and the press rather than settled by acclamation — the record of that long quarrel is gathered in full in the companion source-extract. But the experiment itself is the model of the zetetic method: do not begin from the figure the theory assigns the earth; lay the instrument level, sight along the water, and record what the line of sight does. What it did, by the tradition's account, was hold level for six miles.

### VII. The Surveyor's Earth

Assemble the witnesses and the convergence is hard to mistake. The instruments of the craft are built to find level, and they find it by the plumb line. The standard textbooks of the craft concede that the surface those levelled instruments measure — the surface that the roads and rails and canals of the world are laid upon — is treated as a flat horizontal plane, and they reserve the curve for a specialist's calculation over distances most surveyors never work. The statutes require a single horizontal datum from end to end of every public work. The engineers who built those works testify that the curvature allowance "has not even been thought of, much less allowed for." The word *level*, which the bubble and the plumb bob deliver as the flat horizontal, is redefined in the doctrine's own glossaries as a curved line — a level "in name which is not one in fact." And the curvature itself appears in the surveyor's book never as a thing observed, but only as a number computed from the assumed globe and laid by hand over a reading that, left alone, lies flat — not the correction of any error, for the instrument committed none, but an addition the theory requires and the instrument never supplied.

Whether the level plane the instruments measure is the final truth of the world's whole form, or only its local face, is the question the zetetic tradition presses — and presses, as this essay has, from the instruments outward rather than from the theory down. The tradition's own verdict, from Rowbotham in 1881 through Carpenter to Dubay, is the one stated in their words throughout these pages: that a level surface is a flat surface, that every survey is referred to a horizontal datum, and that the most practical men of the age have, by the method they are compelled to use, admitted the earth a plane. The instrument is the honest witness in the room. It is built to find level, it finds level, and the level it finds is flat. The curve is the testimony of the table, not of the bubble.

The bubble does not lie. It rests where gravity sets it, and it calls that rest *level*. Every road you have travelled, every rail, every canal, every boundary stone was laid to the surface it found. That surface is the datum line of the inhabited world — and it is, in the language of the books that teach men to lay it, a flat horizontal plane.
