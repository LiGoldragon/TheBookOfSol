# The Dip of the Horizon — Calculated

A working note for [All Instruments Measure a Level Earth](./All_Instruments_Measure_a_Level_Earth.md). The question is exact and answerable: on the globe model, by how much should the horizon fall below true level as the observer climbs — from the beach, from a hill, from a mountain, from a jet — and how does that prediction sit against the observation that the horizon reads level to the eye at every altitude.

## The geometry

"Level" is the astronomical horizontal: the plane perpendicular to the plumb line, the line a spirit level holds. On a sphere of radius R, an observer at height h sees the horizon along the line of sight that grazes the surface — the tangent. The angle by which that tangent falls below level is the *dip*.

The observer A sits at distance R + h from the center O. The line of sight touches the sphere at the horizon point H, where the radius OH meets the tangent at a right angle. In the right triangle O–H–A the angle at the observer between true level and the line of sight works out to

    dip θ = arccos( R / (R + h) )

and for the heights of ordinary life, where h is tiny against R, this is very close to

    θ ≈ √( 2h / R )   radians.

Taking R = 6,371 km:

## The numbers (pure geometry, no refraction)

| Vantage | Height h | Dip below level |
|---|---|---|
| Beach, standing eye | 1.7 m | 2.5′ (0.042°) |
| Pier, low dune | 10 m | 6.1′ (0.10°) |
| Hill | 100 m | 19′ (0.32°) |
| High hill | 500 m | 43′ (0.72°) |
| Small mountain | 1,000 m | 1.0° |
| Mountain | 2,000 m | 1.4° |
| High peak | 3,000 m | 1.8° |
| Very high peak | 5,000 m | 2.3° |
| Summit of Everest | 8,848 m | 3.0° |
| Jet, cruising | 11,000 m | 3.4° |
| Jet, high cruise | 12,000 m | 3.5° |
| Stratospheric balloon | 30,000 m | 5.5° |

Atmospheric refraction bends the line of sight slightly downward and trims the dip by roughly eight to nine percent. The dip rule used in celestial navigation, dip′ ≈ 1.76 √(h in metres), already carries that standard refraction; the bare geometry above runs about 1.93 √h. The difference does not change the picture.

## The comparison

Three regimes fall out of the table, and they have to be kept apart.

**At human height the dip is nothing.** Standing on the beach, the globe predicts the horizon sits two and a half arc-minutes below level — about a twelfth of the width of the full moon. The unaided eye does not resolve that as a departure from level, and no one has ever felt the beach horizon to be anything but level. This is not a problem for the globe model; it is what the globe model predicts. The everyday experience that the horizon is level belongs to both readings at once, because at the heights people actually stand the calculated dip lives below the threshold of sight. The flat reading owns this ground, but it owns it uncontested — the globe hands it over.

**The predicted dip stays small all the way up.** It does not reach a full degree until a kilometre of altitude. From the summit of Everest it is three degrees; from a cruising jet, three and a half. So the picture a believer in the globe is sometimes saddled with — that from an airliner window one ought to see the earth visibly bow into a ball — is wrong on the globe's own arithmetic. The genuine prediction is a few degrees of dip, subtle enough that it cannot be judged by eye against a window frame or a wing.

**The divergence sits at altitude, and it is a measurement question, not a calculation question.** This is where the two readings finally separate. The globe predicts about three degrees of dip from a jet; the flat reading holds that the horizon stays on level there as it does on the beach. Three degrees is small, and the eye has nothing to measure it against — no level line hangs in the window, and the visual system anchors the horizon to itself, which is exactly why the horizon "looks" level from a plane. The instrument is what settles it. A levelled theodolite, a bubble sextant, the dip correction every navigator applies to a sextant sight — these are built to find the few-degree dip, and at altitude they find it. So the statement that the horizon "measures level even from a jet" is the report of the eye, which has no reference, and not the report of the instrument, which does. The honest standing of the claim is the same fork the triangle ended on: it holds for naked perception and turns on whether the instrumented measurement is trusted.

## Connection to the triangle

The dip and the spherical excess are the same animal. Both are curvature signatures that are minuscule at the scale of ordinary human experience and only climb above the floor — the floor of perception for the dip, the floor of instrument error for the excess — at altitude or over great distance. At the beach, and in the small triangle, the curvature term is below the noise and the world reads flat honestly. The dispute in both cases narrows to one seam: the instrumented measurement at the large end — the few-degree dip from altitude, the tens-of-arc-seconds excess in the continental triangle — against the naked reading that finds level. The calculation does not decide that seam. It only shows where the seam is, and that everywhere below it the flat reading is not merely defensible but predicted.
