# The Dip of the Horizon — Calculated

A working note for [All Instruments Measure a Level Earth](./All_Instruments_Measure_a_Level_Earth.md). The question is exact and answerable: on the globe model, by how much should the horizon fall below true level as the observer climbs — from the beach, from a hill, from a mountain, from a jet — and how does that prediction sit against the observation that the horizon reads level to the eye at every altitude.

## The geometry

"Level" is the astronomical horizontal: the plane perpendicular to the plumb line, the line a spirit level holds. On a sphere of radius R, an observer at height h sees the horizon along the line of sight that grazes the surface — the tangent. The angle by which that tangent falls below level is the *dip*.

The observer A sits at distance R + h from the center O. The line of sight touches the sphere at the horizon point H, where the radius OH meets the tangent at a right angle. In the right triangle O–H–A the angle at the observer between true level and the line of sight works out to

    dip θ = arccos( R / (R + h) )

and for the heights of ordinary life, where h is tiny against R, this is very close to

    θ ≈ √( 2h / R )   radians.

Taking R = 6,371 km:

## The numbers (pure geometry, no refraction)

| Vantage | Height h | Dip below level |
|---|---|---|
| Beach, standing eye | 1.7 m | 2.5′ (0.042°) |
| Pier, low dune | 10 m | 6.1′ (0.10°) |
| Hill | 100 m | 19′ (0.32°) |
| High hill | 500 m | 43′ (0.72°) |
| Small mountain | 1,000 m | 1.0° |
| Mountain | 2,000 m | 1.4° |
| High peak | 3,000 m | 1.8° |
| Very high peak | 5,000 m | 2.3° |
| Summit of Everest | 8,848 m | 3.0° |
| Jet, cruising | 11,000 m | 3.4° |
| Jet, high cruise | 12,000 m | 3.5° |
| Stratospheric balloon | 30,000 m | 5.5° |

Atmospheric refraction bends the line of sight slightly downward and trims the dip by roughly eight to nine percent. The dip rule used in celestial navigation, dip′ ≈ 1.76 √(h in metres), already carries that standard refraction; the bare geometry above runs about 1.93 √h. The difference does not change the picture.

## The comparison

Three regimes fall out of the table, and they have to be kept apart.

**At human height the dip is small to the eye, but to an instrument it is large.** On the globe model the beach horizon (eye ~1.7 m) sits 2.5 arc-minutes, or 151 arc-seconds, below level. That is near the limit of unaided sight, which in any case has no level line to judge it against; but it is roughly 150 times the arc-second floor of a theodolite. The dip is therefore instrument-measurable from sea level upward, and the discriminator is live at every height, not only at altitude: the globe predicts 151″ of dip on the beach, the flat model predicts 0″. What a levelled instrument reads there is already the test.

**The predicted dip stays small all the way up.** It does not reach a full degree until a kilometre of altitude. From the summit of Everest it is three degrees; from a cruising jet, three and a half. So the picture a believer in the globe is sometimes saddled with — that from an airliner window one ought to see the earth visibly bow into a ball — is wrong on the globe's own arithmetic. The genuine prediction is a few degrees of dip, subtle enough that it cannot be judged by eye against a window frame or a wing.

**The whole question reduces to one reading: what a plumb-levelled instrument sighting the horizon reports, at known altitude.** The two models give exact, opposite numbers at every height: the globe, θ(h), from 151″ on the beach to 3.4° from a jet; the flat model, 0″ throughout. This is a measurement, and it is the measurement that decides the matter. The indirect evidence on record is the dip correction of celestial navigation: a nonzero dip that grows as √h, subtracted from every sextant sight, which makes the computed fix come out right and would spoil it if the horizon stood at level. Whether a clean, independent, plumb-levelled measurement at altitude confirms that dip or reads zero is the open crux of the whole subject, and it earns the direct measured figures, with instrument, altitude, and refraction logged, rather than assertion from either side.

## Connection to the triangle

The dip and the spherical excess are the same animal. Both are curvature signatures that are minuscule at the scale of ordinary human experience and only climb above the floor — the floor of perception for the dip, the floor of instrument error for the excess — at altitude or over great distance. At the beach, and in the small triangle, the curvature term is below the noise and the world reads flat honestly. The dispute in both cases narrows to one seam: the instrumented measurement at the large end (the few-degree dip from altitude, the tens-of-arc-seconds excess in the continental triangle) against the naked reading that finds level. The calculation does not decide that seam. It only shows where the seam is, and that everywhere below it the flat reading is exactly what the globe model predicts.
