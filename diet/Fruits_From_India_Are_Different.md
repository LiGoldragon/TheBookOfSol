# "Fruits From India Are Different"

## On the Deflection That Protects the Fruit Myth From Its Own Scriptures

When the classical texts are cited — when the *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā* names *anna* (cooked grain) as the yogin's food; when the *Caraka Saṃhitā* classifies fruit after fruit as *guru* (heavy), distending, and *doṣa*-aggravating; when the *Bhagavad Gītā* requires sāttvic food to be *snigdha* (unctuous) and *sthira* (stable), qualities fruit lacks — the modern fruitarian has a ready escape:

"Those texts are talking about Indian fruits. Tropical fruits are different. The fruits I eat — mangoes from Mexico, bananas from Ecuador, papayas from Thailand — are not the fruits Caraka was warning about."

This deflection fails on two grounds, and both are fatal.

### First: Yoga Is a Universal Science

The assumption behind the deflection is that the classical texts describe a local cuisine adapted to subcontinental conditions, with yoga as a regional product of climate, agriculture, and genetics. This is the standard academic framing, and it is wrong.

There was a prior civilization. Its sciences were universal.

The oldest memories of humanity converge on the same starting point: long-lived, powerful, deeply knowledgeable ancestors whose understanding of physiology, purity, and the subtle energies of the body made longevity natural. India remembered. Egypt remembered. The Toltec seers remembered. Genesis records lifespans of nine hundred years without embarrassment. The Atharva-veda asks to "obtain the long life that our ancestors attained." The Ṛg-veda describes sages who "found the hidden light" and "set it down in the hidden place."

After the collapse of that age — recorded as flood, fire, and upheaval across every surviving tradition — the knowledge scattered. It survived in fragments, carried by lineages that fled to wherever geography offered protection from the chaos that followed. India preserved the largest written corpus because the Himalayas — the greatest mountain range on earth — shielded its guardians from the waves of destruction, invasion, and cultural erasure that consumed knowledge-keepers elsewhere. Egypt preserved its fragment in stone and priesthood. The Toltecs preserved theirs in oral lineage and direct practice. Each carried a piece of the same original science.

The Sanskrit scriptures are the most intact written remnants of a worldwide civilization's wisdom, preserved behind a wall of rock that happened to be tall enough.

To say "those texts are about Indian fruits" is to misunderstand what the texts are. They are the surviving medical science of a civilization that predates India as a political or cultural entity. Their observations on fruit apply to fruit as such — at any latitude, in any climate, of any cultivar. The physiology they describe is human physiology. The *doṣas* they name are universal forces. *Vāta* rises in a fruitarian in California exactly as it rises in a fruitarian in Kerala.

### Second: All Fruits Do the Same Thing

Even if we set aside the question of origin and treat the texts as purely empirical — as clinical observations about what fruit does to a living body — the deflection still collapses, because all fruits, regardless of their latitude, produce the same physiological pattern when consumed in excess.

The Caraka Saṃhitā does not warn against fruit because of something peculiar to the Indian subcontinent's cultivars. It warns because of what fruit *is*: light, cold, dispersing, and sweet. These are qualities intrinsic to the category. A mango from Ratnagiri and a mango from Oaxaca are both guru, both sweet, both kapha-aggravating. A banana from Tamil Nadu and a banana from Honduras are both heavy, both cold, both distending. A papaya from Varanasi and a papaya from Chiang Mai are both dispersing, both pitta-provoking when sour, both kapha-provoking when ripe. The latitude changed. The qualities did not.

This is because the qualities the Caraka Saṃhitā describes — guru (heavy), laghu (light), śīta (cold), uṣṇa (hot), snigdha (unctuous), rūkṣa (rough) — are not cultural categories. They are descriptions of how substance interacts with living tissue. A fruit that is sweet, cold, and heavy in Varanasi is sweet, cold, and heavy in Tulum. The organism that processes it does not change its doṣic response because the fruit crossed an ocean.

The arc of the exclusive fruit diet is the same everywhere it has been tried:

Initial clearing — lightness, sharpness, relief from accumulated burden. The body, freed from chloride, sulphur residue, and kapha congestion, responds with gratitude. This phase is real, and universal.

Then dispersion — internal fluids thin, ojas depletes, vāta rises. Anxiety, insomnia, cold extremities, constipation, drying of the colon, racing thoughts. The organism, having expelled its burdens, begins to consume its own reserves. This phase is also real, also universal, and occurs whether the fruit was grown in Maharashtra, Costa Rica, or the Philippines.

Steve Jobs ate fruit in California. His pancreas failed. Ashton Kutcher ate fruit in Los Angeles. His pancreas rebelled within weeks. A man in Europe ate primarily apples for two years. His mind dissolved. None of them were eating Indian fruit. All of them experienced the same depletion, because the depletion is not caused by the origin of the fruit. It is caused by fruit.

### The Deflection Exposed

"Fruits from India are different" is a dodge. It protects the fruit myth by narrowing the scope of the warning to a geography, so the practitioner can continue eating unlimited fruit while claiming the ancient physicians were talking about someone else's fruit.

They were talking about fruit. The body they were describing is the same body — yours, theirs, everyone's. The *doṣas* do not check passports. *Vāta* does not pause at the border. *Agni* does not ask where the mango was grown before deciding whether to struggle with it.

The science is universal because the civilization that produced it was universal. The physiology is universal because physiology does not vary by postal code. Both roads lead to the same conclusion:

Fruit clears. Fruit does not sustain. This is true in India. True everywhere else. The texts that say so are the surviving medical science of the world's oldest known civilization, preserved behind the Himalayas because that is where the mountains were tall enough to keep the knowledge alive.
