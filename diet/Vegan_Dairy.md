# Vegan Dairy

## *The Ethics of Milk Rests on the Relationship*

Veganism was originally defined as a diet that proceeds from the non-abuse of animals. It is an ethical criterion, asking whether animals are abused in the process, rather than a rule that excludes entire categories of substance by origin alone.

Milk does not require abuse by necessity. Lactation exists to nourish others, and its expression is cooperative and biologically purposive. Human breastfeeding makes this self-evident. A woman feeding her child is not abused by producing milk, nor is the child abusing her by receiving it. The act is cooperative, voluntary, and life-giving.

The same principle applies to other mammals. A cow can live in a protected, cared-for, non-coerced relationship with humans in which milk is shared without deprivation, fear, or injury. Such a relationship can be mutually beneficial: the cow receives food, shelter, protection, medical care, and a full natural lifespan, while humans receive nourishment. No ethical violation is implied by the act itself.

This understanding is central in Vedic traditions and the Upaniṣadic worldview, where the cow is explicitly to be protected. The cow nourishes humans with her milk, and therefore humans are obligated to nourish and safeguard her life. The relationship is moral and reciprocal. Where abuse enters, it nullifies the legitimacy of the relationship entirely.

Milk products such as ghee, butter, and yogurt therefore inherit their ethical status from the conditions under which the milk was obtained. They become non-vegan when their production involves abuse: forced breeding, calf separation, confinement, deprivation, or premature slaughter. These practices are contingent industrial choices, not biological requirements.

To declare all dairy non-vegan by definition collapses an ethical framework into a material classification system. It mistakes historically recent industrial abuse for an intrinsic property of milk itself. Under the original definition of veganism—non-abuse of animals—what matters is the relationship that produced the food.

Veganism is a relationship of non-abuse, and a cared-for cow's milk keeps it.
