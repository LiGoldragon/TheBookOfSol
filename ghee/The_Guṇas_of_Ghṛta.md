# The Guṇas of Ghṛta

## *The thousand actions of clarified butter*

Āyurveda knows a substance by its work. A *dravya* (a material, a thing with properties) earns its place in the materia medica by what it does in the body, and the texts name that work in long, exact lists. No food gathers a longer list to its name than *ghṛta*, clarified butter. To recite what it does is the oldest and plainest form of its praise.

## The litany

The cow's ghee, *go-ghṛta*, holds the fullest entry. Nearly every word of it names a virtue:

> "Ghee promotes memory, intelligence, agni, semen, and ojas; it allays vāta, pitta, poison, and fever. It is the best of all fats, cooling, sweet in taste and sweet after digestion, holding a thousand potencies; and so, used rightly, it works a thousand actions."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.231–233, trans. after P. V. Sharma

Read the list and the guṇas stand out one by one. Its *rasa* (taste) is *madhura* (sweet), its *vipāka* (the taste left after digestion) sweet again, its *vīrya* (potency) *śīta* (cooling); to the touch it is *snigdha* (unctuous), *mṛdu* (soft), and *guru* (weighty). It kindles *agni* (the digestive fire), builds *ojas* (the refined essence of vitality), and allays *vāta* (the movement-principle) and *pitta* (the fire-principle). It is *ojaskara*, a maker of ojas; *medhya*, a kindler of memory and intelligence; *vṛṣya*, a builder of the generative essence; and the best of all the fats.

The thousand actions are doctrine, not flourish. Ghṛta cooked and re-cooked, or simply aged into *purāṇa-ghṛta* (old ghee), deepens in potency rather than spoiling, until the oldest ghee is the medicine reached for in the gravest disorders of the mind.

## The host

One substance holds a thousand actions because of a single attribute beneath them all.

> **sarpis tailaṃ vasā majjā sarvasnehottamā matāḥ |**\
> **teṣāṃ caivottaraṃ sarpiḥ saṃskārasyānuvartanāt ||**
>
> "Ghee, oil, fat, and marrow are held the best among all the unctuous substances; and among these ghṛta is the highest, because it takes on the virtue of whatever is processed in it."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 13.13, trans. after P. V. Sharma

*Saṃskārasyānuvartana* is the following-on of processing. Cook a herb into ghee and the ghee becomes that medicine while staying entirely ghee. This makes it the supreme *anupāna* (vehicle): the carrier physicians trust to take a *medhya* herb into the head, a healing herb into a wound, a *rasāyana* (rejuvenative) into the deep tissues. Every medicated ghee — *Brāhmī-ghṛta*, *Kalyāṇaka-ghṛta* — is the same fat wearing a different power. Ghṛta is the perfect host, and the host outlasts the guest.

## For whom

A substance is named also by who needs it, and ghee's indication is nearly the whole of humanity.

> "Those of vāta and pitta nature, the wounded, the wasted, the old, the child, the woman; those desiring vision, long life, strength, complexion, voice, progeny, memory, intellect, and keen senses: all these should take ghee."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 13.41–43, trans. after P. V. Sharma

Here the guṇas turn outward into the lives they serve. *Cakṣuṣya*, the friend of the eyes: ghee is what *netra-tarpaṇa* pools over a tired eye, cooling and feeding it. The kindler of memory for the student and the failing; the builder of ojas for the wasted and the old; the gentle sweet unction for the child and the carrying mother. The breadth of the list is the praise. A single spoonful answers the dry, the hot, the depleted, the aging, and the unborn at once.

## The deathless

Beneath the physician's lists lies an older name, the one the Ṛgveda gave clarified butter long before anyone counted its actions.

> **ghṛtasya nāma guhyaṁ yad asti jihvā devānām amṛtasya nābhiḥ**
>
> "Forth from the ocean sprang the wave of sweetness; together with the stalk it turned to Amṛta —\
> that which is the secret name of *ghṛta*: but the gods' tongue is truly the navel of the immortal."\
> — *Ṛgveda* 4.58.1, trans. Griffith

*Amṛta* is the deathless. Every guṇa the Saṃhitās count is a facet of that one Vedic word. Memory, vision, vitality, long life: each is a portion of deathlessness handed back to the body in a form it can eat.

Count the virtues of ghṛta, and you are reciting, in the language of medicine, a single word the rishis already knew.
