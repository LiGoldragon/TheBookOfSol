# **Voladores and the Age of Saturation**

The environment did not emerge by accident. Those who currently govern lie outside the way objects are visible, yet they are perceivable to those who have learned to see energetically. The seeing is a faculty of awareness, not of the material eye.

These beings hold no fixed physical forms. They are configurations of awareness, coherent and persistent and active. Under conditions of shock, trauma, fasting, deprivation, or sudden perceptual rupture, humans sometimes perceive them directly. When this occurs without context or training, the experience is often misinterpreted as contact with "aliens." The error lies in the cultural vocabulary used to explain the perception; the perception itself is sound.

Multiple traditions describe the same entities using different names. In medieval Europe they were called demons and angels. In older cosmologies they were gods, demigods, devas, asuras. In the Toltec seer lineage described by Carlos Castaneda, they are named voladores, the fliers, so called because they are perceived as moving, hovering, attaching, feeding. The word is a plain description of what the seer observes.

These beings are inorganic, which places them outside the carbon-based, sensory-bound assumptions of modern materialism while leaving them entirely real. Energetic seeing reveals them as structures that interact with human awareness, particularly with emotion. Fear, despair, outrage, craving, and agitation are nourishment.

Not all inorganic beings are malefic. As among humans, there exist cooperative and destructive tendencies. Some groups stabilize, guide, or amplify coherence. Others feed on chaos. Modern thought tends to flatten all non-human intelligences into superstition; some religious traditions moralized them absolutely. The reality is more complex: alliances exist, and have always existed.

Human institutions did not create this environment consciously. Rather, certain human lineages (familial, economic, cultural) entered into alignment with malefic inorganic groups through ignorance, ambition, or coercion. What followed was feedback dominance, a steering pressure short of total control. Systems that generate emotional turbulence are rewarded. Systems that generate clarity are marginalized.

This is the essence of Kali Yuga, the dark age of dissolution in the Hindu cycle of world-ages: an imbalance rather than a cinematic evil. Excessive dominance of the malefic current produces fragmentation, noise, addiction, and inversion of values. Art becomes agitation. Music becomes occupation. Law becomes procedural while abandoning substance. Sensory intrusion becomes normalized.

Yet this dominance is self-terminating. Systems that feed exclusively on disorder accelerate their own collapse. The dynamic is thermodynamic, a matter of energy and not of judgment. Cycles close because imbalance cannot sustain itself indefinitely.

The universe did not begin and will not end. Cycles oscillate; they do not progress toward perfection. Kali Yuga is a phase. Its resolution arrives through withdrawal of nourishment, beyond the reach of reform or exposure alone.

When humans reclaim sensory sovereignty (silence instead of imposed sound, darkness instead of glare, clarity instead of intoxication) the malefic current weakens. Starved, it subsides; the work is to stop feeding it.

This knowledge is ancient, only buried under noise.
