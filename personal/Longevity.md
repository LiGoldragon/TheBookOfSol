# **On True Longevity: Conservation, Not Consumption**

Reports of extreme human longevity appear implausible only when filtered through modern assumptions about nutrition, physiology, and aging. Within the classical framework of **Āyurveda** and **yoga**, such reports are coherent, internally consistent, and expected under specific conditions of life.

Dr. Robert McCarrison, of the British Army Medical Service in India, documented one such case in the early twentieth century. Writing of a Himalayan population, he observed:

> "Men well over 200 years of age were working in the fields with much younger men, doing as much work, and looking so much like the younger men, that I was unable to distinguish the old from the young."

— K. L. Coe, *Correct Eating & Strength*, March 1931

McCarrison verified that no error existed in their reckoning of time. The anomaly, therefore, was not chronological but conceptual: their longevity did not fit the prevailing model of how life is supposedly sustained.

Āyurveda offers a different model. Longevity is governed by **ojas**, the most refined essence of nourishment. Ojas emerges only when digestion (**agni**) is stable, intake is moderate, and losses are restrained. The preservation of ojas, rather than its rapid manufacture, determines lifespan.

A central Āyurvedic principle is that excessive outflow shortens life. Excessive urination, defecation, sweating, ejaculation, bleeding, and purgation are all described as depleting forces when habitual. Classical sources repeatedly warn that **ati-pravṛtti** (uncontrolled expenditure) leads to degeneration, while **saṃgraha** (containment) supports longevity. Orderly hunger preserves the body; leakage depletes it.

Within this physiological logic, **Amarolī** occupies a precise role. Described in **Haṭha Yoga** and **tantrika** lineages, Amarolī is a technique of internal recycling. Urine, in this model, is treated as a late-stage filtrate of blood that still carries regulatory and nutritive elements. When used in controlled contexts, it forms a closed-loop feedback mechanism that reduces dependency on external intake and slows systemic depletion.

Amarolī is inseparable from fasting, minimal diet, **brahmacarya**, and restraint of sensory excess. Without these conditions, the practice is incoherent. With them, it aligns with the broader Āyurvedic objective: reducing loss rather than increasing supply.

The Himalayan populations observed by McCarrison match this profile. Their diets were sparse, free of habitual chloride intake, and unburdened by constant stimulation. Physical labor was steady but not exhaustive. Sexuality was regulated rather than indulgent. Periodic fasting was normal. Life proceeded at a pace that allowed ojas to accumulate rather than be spent.

From this perspective, extreme longevity follows as a consequence. When the body is spared perpetual replacement mode, constantly rebuilding what is unnecessarily lost, aging slows. What modern thought interprets as "anti-aging" through intervention, Āyurveda understands as aging prevented through non-dissipation.

True longevity is the mastery of conservation. It is achieved by closing leaks, stabilizing agni, and preserving the most refined products of life. The accounts that astonish modern observers cease to be miraculous once the underlying logic is restored.
