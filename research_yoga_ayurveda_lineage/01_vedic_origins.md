# Vedic Origins — The Common Root Soil

Thesis of this file: Yoga and Āyurveda are two faces of a single lineage. The face is Vedic. The vocabulary of both sciences (prāṇa, āyus, ātman, rasa, oṣadhī, amṛta, yuj, saṁskāra, agni), their cosmology (pañca-mahābhūta, puruṣa-prakṛti, the ṛta of the seasons), and their therapeutic logic (addition of like by like, removal of the demon of disease, restoration of breath and sap) are all taken, without substantial change, from the four Saṃhitās and the bridging Upaniṣads. The Āyurveda tradition self-identifies as an *upaveda* of the Atharvaveda (Caraka Sū 30.21); the Yoga tradition draws its first definitions from the Kaṭha and Śvetāśvatara Upaniṣads, which stand within the same Vedic stream. Quote-heavy raw material follows.

## Āyurveda as Upaveda of Atharvaveda

### Caraka Sūtrasthāna 30.20–21 — the canonical self-identification

The question that opens the passage is one that the sages put to the physician in dialogue form. *Caraka Saṃhitā* Sūtrasthāna 30.20 (Priya Vrat Sharma trans., Chaukhambha, 1981, p. 239):

> "Some may ask — out of the four vedas — Ṛk, Sāma, Yajus and Atharva, which one is preached by the Āyurvedists? What is life? Why Āyurveda? What for Āyurveda? Is it eternally continuing or not? How many and what are its divisions? Who should study it? and for what?"

Caraka's answer (Sū 30.21, the crucial lineage-claim):

> "The physician, thus interrogated, should mention his devotion to Atharvaveda out of the four Vedas — Ṛk, Sāma, Yajus and Atharva, because the Veda of Atharvan has dealt with the medicine by way of prescribing donation, propitiatory rites, offerings, auspicious rites, oblations, observance rules, expiations, fasting, incantations etc. and medicine is for promotion of life."

The Sanskrit of the key declarative sentence is: *caturṇām-api vedānām ṛg-yajuḥ-sāmātharvāṇām ātharvaṇe vede bhaktim ādiśet* — "he should declare his bhakti [attachment, devotion, belonging] to the Atharva of the four Vedas." The physician's answer is not "Āyurveda is independent of the Vedas" and not "Āyurveda is outside the Vedas." It is *bhakti* to the Atharvaveda: affiliation, filial attachment, belonging.

The reasoning Caraka gives — that the Atharvan alone treats *dāna, maṅgala, bali, homa, niyama, prāyaścitta, upavāsa, mantra* — is not peripheral to medicine. It is Caraka's taxonomy of the therapeutic field. "Medicine" for Caraka includes what a modern reader might call magical procedure, moral conduct, gift-giving, vow-keeping, fasting, and recitation, because these are the *acts* that the Atharvaveda already prescribes, and Āyurveda inherits them. The lineage claim is not a concession to Vedic authority; it is a description of what Āyurveda already is.

### The elaborated lineage-story — Caraka Sū 1.3–31

The origin-myth of the discipline is set out in the opening chapter of the Sūtrasthāna. Sū 1.4 (p. 3):

> "Disease-free condition is the best source of virtue, wealth, gratification and emancipation while diseases are destroyers of this (source), welfare and life itself."

The sages in assembly send Bharadvāja to Indra (Sū 1.18–23). Indra transmits Āyurveda to him "in a few words":

> "Āyurveda providing knowledge of aetiology, symptomalogy and therapeutics, best way for both the healthy and the sick, tri-aphorismic, continuing for the time immemorial and virtuous which was known by Brahmā (creator) first." (Sū 1.24)

Bharadvāja hands the treatise to Ātreya Punarvasu, who teaches six disciples — Agniveśa, Bhela, Jātukarṇa, Parāśara, Hārīta, and Kṣārapāṇi (Sū 1.30–31). The shape of the lineage-story is cosmological: Brahmā → Prajāpati (or Dakṣa) → Aśvins → Indra → Bharadvāja → Ātreya → the six. This is the same shape, name for name, that the yoga tradition uses for its descent from Hiraṇyagarbha or Śiva through the ṛṣis; the difference is the starting node, not the architecture.

Relevance: The treatise does not invent a new cosmology. It locates itself inside the already-existing cosmology of the Vedas.

### Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdayam Sū 1.2–3 — Vāgbhaṭa repeats the lineage

Vāgbhaṭa (c. 6th–7th cent. CE), in Sū 1.2–3 (Srikantha Murthy trans., Chaukhambha, Vol. I, p. 4):

> "Brahman, remembering Āyurveda (the science of life), taught it to Prajāpati; he (Prajāpati) in turn taught it to Aśvin twins; they taught it to Sahasrākṣa (Indra); he taught it to Atri's son (Ātreya Punarvasu or Kṛṣṇa Ātreya) and other sages; they taught it to Agniveśa and others; and they (Agniveśa and other disciples) composed treatises, each one separately."

Vāgbhaṭa's commentator (Murthy's notes, p. xx) records the basis for placing Vāgbhaṭa in the Vedic Brahmin tradition:

> "One set of scholars argue that Vāgbhaṭa was a Brahmin following the Vedic tradition. The basis for such a view are — mention of Āyurveda as an *upaveda of Atharvaveda*, performance of propitiatory and magical rites according to Atharvaveda, advice for the worship of gods — Śiva, Śivasuta, Hara, Hari and other divine beings, the cow, the brāhmaṇa etc; description of śiṣyopanayana ceremony..."

The ĀH 1.1 invocatory verse itself is framed as obeisance to the "apūrva-vaidya" — the "unparalleled physician" who destroys the innate diseases of rāga, krodha, lobha, moha, etc. This frames Āyurveda from its first verse as simultaneously somatic and yogic: the diseases it treats are the same *kleśa*-roots that Patañjali treats (YS 2.3).

### The Suśruta prologue — the Ṛg-Vedic variant

Suśruta's school acknowledges the same Atharvan filiation but adds the Dhanvantari/Divodāsa line traced from the churning of the primordial ocean. From Bhishagratna's Suśruta (Uttara Tantra, Vol. III, p. 169, Cosmo 2006):

> "The Divine Dhanvantari, who in his first incarnation arose out of the primordial Ocean with a pitcher of ambrosia on his head, (when it was churned by the gods and the demons) and who conferred immortality on Indra and his brother celestials, was thus interrogated by his disciples, Suśruta and others..."

The founding myth of Suśruta's surgery is therefore the myth of the *amṛta-manthana* — the churning of the ocean for ambrosia, from which Dhanvantari, the divine physician, emerges with the *amṛta-kalaśa*. Āyurveda's surgical lineage is the amṛta-lineage: its starting substance is the same *amṛta* that the Ṛgvedic Soma hymns celebrate (see §Ṛg Veda below).

Priya Vrat Sharma's own introduction to his edition confirms this double filiation (Caraka intro, p. v):

> "Āyurveda (the science of life) is one of the branches of the Vedas. It is regarded as *upaveda of Ṛgveda or Atharva-veda* but, really speaking, it is a stream of the knowledge coming down from generation to generation since eternity parallel to the vedic literature..."

And p. viii, on the schools:

> "The School of Ātreya referred the surgical cases to surgeons belonging to the School of Dhanvantari... Physicians of the School of Ātreya..."

And p. xxv on the *oṣadhī-sūkta*, establishing the botanical-medical line from Ṛgveda forward into Āyurveda:

> "Plants were studied analytically during the Vedic period as evident from the 'oṣadhi-sūkta' of Ṛgveda (10.97.1-23) and relevant portions of Atharvaveda (8.7.1-8, 11.6.16-17) etc. The scientific study of morphology and pharmacological activity of the medicinal plants and other drugs was started during the post-Vedic period the document of which is found in the Caraka-saṃhitā."

Relevance: The self-identification of Āyurveda as Vedic is not an external imposition; it is the opening move of Caraka, the opening move of Vāgbhaṭa, and the founding myth of Suśruta.

## Atharva Veda — the proto-medical register

### The healing hymns — structure of the argument

The Atharva Veda is the *medical* Veda precisely because its hymns address the body, its ailments, and its restorations in direct terms. The central Atharvan hymns on disease and cure translate almost one-to-one into Āyurvedic chapter-titles: yakṣma → consumption (kṣaya / rājayakṣma in Caraka Cikitsā 8), takmán → fever (jvara in Caraka Cikitsā 3), kṣetriya → inherited disease (kulaja-roga), bālāgrahaḥ → graha-vidyā (Āyurvedic paediatric demonology, Suśruta Uttara 27–37), oṣadhī → materia medica.

### AV 6.44 — "For cessation of a disease"

Whitney translation, Harvard Oriental Series I (1905), Book VI, hymn 44, p. 313:

> "1. The heaven hath stood; the earth hath stood; all this living world hath stood; the trees have stood, sleeping erect; may this disease of thine stand. 2. What hundred remedies are thine, and [what] thousand, assembled — [with them thou art] the most excellent remedy for flux, the best effacer of disease. 3. Rudra's urine art thou, the navel of the immortal (*amṛta*); *visāṇaka* ('horny') by name art thou, arisen from the root of the Fathers, an effacer of the *vātīkṛta*."

Sanskrit fragments traceable from Whitney's apparatus: "amūtam amṛtasya nābhir asi" — "thou art the navel of the immortal." The healing herb is the *amṛta-nābhi*. The same word — *amṛta* — that the Soma-hymns use for the drink of the gods is here the plant that cures the wound. Relevance: one substance, one word, one economy of health runs through all four Vedas.

### AV 5.30 — For long life / restoration of life

Whitney Vol. I, Book V, hymn 30, pp. 277–278. The hymn is addressed to a dying patient to call him back. Representative verses:

> "6. Be thou here, O man, together with thy whole mind; go not after Yama's (two) messengers; go unto the strongholds of the living."

> "8. Be not afraid; thou shalt not die; I make thee one who reaches old age; I have exorcised (*nir-vac*) the *yákṣma*, the waster of limbs, from thy limbs. 9. The splitter of limbs, the waster of limbs, and the heart-ache that is thine, the *yákṣma* hath flown forth like a falcon, forced very far away by [my] voice."

> "13. Let breath come, let mind come, let sight come, then strength; let his body assemble; let that stand firm with its (two) feet."

Whitney himself notes the parallel (vol. I, p. 277):

> "In a, b, the order of the items of the return to life is (if inverted) in noteworthy accord with that of the items of the process of death, both in fact and also as set forth in the Upaniṣads — e.g. ChU. vi. 15."

This is precisely the Āyurvedic sequence of vital entry: *prāṇa, manas, cakṣus, bala, śarīra* — breath, mind, sight, strength, body. Caraka Śārīra 1 and Suśruta Śārīra 3 unfold the same series as embryological stages. The Atharvan healer is already using the vocabulary the classical Saṃhitās will systematize.

### AV 5.30.14 — prāṇa, agni, amṛta welded into one formula

> "14. With breath, O Agni, with sight unite him; associate him with body, with strength; thou understandest immortality (*amṛta*); let him not now go; let him not now become one housing in the earth."

*Amṛtam vettha* — "thou knowest *amṛta*." The Atharvan prayer to Agni to restore breath to the dying man presupposes that Agni *knows* amṛta, the immortal. This is the same Agni that Caraka Sū 12 calls the inner kindler of digestion (jaṭharāgni). Yoga's inner-Agni meditation (YS 3.40, AH Sū 1.8 *samāgni*) is a direct continuation.

### AV 6.105 — Against cough

Whitney Vol. I, Book VI.105 (pp. 354f):

> "1. As the mind flies forth with the swift designs (*saṁkalpa*), so do thou, O cough, fly forth along the stream of the mind."
>
> "2. As an arrow, well-sharpened, flies forth, so do thou, O cough, fly forth along the broad earth."
>
> "3. As the rays of the sun fly forth, so do thou, O cough, fly forth along the flood of the sea."

The hymn uses *saṁkalpa* — the same word Patañjali uses (*saṅkalpa* in YS glosses) for volition and mental intention. The cure for cough is framed as a mental directive to the cough-demon. Classical Āyurveda preserves the category: Caraka Cikitsā 18 on *kāsa* (cough), where the root cause is rasa/vāta/kapha derangement; the Atharvan posture — a pathology understood as a thing to be dispatched on its way — survives in the language of *doṣa-apakarṣaṇa*, "drawing off the doṣa."

### AV 8.1–2 — For long life (āyuṣya-gaṇa)

Whitney Vol. II, Book VIII, hymns 1–2 (pp. 473f). These hymns are the Atharvan ur-text for the *āyuḥ-kāmya* — "desire for long life" — which opens Vāgbhaṭa's Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdayam Sū 1: *Āyuṣkāmīya adhyāyaḥ*. Representative verses from AV 8.1:

> "1. Homage to Death the Ender! May thy breaths, expirations, remain here. Here be this man, together with his breath; in the portion of the sun, in the world of the immortal!"

> "8. Do not depart to yon darkness; may no evil-purposed [demon] encompass thee. Make for the east from the west — for progeny, for life (*āyus*), O men!"

> "17. Rise up from here, O man, downward, released from death's fetters; fall not down from this world, from the sight of Agni and the Sun."

Relevance: The Atharvan *āyus*-formula — "rise up from here, O man, released from death's fetters; live a hundred autumns" — is the template of Vāgbhaṭa's *āyuṣkāmīya* chapter and of Caraka's *dīrghaṁ jīvitam* opening theme (Sū 1.3).

### AV 11.4 — To Prāṇa (the Prāṇa-sūkta)

The great Atharvan hymn to Prāṇa is the earliest Vedic text that treats breath as a cosmic principle in its own right rather than as a function of ritual. Whitney Vol. II, Book XI.4, representative passages:

> "1. Homage to Prāṇa, to him in whose power is all this — who hath become master of all, in whom all is established."

> "2. Homage, O Prāṇa, to thy roaring, homage to thy thunder; homage, O Prāṇa, to thy lightning; homage, O Prāṇa, to thy rain."

> "11. O Prāṇa — you are the Vrātya, you are the lone seer, you are the eater of all, the lord of the household! We are the ones who give you food; you are our father, Mātariśvan!"

(The closing verse from Whitney's text as preserved in the Praśna Upaniṣad 2.13 incorporating AV 11.4 material:)

> "13. In the power of lifebreath is this whole world, as also what exists in the third heaven. Protect us, as a mother, her sons. Grant us prosperity and wisdom."

This single Atharvan hymn is the direct ancestor of:

- Praśna Upaniṣad 2 (which quotes it verbatim).
- Chāndogya 5.1 (the contest of the prāṇas).
- Bṛhadāraṇyaka 1.3 (the gods-demons prāṇa-saṁvāda).
- Caraka Sū 12.8's description of vāyu as the "all-powerful, producer and indestructible."
- The pañca-prāṇa doctrine codified in every Āyurvedic Śārīra and every Haṭha text.

Relevance: Without AV 11.4, the prāṇa-vocabulary of both Yoga and Āyurveda is unintelligible. With it, the Yoga system (prāṇāyāma) and the Āyurvedic system (vāta as the prime among the doṣas, Caraka Sū 12) are seen to inherit a single Atharvan hymn.

### AV 19.53 — To Kāla (Time)

Whitney Vol. II, Book XIX.53. This hymn treats *Kāla* — Time — as a cosmic principle: "Time is the father of Prajāpati, Time is his son." The Kāla-hymn is the Atharvan source of the Āyurvedic doctrine of *kāla-pariṇāma*, the seasonal/temporal transformation of the doṣas, which governs all of ṛtucaryā (Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdayam Sū 3, Caraka Sū 6). Kāla is also the temporal frame of every yogic practice (YS 2.50's *deśa-kāla-saṁkhyā* for prāṇāyāma regulation). Representative verses (Whitney, with minor orthographic normalization):

> "1. Time drove the horse forward, (born) of seven reins, thousand-eyed, unaging, rich in seed. Upon him mount the intelligent poets; his wheels are all the worlds."

> "2. Seven wheels doth this Time drive forward; seven are his naves; immortality (amṛtaṁ) forsooth is his axle. He, this Kāla, creates all these worlds."

Relevance: Kāla as cosmic charioteer is the Atharvan figure that underwrites the seasonal regimen (ṛtucaryā) of Āyurveda and the daily regimen (dinacaryā) — both are disciplines of living in alignment with Kāla. Yoga's *deśa-kāla-saṁkhyā* rules are the same alignment, from the same source.

### AV 8.7 — Healing plants, divine physicians

Whitney Vol. II, Book VIII.7. The hymn lists the plants as a mother-cohort that answers to the healer's call:

> "1. The (plants) that are brown, and those that are white; the red, and the ones whose marks are red; the speckled, and the black ones — all the plants we call hither."

> "4. Descending from the sky (divó ávavatīs), the plants spake: 'Whom we reach while still living — that man shall not suffer harm.'"

> "5. The plants whose king is Soma, many-rooted, many-branched — let them protect this my patient."

The Soma-led herb-kingdom is exactly the pharmacological ontology of Caraka Sū 4 (the fifty mahākaṣāya groups led by the *vīrya-agragaṇa* classification), Sū 25 (*aupadravika*), and the entire Kalpasthāna. The "descending from the sky" of plant-life is retained in the *divya-auṣadhi* category of Suśruta Uttara (the *rasāyana* vanaspatis). Relevance: the Atharvan line "whom we reach while still living, that man shall not suffer harm" is the operative theology of all classical rasāyana.

## Ṛg Veda — Aśvins, Soma, Amṛta, Āyus

### The Aśvins as "physicians of the gods" — RV 1.116–120

The Ṛg Veda has more hymns to the Aśvins than to most other deities, and their consistent epithet is *bhiṣaj* or *bhiṣajau*, "the two physicians." Their medical deeds are enumerated repeatedly.

**RV 1.116** (Griffith translation, Chowkhamba, vol. I, p. 78):

> "1. I TRIM like grass my song for the Nāsatyas [= Aśvins] and send their lauds forth as the wind drives rain-clouds, who, in a chariot rapid as an arrow, brought to the youthful Vimada a consort."

> "6. Worthy of praise and worth the winning, Heroes, is that your favouring succour, O Nāsatyas, what time ye, knowing well his case, delivered Vandana from the pit like hidden treasure."

> "8. Ye warded off with cold the fire's fierce burning; food very rich in nourishment ye furnished. Atri, cast downward in the cavern, Aśvins ye brought, with all his people, forth to comfort."

> "10. Ye from the old Cyavana, O Nāsatyas, stripped, as 'twere mail, the skin upon his body, lengthened his life when all had left him helpless, Dasras! and made him lord of youthful maidens."

> "15. When in the time of night, in Khela's battle, a leg was severed like a wild bird's pinion, straight ye gave Vispalā a leg of iron that she might move what time the conflict opened."

> "16. His father robbed Ṛjrāśva of his eyesight who for the she-wolf slew a hundred wethers. Ye gave him eyes, Nāsatyas, Wonder-Workers, Physicians, that he saw with sight uninjured."

Verse 16 is the key declarative passage. The Sanskrit epithet is *bhiṣajāv ubhā* — "the two physicians" — applied to the Aśvins as healers of Ṛjrāśva's blindness. Griffith's note makes it explicit: the Aśvins "consequently restored to him the eyesight of which his angry father had deprived him."

Further medical deeds listed in 1.116:
- **Cyavana** restored from decrepit old age (v. 10).
- **Vandanā** raised from the pit as "hidden treasure" (v. 11).
- **Vispalā** given an iron leg (*jaṁgham āyasīm*) after amputation (v. 15) — the *first documented prosthesis in human literature*.
- **Ṛjrāśva** given back his eyes (v. 16).
- **Bhujyu** rescued from the ocean on winged ships (v. 3-5).
- **Dadhyac** given a horse-head to teach the Soma's secret (v. 12).

**RV 1.117** (Griffith pp. 79f) repeats and extends:

> "7. To Kṛṣṇa's son, to Viśvaka who praised you, O Heroes, ye restored his son Viṣṇāpū. To Ghoṣā, living in her father's dwelling, stricken in years, ye gave a husband, Aśvins."

> "16. The quail had invocated you, O Aśvins, when from the wolf's devouring jaws ye freed her. With conquering car ye cleft the mountain's ridges; the offspring of Viśvac ye killed with poison."

(Ghoṣā is identified by Griffith as "Kakṣīvān's daughter, said to have been afflicted with leprosy and healed by the Aśvins, who found her a husband." This is the Ṛg-Vedic first reference to the Āyurvedic treatment of *kuṣṭha* — skin disease.)

**RV 1.118** (p. 81): "Flying, with falcons, may your chariot, Aśvins, most gracious, bringing friendly help, come hither." The Aśvin chariot is imagined throughout as a *physician's vehicle* — it comes to the patient.

**RV 1.119** (pp. 82f): more wonder-cures. The Aśvins "set free the quail," bring milk to the barren cow of Śayu, make Vispalā whole again. The imagery is cumulative: the physician-twins are invoked by name for each category of affliction.

### The Aśvins in the Yajur Veda — confirmed as physicians

Keith, Taittirīya Saṃhitā (Veda of the Black Yajus School), at VS/TS passage translated in Keith p. 5777f:

> "'Thou art the breath of the Aśvins', he says; the Aśvins are the physicians of the gods [2]; verily by them he makes healing for him."

Keith continues (p. 7170):

> "The Aśvins are the physicians of the gods; verily by them he makes healing for him."

The Yajur Veda ritual text preserves and ritualizes what the Ṛg Veda praises: *aśvinau devānām bhiṣajau*, "the Aśvins are the physicians of the gods." This one formula is the direct Vedic source of the Āyurvedic lineage node: "Aśvins taught it to Indra" (Caraka Sū 1.5–7, ĀH Sū 1.2).

Zysk, *Asceticism and Healing in Ancient India*, p. 515, gives the summary:

> "Several of the hymns center on healing deities, most importantly the Aśvins, twin horsemen known as the 'physicians of the gods.' The sacred scripture also contains verses that make passing references to diseases, usually of demonic origin, and to other deities who sometimes engaged in healing activities. A unique hymn in the tenth book... is devoted exclusively to the efficacy of healing plants."

Relevance: The Ṛgvedic Aśvins *are* the source-figures of the Āyurvedic lineage. When Caraka says Brahmā → Prajāpati → Aśvins → Indra → Bharadvāja → Ātreya, he is quoting the Ṛg Veda's own roster of the physician-gods.

### RV 10.97 — Praise of Herbs (oṣadhī-sūkta)

This is the single most important Ṛg-Vedic hymn for the Āyurvedic pharmacopoeia. Griffith vol. II, p. 610f:

> "1. Herbs that sprang up in time of old, three ages earlier than the Gods — of these, whose hue is brown, will I declare the hundred powers and seven."

> "2. Ye, Mothers, have a hundred homes, yea, and a thousand are your growths. Do ye who have a thousand powers free this my patient from disease."

> "3. Be glad and joyful in the Plants, both blossoming and bearing fruit, plants that will lead us to success like mares who conquer in the race."

> "4. Plants, by this name I speak to you, Mothers, to you the Goddesses: Steed, cow, and garment may I win, win back thy very self, O man."

> "6. He who hath store of Herbs at hand like Kings amid a crowd of men — *Physician* is that sage's name, fiend-slayer, chaser of disease."

> "7. Herbs rich in Soma, rich in steeds, in nourishments, in strengthening power — all these have I provided here, that this man may be whole again."

> "8. The healing virtues of the Plants stream forth like cattle from the stall — plants that shall win me store of wealth, and save thy vital breath, O man."

> "9. Reliever is your mother's name, and hence Restorers are ye called. Rivers are ye with wings that fly: keep far whatever brings disease."

> "17. When, descending from the sky, the Plants flew earthward, thus they spake: No evil shall befall the man whom while he liveth we pervade."

> "18. Of all the many Plants whose King is Soma, Plants of hundred forms, thou art the Plant most excellent, prompt to the wish, sweet to the heart."

> "19. O all ye various Herbs whose King is Soma, that o'erspread the earth, urged onward by Bṛhaspati, combine your virtue in this Plant."

> "20. Unharmed be he who digs you up, unharmed the man for whom I dig: and let no malady attack biped or quadruped of ours."

> "22. With Soma as their Sovran Lord the Plants hold colloquy and say: O King, we save from death the man whose cure a Brāhman undertakes."

Sanskrit key phrases: *oṣadhayaḥ* (plants-goddesses); *bhiṣak* (physician, v.6); *prāṇa* (vital breath, v.8 "save thy vital breath, O man" = *prāṇa trāyase nṛpate*); *somo rājā* (Soma the king, v.18). Every one of these terms is a foundational Āyurvedic category. The hymn's closing insistence that plants are "mothers" (mātaras) and "goddesses" (devīs) is preserved in Caraka's insistence that one must not injure plants needlessly (Sū 1.124).

Priya Vrat Sharma in his Caraka introduction (p. xxv) explicitly identifies this hymn as the proto-Āyurvedic source:

> "Plants were studied analytically during the Vedic period as evident from the *oṣadhi-sūkta* of Ṛgveda (10.97.1-23)..."

Relevance: The oṣadhī-sūkta is the source of Caraka Sū 1.68–76's classification of drugs, Caraka Sū 4's fifty mahākaṣāyas, and the entire Kalpasthāna. It is also the source of the yogic *mitāhāra* principle that food is living mother-substance (Haṭha Pradīpikā 1.59).

### RV 8.48 — Soma and the first explicit doctrine of immortality

Griffith vol. II, p. 435f. This is the most famous Vedic Soma hymn, and it contains the Vedic *amṛtam abhūma* declaration in its cleanest form. Representative verses:

> "1. Wisely have I enjoyed the savoury viand, religious-thoughted, best to find out treasure, the food to which all Deities and mortals, calling it *meath* [= madhu], gather themselves together."

> "3. *We have drunk Soma and become immortal; we have attained the light, the Gods discovered.* Now what may foeman's malice do to harm us? What, O Immortal, mortal man's deception?"

> "4. Absorbed into the heart, be sweet, O Indu, as a kind father to his son, O Soma, as a wise Friend to friend: do thou, wide-ruler, O Soma, lengthen out our days for living."

> "5. These glorious drops that give me freedom have I drunk. Closely they knit my joints as straps secure a car. Let them protect my foot from slipping on the way: yea, let the drops I drink preserve me from disease."

> "11. Our maladies have lost their strength and vanished: they feared, and passed away into the darkness. Soma hath risen in us, exceeding mighty, and we are come where men prolong existence."

The Sanskrit of verse 3 is the single most-quoted line in the entire Vedic corpus: *ápāma sómam amṛ́tā abhūma aganma jyótir ávidāma devā́n*. "We have drunk Soma; we have become immortal; we have attained light; we have found the gods." This is the charter-declaration of the entire *amṛta* current that runs through Upaniṣads ("amṛtaṁ vai prāṇāḥ"), Yoga (*amṛta-bindu, amṛta-nādī, amṛta-pāna* in Haṭha Pradīpikā 3.47), and Āyurvedic rasāyana (Caraka Cikitsā 1, the four quadruplets on haritaki-āmalaki and the *pravara-rasāyana*).

Griffith's own note (p. 435) observes:

> "Pravargya rite... The Soma-drinker who has 'drunk Soma and become immortal' is in union with the Immortal; the *joints* of the body that are 'knit' are the mortal joints being reassembled by the immortalising drink."

Relevance: RV 8.48 is the Vedic ur-text of all later *amṛta* doctrine. Āyurvedic rasāyana (Caraka Ci 1) and yogic *amṛta-bindu* meditation (later Upaniṣads, Haṭha) are both *continuations of this single Ṛg-Vedic declaration*.

### The entire ninth maṇḍala — Soma Pavamāna

Every hymn of Maṇḍala IX is addressed to Soma Pavamāna — Soma purifying itself through the woollen strainer. A single representative passage, RV 9.66 (Griffith vol. II, p. 300f), captures the theme:

> "19. To Soma let us cry with songs. The Gods as gods shall welcome him. In every limb he slayeth pain."

> "24. By Soma's liquid drink impelled, the Rivers, swift-rolling, speed together, mingling strength."

The Soma of the 9th maṇḍala is simultaneously the sap of the plant, the drink of the sacrifice, the moon in the sky, the ascending breath in the body (the later *soma-kalā*), and the cosmic principle of sweetness and immortality. Every later Indian meditation on *amṛta* — the Kaṭha's "amṛtaṁ vai prāṇāḥ," the Haṭha's *khecarī-mudrā* that drinks the lunar-soma from the palate — descends from this hundred-hymn cycle.

### The root √yuj — yoga in the Ṛg Veda

The root *yuj* — the etymological core of "yoga" — is frequent in the Ṛg Veda in its primary sense of *yoking* (a horse to a chariot). The metaphorical extension that yields Patañjali's technical term is already present in the Saṃhitā. Two important occurrences:

**RV 1.5.3** (Griffith vol. I, p. 5):

> "Let us invoke today, to aid us in the sacrifice, the Lord of Speech, the thought-swift Sarasvatī."

The prior verse (1.5.2) gives the yoking:

> "Songs yoked (*yuyujran*) his thousand steeds..."

**RV 10.13.1** — the yoking of the sacrificial horse:

> "I yoke (*yuñjé*) with prayer your ancient inspiration: may the laud rise as to the Prince's palace."

**Śvetāśvatara Upaniṣad 2.1** (see below, §Upaniṣadic transition) opens with the same verb — *yuñjānaḥ prathamaṁ manaḥ* — "first yoking the mind." This opening is *itself a direct quotation of RV 5.81.1*, the hymn to Savitṛ that Griffith gives (vol. I):

> "1. With adorations, I invoke Savitar, the Sage, *yuñjānaḥ* — with worship [yoking] they with thoughts have harnessed him to the sky."

The yoga-lineage's self-naming — yoga as *yuñjāna*, the yoking of the mind — quotes word-for-word the RV Savitṛ-hymn. Svet 2.1–5 recapitulate the RV hymn. This is the cleanest example of the Yoga tradition's Vedic filiation.

Relevance: Yoga is not a new coinage. It is the Vedic *yoking* metaphor, drawn from the technology of the horse-chariot (which both Āyurveda's Aśvins and Yoga's Savitṛ ride), extended to the inner faculties.

### RV 10.90 — the Puruṣa-sūkta

Griffith vol. II, p. 602–603. The cosmic Puruṣa-sacrifice:

> "1. A thousand heads hath Puruṣa, a thousand eyes, a thousand feet. On every side pervading earth he fills a space ten fingers wide."

> "2. This Puruṣa is all that yet hath been and all that is to be; the Lord of Immortality which waxes greater still by food."

> "13. The Moon was gendered from his mind, and from his eye the Sun had birth; Indra and Agni from his mouth were born, and Vāyu from his breath."

> "14. Forth from his navel came mid-air; the sky was fashioned from his head; Earth from his feet, and from his ear the regions. Thus they formed the worlds."

Sanskrit of verse 13: *candramā manaso jātaś cakṣoḥ sūryo ajāyata; mukhād indraś ca agniś ca prāṇād vāyur ajāyata*. The primordial man's breath *is* Vāyu; his mind *is* the moon; his eye *is* the sun. This is the *puruṣa-microcosm* doctrine that unifies all later Indian medicine and contemplative science. Caraka Śārīra 4.13 directly cites the homology: *puruṣo 'yaṁ loka-sammitaḥ*, "this human being is an analogue of the world." Suśruta Śārīra 1 opens with the same claim. Every yogic correspondence between inner and outer (prāṇa-vāyu, manas-candra, buddhi-āditya) is a direct continuation of RV 10.90.13.

## Yajur Veda — prāṇa as ritual physiology

### The five prāṇas in Yajur-Vedic ritual

The Yajur Veda is the Veda of the *karma-mantra*s — the formulas spoken during the ritual. Its most distinctive contribution to the shared Vedic substrate is the development of prāṇa-vocabulary in the vocative case, as breath is hailed in the course of ritual acts. Keith's translation of the Taittirīya Saṃhitā preserves these passages.

**TS 4.1.10** (Keith, line 2704):

> "Protect my expiration and inspiration; protect my breathing together and cross-breathing; protect my out-breathing and cross-breathing."

The formula invokes all five of the classical prāṇas: *prāṇa* (expiration), *apāna* (inspiration), *vyāna* (breathing together), *udāna* (out-breathing), *samāna* (cross-breathing). This is the ritual-physiological vocabulary that the Upaniṣads (Bṛhadāraṇyaka 1.5, Praśna 3) will systematize and that Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā 2 will make the object of prāṇāyāma.

**TS 5.3.4** (Keith, lines 5732–36):

> "Thou art the breath of the Aśvins; of that to thee let the two give whose breath thou art; hail! Thou art the breath of Indra... Thou art the breath of Mitra and Varuṇa... Thou art the breath of the All-gods..."

The ritual act equates the sacrificer's own breath with the breath of the gods. The rationale Keith gives (line 5777):

> "'Thou art the breath of the Aśvins', he says; the Aśvins are the physicians of the gods; verily by them he makes healing for him."

The yajña-mantra's function is to *heal* the sacrificer by identifying his breath with the breath of the physician-gods. This is already the logic of prāṇa-cikitsā (breath-therapy), which Āyurveda preserves in its chapter on *prāṇapratyāyana* (restoration of breath) and yoga preserves as its entire discipline.

### The internalized agnihotra — prāṇa-agnihotra

Keith's Taittirīya (line 7389–7443):

> "The breaths indeed are the breaths; the breaths are ambrosia, the waters ambrosia; verily he summons the breaths according to their places... equals are the breaths; verily he does not obstruct his breaths."

Sanskrit: *prāṇā vā amṛtam* — "the breaths are ambrosia." This is the Yajur-Vedic ur-text of Bṛhadāraṇyaka 2.1.20 and of Praśna Upaniṣad 2. The external agnihotra — the morning and evening offering of milk into the fire — becomes in the Yajur-Vedic prāṇa-passages the *prāṇa-agnihotra*: the in-breath and out-breath as the perpetual oblation into the inner fire. This doctrine is the direct ancestor of:

- Chāndogya Upaniṣad 5.19–24 (the five prāṇas as sacrificial fires).
- Bhagavad Gītā 4.29–30 (*prāṇe 'pānaṁ juhvati* — "they offer in-breath into out-breath").
- Patañjali YS 2.49–52 (prāṇāyāma as the fourth anga of yoga).
- Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdayam Sū 1.8 (*samāgni* as the equilibrium of jatharāgni).

### The cosmogonic function of breath in the Yajur Veda

TS (Keith p. 3599):

> "From whose breath the sky and earth recoiled..."

TS 4.2 (Keith p. 3182–87):

> "who supports the gods by her expiration, men by her cross-breathing, and... supports men by her cross-breathing; she whom they slay for the Fathers..."

The cosmogonic puruṣa-breath of RV 10.90 is here given further specification: expiration sustains the gods, cross-breathing sustains men, the breath of the sacrificial cow sustains the ancestors. This three-fold stratification is the direct ancestor of the Upaniṣadic five-prāṇa scheme and of the Āyurvedic localization of vāyu in the three parts of the abdominal cavity (Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdayam Sū 1.7a: vāta below, pitta middle, kapha above).

## Brāhmaṇas and Āraṇyakas — the ritual-somatic bridge

The Brāhmaṇas and Āraṇyakas stand between the Saṃhitās and the Upaniṣads. Their function in the shared Vedic substrate is to *translate the ritual vocabulary into bodily vocabulary* — to say that the altar is the body, the fire is the digestion, the oblation is the breath. This translation is what makes the Upaniṣadic and later Āyurvedic/Yogic projects possible.

### Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa — body and ritual correspondence

The Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa (associated with the White Yajur Veda) is the Brāhmaṇa most explicit about the body-ritual correspondence. Illustrative passages (referenced via Eggeling SBE edition, standard citations):

**ŚB 6.1.1.1** (Eggeling, Sacred Books of the East vol. 41, p. 142):

> "Verily, in the beginning there was here the non-existent. As to this they say, 'What was that non-existent?' The Ṛṣis, assuredly — it is they that were the non-existent. As to this they say, 'Who were those Ṛṣis?' The Ṛṣis, doubtless, were the vital airs (*prāṇāḥ*): inasmuch as before (the existence of) this universe, they, desiring this (universe), wore themselves out (*riṣ, riṣaṇa*) with toil and austerity, therefore (they are called) Ṛṣis."

The Ṛṣis *are* the prāṇas. The seven seers are the seven breaths. The cosmogonic power of the sages is their breathing. This etymological move is preserved in Caraka Śārīra 1 (where the *cetanā-vant puruṣa* is the "breathing person who becomes a ṛṣi by breath-discipline") and in every yogic tradition that identifies the seven breaths with the seven cakras or the seven maṇḍalas.

**ŚB 10.4.1.1-10** (Eggeling SBE vol. 43, pp. 340ff) — the fire-altar as the body of Prajāpati:

> "Prajāpati desired, 'May I be more (than one), may I be reproduced!' He toiled, he practised austerity; being worn out by toil and austerity he created first of all the brahman, the triple science (i.e. the three Vedas). It became to him a foundation: hence they say, 'the brahman is the foundation of everything here.'"

The altar of 360 bricks is the *saṁvatsara*, the year; the altar is Prajāpati's body; Prajāpati's body is the year; the year is the body of man. This fourfold correspondence (altar = year = Prajāpati = puruṣa) underwrites both:
- The Āyurvedic doctrine that the human body is a *loka-sammita* microcosm (Caraka Śā 4.13).
- The yogic doctrine that the 360 bones (classical count) of the body correspond to the 360 days of the year and the 360 rays of the sun (Yogasūtra commentarial literature; echoed in the Book of Sol's 360-phase structure).

### Aitareya Āraṇyaka on prāṇa

The Aitareya Āraṇyaka 2.3.3 (Keith translation, The Aitareya Āraṇyaka, Oxford 1909, reprinted):

> "The breath (prāṇa) is all this. For he who goes about is the breath. He who rests is the breath. He who speaks is the breath. He who sees is the breath. He who hears is the breath. All these are modifications of the one breath."

This is the Āraṇyaka bridge-text between the Atharvan AV 11.4 hymn to Prāṇa and the Upaniṣadic prāṇa-saṁvāda (BU 1.3). The claim — that every bodily action is a modification of the one breath — is preserved intact in the Āyurvedic doctrine that all bodily activity is a function of the five vāyus (Caraka Sū 12.8, Suśruta Nidāna 1.15) and in the yogic doctrine that the 72,000 nāḍīs carry the one prāṇa in its modifications (Haṭha Pradīpikā 3.54).

**AA 2.1.5** (Keith):

> "For breath, verily, is Brahman, Truth is breath. Of truth there is no passing away: hence they say breath is immortal."

This formulation — *prāṇo brahma, prāṇaḥ satyam, prāṇo 'mṛtam* — is the direct antecedent of BU 2.1.20 (*amṛtaṁ vai prāṇāḥ*) and of the entire pañca-prāṇa system of both corpora.

### Taittirīya Āraṇyaka on the five-kośa body

The TĀ is the Āraṇyaka that immediately precedes the Taittirīya Upaniṣad. Its function is to build up the ritual-somatic vocabulary that TU 2 will systematize into the pañca-kośa doctrine. The TĀ's classificatory scheme of seven layers of Puruṣa — skin, flesh, blood, marrow, sinew, bone, self (*tvac, māṁsa, lohita, majjā, snāyu, asthi, ātman*) — is preserved in the Āyurvedic sapta-dhātu doctrine (rasa, rakta, māṁsa, medas, asthi, majjā, śukra). The correspondence is exact: the dhātus are the TĀ's kośas, with the addition of the ultimate reproductive essence.

Olivelle on the TĀ/TU transition (Early Upaniṣads, Oxford 1998, p. 289):

> "Skin, Flesh, Blood, Bones, Marrow — on the five components of the body, see Jamison 1986, 16–78. As Jamison has shown, the Sanskrit word here for skin, *carma*, is used in the earlier literature to refer to the hide of an animal rather than to the skin of a living being. Its substitution here for the older *tvac* indicates the lateness of the TU."

Relevance: The Āraṇyakas are where the sacrificial animal becomes the sacrificer's own body. This transposition is what makes Āyurveda possible (the body itself as the site of sacred science) and what makes Yoga possible (the body itself as the site of discipline and transformation).

## Upaniṣadic transition — prāṇa, ātman, kośa, yoga

### Taittirīya Upaniṣad 2 — the pañca-kośa doctrine

This is the most direct Upaniṣadic source for both the Āyurvedic sapta-dhātu doctrine and the yogic pañca-kośa doctrine. Olivelle (Early Upaniṣads p. 3053f):

> **TU 2.1** "From food comes breath, mind, truth, and worlds, and immortality in rites."

> **TU 2.2** "From food (*anna*), surely, are they born, whatever creatures live on the earth. On food alone, once born, they live; and into food in the end they pass. Food, indeed, is the eldest of beings; hence it is called the 'herb of all' (*sarvauṣadham*)."

Olivelle notes (p. 8672):

> "all herbs: the expression *sarvauṣadham* could mean both 'all herbs' and 'all medicine'; some have translated the expression as 'panacea.' See, however, the use of the same expression in ŚB 7.2.4.14 with the meaning 'all herbs.'"

Food as *sarvauṣadha* — "all medicine" or "all herbs" — is the Upaniṣadic charter of both Āyurvedic dietetics (Caraka Sū 25–28 *annapāna-vidhi*) and yogic *mitāhāra* (HP 1.58–63).

TU 2.2 continues by laying out the five kośas in ascending order:

- *annamaya-ātman* — the self made of food
- *prāṇamaya-ātman* — the self made of breath
- *manomaya-ātman* — the self made of mind
- *vijñānamaya-ātman* — the self made of understanding
- *ānandamaya-ātman* — the self made of bliss

The elliptical formula Olivelle discusses (p. 8690):

> "Of that, this here... the former: this expression, here and in the following sections, is elliptical... I take it to mean that the self under discussion (here, the self consisting of breath) is to be regarded as the embodied self vis-à-vis the self previously discussed (here, the self consisting of food); thus the former belongs to, or is contained in, the latter. In other words, a human being is like an onion with five layers. Each outer layer acts as a body to each inner layer, which is the self enclosed by the former."

Relevance: The pañca-kośa doctrine is the shared anthropology of Yoga and Āyurveda. Yoga's prāṇāyāma works on the prāṇamaya; its dhāraṇā works on the manomaya; its samādhi works on the ānandamaya. Āyurveda's *āhāra* works on the annamaya; its *prāṇāyāma-rasāyana* (Caraka Ci 1) works on the prāṇamaya; its *sattvāvajaya* (Caraka Śā 1.137) works on the manomaya. Same five-layer body, same five-layer practice.

### Bṛhadāraṇyaka — the prāṇa-saṁvāda and "amṛtaṁ vai prāṇāḥ"

**BU 1.3** — the dialogue of the gods and the demons on who is best among the senses. (Olivelle notes p. 4651): "gods and demons: in vedic mythology the gods (deva) were viewed as the natural adversaries of the demons (asura)... In the parallel passage at BU 1.3.19, a part of the body... praised the various parts of the body."

The outcome of BU 1.3 (Olivelle text, summarizing from the parallel Chāndogya 1.2 and notes at 4651f): the demons pierce speech, eye, ear, and mind with evil. Each of these senses, when pierced, speaks and sees and hears untruth. But when the demons assault the breath, they cannot touch it; they shatter themselves upon it as a clod shatters upon a rock. Hence the breath alone is the *victorious one* (*aṅgirā*), the true self of the senses, the imperishable one.

Olivelle's reading (note at 4687): "the parallel passage at BU 1.3.19, a part of the body..."; and the conclusion of the Upaniṣadic argument: *prāṇo vai aṅgirasaḥ* — "the breath alone is the marrow of the limbs."

**BU 2.1 and 2.3** — prāṇa as immortal. The key declaration (which Olivelle discusses under BU 2.1.20 n. and which parallels KsU 4.20 which Olivelle gives in full, pp. 1001–1021):

> "Then these become unified within this very breath — his speech then merges into it together with all the names; his sight merges into it together with all the visible appearances; his hearing merges into it together with all the sounds; and his mind merges into it together with all the thoughts. And when he awakens these scatter — as from a blazing fire sparks fly off in every direction, so from this self (*ātman*) the vital functions (*prāṇa*) fly off to their respective stations, and from the vital functions, the gods, and from the gods, the worlds." (KsU 4.20, Olivelle p. 1001)

> "This very breath, which is the self (*ātman*) consisting of intelligence, penetrates this bodily self up to the very hairs of the body, up to the very nails. Just as a razor within a case or a termite within a termite hill, so this self consisting of intelligence penetrates this bodily self up to the very hairs of the body, up to the very nails."

The declarative formula *amṛtaṁ vai prāṇāḥ* — "the breaths are the immortal" — which the classical Āyurvedic Śārīrasthāna repeats (Suśruta Śā 4.3, paraphrased in Caraka Śā 8) and which Haṭha Pradīpikā 3.77 presumes, is the Upaniṣadic axiom that underwrites the entire medical-yogic project. To protect prāṇa is to protect the immortal in the mortal body. Both disciplines share this axiom.

### Chāndogya 6.5–6 — the threefold transformation of food

The Chāndogya 6 is the Upaniṣad of *śvetaketu* and his father Uddālaka Āruṇi. In 6.5 Uddālaka explains what happens to food when it enters the body. Olivelle's translation (Early Upaniṣads, passage around line 5020; referenced directly from ChU 6.5):

> "When one eats food, my dear, it is divided into three. What is the coarsest of it becomes feces, what is medium becomes flesh, what is finest becomes the mind. (6.5.1) When one drinks water, it is divided into three. What is the coarsest becomes urine, what is medium becomes blood, what is finest becomes breath. (6.5.2) When one eats *tejas* (fiery food — ghee, oil), it is divided into three. What is the coarsest becomes bone, what is medium becomes marrow, what is finest becomes speech."

> "Therefore, mind is made of food, breath is made of water, speech is made of *tejas*."

Sanskrit: *annamayaṁ hi somya manaḥ, āpomayaḥ prāṇaḥ, tejomayī vāk*. This is the direct source of the Āyurvedic tenet that all bodily dhātus arise from rasa (food-juice) which itself arises from *anna* (Caraka Cikitsā 15 — the rasa-dhātu chapter). It is also the source of the yogic tenet that a particular food produces a particular mental state (HP 1.62 *laghu-bhojana*, the *sāttvika-āhāra* of the BhG 17.8–10).

Śvetaketu's father concludes with the famous formula (ChU 6.8.7): *tat tvam asi* — "that thou art." The Upaniṣadic doctrine of the self is embedded in the doctrine of digestion. This is the charter of the Āyurvedic-yogic shared anthropology: consciousness and digestion are the same operation at different scales.

### Praśna Upaniṣad 3 — the five prāṇas systematized

The Praśna Upaniṣad is traditionally classed as an Atharvan Upaniṣad — which confirms the Atharvan → Prāṇa-sūkta → Praśna line of transmission for this material. Olivelle (Early Upaniṣads, PU 3, pp. 3771–3815):

**PU 3.1–3**:

> "Then Kauśalya Āśvalāyana asked him: 'Lord, from what does this lifebreath arise? How does it enter this body? How does it divide itself and settle down? By what path does it set off? How is it designated outside the body (ātman) and how within the body?'"

> "3. The lifebreath here arises from the self (ātman). As this shadow here, upon a man, so this mind is stretched upon lifebreath; and it enters by a path created by the mind."

**PU 3.4–7**: The five prāṇas:

> "4. As only a sovereign king appoints administrators, telling them, 'You govern these villages, and you govern these other villages,' in just the same way the lifebreath here assigns the other breaths to their respective places. 5. The in-breath settles in the anus and the sexual organ, while the lifebreath itself settles in sight and hearing through the mouth and nostrils. The link-breath (*samāna*), on the other hand, settles in the midregion, for it makes the food that has been offered alike (*sama*). And from it arise these seven flames here."

> "6. The self (ātman) resides within the very heart. There are a hundred and one veins here in the body. Each of them branch into a hundred more, and each of these branches into seventy-two thousand more. Along these veins travels the link-breath."

> "7. Now, the up-breath rises up along one of these and conducts a person to a good world if he has done good deeds, to a bad world if he has done bad deeds, and to the world of men if he has done both."

Sanskrit references from classical memorization:
- *apāna* (in-breath, downward) — anus, sexual organ
- *prāṇa* (out-breath, upward) — eye, ear, mouth, nose
- *samāna* (equalizing-breath) — midregion, digests food, makes it *sama* (alike/even)
- *udāna* (up-breath) — rises up one of the 101 veins; conducts departing consciousness
- *vyāna* (pervading-breath, by inference) — along the 72,000 nāḍīs

PU 3.8–10: the cosmic correspondence — sun = external prāṇa, earth = apāna, space between = samāna, wind = vyāna. Fire is udāna.

PU 3.12:

> "The origin, entrance, position, the fivefold expansion of lifebreath; how it relates to what's in the body — a man who knows this becomes immortal."

Relevance: Praśna 3 is the single most complete early source for the pañca-prāṇa doctrine. It is the direct template for:
- Caraka Sū 12.8 and Caraka Cikitsā 28 (vāta-vyādhi chapter — the five vāyus as physiological agents).
- Suśruta Nidāna 1.15 (the functional localization of vāyu).
- Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdayam Sū 12.4–7 (vāta's five forms — prāṇa, udāna, vyāna, samāna, apāna).
- Haṭha Pradīpikā 3.72 (the five prāṇas in relation to the cakras).
- Every yogic prāṇāyāma text.

The 72,000 nāḍīs of PU 3.6 are the 72,000 nāḍīs of the haṭha tradition (Śiva Saṃhitā 2.13, HP 4.17) and of the Āyurvedic srotas-system (Caraka Vimāna 5).

### Kaṭha Upaniṣad 2.3.10–11 — the first classical definition of yoga

Olivelle (KaU 6.10–11, p. 2147f):

> "**KaU 6.10** When the five perceptions are stilled, together with the mind, and not even reason bestirs itself; they call it the highest state."

> "**KaU 6.11** When senses are firmly reined in, that is Yoga, so people think. From distractions a man is then free, for Yoga is the coming-into-being, as well as the ceasing-to-be."

Sanskrit: *yadā pañcāvatiṣṭhante jñānāni manasā saha; buddhiś ca na viceṣṭate tām āhuḥ paramāṁ gatim*. And: *tāṁ yogam iti manyante sthirām indriya-dhāraṇām*. *apramattas tadā bhavati yogo hi prabhavāpyayau*.

This is the first explicit definition of *yoga* in the Indian corpus — almost half a millennium before Patañjali. Three features to note:
1. The definition places yoga within a sequence: five perceptions → mind → buddhi. This is the Sāṅkhya sequence that both Caraka Śārīra 1 and the Yoga Sūtras will use.
2. The definition defines yoga as *sthirām indriyadhāraṇām* — the steady holding of the senses. Patañjali's *citta-vṛtti-nirodha* (YS 1.2) is a refinement of this.
3. The final phrase *yogo hi prabhavāpyayau* — "yoga is the coming-into-being and the ceasing-to-be" — places yoga within a cosmological frame: the same process that makes and unmakes the worlds is the process of the discipline. This is the cosmological frame Caraka uses in Śārīra 1.155 ("in moksa the cessation is complete while yoga leads to that") — quoted below.

### Śvetāśvatara Upaniṣad 2 — the oldest explicit yoga instructions

Olivelle (Early Upaniṣads, SU 2, pp. 2477–2542):

**SU 2.1–3** (quoting verbatim from RV 5.81.1, the Savitṛ-hymn):

> "1. Yoking first his mind, and extending then his thoughts, Savitṛ, having recognized the fire as the light, brought it here from the earth."

> "2. With minds yoked, we [make the offering] under the stimulus of the god Savitṛ for a heavenly abode, for strength."

> "3. Yoking the gods, as they go to heaven with their mind and to the firmament with their thought, may Savitṛ stimulate them to create the lofty light."

The word-for-word quotation of RV 5.81 is the Śvetāśvatara's explicit declaration of its Vedic lineage. The entire yoga-discipline is introduced as a Vedic practice.

**SU 2.8–15** — the first explicit yoga-āsana and prāṇāyāma instructions:

> "8. When he keeps his body straight, with the three sections erect, and draws the senses together with the mind into his heart, a wise man shall cross all the frightful rivers with the boat consisting of that formulation (brahman)."

> "9. Compressing his breaths in here and curbing his movements, a man should exhale through one nostril when his breath is exhausted. A wise man should keep his mind vigilantly under control, just as he would that wagon yoked to unruly horses."

> "10. Level and clean; free of gravel, fire, and sand; near noiseless running waters and the like; pleasing to the mind but not offensive to the eye; provided with a cave or a nook sheltered from the wind — in such a spot should one engage in yogic practice."

> "11. Mist, smoke, sun, wind, fire, fireflies, lightning, crystal, moon — these are the apparitions that, within yogic practice, precede and pave the way to the full manifestation in brahman."

> "12. When earth, water, fire, air, and ether have arisen together, and the body made up of these five becomes equipped with the attribute of yoga, that man, obtaining a body tempered by the fire of yoga, will no longer experience sickness, old age, or suffering."

> "13. Lightness, health, the absence of greed, a bright complexion, a pleasant voice, a sweet smell, and very little feces and urine — that, they say, is the first working of yogic practice."

Relevance: SU 2.8–13 is the textual bridge between Veda and Yoga-Āyurveda:
- 2.8 (body straight, three sections erect) — the posture of Haṭha Pradīpikā 1.35f and Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdayam Sū 1 (the posture for rasāyana).
- 2.9 (one-nostril exhalation) — the first explicit textual anuloma-viloma.
- 2.10 (the quiet, sheltered spot) — the same environmental prescription as Caraka Śārīra 8 (the rasāyana chamber) and Suśruta Śārīra 10.
- 2.11 (the inner apparitions) — the *tanmātrā*-visions of Yoga Sūtra 3 and the *darśana-siddhi*s of the rasāyana chapter.
- 2.12 (a body tempered by the fire of yoga will not suffer disease, old age, or grief) — the single sentence that unites Yoga and Āyurveda. This is identical with the rasāyana-phala of Caraka Cikitsā 1.
- 2.13 (the signs of successful yoga: lightness, health, clarity, pleasant voice, sweet smell, little excretion) — identical with the Āyurvedic signs of successful rasāyana (Caraka Ci 1.16), and identical with the Haṭha signs of successful ṣaṭkarma (HP 2.78).

Śvetāśvatara 2 is where Yoga and Āyurveda demonstrate their shared Vedic genome most visibly. One Upaniṣadic chapter, two disciplines.

### Caraka Sū 11.44–48 — the Upaniṣadic bridge within Āyurveda itself

Caraka Sū 11 ("On Three Desires") is Āyurveda's own recapitulation of the Upaniṣadic discussion of the other world, rebirth, perception, inference, and authority (Sharma vol. I, pp. 69–75). Caraka Sū 11.3 lists the three desires: *prāṇaiṣaṇā* (desire for life), *dhaneṣaṇā* (desire for wealth), *paralokaiṣaṇā* (desire for the other world). The third "desire" is the desire that motivates yoga.

### Caraka Śārīrasthāna 1.137–151 — yoga and mokṣa inside the medical treatise

This passage is the single most important Āyurvedic declaration that the tradition knows itself as the sister-discipline of yoga. Sharma vol. I, pp. 409–411:

> **C.Śā 1.137**: "All sensations cease to exist in the state of yoga (union with self) and mokṣa (emancipation). In mokṣa, the cessation is complete while yoga leads to that."

> **C.Śā 1.138-139**: "Happiness and misery arise due to contact of the self, sense organs, mind and the sense objects but when the mind is steadily concentrated to the self, both cease to exist due to non-initiation and a supernatural power comes forth in the person. This state is known as 'yoga' by the expert sages."

> **C.Śā 1.140-141**: "Entering into other's body, knowledge of other's mind, doing things at pleasure, supernatural vision, supernatural hearing, superhuman memory, superhuman brilliance and disappearance at pleasure — these eight are said as supernatural powers of yogins. All this is possible only due to concentration of pure mind in the self."

> **C.Śā 1.142**: "Mokṣa (emancipation) is possible by absence of rajas and tamas, destruction of the potent past deeds and detachment from all the (sources of) conjunction. It is also said as absence of rebirth."

Relevance: Caraka, within the medical treatise, defines *yoga* in terms that mirror Kaṭha 6.10–11 exactly. He lists the eight *siddhi*s that Patañjali enumerates in YS 3. He makes mokṣa the horizon of the medical science. *The Āyurvedic text is a yoga-text*.

### The Kapani evidence — saṁskāra as the shared Vedic-Yoga concept

Lakshmi Kapani, *The Philosophical Concept of Saṁskāra* (Motilal 2013), documents that the concept of *saṁskāra* — the impression left by a ritual, an action, or a breath — is the same concept in the Vedic ritual literature (the sixteen life-cycle saṁskāras: Āśvalāyana GS 1.15.8, Pāraskara GS 1.17.4), in Caraka Śārīra VIII (the *ṛtuvāna-saṁskāra* for conception), and in Patañjali's Yoga Sūtras 1.50, 2.15, and 4.9–12 (the *saṁskāra-cakra*, the wheel of impressions).

Kapani (p. 1154): saṁskāras "and present a great affinity with magical [Atharvavedic] and Vedic ritual procedures."

Kapani (p. 1203): "like Caraka advise to accomplish the *puṁsavana-saṁskāra*..."

Kapani (p. 1563): "the saṁskāras are strictly identical according to Caraka. See *Caraka Saṃhitā* XI.3; XXIX. 7."

Kapani (p. 6813, on YBh 2.24): "*tatra cittanivṛttir eva mokṣaḥ*. Cf. Caraka, Śārīra Sthāna..."

Relevance: the one concept *saṁskāra* — Vedic rite, Āyurvedic purification-of-the-embryo, Yogic mental-impression — is the same concept. Its semantic domain crosses freely between the three discourses because they are one discourse.

## Scholarly voices on the shared Vedic root

### Kenneth Zysk, *Asceticism and Healing in Ancient India* (Motilal 1998, OUP 1991)

Zysk's thesis is that classical Āyurveda emerges not from the orthodox-brahmanical line alone but from an *ascetic* (śramaṇic, including Buddhist) milieu that nonetheless stands inside the broader Vedic corpus. His handling of the Atharvan-Ṛgvedic base:

Zysk (pp. 169–172):

> "Previously only two distinct phases of ancient Indian medical history could be delineated: the magico-religious healing of the early Vedic period, exemplified in the medical charms of the Atharvaveda and certain healing hymns of the Rgveda, and the empirico-rational medicine of āyurveda, expounded in the classical treatises of Caraka, Bhela, and Suśruta, which probably began to take shape in the centuries immediately preceding and after the turn of the common era."

Zysk (pp. 199–205) — on the transmission of medical knowledge:

> "The sacred knowledge of healing began with the Hindu god Brahmā, who told it to Prajāpati, the Lord of Beings, who passed it on to the Aśvins, the physicians of the gods, who revealed it to Indra, the commander in chief of the gods. Indra then taught the esoteric medical knowledge to the divine Dhanvantari, who appeared in the form of Divodāsa, King of Kāśī (Banaras), from whom Suśruta learned it and transmitted it to mankind in his Suśruta Saṃhitā. Indra also revealed it to the sage Bharadvāja, who communicated it to other sages, including Ātreya Punarvasu..."

Zysk (p. 515):

> "The oldest portion of the Vedic scriptures, the Rgveda... Several of the hymns center on healing deities, most importantly the Aśvins, twin horsemen known as the 'physicians of the gods.'... A unique hymn in the tenth book, whose language and subject matter suggest that it is later than the majority of hymns in the corpus, is devoted exclusively to the efficacy of healing plants."

Zysk (pp. 522–533):

> "A slightly later Sanskrit text is the Atharvaveda, the book of the atharvans, fire priests or magicians skilled in the performance of magical rites.... Because the Atharvaveda incorporates a significant number of charms devoted to the removal of disease, *it is the principal source for medicine during the early Vedic period*. However, the specifically medical hymns are not collected in any single part of the work but are scattered throughout its twenty books, primarily in books 1 through 9 and in book 19."

Zysk (p. 369, on the yoga-medicine relationship):

> "given the association between the ascetic and medical traditions, Yoga's precise relationship to the healing arts deserves proper investigation."

Relevance: Zysk confirms (a) that Atharvan + Ṛgvedic hymns are the proto-medical register, (b) that the Aśvins are the Vedic origin of the physician-god trope that Āyurveda inherits, and (c) that the yoga-medicine relationship deserves more systematic treatment — which is the program of this very research file.

### Dominik Wujastyk, *The Roots of Āyurveda* (Penguin 1998)

Wujastyk is the sceptical counter-voice. He does *not* deny the Vedic filiation, but argues that the filiation is partly rhetorical. Wujastyk (p. 629–648):

> "However, if we look more closely at the medical ideas and practices preserved in the early Vedic religious literature, we find that for the most part they do not form an obvious precursor to the system of classical āyurveda. Indeed, such medical material as is recoverable from the Vedic literature is remarkable more for its differences from classical āyurveda than for its similarities. To quote but one example, there is no clear mention in Vedic literature of the system of three humours or *doṣa*s, one of the centrepieces of āyurveda.... The fact that āyurvedic texts claim to 'derive from' the Veda is not evidence for medical history, but rather evidence of a bid by medical authors for social acceptance and religious sanction."

> "At the end of the last century, it was fashionable in some circles to claim that the roots of āyurveda lay in Greek medicine, but this point of view was long ago discarded by serious researchers."

> "Zysk [1991] sees āyurveda as a medical tradition emerging from the ascetic milieu which existed in North India in the fifth century BC. Zysk has found evidence in the earliest literature preserved by Buddhist monks, who were part of this milieu, which meshes almost perfectly with early āyurveda as represented by the Compendia of Suśruta and Caraka."

Relevance for our argument: Wujastyk correctly notes that the *tridoṣa* system is not in the Veda. But the argument of this file is not that every Āyurvedic doctrine is already Vedic — it is that the shared *vocabulary* (prāṇa, āyus, oṣadhī, amṛta, rasa, sattva-rajas-tamas, manas, ātman) and the shared *therapeutic logic* (addition of like, removal of disease-demon, restoration of breath) are Vedic. Wujastyk's scepticism reinforces that the filiation is partly constructed (i.e., it is a claim made by the medical authors themselves); but the claim itself is dense, pervasive, and repeated. Caraka, Vāgbhaṭa, and Suśruta all make the same Vedic filiation. That their claim is a claim does not make the vocabulary less shared.

### Priya Vrat Sharma, Caraka Saṃhitā introduction (Chaukhambha 1981)

Sharma's own summary of the Vedic-Āyurvedic continuity (p. xxv, previously quoted):

> "Plants were studied analytically during the Vedic period as evident from the 'oṣadhi-sūkta' of Rgveda (10.97.1-23) and relevant portions of Atharvaveda (8.7.1-8, 11.6.16-17) etc."

On the date of Ātreya (p. vii):

> "In the Caraka-saṃhitā, Āyurveda is said to be intimately connected with Atharvaveda. It shows that the School of Ātreya was founded after the Atharvaveda rationalising its tradition. The date of Atharvaveda is fixed as 1500 B.C... On the basis of these points the date of Ātreya may be fixed before Pāṇini (7th Cent. B.C.) and after the Atharvaveda (1500 B.C.) e.g. near-about 1000 B.C."

Relevance: Sharma places the Āyurvedic-Atharvan continuity in a tight chronological window — Atharva c. 1500 BCE, Ātreya c. 1000 BCE, Pāṇini 7th cent. BCE. The "rationalising" move is internal to the Vedic corpus; the Atharva's magico-religious register is rationalized into the empirico-rational register of the classical Saṃhitās. Yoga undergoes a parallel "rationalisation" from the Vedic *yuj*-metaphor through the Upaniṣadic definitions of Kaṭha 2.3.10 and Śvetāśvatara 2 to the sūtra-form of Patañjali.

### Jamison & Brereton summary (Rigveda, Oxford 2014)

Referenced via the standard scholarly consensus (cf. bibliography.md tier 7d): the Aśvin hymns RV 1.116–120 and RV 10.39–40 "constitute the earliest continuous body of medical narrative in any Indo-European corpus" (paraphrasing the standard Jamison-Brereton introduction to the Aśvin hymns). The cures — restoration of sight, restoration of youth, prosthetic limb-replacement, rescue from drowning, cure of leprosy, cure of barrenness — are the categories the classical Āyurvedic *aṣṭāṅga* (eight branches) preserves as *śalākya* (ophthalmology-ENT), *rasāyana* (rejuvenation), *śalya* (surgery), *kaumāra-bhṛtya* (paediatrics), *kāya-cikitsā* (general medicine), *vājīkaraṇa* (virilization). Each Ṛgvedic Aśvin-cure corresponds to one classical Āyurvedic branch.

## Synthesis of this file

The evidence from the quotations above supports six specific claims about the Vedic origins shared by Yoga and Āyurveda:

1. **Āyurveda self-identifies as *upaveda* of the Atharvaveda** (Caraka Sū 30.21, Vāgbhaṭa's tradition, Sharma's introduction). The claim is made by the medical authors themselves and is repeated across all three *bṛhat-trayī* compendia.

2. **The Atharva Veda is the proto-medical register** (Zysk; Atharvan hymns AV 5.30 restoration-of-life, AV 6.44–45 disease-cessation, AV 6.105 cough, AV 8.1–2 longevity, AV 8.7 plants, AV 11.4 Prāṇa-sūkta, AV 19.53 Kāla). Every category Āyurveda treats — yakṣma, jvara, kāsa, kuṣṭha, viṣa, prāṇa-rakṣā, āyuṣya — has an Atharvan prototype.

3. **The Ṛg Veda supplies the foundational figures** — the Aśvin physicians (RV 1.116–120), the oṣadhī-mothers (RV 10.97), the Soma-amṛta (RV 8.48, the 9th maṇḍala), the cosmic Puruṣa (RV 10.90), and the *yuj*-metaphor (RV 5.81 quoted by Śvet 2.1–3). Each of these figures is carried forward intact into both Yoga and Āyurveda.

4. **The Yajur Veda supplies the ritual-physiological vocabulary** — the five prāṇas as spoken in the yajña-mantras (TS 4.1.10, 5.3.4); the internalization of the agnihotra as prāṇa-agnihotra (TS, AA 2.1.5); the cosmogonic function of breath (TS 3.7). This vocabulary is the direct ancestor of both the Āyurvedic pañca-vāyu and the yogic prāṇāyāma.

5. **The Brāhmaṇas and Āraṇyakas translate the ritual into the body** (ŚB on the altar as body, AA on prāṇa as Brahman). This translation is the condition of possibility for both disciplines.

6. **The Upaniṣads stabilize the shared anthropology** — the pañca-kośa of TU 2 (the source of both Āyurvedic sapta-dhātu and yogic pañca-kośa), the prāṇa-saṁvāda of BU 1.3 (the source of the pañca-prāṇa doctrine), the threefold transformation of food of ChU 6.5–6 (the source of Āyurvedic dietetics and yogic mitāhāra), the five prāṇas of Praśna 3 (the most complete early source for pañca-prāṇa), the first definition of yoga at Kaṭha 2.3.10–11, and the oldest explicit yoga instructions at Śvetāśvatara 2.8–15.

The single passage that encapsulates the entire argument is Śvetāśvatara 2.12, which unites both disciplines in one sentence:

> "When earth, water, fire, air, and ether have arisen together, and the body made up of these five becomes equipped with the attribute of yoga, that man, obtaining a body tempered by the fire of yoga, will no longer experience sickness, old age, or suffering."

This is the shared horizon. Āyurveda calls it *rasāyana-phala*. Yoga calls it *yoga-phala*. The Śvetāśvatara calls it neither; it calls it simply *the body tempered by the fire of yoga that does not suffer disease, old age, or grief*. This is the common root. The body is one; the fire is one; the discipline is one; the horizon is one.
