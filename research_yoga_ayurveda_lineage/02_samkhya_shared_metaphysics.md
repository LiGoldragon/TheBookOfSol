# Sāṃkhya — The Shared Metaphysical Grammar

Yoga and Āyurveda speak the same language. When Patañjali writes of
*puruṣa* and *prakṛti*, the three *guṇas*, *buddhi*, *ahaṃkāra*,
*manas*, the five *tanmātras* and five *mahābhūtas*, he is using the
vocabulary of Sāṃkhya. When Caraka writes of the same terms, he is
using the same vocabulary. When Suśruta describes the body as the
conjunction of *avyakta, mahān, ahaṃkāra* and the rest, the same
vocabulary recurs. When Vāgbhaṭa grounds his pharmacology on the
*avyakta/vyakta* division of reality, the same vocabulary recurs.
When Svātmārāma teaches that *manas* and *prāṇa* are the two causes
of *citta*, he speaks the same grammar.

The grammar is Sāṃkhya — the 25 tattvas, the three guṇas, the
pañca-mahābhūta, the *antaḥ-karaṇa* triad, the *puruṣa–prakṛti*
duality. This file collects the Sanskrit and English witnesses.

## The 25 tattvas in the Sāṃkhya Kārikā

Īśvarakṛṣṇa's Sāṃkhya Kārikā (c. 1st century CE) divides reality into
four functional categories: productive only, productive and produced,
produced only, and neither.

### SK 3 — the fourfold classification

> *mūla-prakṛtir avikṛtir mahad-ādyāḥ prakṛti-vikṛtayaḥ sapta |*
> *ṣoḍaśakas tu vikāro na prakṛtir na vikṛtiḥ puruṣaḥ ||*

> "The Primal Nature is non-evolute. The group of seven beginning with
> the Great Principle (Buddhi) and the rest are both evolvents and
> evolutes. But the sixteen (five organs of sense, five of action, the
> mind and the five gross elements) are only evolutes. The Spirit is
> neither the evolvent nor the evolute."
>
> — Sāṃkhya Kārikā 3, tr. Swami Virūpākṣananda (Ramakrishna Math,
>   Mylapore, 1995), p. 11

The seven prakṛti-vikṛtis are *mahat/buddhi, ahaṃkāra*, and the five
*tanmātras*. The sixteen vikṛtis are the eleven indriyas (five
*jñānendriyas*, five *karmendriyas*, *manas*) and the five
*mahābhūtas*. The non-productive non-produced is *puruṣa*.

### SK 11 — the signs of the manifest and the Primordial

> *triguṇam aviveki viṣayaḥ sāmānyam acetanaṃ prasavadharmi |*
> *vyaktaṃ tathā pradhānaṃ tad-viparītas tathā ca pumān ||*

> "The Manifest is constituted of the three attributes (of Sattwa,
> Rajas and Tamas), is non-distinguishable, objective, common,
> non-intelligent, and prolific. So also is the Primordial Nature. The
> Spirit is the reverse of both of them and yet is similar in some
> respects."
>
> — SK 11, tr. Virūpākṣananda, p. 41

Vācaspati's gloss: the three guṇas are "pleasure, pain and delusion"
— the essence of sattva, rajas and tamas respectively (p. 41). Every
*vyakta* product is *triguṇa*. Only *puruṣa* is the reverse.

### SK 17 — the existence of puruṣa

> *saṃghāta-parārthatvāt triguṇādi-viparyayād adhiṣṭhānāt |*
> *puruṣo'sti bhoktṛ-bhāvāt kaivalyārthaṃ pravṛtteś ca ||*

> "The Spirit exists because (a) the aggregate is for another's sake;
> (b) of the absence of three guṇas and other properties; (c) there
> must be some controller; (d) there must be some experiencer; and
> (e) of the tendency of activities towards final beatitude."
>
> — SK 17, tr. Virūpākṣananda, p. 56

### SK 21 — the conjunction of puruṣa and prakṛti

> *puruṣasya darśanārthaṃ kaivalyārthaṃ tathā pradhānasya |*
> *paṅgv-andhavad ubhayor api saṃyogas tat-kṛtaḥ sargaḥ ||*

> "For the exhibition of nature to the Spirit and for the emancipation
> of the Spirit, (there is conjunction between the Spirit and Nature)
> like the union between the lame and the blind; from this
> conjunction proceeds creation."
>
> — SK 21, tr. Virūpākṣananda, p. 64

The two-fold purpose of the conjunction — *bhoga* and *apavarga* —
reappears verbatim at YS 2.18 (*bhogāpavargārthaṃ dṛśyam*).

### SK 22 — the order of evolution

> *prakṛter mahāṃs tato'haṃkāras tasmād gaṇaś ca ṣoḍaśakaḥ |*
> *tasmād api ṣoḍaśakāt pañcabhyaḥ pañca bhūtāni ||*

> "From the Primordial Matter evolves the Great Principle; from this
> evolves the I-Principle; from this evolves the set of sixteen; from
> the five of this set of sixteen, evolves the five elements."
>
> — SK 22, tr. Virūpākṣananda, p. 65

Vācaspati: the sixteen are eleven indriyas plus five tanmātras (p. 65).
The downward stair — *prakṛti → mahat → ahaṃkāra → {eleven indriyas +
five tanmātras} → five mahābhūtas* — is restated by Caraka Śārīra 1,
Gītā 13.5, and Vyāsa's bhāṣya on Patañjali.

### SK 38–39 — the distinction between tanmātra and bhūta

> *tanmātrāṇy aviśeṣās tebhyo bhūtāni pañca pañcabhyaḥ |*
> *ete smṛtā viśeṣāḥ śāntā ghorāś ca mūḍhāś ca ||*

> "The tanmātras are the indiscernible; from these five proceed the
> five gross elements; they are remembered as the discernibles,
> because, they are calm, turbulent and deluding."
>
> — SK 38, tr. Virūpākṣananda, p. 87

> "The subtle bodies, bodies born of parents, together with gross
> elements are the three kinds of the specific. Of these, the subtle
> bodies are everlasting and those born of the parents are
> perishable."
>
> — SK 39, tr. Virūpākṣananda, p. 88

The *tanmātra/bhūta* distinction is the axis of both yogic and
Āyurvedic subtle anatomy. The *sūkṣma-śarīra* is composed of
tanmātras; the *sthūla-śarīra* of gross bhūtas.

## The 25 tattvas in Caraka Śārīrasthāna

Caraka's Śārīrasthāna 1 ("Kāṭidhāpuruṣīya Śārīra") is the pivotal
Āyurvedic statement of Sāṃkhya. Dasgupta demonstrated that it
preserves a form of Sāṃkhya older than Īśvarakṛṣṇa's.

### Caraka Śārīra 1.16 — the six-fold and the twenty-four-fold puruṣa

> "The aggregate of five mahābhūtas and consciousness as the sixth one
> is known as 'Puruṣa' (person). Consciousness alone is also known as
> 'Puruṣa'."
>
> — Caraka Śārīra 1.16, tr. Priya Vrat Sharma (Chaukhambha), vol. I,
>   p. 398

Sanskrit: *khāditmakaḥ puruṣaś cetanā-dhātuḥ ṣaṣṭhaḥ smṛtaḥ |*
*cetanāvantam evāhuḥ puruṣaṃ kecid anyathā ||* (Sharma ed., same
page).

The double definition — *puruṣa* as *ṣaḍ-dhātuja* aggregate (five
elements plus cetanā) or as cetanā alone — keeps both the medical
reading (fit for diagnosis) and the Sāṃkhya reading (fit for
liberation).

### Caraka Śārīra 1.17 — the twenty-four entities

> "Again from division of constituents he is known as possessing
> twenty four entities such as mind, ten sense organs, five sense
> objects and prakṛti (matter) consisting of eight entities (avyakta,
> mahat, ahaṃkāra and five tanmātras)."
>
> — Caraka Śārīra 1.17, tr. Sharma, vol. I, p. 398

Caraka's eight-fold *prakṛti* (avyakta + mahat + ahaṃkāra + five
tanmātras) matches Sāṃkhya's evolvents. Caraka counts 24 because he
folds *puruṣa* and *avyakta* together; Īśvarakṛṣṇa counts 25 because
he keeps them distinct.

### Caraka Śārīra 1.18–19 — the definition of manas

> "Mind is defined as the entity which, even on contact with self,
> sense organs and sense objects, is responsible for production or
> otherwise of knowledge by its attending or non-attending
> respectively. Subtleness and oneness are known as two qualities of
> mind."
>
> — Caraka Śārīra 1.18–19, tr. Sharma, vol. I, p. 398

Manas as gatekeeper of sensation, operative only by attending, is
identical in function to Patañjali's *citta* (YS 1.2). Two angles on
the same organ: Patañjali from its silencing, Caraka from its normal
operation.

### Caraka Śārīra 1.20–21 — the object and action of manas

> "The object of thinking, analysing, reasoning, meditating,
> determination and whatever, is to be perceived by mind is its
> object. Action of mind consists of control over senses, self-
> restraint, reasoning, analysing. Beyond that is the jurisdiction of
> buddhi (intellect)."
>
> — Caraka Śārīra 1.20–21, tr. Sharma, vol. I, p. 398

*Manas* analyses and forwards; *buddhi* determines — the function
Patañjali and Vyāsa ascribe to *citta* in its aspects.

### Caraka Śārīra 1.22–23 — the chain of cognition

> "The sense-object is received by sense organs alongwith mind. Then
> the mind analyses it in forms of merits or demerits and forwards it
> to Buddhi (intellect) which produces decisive knowledge by which one
> proceeds to speak or to do something with full knowledge."
>
> — Caraka Śārīra 1.22–23, tr. Sharma, vol. I, p. 398

The pipeline *viṣaya → indriya → manas → buddhi → pravṛtti* is the
Sāṃkhya architecture of perception; Vyāsa spells out the identical
sequence in his bhāṣya on YS 2.18.

### Caraka Śārīra 1.24 — the bhautika senses

> "The five sense organs are composed of five mahābhūtas (Ākāśa etc.)
> with predominance of one in each. They are known by inference
> through their actions and it is they on the basis of which intellect
> proceeds."
>
> — Caraka Śārīra 1.24, tr. Sharma, vol. I, p. 399

Caraka's *bhautika* doctrine — each indriya made from the predominance
of one mahābhūta — matches the pre-classical Sāṃkhya Dasgupta
identifies as older than Īśvarakṛṣṇa's.

### Caraka Śārīra 1.27–30 — the five bhūtas and their guṇas

> "The (five) mahābhūtas are ākāśa, vāyu, agni, ap and prthivī. Sound,
> touch, vision, taste and smell are their properties respectively.
>
> "Out of them, the first one has only one property and there is
> addition of one more property in each of the latter bhūtas. Thus the
> former bhūta alongwith its property is associated with the latter
> one.
>
> "Coarseness, liquidity, mobility, hotness and non-interruption are
> the specific characters of prthivī, ap, vāyu, tejas and ākāśa
> respectively."
>
> — Caraka Śārīra 1.27–30, tr. Sharma, vol. I, p. 399

The accretion-rule — each successive bhūta inherits the previous
bhūta's tanmātra and adds its own — is identical to Vyāsa's bhāṣya
at YS 2.19.

### Caraka Śārīra 1.35 — the aggregate of twenty-four

> "The para (avyakta — the primordial unmanifest) holds the
> conjunction of intellect, sense organs, mind and sense objects. The
> aggregate of twenty four entities is known as 'Puruṣa' (the
> person)."
>
> — Caraka Śārīra 1.35, tr. Sharma, vol. I, p. 399

"Puruṣa" here is the composite twenty-four-fold aggregate — the
person for medical purposes. The *real* puruṣa, cetanā alone, is the
twenty-fifth.

### Caraka Śārīra 1.36 — the role of the guṇas in bondage

> "The person associated with (predominant) rajas and tamas has
> endless conjunction of this type but when these two are subdued
> with the predominance of sattwa, the conjunction ceases to exist."
>
> — Caraka Śārīra 1.36, tr. Sharma, vol. I, p. 399

The whole Sāṃkhya-Yoga soteriology in one line: rajas and tamas
maintain bondage; predominant sattva dissolves it. Identical to SK
63–68 and YS 2.18–26.

### Caraka Śārīra 1.63–65 — the fullest enumeration

> "Ākāśa etc. (ākāśa, vāyu, tejas, ap and prthivī), buddhi (mahat-
> intellect), avyakta (the unmanifest primordial nature) and ahaṃkāra
> (ego), the eighth one, constitute bhūtaprakṛti (original source of
> creatures). Vikāras (products) are sixteen — five sense organs, five
> motor organs, mind and five objects (bhūtas).
>
> "This (aggregate) except the unmanifest one is known as kṣetra
> (body). The unmanifest one is known as the knower of this body by
> the sages."
>
> — Caraka Śārīra 1.63–65, tr. Sharma, vol. I, pp. 402–403

Eight bhūta-prakṛti + sixteen vikāra = 24; *avyakta* = *kṣetrajña* =
knower of the field. Identical to Gītā 13 terminology. Caraka's
*kṣetra/kṣetrajña* = Sāṃkhya's *vyakta/avyakta* = Gītā's
*prakṛti/puruṣa*.

### Caraka Śārīra 1.66 — the evolutionary order

> "From the unmanifest (primordial nature) intellect evolves, then ego
> comes forth, from ego five bhūtas emanate in order. Thus evolved and
> complete with all the organs the person is born and is said as
> emerged."
>
> — Caraka Śārīra 1.66, tr. Sharma, vol. I, p. 403

SK 22 in a physician's words: *avyakta → buddhi → ahaṃkāra →
pañca-bhūta*.

### Caraka Śārīra 1.67–69 — pralaya and the guṇas

> "At the time of dissolution, the self gets detached from these
> enjoyable entities. Thus (the self), subdued by rajas and tamas,
> gets manifested from the unmanifest state and then reverts to the
> unmanifest and thus moves continuously like a wheel (from one state
> to another). Those who are attached excessively to duals and are
> engrossed by ego are subjected to origin and destruction but not
> others who are contrary to them."
>
> — Caraka Śārīra 1.67–69, tr. Sharma, vol. I, p. 403

The cyclic *pralaya/sarga* and wheel-image are classical
Sāṃkhya-Yoga saṃsāra.

## The 25 tattvas in Suśruta Śārīrasthāna

Suśruta's cosmology is explicitly Sāṃkhya. Bhishagratna's introduction
to Vol I summarizes:

> "Suśruta's Theory of Cosmogony is based on the old Sāṅkhya Duality
> of Prakṛti (Objective) and Puruṣa (Subjective). The two are coeval
> and co-extensive realities. Out of the Avyakta (unmanifest) or
> Prakṛti has evolved the Mahān, the (animated cosmic matter). Out of
> this cosmic matter has evolved Ahaṃkāra (the sense of individuality
> or more correctly egoism) which is divided into three kinds such as
> the Vaikārika (phenomenal, thought-form), Taijasa (kinetic), and
> Bhūtādi (pertaining to the first form of matter). This Vaikārika
> Ahaṃkāra in combination with the Taijasa Ahaṃkāra has fathered the
> eleven sense organs, which, in combination with the Bhūtādi, have
> produced the five Tanmātras or proper sensibles of touch, sight,
> hearing, etc."
>
> — Bhishagratna, *The Sushruta Samhita*, Vol I, Introduction,
>   pp. xxvi–xxvii

Continuing:

> "In other words, these Tanmātras may be defined as the atomic
> essences of the material principles of sound, light, ether, etc. In
> addition to these, Suśruta, like Kapila, admits the existence of a
> kind of atom-like units of consciousness, which he calls Puruṣa. The
> combination of the sixteen aforesaid categories and the Puruṣa is
> for the expansion and liberation of the latter. A human being
> (individual), who is the fit subject for medical treatment, is the
> product of the combination of Puruṣa with the five primordial
> material principles (Mahābhūtas)."
>
> — Bhishagratna, Vol I Intro, p. xxvii

The threefold division of *ahaṃkāra* — *vaikārika/taijasa/bhūtādi* —
places Suśruta's Sāṃkhya in the *sāttvika/rājasa/tāmasa* lineage of
the classical system (SK 25): sattvic ahaṃkāra generates the
sense-organs; tamasic ahaṃkāra generates the tanmātras; rajasic
ahaṃkāra supplies the dynamic connection.

Uttaratantra 65 states the Sāṃkhya position directly:

> "Vyākhyāna is the description or explanation of the details. For
> example — Puruṣa as the twenty-fifth factor has been dealt with in
> this book. While only the twenty-four factors constituting this
> body have been dealt with in other works."
>
> — Suśruta, Uttaratantra 65.29, tr. Bhishagratna, *Complete English
>   Translation*, p. 411

Suśruta stands explicitly with 25 tattvas, against the older 24-tattva
works (Caraka-Pañcaśikha).

Uttaratantra 66 confirms the composition:

> "Puruṣa or human body has sixteen sub-strata. The Prāṇas (viz. the
> organs of sense) are eleven in number… The three qualities (viz.,
> Sattva, Rajas and Tamas) have also been explained in connection
> with the (three) different Doṣas."
>
> — Suśruta, Uttaratantra 66.5–6, tr. Bhishagratna, p. 414

Bhishagratna: "The sixteen sub-strata are the five elementary
principles… and the eleven sense-organs" (p. 414). The
*ṣoḍaśaka-gaṇa* of SK 3 = the *ṣoḍaśa-prakṛti* of Suśruta.

## The 25 tattvas in Patañjali's Yoga Sūtras

The Yoga Sūtras presuppose Sāṃkhya's metaphysics. The second pāda is
the most explicit.

### YS 2.6 — asmitā defined as the confusion of seer and seen

> *dṛg-darśana-śaktyor ekātmatevāsmitā ||*

> "Ego is [to consider] the nature of the seer and the nature of the
> instrumental power of seeing to be the same thing."
>
> — YS 2.6, tr. Edwin Bryant, *The Yoga Sūtras of Patañjali* (Farrar
>   Straus Giroux / North Point 2009), ch. II

Bryant: "dṛk, the seer, is a reference to the awareness of puruṣa…
The instrumental power of sight, darśana… refers to the intelligence
aspect of the citta, that is, to buddhi as the instrument of
awareness. Buddhi is the first prākṛtic layer enveloping puruṣa and
presents images of the sense objects in the world, and indeed all
vṛttis, to the puruṣa" (YS 2.6 comm.).

The *dṛk* (puruṣa) / *darśana-śakti* (buddhi) distinction is pure
Sāṃkhya.

### YS 2.18 — the nature of the seen

> *prakāśa-kriyā-sthiti-śīlaṃ bhūtendriyātmakaṃ bhogāpavargārthaṃ
> dṛśyam ||*

> "That which is knowable has the nature of illumination, activity,
> and inertia [sattva, rajas, and tamas]. It consists of the senses
> and the elements, and exists for the purpose of [providing] either
> liberation or experience [to puruṣa]."
>
> — YS 2.18, tr. Bryant

Bryant: "The commentators correlate the illumination, prakāśa… with
sattva… activity, kriyā, with rajas… and inertia, sthiti, with tamas"
(YS 2.18 comm.). The four-word signature *bhogāpavargārthaṃ dṛśyam*
is verbatim SK 21's definition of why *prakṛti* conjoins with *puruṣa*.

### YS 2.19 — the stages of the guṇas

> *viśeṣāviśeṣa-liṅga-mātrāliṅgāni guṇa-parvāṇi ||*

> "The different stages of the guṇa qualities consist of the
> particularized, the unparticularized, the distinctive, and the
> indistinctive."
>
> — YS 2.19, tr. Bryant

Bryant: "the particularized, viśeṣa, refers to all final evolutes of
prakṛti… the unparticularized, aviśeṣa, refers to the evolutes that
do produce further products… the gross elements (ether, air, fire,
water, earth) are the particularized aspects of the unparticularized
subtle elements (sound, touch, sight, taste, smell)" (YS 2.19 comm.).

The four *guṇa-parvas* map exactly to Sāṃkhya:
1. *viśeṣa* = sixteen final evolutes (five bhūtas, ten indriyas, manas)
2. *aviśeṣa* = five tanmātras + ahaṃkāra
3. *liṅga-mātra* = buddhi/mahat
4. *aliṅga* = mūla-prakṛti

SK 3's twenty-three-evolute-plus-prakṛti hierarchy, restated.

### YS 2.23 — the conjunction and its function

> *sva-svāmi-śaktyoḥ svarūpopalabdhi-hetuḥ saṃyogaḥ ||*

> "[The notion of] conjunction is the means of understanding the real
> nature of the powers of the possessed and of the possessor."
>
> — YS 2.23, tr. Bryant

### YS 2.24 — ignorance as the cause of conjunction

> *tasya hetur avidyā ||*

> "The cause of conjunction is ignorance."
>
> — YS 2.24, tr. Bryant

### YS 2.25 — removal of conjunction is liberation

> *tad-abhāvāt saṃyogābhāvo hānam tad-dṛśeḥ kaivalyam ||*

> "By the removal of ignorance, conjunction is removed. This is the
> absolute freedom of the seer."
>
> — YS 2.25, tr. Bryant

*Kaivalya* is Īśvarakṛṣṇa's word (SK 68), taken by Patañjali without
comment.

### YS 2.26 — discriminative discernment

> *viveka-khyātir aviplavā hānopāyaḥ ||*

> "The means to liberation is uninterrupted discriminative
> discernment."
>
> — YS 2.26, tr. Bryant

YS 2.18–26 is structurally identical to Caraka Śārīra 1.36: sattva
predominates, rajas/tamas subside, conjunction ceases. Two texts,
one mechanism.

### YS 3.35 — saṃyama on the distinction between buddhi and puruṣa

> *sattva-puruṣayor atyantāsaṃkīrṇayoḥ pratyayāviśeṣo bhogaḥ
> parārthatvāt svārtha-saṃyamāt puruṣa-jñānam ||*

> "Worldly experience consists of the notion that there is no
> distinction between the puruṣa self and pure intelligence, although
> these two are completely distinct. Worldly experience exists for
> another [i.e., for puruṣa]. [By saṃyama] on that which exists for
> itself [i.e., on puruṣa], comes knowledge of puruṣa."
>
> — YS 3.35, tr. Bryant

*Sattva* here = *buddhi*. *Parārthatvāt* = SK 17's
*saṃghāta-parārthatvāt*. Identical logic.

### YS 4.13 — guṇas as substance of past, present, and future

> *te vyakta-sūkṣmā guṇātmānaḥ ||*

> "The past, present, and future have the guṇas as their essence and
> are either manifest or latent."
>
> — YS 4.13, tr. Bryant

Bryant: "Everything is essentially simply a combination of the three
guṇas… all three are just the permutations of the guṇas" (YS 4.13
comm.). The *vyakta/sūkṣma* couple is verbatim Sāṃkhya's
*vyakta/avyakta* (SK 10–11).

### YS 1.16 — higher dispassion

> *tat paraṃ puruṣa-khyāter guṇa-vaitṛṣṇyam ||*

> "Higher than renunciation is indifference to the guṇas [themselves].
> This stems from perception of the puruṣa, soul."
>
> — YS 1.16, tr. Bryant

The target of highest dispassion is the guṇas themselves — the
terminal move of Sāṃkhya's path.

## The three guṇas across the three corpora

### Guṇas in the Sāṃkhya Kārikā

SK 11 establishes the guṇas as the essence of every *vyakta* product.
SK 12 defines them:

> "The attributes possess the natures of pleasure, pain and
> bewilderment respectively; they are meant for illumination, activity
> and restraint… they mutually dominate, depend on, produce and keep
> company with each other."
>
> — SK 12, tr. Virūpākṣananda, p. 43

The guṇas are three aspects — *prakāśa, pravṛtti, niyama* — whose
shifting predominance yields the manifest world.

### Guṇas in Patañjali

Patañjali names them at YS 2.18 (*prakāśa-kriyā-sthiti*), at 2.19
(*guṇa-parvāṇi*), at 1.16 (*guṇa-vaitṛṣṇya*), at 4.13 (*guṇātmānaḥ*),
and at 4.34 (*puruṣārtha-śūnyānām guṇānām prati-prasavaḥ kaivalyam* —
the final liberation defined as the reabsorption of the guṇas).

### Guṇas in Caraka — as mental doṣa

Caraka treats the three guṇas as the three *mānasa-doṣas* — the
bridge between Sāṃkhya metaphysics and Āyurvedic clinical practice.

#### Caraka Vimāna 8.119 — the three-fold sattva

> "One should also examine in respect of sattva (psyche). Sattva is
> known as mind. It controls the body by conjunction with the self.
> Psyche is of three types according to strength — superior, medium
> and inferior. Accordingly the persons are also (of three types)
> having superior, medium and inferior psyche."
>
> — Caraka Vimāna 8.119, tr. Sharma, vol. I, p. 382

The three grades *pravara/madhya/avara* are the guṇa-constitutions
of the patient's mind. Sharma's translation continues:

> "Amongst them, those having superior psyche are, in fact,
> sattwasāra… seen unmoved even in severe afflictions… due to
> predominance of sattwa quality. Those having medium psyche sustain
> themselves at the instance of others… But those possessing inferior
> mind can sustain neither by themselves nor by others… associated
> with fear, grief, greed, confusion and conceit."
>
> — Caraka Vimāna 8.119 (continued), tr. Sharma, vol. I, p. 382

Sāṃkhya's sattva/rajas/tamas translated into a clinical assessment
protocol. The physician reads the guṇa-composition the way he reads
the pulse.

#### Caraka Śārīra 3.13 — the three-fold classification

> "The psyche is of three types — pure, rājasa and tāmasa. The quality
> which is dominant in mind associates in the subsequent birth as
> well. In case the psyche is pure, one recollects the (incidents of)
> the previous birth also."
>
> — Caraka Śārīra 3.13, tr. Sharma, vol. I, p. 424

Ayurvedic transmigration is Sāṃkhya transmigration: what travels is
a guṇa-complex.

### Guṇas in Suśruta

Suśruta Uttaratantra 66.6: "The three qualities (viz. Sattva, Rajas
and Tamas) have also been explained in connection with the (three)
different Doṣas." The guṇas are correlated with *vāta, pitta, kapha*
inside one explanatory grammar.

### Guṇas in the Bhagavad Gītā

> *sattvaṃ rajas tama iti guṇāḥ prakṛti-sambhavāḥ |*
> *nibadhnanti mahā-bāho dehe dehinam avyayam ||*

> "The guṇas called sattva, rajas, and tamas are born from Prakṛti,
> and they fetter the eternal embodied souls to their bodies."
>
> — BG 14.5, tr. J. A. B. van Buitenen, *The Bhagavadgītā in the
>   Mahābhārata* (University of Chicago Press 1981), p. 127

> "Among these guṇas, sattva, which because of its spotlessness is
> illumining and salubrious, binds the soul by means of an attachment
> to joy and an attachment to knowledge… Know that rajas is
> characterized by passion and arises from an attachment to craving;
> it binds the embodied soul by an attachment to action… Know, on the
> other hand, that tamas arises from ignorance and deludes the
> embodied souls; it binds through absentmindedness, sloth, and
> sleep."
>
> — BG 14.6–8, tr. van Buitenen, p. 127

> "Sattva predominates by suppressing rajas and tamas, Bharata; rajas
> by suppressing sattva and tamas, tamas by suppressing sattva and
> rajas."
>
> — BG 14.10, tr. van Buitenen, p. 127

The mutual-suppression doctrine of SK 12. BG 14 is perhaps the
earliest extended literary witness to the classical triguṇa scheme
codified at SK 11–13, enumerated at Caraka Vimāna 8.119 and Śārīra
3.13, presupposed throughout Patañjali.

### Guṇas in the Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā

Svātmārāma at HYP 4.21 and the Muktibodhānanda commentary:

> "Through restraining the prāṇa, thought/counterthought is
> restrained and through restraint of thought/counterthought, prāṇa
> (air) is restrained."
>
> — HYP 4.21, tr. Swami Muktibodhānanda (Bihar School of Yoga, 1998),
>   p. 499

Muktibodhānanda's commentary makes the Sāṃkhya-grammar explicit:

> "The word for mind is manas. However, it is used to indicate a
> particular aspect of mind. The total individual mind is known as
> antaḥ karaṇa. Manas is a part of the antaḥ karaṇa. It is thought and
> counter thought. The word chitta is also used to indicate mind,
> individual consciousness and an aspect of antaḥ karaṇa which is
> memory. Some scholars even consider chitta and buddhi as identical…
> We are considering the whole finite mind, or finite consciousness.
> In order to dissolve the whole finite mind each of the elements or
> tattwas constituting it has to be separated."
>
> — Muktibodhānanda commentary on HYP 4.21, p. 500

The *antaḥ-karaṇa* (manas/buddhi/ahaṃkāra/citta) is the four-fold
internal organ Sāṃkhya bequeaths to Yoga. Haṭha's *manonmanī /
samādhi* is framed as *tattva*-dissolution — the inverse of the
Sāṃkhya evolutionary order.

## Puruṣa and prakṛti — shared

SK 17 (above, proof of puruṣa) and SK 11 ("The Spirit is the reverse
of both of them") are the source. The later traditions refer back.

### Caraka: puruṣa = cetanā; prakṛti = avyakta

Caraka Śārīra 1.16 — *cetanāvantam evāhuḥ puruṣaṃ kecid anyathā* —
makes puruṣa = cetanā. 1.62 says of the self:

> "The self which is the knower of the body, eternal, omnipresent and
> indestructible is the unmanifest. Contrary to that is the manifest
> one."
>
> — Caraka Śārīra 1.62, tr. Sharma, vol. I, p. 403

### Suśruta: puruṣa = twenty-fifth; prakṛti = avyakta

Established by Bhishagratna's introduction (above) and by
Uttaratantra 65.29.

### Patañjali: puruṣa = draṣṭṛ; prakṛti = dṛśya

YS 2.17–26 is the full Patañjalian exposition. *Puruṣa* = *draṣṭṛ*;
*prakṛti* = *dṛśya*. YS 2.20: *draṣṭā dṛśi-mātraḥ* — "the seer is
merely seeing" (tr. Bryant). YS 4.34: *puruṣārtha-śūnyānāṃ guṇānām
prati-prasavaḥ kaivalyam* — the Sāṃkhya formula verbatim.

### Gītā: puruṣa and prakṛti are both beginningless

> *prakṛtiṃ puruṣaṃ caiva viddhy anādī ubhāv api |*
> *vikārāṃś ca guṇāṃś caiva viddhi prakṛti-sambhavān ||*

> "Prakṛti is the source of the evolutes and the guṇas. Prakṛti is
> stated to be the cause in the production of effect from cause, and
> the person is stated to be the cause of the experience of pleasure
> and pain. Since the person stands in Prakṛti, he experiences the
> guṇas that spring from Prakṛti; his involvement with the guṇas is
> the cause of his births…"
>
> — BG 13.19–21, tr. van Buitenen, p. 125

The Sāṃkhya doctrine — prakṛti as cause of karma-evolution, puruṣa
as ground of experience — stated in Gītā Sanskrit.

## Pañca-mahābhūta

SK 22 gives the five bhūtas as terminal evolutes of the five
tanmātras. SK 38 distinguishes them: tanmātras are *aviśeṣa*; bhūtas
are *viśeṣa*, marked by the three-fold guṇa predominance (*śānta,
ghora, mūḍha*). SK 38 is the metaphysical ancestor of every
subsequent five-bhūta enumeration in the medical and yogic corpora.

### Caraka: the five bhūtas and their guṇas

Caraka Śārīra 1.27–30 (above): *ākāśa, vāyu, agni, ap, pṛthivī* in
SK 22 order; *khara, drava, cala, uṣṇa, apratighāta* as the Sāṃkhya
*viśeṣa* expanded into clinical physics.

### Suśruta: the bhautika composition of the body

Suśruta Uttaratantra 66.5 (above): sixteen sub-strata = five
mahābhūtas + eleven indriyas. The doṣas resolve bhautikally —
*vāta* = ākāśa+vāyu, *pitta* = agni, *kapha* = ap+pṛthivī. Classical
Sāṃkhya five-bhūta doctrine applied to aetiology.

### Vāgbhaṭa: the vyakta/avyakta doctrine in pharmacology

Aṣṭāṅga-Hṛdaya Sūtra 9.17–18 invokes Sāṃkhya's *vyakta/avyakta*
polarity to explain the primacy of *uṣṇa* and *śīta* as the two
cardinal *vīryas*:

> "Some others (authorities) consider uṣṇa (hot) and śīta (cold) only
> the two (guṇas) as vīryas, because even though, substances are of
> many kinds and qualities, only agni (tejas) and soma (ap) are very
> strong (powerful) just as Vyakta (manifest) and Avyakta (unmanifest)
> are for this universe and these cannot be surpassed (vanquished,
> belittled)."
>
> — Vāgbhaṭa, Aṣṭāṅga-Hṛdaya Sūtra 9.17–18, tr. K. R. Srikantha Murthy
>   (Krishnadas Academy), vol. I, p. 140

Srikantha Murthy's own footnote:

> "The simile of vyakta and avyakta and the universe in this verse
> also points to another doctrine of the Sāṃkhya philosophy, which
> states that in the very beginning (before the evolution of the
> universe) there existed only one principle and it was Avyakta
> (unmanifest). From this were evolved many principles which became
> Vyakta (manifest in form). So it is categorically said that Avyakta
> (unmanifest) and vyakta (manifest, evolutes forming all the
> different substances) are the two important principles which cannot
> be surpassed."
>
> — Srikantha Murthy, Aṣṭāṅga-Hṛdaya Sūtra 9 note, vol. I, p. 140

The two-vīrya theory presupposes Sāṃkhya's vyakta/avyakta metaphysics.

### Patañjali and the bhūtas

YS 2.18: *bhūtendriyātmakaṃ dṛśyam* — the knowable is bhūtas plus
indriyas. YS 3.44: *sthūla-svarūpa-sūkṣma-anvaya-arthavattva-
saṃyamād bhūta-jayaḥ* — saṃyama on five aspects of the bhūta (gross,
nature, subtle, all-pervasion, purposiveness) yields *bhūta-jaya*.
The five-stage analysis — from gross body to tanmātra to ahaṃkāra —
is the Sāṃkhya structure recited again by the yogin.

## Dasgupta — Caraka preserves pre-classical Sāṃkhya

Dasgupta, *A History of Indian Philosophy* Vol I (Cambridge 1922),
ch. VII sub-section "An Early School of Sāṃkhya": Caraka preserves a
Sāṃkhya older than Īśvarakṛṣṇa's.

### The thesis stated

> "It is important for the history of Sāṃkhya philosophy that
> Caraka's treatment of it, which so far as I know has never been
> dealt with in any of the modern studies of Sāṃkhya, should be
> brought before the notice of the students of this philosophy."
>
> — Dasgupta, *A History of Indian Philosophy*, vol. I (Cambridge
>   University Press 1922), p. 213

### The enumeration in Caraka

> "According to Caraka there are six elements (dhātus), viz. the five
> elements such as ākāśa, vāyu etc. and cetanā, called also puruṣa.
> From other points of view, the categories may be said to be
> twenty-four only, viz. the ten senses (five cognitive and five
> conative), manas, the five objects of senses and the eightfold
> prakṛti (prakṛti, mahat, ahaṃkāra and the five elements). The
> manas works through the senses. It is atomic and its existence is
> proved by the fact that in spite of the existence of the senses
> there cannot be any knowledge unless manas is in touch with them."
>
> — Dasgupta, vol. I, pp. 213–214

### The bhautika theory of senses

> "Each of the five senses is the product of the combination of five
> elements but the auditory sense is made with a preponderance of
> ākāśa, the sense of touch with a preponderance of air, the visual
> sense with a preponderance of light, the taste with a preponderance
> of water and the sense of smell with a preponderance of earth.
> Caraka does not mention the tanmātras at all."
>
> — Dasgupta, vol. I, p. 214

The absence of tanmātras in Caraka is Dasgupta's key marker of
archaism. The classical tanmātra doctrine is later; Caraka precedes
it.

### The fusion of avyakta and puruṣa

> "Caraka identifies the avyakta part of prakṛti with puruṣa as
> forming one category. The vikāra or evolutionary products of prakṛti
> are called kṣetra, whereas the avyakta part of prakṛti is regarded
> as the kṣetrajña (*avyaktam asya kṣetrasya kṣetrajñam ṛṣayo viduḥ*).
> This avyakta and cetanā are one and the same entity."
>
> — Dasgupta, vol. I, p. 214

The decisive doctrinal difference: the 25-tattva classical system
keeps puruṣa and avyakta-prakṛti apart; Caraka's 24-tattva system
folds them, identifying avyakta-cetanā with the knower. Dasgupta
identifies this as the older position.

### The main features of Caraka's Sāṃkhya

> "The main features of the Sāṃkhya doctrine as given by Caraka are
> thus: 1. Puruṣa is the state of avyakta. 2. By a conglomeration of
> this avyakta with its later products a conglomeration is formed
> which generates the so-called living being. 3. The tanmātras are
> not mentioned. 4. Rajas and tamas represent the bad states of the
> mind and sattva the good ones. 5. The ultimate state of
> emancipation is either absolute annihilation or characterless
> absolute existence and it is spoken of as the Brahman state; there
> is no consciousness in this state, for consciousness is due to the
> conglomeration of the self with its evolutes, buddhi, ahaṃkāra etc.
> 6. The senses are formed of matter (bhautika)."
>
> — Dasgupta, vol. I, p. 216

### The convergence with Pañcaśikha and the three-school Mahābhārata witness

> "This account of Sāṃkhya agrees with the system of Sāṃkhya
> propounded by Pañcaśikha (who is said to be the direct pupil of
> Āsuri the pupil of Kapila, the founder of the system) in the
> Mahābhārata XII. 219. Pañcaśikha of course does not describe the
> system as elaborately as Caraka does. But even from what little he
> says it may be supposed that the system of Sāṃkhya he sketches is
> the same as that of Caraka."
>
> — Dasgupta, vol. I, p. 216

> "In Mahābhārata XII. 318 three schools of Sāṃkhya are mentioned,
> viz. those who admitted twenty-four categories (the school I have
> sketched above), those who admitted twenty-five (the well-known
> orthodox Sāṃkhya system) and those who admitted twenty-six
> categories. This last school admitted a supreme being in addition
> to puruṣa and this was the twenty-sixth principle. This agrees with
> the orthodox Yoga system and the form of Sāṃkhya advocated in the
> Mahābhārata."
>
> — Dasgupta, vol. I, p. 217

Dasgupta's alignment: Caraka = Pañcaśikha = Mahābhārata XII.219 =
older 24-tattva Sāṃkhya.

### The conclusion — Caraka antedates Īśvarakṛṣṇa

> "We do not know anything about Āsuri the direct disciple of Kapila.
> But it seems probable that the system of Sāṃkhya we have sketched
> here which appears in fundamentally the same form in the
> Mahābhārata and has been attributed there to Pañcaśikha is probably
> the earliest form of Sāṃkhya available to us in a systematic form.
> Not only does Guṇaratna's reference to the school of Maulikya
> Sāṃkhya justify it, but the fact that Caraka (78 A.D.) does not
> refer to the Sāṃkhya as described by Īśvarakṛṣṇa and referred to in
> other parts of Mahābhārata is a definite proof that Īśvarakṛṣṇa's
> Sāṃkhya is a later modification, which was either non-existent in
> Caraka's time or was not regarded as an authoritative old Sāṃkhya
> view."
>
> — Dasgupta, vol. I, p. 218

Conclusion: Caraka preserves a Sāṃkhya older than Īśvarakṛṣṇa's. The
medical Sāṃkhya is the archaic Sāṃkhya. The philosophical tradition
later refined and reshaped it; the Āyurvedic tradition fossilized it.
To read Caraka is to read Sāṃkhya before its classical codification.

### Patañjali's awareness of Caraka's Sāṃkhya

> "The earliest descriptions of a Sāṃkhya which agrees with
> Īśvarakṛṣṇa's Sāṃkhya (but with an addition of Īśvara) are to be
> found in Patañjali's Yoga sūtras and in the Mahābhārata; but we are
> pretty certain that the Sāṃkhya of Caraka we have sketched here was
> known to Patañjali, for in Yoga sūtra I.19 a reference is made to a
> view of Sāṃkhya similar to this."
>
> — Dasgupta, vol. I, p. 219

Patañjali follows the classical 25-tattva Sāṃkhya and knows the older
24-tattva Sāṃkhya (cited at YS 1.19 on *bhava-pratyaya*). The Yoga
Sūtras are a witness to the priority of Caraka's Sāṃkhya.

### The Caraka-Gītā convergence — Dasgupta Vol II

> "A similar view regarding the production of action is to be found in
> Caraka's account of Sāṃkhya, where it is said that all actions are
> produced as a result of a collocation of causes — that actions are
> the results of the collocation of other entities with the agent
> (kartṛ)."
>
> — Dasgupta, *A History of Indian Philosophy*, vol. II (Cambridge
>   1932), p. 472

And again on the Gītā and Caraka as two witnesses to the same pre-
classical Sāṃkhya:

> "There are, no doubt, here the scattered elements of an older
> philosophy, from which not only the Sāṃkhya of Īśvarakṛṣṇa or the
> Ṣaṣṭi-tantra (of which Īśvarakṛṣṇa's work was a summary) developed,
> but even its earlier version, as found in Caraka's account, could
> be considered to have developed. There is no doubt that the Gītā's
> account of Sāṃkhya differs materially from the Sāṃkhya of the
> Ṣaṣṭi-tantra or of Īśvarakṛṣṇa, from the Sāṃkhya of Caraka, from
> the Sāṃkhya of Pañcaśikha in the Mahābhārata and from the Sāṃkhya
> of Patañjali and the Vyāsa-bhāṣya."
>
> — Dasgupta, vol. II, p. 476

> "On some minor points (e.g. the absence of tan-mātras, the nature of
> the production of knowledge and action, etc.) the Gītā philosophy
> has some similarities with the account of the Sāṃkhya given in the
> Caraka-saṃhitā, IV.1, as already described in the first volume of
> this work."
>
> — Dasgupta, vol. II, p. 477

### Dasgupta Vol II on the medical schools

Ch. XIII ("Speculations in the Medical Schools") opens with the
programmatic claim:

> "It may be urged that the speculations of the thinkers of the
> medical schools do not deserve to be recorded in a History of
> Indian Philosophy. But the force of such an objection will lose
> much in strength if it is remembered that medicine was the most
> important of all the physical sciences which were cultivated in
> ancient India, was directly and intimately connected with the
> Sāṃkhya and Vaiśeṣika physics and was probably the origin of the
> logical speculations subsequently codified in the Nyāya-sūtras."
>
> — Dasgupta, vol. II, p. 273

Dasgupta's thesis at its clearest: medicine is not applied Sāṃkhya,
it is the matrix from which Sāṃkhya (and Vaiśeṣika, Nyāya) was
distilled. The philosophers took categories from the physicians.

## Larson, Burley — Sāṃkhya as common trunk

### Gerald Larson on Caraka and Pañcaśikha

Larson, *Classical Sāṃkhya* (Motilal 1969), extends Dasgupta:

> "The texts relating to the development of the Sāṃkhya may be
> arranged conveniently into four basic periods. These periods may be
> designated as follows: (1) ancient speculations, including the
> speculative Vedic hymns and the oldest prose Upaniṣads… (2) proto-
> Sāṃkhya speculations, including the 'middle' Upaniṣads, such texts
> as the Carakasaṃhitā and the Buddhacarita, the Bhagavadgītā, and
> the speculative passages from the Mokṣadharma portion of the
> Mahābhārata. This period extends from about the fourth century B.C.
> through the first century A.D., (3) classical Sāṃkhya speculation,
> including the Sāṃkhyakārikā, the Yogasūtra and related
> commentaries."
>
> — Gerald Larson, *Classical Sāṃkhya* (Motilal Banarsidass 1969),
>   p. 75

Caraka, Buddhacarita, and Gītā are proto-Sāṃkhya; the Sāṃkhyakārikā
opens the classical period.

> "Caraka and Pañcaśikha both accept twenty-four rather than
> twenty-five tattvas… Another early form of Sāṃkhya appears to be
> present in Aśvaghoṣa's Buddhacarita — convincingly dated by Johnston
> in the 1st century A.D. This form of Sāṃkhya is related in turn to
> the summary of Sāṃkhya found in the Carakasaṃhitā and in the
> 'Pañcaśikhavākya' of the Mokṣadharma."
>
> — Larson, p. 155 and p. 73

Larson on the inseparability of early Sāṃkhya and Yoga:

> "In these early forms, Sāṃkhya is closely allied with Yoga, and
> both represent 'ways' or 'methods' of salvation. Edgerton has
> shown the 'practical' (i.e., saving or soteriological) emphasis in
> all forms of ancient Indian speculation."
>
> — Larson, p. 73

Larson on what is missing in the proto-classical forms:

> "The classical doctrines of prakṛti, satkārya-vāda, tattva-vikāra
> and the tanmātras are not present in these early forms of Sāṃkhya."
>
> — Larson, p. 73

The tanmātras — Dasgupta's classical/pre-classical marker — are
absent from the Caraka stratum. Larson confirms Dasgupta.

### Mikel Burley on the shared metaphysics of Sāṃkhya and Yoga

Burley, *Classical Sāṃkhya and Yoga* (Routledge 2007):

> "Sāṃkhya and Yoga are not merely the theoretical and practical
> sides of a single system, for they each contain elements of both of
> these aspects. They do, however, each have a primary focus or
> emphasis, and this is legitimately characterized as theoretical in
> the case of Sāṃkhya and practical in that of Yoga. A study that
> includes them both, and which looks to the one in order to help
> explain certain features of the other, is therefore in a stronger
> position to understand the metaphysics that Sāṃkhya and Yoga
> largely share than one which views the two systems as antagonistic
> or (as Feuerstein hyperbolically puts it) divided by a 'chasm'."
>
> — Mikel Burley, *Classical Sāṃkhya and Yoga* (Routledge 2007), p. 55

Gavin Flood's Series Editor's Preface to Burley:

> "The book also offers a convincing account of the relationship
> between Sāṃkhya and Yoga showing how they share a dualist
> metaphysics. One of the original features of the book is the way
> it deals with the problem of the fundamental categories or tattvas,
> having both a cosmological and psychological designation."
>
> — Gavin Flood, Series Editor's Preface to Burley, p. vii

Burley on the shared framework:

> "One of my principal aims in this study as a whole is to show that
> the metaphysical framework that is for the most part shared by
> Sāṃkhya and Yoga is best regarded as the result of a close analysis
> of experience rather than as a cosmogony or creation myth."
>
> — Burley, p. 56

The same *tattvas* — puruṣa, prakṛti, mahat, ahaṃkāra, manas, the
indriyas, tanmātras, bhūtas — are the shared architecture.

## Scholarly voices

### Swami Virūpākṣananda on Sāṃkhya and Āyurveda

Publisher's Note to the Sāṃkhya Kārikā (Ramakrishna Math 1995):

> "Vedanta accepts most of the basic concepts of Sāṃkhya — like the
> nature of the misery-go-round called Saṃsāra; the triple sufferings
> we are heir to; the three guṇas of Prakṛti in terms of which can be
> explained not only the manifold objects of the universe, but also
> the workings of the mind and the psyche and even the rationale of
> medical therapy (Āyurveda); the process of evolution, long before
> Western science began to think in terms of it; and the nature of
> Pure Consciousness in which the individual must merge for total
> liberation."
>
> — Swami Virūpākṣananda, Publisher's Note to the Sāṃkhya Kārikā
>   (Ramakrishna Math 1995), p. iii

Āyurveda as direct application of the Sāṃkhya triguṇa doctrine — the
classical Indian position.

### Dasgupta on Sāṃkhya and Āyurveda as shared enterprise

> "Medicine was the most important of all the physical sciences
> which were cultivated in ancient India, was directly and intimately
> connected with the Sāṃkhya and Vaiśeṣika physics and was probably
> the origin of the logical speculations subsequently codified in the
> Nyāya-sūtras. The literature contains, moreover, many other
> interesting ethical instructions and reveals a view of life which
> differs considerably from that found in works on philosophy;
> further, it treats of many other interesting details which throw a
> flood of light on the scholastic methods of Indian thinkers. Those,
> again, who are aware of the great importance of Haṭha Yoga or
> Tantra physiology or anatomy in relation to some of the Yoga
> practices of those schools will no doubt be interested to know for
> purposes of comparison or contrast the speculations of the medical
> schools on kindred points of interest."
>
> — Dasgupta, vol. II, p. 273

Dasgupta names Haṭha Yoga and the medical schools together as common
participants in Sāṃkhya-rooted *tattva*-discourse.

### Bhishagratna on Suśruta and Kapila

> "In addition to these, Suśruta, like Kapila, admits the existence
> of a kind of atom-like units of consciousness, which he calls
> Puruṣa."
>
> — Bhishagratna, Suśruta Saṃhitā Vol I Intro, p. xxvii

Suśruta's medical psychology identified with the Kapilian Sāṃkhya
founder.

### Srikantha Murthy on Vāgbhaṭa's Sāṃkhya substrate

> "The simile of vyakta and avyakta and the universe in this verse
> also points to another doctrine of the Sāṃkhya philosophy, which
> states that in the very beginning (before the evolution of the
> universe) there existed only one principle and it was Avyakta
> (unmanifest). From this were evolved many principles which became
> Vyakta (manifest in form)."
>
> — Srikantha Murthy, Aṣṭāṅga-Hṛdaya Sū. 9 footnote, vol. I, p. 140

Every major Āyurvedic translator — Sharma (Caraka), Bhishagratna
(Suśruta), Srikantha Murthy (Vāgbhaṭa) — identifies the Sāṃkhya
substrate. The reading is not forced; it is what the texts say.

### The convergence

Read in parallel:

- SK 3: *mūla-prakṛtir avikṛtiḥ … sapta … ṣoḍaśakas tu vikāraḥ*
- Caraka Śārīra 1.63: *avyaktam, mahat, ahaṃkāra, + five bhūtas* =
  eight *prakṛti*; five jñānendriyas + five karmendriyas + manas +
  five viṣayas = sixteen *vikāra*.
- Suśruta Uttaratantra 66.5: *sixteen sub-strata* (five bhūtas +
  eleven indriyas), *three guṇas*, 62 doṣa-combinations built on them.
- YS 2.19: *viśeṣa, aviśeṣa, liṅga-mātra, aliṅga* = the four guṇa-
  stages = the four Sāṃkhya levels.
- BG 13.5: *mahā-bhūtāni, ahaṃkāraḥ, buddhiḥ, avyaktam eva ca |
  indriyāṇi daśaikaṃ ca pañca cendriya-gocarāḥ* — "the elements, the
  ahaṃkāra, the buddhi, the unmanifest, the ten faculties plus one
  [manas], and the five realms of these faculties" (van Buitenen
  p. 123). This is the SK 3 list in Gītā meter.

One enumeration, five voices.

Yoga and Āyurveda do not merely share a metaphysics — they share a
metaphysics learned in the same classroom: the proto-Sāṃkhya stratum
of the Gītā, the Buddhacarita, the Mokṣadharma's "Pañcaśikhavākya",
Caraka, the early Yoga tradition, and later Suśruta and Vāgbhaṭa.
Īśvarakṛṣṇa's Kārikā is a later crystallization. The Āyurvedic texts
fossilize an earlier stratum of that material. The Yogic texts use
vocabulary that is sometimes classical (Patañjali's
*viśeṣa/aviśeṣa*), sometimes archaic (the Pañcaśikha register).

To say that Yoga and Āyurveda "share Sāṃkhya metaphysics" is too weak.
They are both downstream forms of the same parent. The parent is
Sāṃkhya. The two children are two applications — soteriological
(Yoga) and clinical (Āyurveda) — of the same 25-tattva analysis of
the *puruṣa–prakṛti* conjunction.
