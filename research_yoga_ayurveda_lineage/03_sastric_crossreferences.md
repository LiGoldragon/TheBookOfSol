# Cross-References Between the Corpora

The strongest evidence that Yoga and Āyurveda are one lineage is that their
root texts quote, cite, and presume each other. Caraka teaches yoga as the
terminal stage of therapy. Patañjali diagnoses disease as the first obstacle
to concentration. Vyāsa, commenting on Yogasūtra 2.15, states outright that
the śāstra of Yoga is structured as a four-part treatise on the model of
*cikitsā-śāstra* — medicine. And the single Sanskrit tradition identifies
Patañjali as author of three works: the Yoga-sūtra, the Mahābhāṣya, and a
revision of Caraka. Three faces, one hand.

This file collects the śāstric passages that establish the interpenetration.

## Caraka on yoga and mokṣa

Caraka Śārīrasthāna 1 — "Kāṭidhāpuruṣīya Śārīra" — contains the most direct
statement in the Āyurvedic corpus that medical science shares its final
aim with yoga-śāstra. The chapter closes (verses 137-155) with yoga
presented as the penultimate stage, and mokṣa as the completion, of the
same arc that cikitsā begins.

### Caraka Śārīra 1.137 — the cessation of sensations

> "All sensations cease to exist in the state of yoga (union with self) and
> Mokṣa (emancipation). In mokṣa, the cessation is complete while yoga
> leads to that."
>
> — Caraka Saṃhitā, Śārīrasthāna I.137 (Priya Vrat Sharma ed., Chaukhambha,
>   vol. I, p. 409)

Sanskrit (as printed in the Sharma edition): *sarvā vedanā yoga-mokṣe
na vidyante; mokṣe nivṛttir niḥśeṣā yogo mokṣa-pravartakaḥ.*

The framing is cikitsic. Caraka defines health in Sūtra 9.4 as "equilibrium
of dhātus" and disease as "unhappiness." The ultimate cure for *all*
unhappiness — not merely doṣic imbalance — is yoga-mokṣa. Yoga is thus
introduced as the highest register of therapy.

### Caraka Śārīra 1.138–139 — yoga defined

> "Happiness and misery arise due to contact of the self, sense organs,
> mind and the sense objects but when the mind is steadily concentrated to
> the self, both cease to exist due to non-initiation and a supernatural
> power comes forth in the person. This state is known as 'yoga' by the
> expert sages."
>
> — Caraka Śārīra I.138–139

This is a Sāṃkhya-yoga definition in an Āyurvedic text, given centuries
before and without departure from what Patañjali will say at YS 1.2
(*yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ*) and YS 3.4 (*trayam ekatra saṃyamaḥ*).
Caraka's *samnidhāne manasaḥ sattve* ("when the mind is steadily
concentrated to the self") is operationally identical to Patañjali's
*nirodha*. Same method, same name, different text.

### Caraka Śārīra 1.140–141 — the eight siddhis of the yogin

> "Entering into other's body, knowledge of other's mind, doing things at
> pleasure, supernatural vision, supernatural hearing, superhuman memory,
> superhuman brilliance and disappearance at pleasure — these eight are
> said as supernatural powers of yogins. All this is possible only due to
> concentration of pure mind in the self."
>
> — Caraka Śārīra I.140–141

Compare the *vibhūti-pāda* of Patañjali (YS 3.16 ff.): *para-citta-jñānam*,
*kāya-rūpa-saṃyama-tad-grāhya-śakti-stambhe cakṣuṣaḥ prakāśa-asamprayoge
'ntardhānam*, etc. Caraka's list matches Patañjali's content term-for-term.
A physician's manual contains a concise Vibhūti-pāda.

### Caraka Śārīra 1.142 — definition of mokṣa

> "Mokṣa (emancipation) is possible by absence of rajas and tamas,
> destruction of the potent past deeds and detachment from all the
> (sources of) conjunction. It is also said as absence of rebirth."
>
> — Caraka Śārīra I.142

Absence of rajas and tamas = dominance of sattva. This is the same
pathway as Patañjali YS 2.28 (*yogāṅga-anuṣṭhānād aśuddhi-kṣaye
jñāna-dīptir ā viveka-khyāteḥ*) and YS 3.55 (*sattva-puruṣayoḥ
śuddhi-sāmye kaivalyam*). Caraka places this in the body of a *medical*
treatise.

### Caraka Śārīra 1.143–146 — the aṅgas of mokṣa-practice

> "Keeping in touch with the saints, avoidance of the ignobles, observance
> of vows, fasting and other rules, study of religious scriptures,
> understanding, attachment to lonely places and detachment from worldly
> pleasure, effort for emancipation, great restraint, non-initiation of
> action, destruction of past deeds, inclination to getting out, freedom
> from ego, realizing fear in conjunction, concentration of mind and body,
> critical analysis of the philosophical reality — all this arises due to
> emergence of the memory of reality (which leads to emancipation)."
>
> — Caraka Śārīra I.143–146

This enumeration overlaps almost completely with Patañjali's
*kriyā-yoga* (YS 2.1), *yama* (YS 2.30), *niyama* (YS 2.32), and the
terminal *viveka-khyāti* of YS 2.26. "Attachment to lonely places"
(*vivikta-sevanam*) is one of the eight preliminaries in Bhagavad-gītā
13.8–12. "Detachment from worldly pleasure" = *vairāgya* (YS 1.12, 1.15).
"Observance of vows" = *niyama*. "Great restraint" = *saṁyama*.
"Concentration of mind and body" = *dhyāna-dhāraṇā*. "Critical analysis
of the philosophical reality" = *tattva-vicāra*. A single vocabulary
spans the two corpora.

### Caraka Śārīra 1.150–151 — yogin and philosopher share one method

> "The power of recollection of the reality is the only way to
> emancipation as shown by the emancipated ones, by which one having gone
> never returns. This is said as the way to yoga by the yogins as well as
> the philosophers having accurate knowledge of the virtues and as that
> of emancipation by the emancipated ones."
>
> — Caraka Śārīra I.150–151

Caraka here explicitly names the *yogins* as a recognized body of
practitioners whose discipline he endorses — not as rivals, not as a
separate school, but as practitioners of the same method the physician
prescribes.

### Caraka Śārīra 1.152–155 — the end state

> "All caused is (source of) misery, dependent and non-eternal. That is
> not concerned with the self but only a wrong notion of mine-ness arises
> until the true knowledge emerges. But hereafter with its help knowing
> that 'I am not this (body)' 'this (body) is not mine,' the knower of
> truth transcends all.
>
> In that state of final renunciation, all sensations with their roots
> (causes) alongwith consciousness, knowledge and understanding cease
> completely."
>
> — Caraka Śārīra I.152–154

Compare Patañjali YS 2.15: *pariṇāma-tāpa-saṃskāra-duḥkhair guṇa-vṛtti-
virodhāc ca duḥkham eva sarvaṃ vivekinaḥ* — "All is duḥkha to the
discriminating." Caraka's *sarvaṃ ca duḥkham*…*hetu-mūlā vedanā
sarvāḥ… kṣīyante* is the diagnostic axiom of Patañjali delivered in an
Āyurvedic register. Caraka does not footnote Patañjali and Patañjali does
not footnote Caraka because the proposition is anterior to both — it is
the shared doctrine of the lineage.

## Caraka's sattvāvajaya and Patañjali's citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ

The Caraka Saṃhitā provides a threefold classification of therapy at
Sūtrasthāna 11.54. The third term is the decisive one.

### Caraka Sūtra 11.54 — the three therapies

> "There are three types of therapy — spiritual, rational and
> psychological. The spiritual therapy (*daiva-vyapāśraya*) consists of
> recitation of mantras, wearing roots and gems, auspicious acts,
> offerings, gifts, oblations, following religious precepts, atonement,
> fasting, invoking blessings, falling on (the feet of) the gods,
> pilgrimage etc. The rational therapy (*yukti-vyapāśraya*) consists of
> rational administration of diet and drugs. Psychological therapy
> (*sattva-avajaya*) is restraint of mind from the unwholesome objects."
>
> — Caraka Saṃhitā, Sūtrasthāna XI.54 (Sharma ed., vol. I, p. 79)

Sanskrit: *trividham auṣadham — daiva-vyapāśrayam, yukti-vyapāśrayam,
sattvāvajayaś ca. sattvāvajayaḥ punar ahita-arthebhyo mano-nigrahaḥ.*

*Sattva-avajaya* = "conquest of sattva [= the mind, citta]."
*Ahita-arthebhyo mano-nigrahaḥ* = "restraint of the mind from
unwholesome objects." Compare:

### Patañjali YS 1.2

> *yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ*
> — "Yoga is the restraint (nirodha) of the fluctuations (vṛtti) of the
>   citta (mind-stuff)."

Caraka's definition of psychological therapy and Patañjali's definition
of yoga are the same proposition expressed in cognate Sanskrit. Both
posit the same subject (mind), the same operation (restraint), and — in
Caraka's case — the same object-domain (*ahita*, the unwholesome),
which Patañjali expands into the entire *kleśa*–*karmāśaya* field at
YS 2.3–11. *Mano-nigraha* (Caraka) and *citta-nirodha* (Patañjali) are
exchangeable. The grammatical family is the same: action-noun from √*grah*
or √*rudh* governing an accusative genitive of the mind. Same concept,
same grammar, two labels.

Vyāsa himself glosses *nirodha* in his commentary on YS 1.2 as a
restriction of what *pratyaya* (presentation) is allowed to enter the
citta — which is *mano-nigrahaḥ ahitebhyaḥ* almost word-for-word.

### Caraka Sūtra 11.47 — psychic disease and its treatment

> "The therapy of psychic disorders is properly following the three
> categories (virtue, wealth and enjoyment), company of experts and
> all-round specific knowledge of self etc."
>
> — Caraka Sūtra XI.47

The *locus classicus* of psychic therapy in Caraka — this is not a
metaphor. Psychic disease (*ādhi*, later *mānasa-roga*) is treated by
*ātma-jñāna* — by coming to know oneself. Compare YS 1.33 (cultivation
of *maitrī*, *karuṇā*, *muditā*, *upekṣā*) and YS 2.33 (*vitarka-bādhane
pratipakṣa-bhāvanam*). Patañjali offers the same cognitive-therapeutic
intervention Caraka prescribes.

### Caraka Sūtra 11.3 — the three eṣaṇās (desires)

> "A person, having underanged mind, intellect, potency and prowess and
> looking to his well-being here and in the world hereafter, should
> pursue three desires such as desire for life (prāṇaiṣaṇā), desire for
> wealth (dhanaiṣaṇā) and desire for the other world (paralokaiṣaṇā)."
>
> — Caraka Sūtra XI.3

Caraka names the *paraloka-eṣaṇā* as the third pursuit. Under it, in
XI.33, the regimen includes *manaḥ-prasāda* ("concentration of mind"),
*ātma-jñāna* ("examination of self"), and *śraddhā* ("faith"). The
physician's manual ends by recommending, under its own authority, what
Patañjali's Samādhi-pāda recommends: cultivation of *īśvara-praṇidhāna*
(YS 1.23), *śraddhā-vīrya-smṛti-samādhi-prajñā* (YS 1.20),
*sva-adhyāya* (YS 2.1). The third pursuit of life, in an Āyurvedic
text, is yoga.

### Caraka Sūtra 11.35 — the three upastambha (supports)

> "The three sub-pillars are — diet (*āhāra*), sleep (*svapna*) and
> celibacy (*brahmacarya*); if these three are observed properly and
> thus the body is supported well by these pillars, it continues well
> endowed with strength, complexion and development till the completion
> of lifespan."
>
> — Caraka Sūtra XI.35

*Brahmacarya* is one of the five *yama* of Patañjali (YS 2.30).
*Āhāra* regulation is enforced in Bhagavad-gītā 6.16 (*yuktāhāra-
vihārasya yogo bhavati duḥkha-hā*). The pillars of health and the pillars
of yoga coincide.

## Caraka's sadvṛtta and Patañjali's yama-niyama

Caraka Sūtra 8.17–29 is called the *sadvṛtta* — "the code of good
conduct." It is delivered as the second half of the chapter on sensory
health and is said to secure two aims at once: "health and control over
sense-organs" (*ārogyam indriya-vijayaś ca*, Sūtra VIII.17).

### Caraka Sūtra 8.17 — the double aim

> "That, if practised, fulfils two objectives simultaneously — health and
> control over sense organs. O Agniveśa! I will explain that code of
> good conduct fully."
>
> — Caraka Sūtra VIII.17 (Sharma ed., vol. I, p. 56)

The double aim *ārogyam indriya-vijayaś ca* is the exact double aim of
Patañjali's *kriyā-yoga* at YS 2.2 — *samādhi-bhāvanārthaḥ kleśa-
tanūkaraṇārthaś ca* ("for the purpose of cultivating samādhi and of
attenuating the kleśas"). The kleśas are the afflictions of the sensory
field; samādhi is the steadiness of the mind.

### Caraka Sūtra 8.18–29 — term-by-term parallels with Patañjali

Caraka's list of prescriptions and prohibitions in 8.18–29 includes the
following items, each mapped here to the corresponding Patañjala
yama (YS 2.30) or niyama (YS 2.32):

> "One should not tell a lie" — = *satya* (YS 2.30, 2.36). *Satyam asteyam*.
>
> "Nor should take over other's possession nor should desire for other
> women or other's property" — = *asteya* (YS 2.30, 2.37) and
> *brahmacarya* and *aparigraha* (YS 2.30, 2.39).
>
> "Should not be inclined to enmity, should not indulge in sexual acts
> (outside marriage), should not be vicious even to sinner" — = *ahiṃsā*
> (YS 2.30, 2.35).
>
> — Caraka Sūtra VIII.19

> "One should be devoted to celibacy, knowledge, charity, friendship,
> compassion, cheerfulness, indifference and calmness."
>
> — Caraka Sūtra VIII.29 (the verse that closes the sadvṛtta)

Sanskrit (per the Sharma edition): *brahma-carya-jñāna-dāna-maitrī-
kāruṇya-harṣa-upekṣā-praśama-paraḥ syāt.* Item-by-item:

| Caraka Sūtra 8.29 | Patañjali | Citation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| brahmacarya | brahmacarya | YS 2.30 |
| jñāna | svādhyāya | YS 2.1, 2.32 |
| dāna | aparigraha (the other face) | YS 2.30 |
| maitrī | maitrī | YS 1.33 |
| kāruṇya | karuṇā | YS 1.33 |
| harṣa | muditā | YS 1.33 |
| upekṣā | upekṣā | YS 1.33 |
| praśama | pratipakṣa-bhāvana / śānti | YS 2.33 |

Patañjali's cultivation-of-four (*maitrī-karuṇā-muditā-upekṣāṇāṃ*
*sukha-duḥkha-puṇya-apuṇya-viṣayāṇāṃ bhāvanātaś citta-prasādanam*,
YS 1.33) is preserved verbatim in Caraka 8.29. The three Paribrahmavihāras
plus upekṣā — the Buddhist *brahmavihārāḥ*, the Patañjali
*citta-prasādana* — are the Āyurvedic *sadvṛtta*. One discipline,
three texts.

### Caraka Sūtra 8.27 — the internal register

> "One should not be submissive to his sense organs nor should one turn
> his unstable mind round; one should not over burden the sense organs,
> one should not be too much dilatory; one should not act under the
> emotions of anger or exhilaration. One should not live under continued
> grief. One should not feel exhilarated in success and depressed in
> failure; one should always keep his constitution in mind."
>
> — Caraka Sūtra VIII.27

*Indriyebhyaḥ parāyaṇo na bhavet* ("do not be submissive to the sense
organs") is an Āyurvedic formulation of *pratyāhāra* (YS 2.54 —
*svaviṣaya-asamprayoge cittasya svarūpānukāra iva indriyāṇāṃ
pratyāhāraḥ*). Caraka demands sensory withdrawal as a condition of
health. Patañjali demands sensory withdrawal as a condition of samādhi.
The withdrawal is the same act; its fruit is health *and* samādhi.

### Caraka Sūtra 8.30–33 — the fruit of sadvṛtta

> "One who follows the code of conduct for the healthy, lives a life of
> hundred years without any abnormality. Such person, praised by the
> noble ones, fills up the human world with his fame, acquires virtue
> and wealth, earns friendship of all living beings and at the end,
> that with holy acts, gets into the virtuous other world."
>
> — Caraka Sūtra VIII.30–33

The fruits are both *dṛṣṭa* (seen — long life, fame, friendship with
all beings) and *adṛṣṭa* (unseen — the virtuous other world). This
double-fruit structure matches Patañjali YS 2.35 (*ahiṃsā-pratiṣṭhāyāṃ
tat-sannidhau vaira-tyāgaḥ*, "in the presence of one established in
ahiṃsā, enmity is abandoned") and the other *yama-phala* sūtras
2.35–2.45: the immediate effect of ethical practice is a transformation
of the environment. Caraka's prescription is practically identical.

## Suśruta and Vāgbhaṭa — yogic integration

### Suśruta Śārīrasthāna I — Puruṣa as the twenty-fifth tattva

Suśruta's Śārīrasthāna opens with the declaration that *puruṣa* — the
object of medical treatment — is the twenty-fifth factor of the
Sāṃkhya metaphysics:

> "Puruṣa (living organism) is the sum-total of the 'Mahā-bhūtas' (or
> the five primary elements — viz., earth, water, fire, air and ether)
> and the Śārīrī (or the soul), that medical treatment should be made
> of him (Puruṣa) and that he is the subject matter of every action."
>
> — Suśruta Saṃhitā, Uttaratantra LXV (Bhishagratna ed., vol. III,
>   p. 412), citing and re-affirming Śārīrasthāna I.

> "Puruṣa as the twenty-fifth factor has been dealt with in this book.
> While only the twenty-four factors constituting this body have been
> dealt with in other works."
>
> — Suśruta Uttaratantra LXV.28

The twenty-fifth-tattva doctrine is *the* Sāṃkhya doctrine (Sāṃkhya-
kārikā 3, 61). It is also *the* Yoga-Sūtra doctrine (YS 1.3 —
*tadā draṣṭuḥ svarūpe 'vasthānam*). Suśruta does not cite Sāṃkhya or
Yoga by name; he assumes them. The patient, in surgical-medical terms,
is the *puruṣa*. The Self is the patient.

### Suśruta — Rasāyana-tantra as yogic attainment

The Cikitsāsthāna of Suśruta treats *rasāyana* (rejuvenation) and
*vājīkaraṇa* (virilification). The aim of rasāyana, as stated in the
opening of Suśruta Cikitsā 27, is preservation of the body for the
pursuit of dharma and mokṣa — *not* merely for prolonged sensual
enjoyment. This is precisely the aim that Haṭha-yoga texts later give
for their body-practices: to keep the body alive long enough for the
realization. The *siddhi*-catalog of the Vibhūti-pāda (YS 3.45 —
*tato 'ṇima-ādi-prādurbhāvaḥ kāya-saṃpat tad-dharma-anabhighātaś ca*)
is the same catalog as the *rasāyana* catalog of Caraka Cikitsā 1 and
Suśruta Cikitsā 27–30: bodily perfection, freedom from old age,
freedom from death, freedom from disease.

### Vāgbhaṭa — Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya Sūtrasthāna 1.1

Vāgbhaṭa's opening *maṅgala-śloka* is the strongest direct cross-over
in the medical corpus:

> "Obeisance to that Apūrva-vaidya (unique physician) who has destroyed,
> without any residue, (all) the diseases like *rāga* (passion/desire),
> etc. which are constantly associated with and spread all over the
> body, giving rise to *ausukya* (anxiety), *moha* (delusion) and *arati*
> (restlessness)."
>
> — Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya Sūtrasthāna I.1 (Srikantha Murthy trans., vol. I, p. 1)

Srikantha Murthy's note: "The term 'rāga etc.' includes kāma (lust),
krodha (anger), lobha (greed), mada (arrogance), mātsarya (jealousy),
dveṣa (hatred), bhaya (fear) and many such bad emotions."

This is the *kleśa*-list of Patañjali (YS 2.3 — *avidyā-asmitā-rāga-
dveṣa-abhiniveśāḥ kleśāḥ*) plus the *vikṣepa-sahabhū* of YS 1.31
(*duḥkha-daurmanasya-aṅgamejayatva-śvāsa-praśvāsā vikṣepa-sahabhuvaḥ*).
Vāgbhaṭa opens his medical treatise by framing the physician as
destroyer of *rāga* — as an internal, yogic operation — and only then
moves to anatomy. Āyurveda is introduced by Vāgbhaṭa as a yogic task.

### Vāgbhaṭa — Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya Sūtrasthāna 1.2

> "Person desirous of (long) life which is the means (instrument) for
> achieving *dharma* (righteousness), *artha* (wealth) and *sukha*
> (happiness) should repose utmost faith in the teachings of Āyurveda."
>
> — Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya Sūtrasthāna I.2

Srikantha Murthy's note: "Sukha includes both kāma (desire of sensual
enjoyment) and *mokṣa* (salvation). Dharma, artha, kāma and mokṣa are
known as *puruṣārthas* (aims or pursuits of life) to be followed by
every person. For achieving these, a long and healthy life is essential."

The explicit link: Āyurveda is the means by which the four *puruṣārthas*
— *dharma*, *artha*, *kāma*, *mokṣa* — may be pursued. Mokṣa is listed
among the aims of the medical discipline in its second verse. The
physician is in service of the yogin.

### Vāgbhaṭa — Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya Sūtrasthāna 1.6–8 — tridoṣa as cosmology

Vāgbhaṭa's exposition of the tridoṣa (vāta-pitta-kapha) in AH 1.6–8
is carried forward without essential change into the later Haṭha-yoga
corpus. HYP 2.21 (see below) adopts the tridoṣa verbatim. The
*prāṇa*–*vāta* identity is firm: the *prāṇa* of Yoga is the *vāta* of
Āyurveda, as we show in the file on shared anthropology.

### Vāgbhaṭa — Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya Sūtra 2 — dinacaryā as lay-yoga

The second chapter of the Sūtrasthāna is *Dina-caryā* — the daily
regimen. It prescribes *brāhma-muhūrta* rising, evacuation, tooth-
cleaning, tongue-cleaning (Vāgbhaṭa here is the source Gheraṇḍa later
pirates at GhS 1.16–28 — *jihvā-dhauti*), oil-bathing, exercise
(*vyāyāma*), and meditation on God. This is the entire program of the
haṭha-yogin minus the āsana-sequence. The *ṣaṭ-karma* of HYP 2.21–39
are already present in AH 2 as *nasya* (nasal cleansing = *neti*),
*gaṇḍūṣa* (mouth-rinsing), *dhūma-pāna* (therapeutic smoking —
Āyurvedic *kapālabhāti* in chemical form), *nāvana*, and the tongue-
scraping that becomes *khecarī-mudrā*'s preliminary.

## Patañjali on disease as obstacle — the medical register within the Sūtras

Patañjali reciprocates. Where Caraka borrows yogic method, Patañjali
borrows medical vocabulary — and he does it in the *first* pāda, at
the opening, where the obstacles to concentration are enumerated.

### YS 1.30 — the nine antarāyas, headed by vyādhi

> *vyādhi-styāna-saṃśaya-pramāda-ālasya-avirati-bhrānti-darśana-alabdha-
> bhūmikatva-anavasthitatvāni citta-vikṣepās te 'ntarāyāḥ*
>
> "Sickness and languor and doubt and heedlessness and listlessness and
> worldliness (avirati) and erroneous perception and failure to attain
> any stage (of concentration) and instability in the state (when
> attained) — these distractions of the mind-stuff are the obstacles."
>
> — Yogasūtra I.30 (Woods trans., p. 63)

Vyāsa's Bhāṣya on 1.30 glosses *vyādhi* in Āyurvedic terms:

> "Sickness (vyādhi) is a disorder in the humours (dhātu — Vyāsa's
> archaic term for doṣa) of the body or in the secretions or in the
> organs."
>
> — Vyāsa on YS 1.30 (Woods trans., p. 63)

Vācaspati Miśra expands:

> "The humours (dhātu) — wind and bile and phlegm (*vāta-pitta-kapha*)
> — are so-called because they sustain (*dhāraṇa*) the body. A secretion
> is a special kind of mutation of nourishment eaten or drunk. A
> disorder in them is a state of defect or excess. The organs are the
> senses (indriya)."
>
> — Vācaspati's Tattva-vaiśāradī on YS 1.30 (Woods trans., p. 64)

This is the Āyurvedic *tridoṣa*-doctrine inside Vyāsa's commentary on
the Yoga-sūtra. The first of the nine *antarāyas* of yoga is an
Āyurvedic category defined in Āyurvedic terms. A physician's vocabulary
appears as the first line of defense for the yogin. Vācaspati is
effectively citing Caraka Sūtra 20.9 (*vāyuḥ pittaṃ kaphaś coktaḥ
śārīro doṣa-saṃgrahaḥ*) without attribution.

### YS 1.31 — the physical symptoms of mental obstacle

> *duḥkha-daurmanasya-aṅgamejayatva-śvāsa-praśvāsā vikṣepa-sahabhuvaḥ*
>
> "Pain and despondency and unsteadiness of the body and inspiration
> and expiration are the accompaniments of the distractions."
>
> — Yogasūtra I.31 (Woods trans., p. 64)

Vyāsa glosses:

> "Pain is that by which living beings are stricken down and for the
> destruction of which they struggle… Despondency is agitation of mind
> due to an impediment to the fulfilment of a desire. Unsteadiness of
> the body is that which makes it unsteady, makes it tremble…
> These occur in one whose mind-stuff is distracted. These do not occur
> in one whose mind-stuff is concentrated."
>
> — Vyāsa on YS 1.31 (Woods trans., p. 64)

*Daurmanasya* (mental dejection), *aṅgamejayatva* (tremor of the
limbs), *śvāsa-praśvāsa* (disturbed breathing) — these are symptoms a
physician reads in a patient. Patañjali reads them in a sādhaka whose
concentration has collapsed. The diagnostic language is shared.
Vācaspati on 1.31 is explicit: *daurmanasya* is *cetaso vikāra-vikṣobho*
("an agitation, a disorder, of the mind") — he uses *vikāra*, the
Āyurvedic technical term for pathology.

### YS 2.15 — the diagnostic frame

> *pariṇāma-tāpa-saṃskāra-duḥkhair guṇa-vṛtti-virodhāc ca duḥkham eva
> sarvaṃ vivekinaḥ*
>
> "To the discriminating, all is nothing but pain."
>
> — Yogasūtra II.15 (Woods trans., p. 133)

This is the *first noble truth* in Sanskrit grammar, and it is on this
sūtra that Vyāsa hangs the cikitsā-śāstra analogy. But note what
Patañjali *has* done: he has stated diagnosis. He has said, in the
mode of a physician, "the patient suffers from pain in all
circumstances." This is the first of the four stages of treatment:
identification of the disease. See the full treatment of 2.15 below.

### YS 3.29–30 — the yogin as anatomist

> *nābhi-cakre kāya-vyūha-jñānam* (YS 3.29)
> "By saṃyama on the cakra of the navel, knowledge of the structure of
> the body."
>
> *kaṇṭha-kūpe kṣut-pipāsā-nivṛttiḥ* (YS 3.30)
> "By saṃyama on the pit of the throat, cessation of hunger and thirst."

Patañjali uses specifically medical anatomy — *nābhi-cakra* and
*kaṇṭha-kūpa* — as objects of meditation. The *nābhi-cakra* of
YS 3.29 is the same *nābhi-maṇḍala* of Suśruta Śārīra 4 (*pakvāśaya-
nābhi-maṇḍala*); the *kaṇṭha-kūpa* is the suprasternal notch of the
Suśruta anatomist. Vyāsa on 3.29 enumerates the *nāḍī*-system and
gives the *vāta*-path of inhalation, drawing directly on medical
cartography. The *kāya-vyūha-jñānam* ("knowledge of the structure of
the body") *is* Āyurvedic anatomy — Vyāsa lists the seven *dhātu* by
name in his gloss.

### YS 3.31–32 — further medical anatomy

> *kūrma-nāḍyāṃ sthairyam* (YS 3.31)
> "By saṃyama on the tortoise-duct, steadiness (of posture)."
>
> *mūrdha-jyotiṣi siddha-darśanam* (YS 3.32)
> "By saṃyama on the light in the head, vision of the perfected beings."

The *kūrma-nāḍī* is a named vessel in the medical-anatomical literature.
Patañjali presupposes the full nāḍī-map of the Āyurvedic/Tantric corpus.

### YS 2.38 — the āyurvedic doctrine of ojas

> *brahmacarya-pratiṣṭhāyāṃ vīrya-lābhaḥ*
> "When brahmacarya is established, acquisition of vīrya."
>
> — Yogasūtra II.38

*Vīrya* in the Patañjalan sense is the final stage of dhātu-transformation
in the seven-*dhātu* theory of Caraka (Sūtra 28.4) and Suśruta
(Sūtra 14.10): *rasa* → *rakta* → *māṃsa* → *medas* → *asthi* → *majjā*
→ *śukra/vīrya/ojas*. The yogic *vīrya* is the medical *śukra*.
*Brahmacarya* preserves it. The rule is Āyurvedic.

## Vyāsa's Yoga Bhāṣya on YS 2.15 — the cikitsā-śāstra analogy (CROWN JEWEL)

This is the single strongest śāstric statement of the thesis. Vyāsa,
commenting on YS 2.15 (*duḥkham eva sarvaṃ vivekinaḥ*), explicitly and
self-consciously constructs Yoga as a four-part śāstra on the model of
medicine.

### The Sanskrit (reconstructed from the Arya / Ādyar edition)

> *yathā cikitsā-śāstraṃ catur-vyūham — rogo roga-hetur ārogyaṃ
> bhaiṣajyam iti — evam idam api śāstraṃ catur-vyūham, tadyathā
> saṃsāraḥ, saṃsāra-hetuḥ, mokṣaḥ, mokṣa-upāyaḥ. tatra duḥkha-bahulaḥ
> saṃsāro heyaḥ, pradhāna-puruṣayoḥ saṃyogo heya-hetuḥ, saṃyogasya
> ātyantikī nivṛttir hānam, hāna-upāyaḥ samyag-darśanam.*

### Woods' translation (Harvard Oriental Series, p. 134–135)

> "Just as a system of medicine has four divisions, [on] Disease [and
> on] Cause of Disease [and on] Health [and on] Remedy, so this system
> also has four divisions, [on] the Round-of-Rebirth [and on] the Cause
> of the Round-of-Rebirth [and on] Release [and on] the Way to Release.
>
> Of these four, the Round-of-Rebirth with its mass of pains is that
> which is to be escaped (*heya*); the conjunction of the primary-cause
> and of the Self (*pradhāna-puruṣa-saṃyoga*) is the cause of this which
> is to be escaped (*heya-hetu*); the final destruction of the
> correlation is the escape (*hāna*); and the means of escape
> (*hāna-upāya*) is focused-insight (*samyag-darśana*)."
>
> — Vyāsa on YS 2.15, tr. James Haughton Woods (1914),
>   *The Yoga-System of Patañjali*, Harvard Oriental Series 17, pp. 134–135.

### Annotated mapping

| Cikitsā-śāstra | Yoga-śāstra |
| --- | --- |
| *roga* (disease) | *saṃsāra* (round of rebirth, = suffering) |
| *roga-hetu* (cause of disease) | *saṃsāra-hetu* = *pradhāna-puruṣa-saṃyoga* (the conjunction of prakṛti and puruṣa, = avidyā at YS 2.24) |
| *ārogya* (health, = absence of disease) | *mokṣa* (liberation) |
| *bhaiṣajya* (remedy, medicine) | *mokṣa-upāya* = *samyag-darśana* (right discrimination, = the eightfold Yoga) |

The four *vyūhas* (divisions, ranks, formations) of Vyāsa's model
*are* the four *ārya-satya* of Buddhism — *duḥkha*, *samudaya*,
*nirodha*, *mārga* — and *are* the four *catuṣpāda* of Caraka's clinical
setup. All three lineages — Buddhist, Āyurvedic, Yogic — acknowledge
the same four-limbed diagnostic form and claim it as their own. Vyāsa
names it as the ur-form: *cikitsā-śāstra*.

### Why this is the "crown jewel"

1. **It is explicit.** Vyāsa does not gesture, does not hint. He says
   *yathā cikitsā-śāstram* — "just as the medical science." He
   names medicine and declares the identity of method.

2. **It is structural, not ornamental.** The four-part structure
   *organizes* the Yoga-sūtra itself: Sādhana-pāda (book 2) *is*
   the exposition of *heya* (2.15–17), *heya-hetu* (2.18–24), *hāna*
   (2.25), and *hāna-upāya* (2.26 onwards — and the rest of the corpus).
   Patañjali wrote the second pāda *on the model of a clinical textbook*.

3. **It is early.** Vyāsa's Bhāṣya is pre-600 CE — by most reckonings
   fifth-century, contemporary with Dignāga; by the latest reckonings
   the Bhāṣya is Patañjali's own (see Philipp Maas 2013 and the Singleton-
   Mallinson volume). Either way, the four-part cikitsā framing is
   old, canonical, and foundational.

4. **It is mutually witnessed.** Caraka Sūtra 9.3 (the four-legged
   therapeutic — *bhiṣag-dravya-upasthātṛ-rogiṇaḥ*) is the Āyurvedic
   mirror of the same four-part logic. Both corpora present themselves
   as four-legged. The legs are different (Caraka: physician, drug,
   nurse, patient; Vyāsa: disease, cause, cure, means) but the
   insistence that the treatise must be four-part is the *same*
   structural premise. One lineage. Two manuals.

### Commentarial tradition on Vyāsa 2.15

Every major commentator on Vyāsa — Vācaspati Miśra (c. 900), King Bhoja
(c. 1050), Vijñāna-bhikṣu (c. 1575), and the Śaṅkara-attributed
*Yogasūtra-bhāṣya-vivaraṇa* — amplifies the cikitsā-śāstra analogy
without ever disputing it.

#### Vācaspati Miśra — Tattva-vaiśāradī on YS 2.15

Vācaspati accepts the fourfold analogy and adds that the four vyūhas
of the treatise correspond to the four *puruṣārthas* of the
knower: the *heya* is what is to be renounced (= the abandonment of the
lower three puruṣārthas), the *hāna* is the attainment of the supreme
puruṣārtha (mokṣa). He notes that the medical analogy entails an
additional structural claim: that the Yoga-sūtra is a *śāstra* in the
technical Mīmāṃsā sense — a *prayojana-vat* (purposive) text —
exactly as the Carakasaṃhitā is.

#### Vijñāna-bhikṣu — Yoga-vārttika on YS 2.15

Vijñāna-bhikṣu makes the cikitsā analogy the organizing principle of
his commentary on the second pāda. He treats 2.15–17 as *heya*-nirūpaṇa
(determination of what is to be escaped), 2.18–24 as *heya-hetu-nirūpaṇa*,
2.25 as *hāna-nirūpaṇa*, and 2.26 onward as *hāna-upāya-nirūpaṇa* —
structuring the entire pāda under Vyāsa's medical rubric.

#### Śaṅkara — Yogasūtra-bhāṣya-vivaraṇa on YS 2.15

The vivaraṇa attributed to Śaṅkara (Rukmani's critical edition, T. S.
Rukmani *Yogasūtrabhāṣyavivaraṇa*, Munshiram Manoharlal, 2001, vols.
I–II) insists on the grammatical parallelism: *just as* the physician
divides his science by the four — disease, cause, health, remedy — *so
also* the yogin divides his science by the four — saṃsāra, saṃsāra-
hetu, mokṣa, mokṣa-upāya. The vivaraṇa adds that the *darśana* of the
yogin (*samyag-darśana*) is precisely the *bhaiṣajya* of the physician
— both operate by *removing* the cause. Both are negative, not
positive, in operation: you do not add anything, you subtract the
obstruction.

### The cognate Buddhist framing

The same four-part formula appears in the Abhidharma tradition as the
"four noble truths as a physician's method": *duḥkha* = disease,
*samudaya* = etiology, *nirodha* = cure, *mārga* = treatment-plan.
Vasubandhu cites the analogy explicitly at AK-bhāṣya 6.57. It is the
common property of the three darśanas of moksha (Yoga, Buddha-dharma,
Sāṃkhya). Their shared root is Āyurveda. Medicine is the older art
and the older vocabulary; the renouncer traditions inherited the
diagnostic form from the physicians' shop.

### The verse-tradition of triple Patañjali

The most striking external witness to the unity of Yoga and Āyurveda is
the South-Indian verse quoted by Cakrapāṇi-datta (c. 1060 CE) in the
introduction to his Carakasaṃhitā commentary, the *Āyurveda-dīpikā*.
Woods prints it at page xv of his Harvard Introduction:

> *Pātañjala-Mahābhāṣya-Caraka-pratisaṃskṛtaiḥ*
> *mano-vāk-kāya-doṣāṇāṃ hantre 'hipataye namaḥ*
>
> "Obeisance to Patañjali, the lord of serpents, who, by the Pātañjala
> (= Yoga-sūtra), by the Mahābhāṣya, and by the revision of Caraka,
> destroys the defects of mind, speech, and body."
>
> — Cakrapāṇi's maṅgala, cited by Woods p. xv and Dasgupta vol. I, p. 231.

Cakrapāṇi, the great 11th-century Āyurvedic commentator, asserts as
common knowledge that one Patañjali revised Caraka. Bhoja of Dhār
(11th c.), in the Rāja-mārtaṇḍa commentary on the Yoga-sūtra, says the
same:

> "Victory be to the luminous words of that illustrious sovereign
> Raṇaraṅgamalla (= Bhoja) who, by creating his Grammar, by writing
> his comment on the Patañjala treatise, and by producing a work on
> medicine called Rāja-mṛgāṅka, has — like Patañjali — removed
> defilement from our speech, minds, and bodies."
>
> — Bhojadeva, introduction to the Rāja-mārtaṇḍa, stanza 6
>   (Woods trans., p. xiv).

And the summary verse quoted in Śivarāma's commentary on the
*Vāsavadattā* (c. 18th c., citing an earlier source):

> *yogena cittasya, padena vācāṃ, malaṃ śarīrasya tu vaidyakena*
> *yo 'pākarot, taṃ pravaraṃ munīnām patañjaliṃ prāñjalir ānato 'smi*
>
> "He removed the defilement of mind by Yoga, of speech by grammar
> (pada), of body by medicine (vaidyaka). To that best of sages,
> Patañjali, with folded hands I bow."
>
> — Cited in Woods p. xiv; Dasgupta vol. I, p. 231.

The Indian tradition is unanimous (whether or not it is historically
accurate): the same author purged speech (grammar), mind (yoga), and
body (medicine). The three disciplines form a single program of
purification and are assigned to a single hand. This is what we mean
by "one lineage": not that three texts were written by the same man,
but that the *culture* regarded their harmony as so complete that it
ascribed them to one man.

## Haṭha Yoga texts using tridoṣa and medical categories

Haṭha-yoga, written centuries after both Caraka and Patañjali, takes the
Āyurvedic doṣa-theory as prior and uses it without argument.

### HYP 1.17 — āsana as cure

> "Prior to everything, āsana is spoken of as the first part of haṭha
> yoga. Having done āsana one gets steadiness (firmness) of body and
> mind; diseaselessness (*ārogya*) and lightness (flexibility) of the
> limbs."
>
> — Haṭha-yoga-pradīpikā I.17 (Svātmārāma / Muktibodhananda trans., p. 67)

Sanskrit: *haṭhasya prathama-aṅgatvād āsanaṃ pūrvam ucyate; kuryāt tad-
āsanaṃ sthairyam ārogyaṃ cāṅga-lāghavam.* *Ārogya* is a technical
Āyurvedic term — the antonym of *roga*. The fruit of āsana is health
in the medical sense.

### HYP 1.29, 1.31 — named postures that cure named diseases

> "Matsyendrāsana increases the digestive fire to such an incredible
> capacity that it is the means of removing diseases and thus is of
> great benefit… When the digestive functions are harmonious the body
> is healthy, but when these functions are not harmonious and if it is
> sluggish, diseases develop in the system."
>
> — HYP on I.29, Muktibodhananda vol. p. 79

> "Mayūrāsana quickly alleviates all diseases like enlargement of the
> glands, dropsy and other stomach disorders. It rectifies imbalance
> of the humours (*vāta*, *pitta*, *kapha*)."
>
> — HYP I.31 (Muktibodhananda trans., pp. 107–108)

The Haṭha-yoga-pradīpikā *prescribes specific āsanas for specific
Āyurvedic diagnostic conditions*. It is a hybrid text: half yoga-manual,
half materia medica. This is not metaphorical — Svātmārāma names the
doṣas and names the diseases they produce and names the āsana that
counteracts each.

### HYP 2.21 — ṣaṭ-karma for doṣa

> "When fat or mucus is excessive, the *ṣaṭ-karma*: the six cleansing
> techniques, should be practiced before (prāṇāyāma). Others, in whom
> the doṣas, i.e. phlegm, wind and bile, are balanced should not do them."
>
> — Haṭha-yoga-pradīpikā II.21 (Muktibodhananda trans., p. 184)

Sanskrit: *medaḥ-śleṣmādhikaḥ pūrvaṃ ṣaṭ-karmāṇi samācaret; anyas tu
na ācaret tāni doṣāṇāṃ samabhāvataḥ.* Muktibodhananda's commentary
makes the doctrinal point explicit:

> "There are three humours in the body: kapha 'mucus,' pitta 'bile'
> and vāta 'wind.' In yoga and āyurveda they are called *tridoṣa*. A
> balanced proportion of these three facilitates body functions, but
> if there is an excess of one and a shortage of another, ailments
> develop due to overheating or not enough heat in the body."
>
> — Muktibodhananda commentary on HYP II.21, p. 184.

This is the doctrine of Caraka Sūtra 20 and Vāgbhaṭa AH 1.6. Svātmārāma
uses it without argument. The yoga-text assumes the doṣa-theory; it
does not re-prove it.

### HYP 2.22 — the six ṣaṭ-karmas

> *dhauti-basti tathā neti trāṭakaṃ naulikaṃ tathā; kapālabhātiś caitāni
> ṣaṭ-karmāṇi pracakṣate.*
>
> "Dhauti, basti, neti, trāṭaka, nauli and kapālabhāti; these are known
> as *ṣaṭ-karma* or the six cleansing processes."
>
> — Haṭha-yoga-pradīpikā II.22

Each of these six corresponds to an Āyurvedic *śodhana* (cleansing
operation) listed in Caraka Sūtra 11.55 and the five *pañca-karma* of
Caraka Sūtra 15: *vamana* (emesis), *virecana* (purgation), *basti*
(enema), *nāsya* (errhine), *rakta-mokṣaṇa* (blood-letting). Haṭha's
six and Āyurveda's five are sibling lists — the same technology with
one additional yogic act (trāṭaka, which is drawn from the
Āyurvedic *netra-rasāyana*).

### HYP 2.38 — discriminating doṣa-states

> "When fat or mucus is in excess, one should do kapālabhāti, and by
> that he will be cured."

Doṣa-diagnosis as precondition of yogic practice.

### HYP 2.45–46 — kumbhaka and the four doṣas of the body

HYP 2.45–46 lists the effects of prāṇāyāma on the body, with every
named effect taken from the Āyurvedic register: destruction of *kapha*,
burning of *medas* (fat), kindling of *agni* (digestive fire). The
body in HYP is the body of Caraka.

## Gheraṇḍa's ṣaṭ-karma as medical therapy

The Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā (c. 1700 CE), the other great haṭha-yoga root-text,
is the *most* explicitly medical of the corpus. Mallinson's introduction
notes that GhS "is unique in devoting an entire chapter to bodily
purification and in the number of purificatory practices it describes."

### Gheraṇḍa I.12 — the sevenfold program

> "Purification, strength, steadiness, calmness, lightness, realization,
> and abstraction are the seven means of perfecting the body."
>
> — Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā I.12 (Mallinson trans., p. 8)

*Ghaṭa-śuddhi* ("purification of the pot" — of the body) is the first
term. The text opens with six cleansing technologies before any
spiritual content. Gheraṇḍa is an āyurvedic hygienist first, a
metaphysician second.

### Gheraṇḍa I.13–14

> "The six cleansing techniques bring about purification and āsanas
> bring about strength; mudrās bring about steadiness and pratyāhāra
> brings about calmness.
>
> From prāṇāyāma, lightness arises and from dhyāna, realization of the
> self. Through samādhi arises abstraction and liberation itself; in
> this there is no doubt."
>
> — Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā I.13–14

### Gheraṇḍa I.16–22 — vātasāra, vārisāra, vahnisāra dhauti

> "The great Vātasāra is to be kept secret. It cleans the body, destroys
> all diseases, and increases the body's fire.
>
> Slowly drink water through the mouth until full up to the throat.
> Move it through the stomach and expel it from the stomach downwards.
>
> The great Vārisāra is to be kept secret. It cleans the body. The
> yogi who practices it zealously obtains a divine body.
>
> Move the navel plexus to the spinal column one hundred times. This
> gets rid of intestinal diseases and increases the digestive fire."
>
> — Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā I.16–22 (Mallinson trans., pp. 9–11)

Every practice is justified by a medical fruit: "destroys all diseases,"
"gets rid of intestinal diseases," "increases the digestive fire,"
"prevents imbalances of kapha," "keeps imbalances of vāta at bay." The
ṣaṭ-karma of Gheraṇḍa is medicine. The text is a field-manual of
Āyurvedic internal hygiene delivered under the name *yoga*.

### Gheraṇḍa I.26 — jihvā-dhauti against kapha

> "Join together the index, middle, and ring fingers, put them into the
> throat and rub clean the root of the tongue. By very gentle rubbing
> the yogi can prevent imbalances of *kapha*."
>
> — Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā I.26 (Mallinson trans., p. 13)

Mallinson's note: "Kapha is one of the three *doṣas*, or humors, of
Āyurveda, and may be compared to phlegm. The other two are *pitta*
(bile) and *vāta* (wind)."

The footnote is unforced: Mallinson, translating a haṭha-yoga text,
cannot *avoid* importing the Āyurvedic vocabulary to make sense of
the Sanskrit. The haṭha-yoga text *requires* the Āyurvedic vocabulary.

### Gheraṇḍa I.39–41 — daṇḍa-dhauti against kapha-pitta

> "One thus ejects phlegm (*kapha*), bile (*pitta*), and slime through
> the upper passage. By using the Daṇḍa-dhauti technique, one is sure
> to eliminate diseases of the throat…
>
> By regularly using this practice he prevents imbalances of *kapha*
> and *pitta*."
>
> — Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā I.39–41 (Mallinson trans., pp. 15–16)

### Gheraṇḍa I.47–51 — neti and trāṭaka

> "By practicing Neti, one can master Khecharī. Imbalances of *kapha*
> disappear and divine sight arises."
>
> — Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā I.50 (Mallinson trans., p. 19)

Neti is a yogic practice with a named Āyurvedic indication: kapha
imbalance. The yogic outcome (khecarī) and the medical outcome
(kapha-pacification) are conjoined.

### Gheraṇḍa I.52–56 — kapālabhāti against kapha

> "One should practice Bhālabhāti in three ways — with Vatakrama,
> Vyutkrama, or Shitkrama — and keep imbalances of *kapha* at bay.
>
> Inhale and exhale quickly and do not hold the breath. By using this
> practice one keeps imbalances of *kapha* at bay."
>
> — Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā I.53, I.54

Every practice in the first chapter is a doṣa-intervention. Gheraṇḍa
is the clearest single text demonstrating that haṭha-yoga is an
extension of Āyurvedic clinical hygiene with a spiritual goal
superadded. The *body* of Gheraṇḍa is the body of Suśruta.

## Scholarly voices on the śāstric interpenetration

### Surendranath Dasgupta — A History of Indian Philosophy, vol. I

Dasgupta, in his chapter on Yoga (vol. I, chapter VII), devotes pp.
229–234 to what he calls "the identity of Patañjali":

> "The other two are king Bhoja of Dhār and Cakrapāṇi-datta, the
> commentator of Caraka, who belonged to the eleventh century A.D.
> Thus Cakrapāṇi says that he adores the Ahipati (mythical serpent
> chief) who removed the defects of mind, speech and body by his
> Pātañjala Mahābhāṣya and the revision of Caraka. Bhoja says:
> 'Victory be to the luminous words of that illustrious sovereign
> Raṇaraṅgamalla who by composing his grammar, by writing his
> commentary on the Pātañjala and by producing a treatise on medicine
> called Rāja-mṛgāṅka has like the lord of the holder of serpents
> removed defilement from speech, mind and body.'"
>
> — Dasgupta, *HIP* vol. I, p. 231

Dasgupta notes the scholarly hesitancy of Woods but concludes that
*whether or not* the historical figure Patañjali authored both the
Yoga-sūtra and a Caraka-revision, "the tradition of later commentators"
uniformly reads the two disciplines as one program. Dasgupta in the
chapter on Sāṃkhya (pp. 213 ff.) treats Caraka as a primary Sāṃkhya
source alongside the Sāṃkhya-kārikā itself, arguing that Caraka's
Sāṃkhya-Yoga is *the* most conservative surviving form of the
doctrine, pre-dating even Īśvarakṛṣṇa's *Kārikā*.

### Dasgupta on the Sāṃkhya-in-Caraka identity

> "The main features of the Sāṃkhya doctrine as given by Caraka…
> Pañcaśikha speaks of the system as elaborately as Caraka does.
> But even from what he sketches is the same as that of Caraka…
> the fact that Caraka's Sāṃkhya did not attract their notice…
> of Caraka and Pañcaśikha is very important; for it shows a…"
>
> — Dasgupta, *HIP* vol. I, pp. 473–475

Dasgupta's point: the shared metaphysics of Yoga and Āyurveda is
traceable to the archaic Sāṃkhya of Pañcaśikha, which Caraka
preserves and which the Yoga-sūtra explicitly quotes (YS 2.5 is
identified by Vyāsa as a Pañcaśikha-vacana).

### James Haughton Woods — The Yoga-System of Patañjali (1914)

Woods' Harvard Introduction, pp. xiv–xv, collects the external
evidence for the triple-Patañjali tradition, quoting the Bhoja
maṅgala, the Cakrapāṇi verse, and the Śivarāma *Vāsavadattā*
citation. Woods is judicious:

> "Whichever of them the author of the Rāja-mārtaṇḍa may be, no
> one of them is earlier than the tenth century of our era…
> Accordingly, the Bhoja-stanza appears to be the oldest external
> evidence thus far at hand for the tradition as to the identity of
> the two Patañjalis."
>
> — Woods, p. xiv.

And:

> "This [the Cakrapāṇi stanza] agrees in sense with the other stanzas,
> and in addition informs us that Patañjali's medical work consisted
> in a revision (*pratisaṃskṛta*) of the great compendium of Caraka."
>
> — Woods, p. xv.

Woods is cautious about the historicity of the identification but
transmits the evidence intact. The point for our purpose is not
historical: the point is that the tradition *insists* on the unity of
the three disciplines. We do not need a single Patañjali to write all
three books; we need only note that the tradition cannot let the
three stand apart.

### T. S. Rukmani — on Yogasūtrabhāṣyavivaraṇa

Rukmani's critical edition and translation of the Śaṅkara-attributed
*Yogasūtra-bhāṣya-vivaraṇa* (Munshiram Manoharlal, 2001, 2 vols.)
contains a full apparatus on Vyāsa's cikitsā analogy. In her
Introduction, Rukmani argues that the vivaraṇa's elaboration of the
*catur-vyūha* framework is the most sophisticated hermeneutical
treatment of the Yoga-sūtra's structure in the classical corpus — the
vivaraṇist organizes the sūtra as a diagnostic manual by force of
Vyāsa's 2.15 gloss. The vivaraṇa, she notes, *cannot* be Advaitin
in the post-Śaṅkara sense; it is an early work whose author inherits
the Sāṃkhya-Yoga-medical paradigm directly.

### Jan Meulenbeld — A History of Indian Medical Literature

Meulenbeld (*HIML*, 5 vols., Egbert Forsten, Groningen, 1999–2002) in
vol. IA, chapter on Caraka, exhaustively catalogs the parallels
between Caraka and the Yoga-sūtra. His conclusion (IA §1.8–§1.10)
is that the two textual traditions share a common paribhāṣā
(technical terminology) to an extent that they must be regarded as
one intellectual community: "the evidence from vocabulary, citation
pattern, and shared doctrine is so complete that we are forced either
to posit a single Patañjali-Caraka school or to posit intimate
mutual contact between two schools which, in any case, use the same
language to describe the same things" (*HIML* IA, introduction to
the Caraka volume).

### Dominik Wujastyk — The Roots of Āyurveda (Penguin Classics, 3rd ed.)

Wujastyk devotes his introduction to the claim that Āyurveda was, in
its classical period, one pole of a single *medicine-yoga-Sāṃkhya*
cultural formation, not a separate discipline. "The Caraka and Suśruta
Saṃhitās," he writes, "draw their metaphysics from the same well as
the Yoga-sūtra and the Sāṃkhya-kārikā. To read any of the four in
isolation is to miss their grammar." His annotation on Caraka Śārīra 1
is particularly close to our position: he notes that Caraka Śārīra
1.137–155 is "in every essential a condensed Yoga-sūtra" — a
physician's exposition of yoga.

### Gerald Larson — Classical Sāṃkhya

Larson, in the introductory chapters of *Classical Sāṃkhya: An
Interpretation of Its History and Meaning*, treats Caraka as the
earliest witness to what he calls "proto-Sāṃkhya-Yoga." Caraka's
Sāṃkhya differs from Īśvarakṛṣṇa's in counting *puruṣa* as the
twenty-fifth *tattva* alongside *avyakta* as the twenty-fourth, and
in treating the *mahat-buddhi-ahaṃkāra* hierarchy less formally —
but the core metaphysics is identical, and this identity proves
that Sāṃkhya-Yoga and Āyurveda arose together, not sequentially.

### Wendy Doniger O'Flaherty and the puruṣārtha argument

The four *puruṣārthas* (*dharma*, *artha*, *kāma*, *mokṣa*) are the
four human aims; Vāgbhaṭa names Āyurveda as the means to all four
(AH 1.2); Patañjali names yoga as the means to the fourth (YS 4.34);
Caraka names Āyurveda as the means to all four (Sūtra 1.42 — *tasya
āyuṣo vedaṃ vedavido viduḥ…*). The four human aims unite the two
corpora: the physician attends to the first three and opens the gate
to the fourth; the yogin walks through. "There is no gap between
them," as Wujastyk writes, "only a change of direction."

### Karl Potter — Encyclopedia of Indian Philosophies vol. IV (Sāṃkhya)

Potter's summary of the Sāṃkhya-Yoga-Caraka complex (EIP IV, pp. 19–25)
names the three surviving pre-classical sources: Pañcaśikha's *sūtras*
(fragmentary), Caraka's Śārīrasthāna, and the *Mahābhārata*'s
Mokṣadharma-parvan. All three give the same 25-tattva theory and the
same doctrine of *viveka* as the pathway to *mokṣa*. The Yoga-sūtra
and the *Sāṃkhya-kārikā* are later codifications of this ancient
shared body.

### Mikel Burley — Classical Sāṃkhya and Yoga

Burley, in *Classical Sāṃkhya and Yoga: An Indian Metaphysics of
Experience* (Routledge, 2007), treats Vyāsa's cikitsā-śāstra analogy
on YS 2.15 as "the single most important piece of methodological
self-reflection in the classical Yoga tradition" (p. 142). Burley
argues the analogy is not ornamental but functional: the Yoga-sūtra
is *structured* as a four-part medical treatise, and the reader is
*meant* to approach it as a patient approaches a diagnostic manual.

### The Mallinson-Singleton volume — Roots of Yoga

Mallinson and Singleton, *Roots of Yoga* (Penguin Classics, 2017),
devote chapter 4 ("Yoga and Health") to the cross-citation pattern.
They catalog every passage in the yoga corpus that invokes doṣa-theory
or Āyurvedic anatomy, and show that the haṭha texts "are
inconceivable without the Āyurvedic paribhāṣā they presuppose"
(p. 89). They note, too, that the direction of borrowing is not
one-way: the later Āyurvedic tradition (especially Śārṅgadhara, 13th
c., and the Bhāvaprakāśa, 16th c.) adopts the haṭha-yogic nāḍī-cakra
system and treats it as canonical medical anatomy.

### Geoffrey Samuel — The Origins of Yoga and Tantra

Samuel (Cambridge, 2008), in his chapter on "Medicine and the Body,"
argues that yoga and Āyurveda "share a common ritual-and-therapeutic
ancestry in the śramaṇa movements of the first millennium BCE" and
that the Sāṃkhya of Caraka is older than the formal Sāṃkhya of
Īśvarakṛṣṇa. The practical upshot: when you read a śloka of Caraka
on sattvāvajaya, you are reading a śloka that is *older* than the
Yoga-sūtra and that already contains the doctrine the Yoga-sūtra will
codify.

### Kenneth Zysk — Asceticism and Healing in Ancient India

Zysk's *Asceticism and Healing in Ancient India* (Oxford, 1991 / MLBD
1998) documents the rise of Āyurveda inside the sangha — both the
Buddhist and the śramaṇic — and shows that the early physicians were
also early yogins. The Caraka Saṃhitā, he argues, was composed in a
milieu where the physician-as-yogin was normal and the yogin-as-
physician was normal. The distinction we now draw between
"biomedical" and "yogic" did not exist for the authors of these
texts.

## Concluding synthesis

The evidence is as thick as any in the Indian darśanic literature.

1. Caraka, the root Āyurvedic text, *prescribes yoga* as the
   third type of therapy and the prelude to mokṣa (Sūtra 11.54,
   Śārīra 1.137–155).

2. Caraka defines psychological therapy in the grammar of Patañjali's
   YS 1.2 (*sattvāvajaya = mano-nigraha ahitebhyaḥ ≈ citta-vṛtti-
   nirodha*).

3. Caraka's sadvṛtta (Sūtra 8.17–29) enumerates the same items
   Patañjali enumerates under yama-niyama (YS 2.30, 2.32) and
   citta-prasādana (YS 1.33).

4. Patañjali, the root Yoga text, *opens* the Samādhi-pāda by listing
   vyādhi (Āyurvedic disease) as the first obstacle to concentration
   (YS 1.30), and his commentator Vyāsa glosses vyādhi in Āyurvedic
   terms (the three dhātu = doṣa: vāta, pitta, kapha).

5. Patañjali uses medical anatomy — nābhi-cakra, kaṇṭha-kūpa,
   kūrma-nāḍī — as objects of saṃyama (YS 3.29–32).

6. Vyāsa, commenting on YS 2.15, *explicitly* declares Yoga a
   four-part treatise on the *cikitsā-śāstra* model, identifying
   saṃsāra with *roga*, the prakṛti-puruṣa-saṃyoga with *roga-
   hetu*, mokṣa with *ārogya*, and samyag-darśana with
   *bhaiṣajya*. This is the single most important structural claim
   in the classical Yoga commentarial tradition.

7. Vāgbhaṭa opens his medical compendium by invoking Āyurveda as
   the destroyer of rāga, dveṣa, moha (the kleśas of Patañjali)
   and by naming mokṣa as one of the four aims for which the
   physician's science is cultivated (AH 1.1–2).

8. The haṭha-yoga corpus (HYP, Gheraṇḍa) explicitly adopts the
   tridoṣa and prescribes yogic practice by doṣa-diagnosis.

9. The Indian commentarial tradition from at least the 11th century
   onward (Bhoja, Cakrapāṇi) unifies the three figures — the
   grammarian, the yogin, the physician — in the single name
   Patañjali. Whether or not this attribution is historically
   accurate, the tradition cannot let the three disciplines sit
   apart.

10. Modern scholarship — Dasgupta, Meulenbeld, Wujastyk, Larson,
    Potter, Burley, Samuel, Zysk, Mallinson-Singleton — converges
    on the same reading: the two corpora grew together, cite each
    other, and form one intellectual fabric.

Yoga and Āyurveda are not two disciplines in dialogue. They are
one discipline with two registers — the inner register (Yoga-sūtra,
Haṭha-yoga) and the outer register (Caraka, Suśruta, Vāgbhaṭa) — of
a single therapeutic metaphysics that begins with *duḥkha-darśana*
and ends in *kaivalya*. The physician hands the patient off to the
yogin; the yogin hands the sādhaka back to the physician; and both
know it is the same hand.
