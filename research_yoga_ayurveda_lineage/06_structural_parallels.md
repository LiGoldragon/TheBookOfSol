# Structural Parallels — Two Eight-Fold Disciplines of the Whole Life

Claim. Patañjali's aṣṭāṅga-yoga and the Āyurvedic discipline (dinacaryā, ṛtucaryā, sadvṛtta, āhāra-vidhi, cikitsā-catuṣpāda, pañca-karma, puruṣārtha) are not loose cousins. They are a single discipline, spoken twice — once to the renunciate in the forest, once to the householder at home. Limb for limb, verse for verse, the correspondence is nearly lexical. The same ethical atoms (ahiṁsā, satya, asteya, brahmacarya, aparigraha, śauca, santoṣa, tapas, svādhyāya, īśvara-praṇidhāna) appear in both. The same daily acts (brāhma-muhūrta waking, jihvā-nirlekhana, danta-dhāvana, nasya, abhyaṅga, snāna, dhyāna) appear in both. The same therapeutic modalities (vamana, virecana, basti, nasya, rakta-mokṣaṇa on one side; dhauti, basti, neti, trāṭaka, nauli, kapāla-bhāti on the other) appear in both. The same four-pāda diagnostic grid (roga, roga-hetu, ārogya, bhaiṣajya) is used by Vyāsa to define yoga itself. And the keystone verse of all of Āyurveda — Caraka's *sama-doṣaḥ samāgniś ca sama-dhātu-mala-kriyaḥ* — defines health (svastha) as the exact condition Patañjali calls samādhi: *prasanna-ātmendriya-manāḥ*, the clear, settled, serene self-senses-and-mind. Health is samādhi. Samādhi is health. This is the unity the whole research project turns on.

## Patañjali's aṣṭāṅga-yoga — the eight limbs

### The root sūtra (YS 2.29)

**Sanskrit** (Bryant, following the Benares text):
> *yama-niyamāsana-prāṇāyāma-pratyāhāra-dhāraṇā-dhyāna-samādhayo 'ṣṭāv aṅgāni*
> (*Yoga Sūtra* II.29)

**Translation (Bryant)**:
> "The eight limbs are abstentions, observances, posture, breath control, disengagement of the senses, concentration, meditation, and absorption."
> — *The Yoga Sūtras of Patañjali*, Edwin Bryant, trans. & comm., on YS II.29.

Bryant observes that the notion of yoga having *aṅgas*, limbs, "is derived in all likelihood from the older *Veda-aṅgas*. The successful performance of Vedic ritual in the later Vedic period was seen as dependent on the mastery of the six limbs of the Vedic ritual: phonetics, meter, grammar, etymology, astronomy, and ritual (mentioned in, e.g., *Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad* I.1.5). Likewise, the goal of yoga expressed in I.2 is dependent on the successful performance of the eight auxiliary limbs of yoga indicated in this verse" (Bryant on YS II.29). The word *aṅga* — limb, auxiliary, ingredient — is the same word Caraka uses to title the eight branches of medicine (aṣṭāṅga-āyurveda: kāyacikitsā, śalya, śālākya, kaumāra-bhṛtya, bhūta-vidyā, agada-tantra, rasāyana, vājīkaraṇa) and the same word Vāgbhaṭa uses in the title of his compendium *Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya* — the "heart of the eight limbs." Both systems call themselves the eight-limbed discipline. Both systems are fully whole only when all eight limbs are present together.

### The five yamas (YS 2.30)

**Sanskrit** (Bryant):
> *ahiṁsā-satyāsteya-brahmacaryāparigrahā yamāḥ*
> (*Yoga Sūtra* II.30)

**Translation (Bryant)**:
> "The yamas are nonviolence, truthfulness, refrainment from stealing, celibacy, and renunciation of [unnecessary] possessions."

Vyāsa takes ahiṁsā as the root of the other yamas. "Just as the footprints of an elephant cover the footprints of all other creatures, says Vijñānabhikṣu, so does ahiṁsā cover all the other yamas. According to Vyāsa, the goal of the other yamas is to achieve ahiṁsā and enhance it, and he quotes an unidentified verse stating that one continues to undertake more and more vows and austerities for the sole purpose of purifying ahiṁsā" (Bryant on YS II.30).

### Universality of the yamas (YS 2.31)

**Sanskrit**:
> *jāti-deśa-kāla-samayānavacchinnāḥ sārva-bhaumā mahā-vratam*
> (*Yoga Sūtra* II.31)

**Translation (Bryant)**:
> "[These yamas] are considered the great vow. They are not exempted by one's class, place, time, or circumstance. They are universal."

Relevance: Patañjali is conspicuously emphatic. The yamas are *mahāvrata*, the "great vow," *sārva-bhauma*, "universal across all terrestrial ground." No exemption by caste, no exemption by place, no exemption by time, no exemption by circumstance. Compare below with Caraka's sadvṛtta, which is framed as the code for all *who wish to remain healthy and long-lived* and is similarly applied without exemption. The first limb of yoga and the first limb of the Āyurvedic code for the healthy both refuse to grant that any class of persons is exempt from the ethical atoms.

### The five niyamas (YS 2.32)

**Sanskrit** (Bryant):
> *śauca-santoṣa-tapaḥ-svādhyāyesvara-praṇidhānāni niyamāḥ*
> (*Yoga Sūtra* II.32)

**Translation (Bryant)**:
> "The observances are cleanliness, contentment, austerity, study [of scripture], and devotion to God."

Bryant, on the ingredients: "Vyāsa divides the first item on Patañjali's list, cleanliness, śauca, into external and internal types. External cleanliness pertains to the body and consists (in the premodern world of our commentators and rural India today) of cleaning with water and clay ... and of ingesting pure foodstuff (which, Vācaspati Miśra notes, should be limited in quantity). In terms of ingestion, Hariharānanda reiterates that meat and intoxication cause the mind to be agitated and stimulated — they incite rajas and tamas — and yoga requires a steady and peaceful mind. Therefore, a yogī never imbibes such substances. **He quotes Caraka, a traditional authority on āyurveda**: 'Whatever is good or most desired in this life or the next is attained by intense concentration of the mind. Alcohol creates a disturbance in the mind. Those blinded by addiction to alcohol lose sight of their best self-interest'" (Bryant on YS II.32).

That is the deepest possible citation for our thesis: the standard commentator on the *Yoga Sūtra* quotes *Caraka* to explain a *niyama*. The commentator does not reach for a yoga text to explain a yoga text; he reaches for a medical text. This is because yoga and medicine are one discipline.

### Third limb: āsana (YS 2.46–48)

**Sanskrit** (Bryant):
> *sthira-sukham āsanam* (II.46)
> *prayatna-śaithilyānanta-samāpattibhyām* (II.47)
> *tato dvandvānabhighātaḥ* (II.48)

**Translation (Bryant)**:
> "Posture should be steady and comfortable" (II.46).
> "[Such posture should be attained] by the relaxation of effort and by absorption in the infinite" (II.47).
> "From this, one is not afflicted by the dualities of the opposites" (II.48).

Bryant: "Essentially, posture is a limb of the actual goal of yoga to the extent that it allows the meditator to sit firmly, *sthira*, and comfortably, *sukha*, for meditation. Indeed, *āsana* literally means seat. … In other words, āsana's relevance and function for the classical Yoga tradition are to train the body so that it does not disturb or distract the mind of the yogī in any way when sitting in meditation" (Bryant on YS II.46). Patañjali gives āsana only three sūtras out of nearly two hundred — a point often forgotten by modern schools that identify "yoga" with posture alone. In Vyāsa's bhāṣya he lists eleven seats: padmāsana, vīrāsana, bhadrāsana, svastikāsana, daṇḍāsana, sopāśraya, paryaṅka, krauñca-niṣadana, hasti-niṣadana, uṣṭra-niṣadana, sama-saṁsthāna, and "etc." (Bryant on YS II.46). All of them are sitting postures. The fruit of āsana is *dvandvānabhighāta* — being unafflicted by the pairs (hot/cold, hunger/thirst, pleasure/pain) — which is also the hallmark of the *svastha* in the Āyurvedic sense.

### Fourth limb: prāṇāyāma (YS 2.49–53)

**Sanskrit**:
> *tasmin sati śvāsa-praśvāsayor gati-vicchedaḥ prāṇāyāmaḥ* (II.49)
> *bāhyābhyantara-stambha-vṛttiḥ deśa-kāla-saṅkhyābhiḥ paridṛṣṭo dīrgha-sūkṣmaḥ* (II.50)
> *bāhyābhyantara-viṣayākṣepī caturthaḥ* (II.51)
> *tataḥ kṣīyate prakāśāvaraṇam* (II.52)
> *dhāraṇāsu ca yogyatā manasaḥ* (II.53)

**Translation (Bryant)**:
> "When that [āsana] is accomplished, prāṇāyāma, breath control, [follows]. This consists of the regulation of the incoming and outgoing breaths" (II.49).
> "[Prāṇāyāmaḥ] manifests as external, internal, and restrained movements [of breath]. These are drawn out and subtle in accordance to place, time, and number" (II.50).
> "The fourth [type of prāṇāyāma] surpasses the limits of the external and the internal" (II.51).
> "Then, the covering of the illumination [of knowledge] is weakened" (II.52).
> "Additionally, the mind becomes fit for concentration" (II.53).

Bryant on II.52: "He quotes a verse that 'there is no greater ascetic practice than prāṇāyāma, from which defects are purified and the light of knowledge shines forth.' Manu, too, states that from the performance of prāṇāyāma accompanied by the repetition of the *oṁ* mantra, 'the impurities of the sensory powers are burnt away, just as the defiling impurities of metal ore are burnt away in the heat of the furnace' (Manu VI.72)" (Bryant on YS II.52).

The metaphor is metallurgical: prāṇāyāma as a smelting operation on the nervous system. The Āyurvedic equivalent is nasya — therapeutic nasal evacuation, to which Vāgbhaṭa devotes significant space in dinacaryā.

### Fifth limb: pratyāhāra (YS 2.54–55)

**Sanskrit**:
> *svaviṣayāsamprayoge cittasya svarūpānukāra ivendriyāṇāṁ pratyāhāraḥ* (II.54)
> *tataḥ paramā vaśyatendriyāṇām* (II.55)

**Translation (Bryant)**:
> "Pratyāhāra, withdrawal from sense objects, occurs when the senses do not come into contact with their respective sense objects. It corresponds, as it were, to the nature of the mind [when it is withdrawn from the sense objects]" (II.54).
> "From this comes the highest control of the senses" (II.55).

Note the language: *paramā vaśyatā indriyāṇām* — the highest control over the senses. This is *exactly* what Caraka titles Sūtra 8 (indriyopakramaṇīya, "on the control of the senses"), whose sadvṛtta section immediately follows.

### Sixth, seventh, eighth: dhāraṇā, dhyāna, samādhi (YS 3.1–3)

**Sanskrit**:
> *deśa-bandhaś cittasya dhāraṇā* (III.1)
> *tatra pratyayaika-tānatā dhyānam* (III.2)
> *tad evārtha-mātra-nirbhāsaṁ svarūpa-śūnyam iva samādhiḥ* (III.3)

**Translation (Bryant)**:
> "Concentration is the fixing of the mind in one place" (III.1).
> "Meditation is the one-pointedness of the mind on one image" (III.2).
> "Samādhi is when that same dhyāna shines forth as the object alone and [the mind] is devoid, as it were, of its own [reflective] nature" (III.3).

Bryant on the progression: "A gradual progression from the external to the internal throughout the limbs. The yamas are the most external, proscribing relations with other beings … The niyamas deal more internally with one's own practices … Āsanas focus exclusively on one's personal body, and prāṇāyāma, more internal still, on the breath within the body. Pratyāhāra continues this progression of internalization by going still deeper within by withdrawing consciousness itself from the senses. This process of consecutive stages of internalization continues throughout the remaining three limbs" (Bryant on YS III.1). The eight limbs are a spiral from outside to inside. So, as we will see, is dinacaryā — which moves from waking and toileting, through tooth-cleaning, through eye and nose, through oil, through bath, through meditation, through sleep — always from gross to subtle.

## Caraka's sadvṛtta and Patañjali's yama-niyama — the shared ethical atoms

Caraka dedicates *Sūtrasthāna* 8 ("indriyopakramaṇīya-adhyāya" — on the control of the sense-organs) to the code of good conduct for the person who wishes to remain healthy. The chapter's framing verse states the thesis in five words:

**Caraka Sūtra 8.17** (P. V. Sharma, trans.):
> "That, if practised, fulfils two objectives simultaneously — **health and control over sense organs**. O Agniveśa! I will explain that code of good conduct fully."
> — *Caraka Saṁhitā* Sūtrasthāna 8.17, Priya Vrat Sharma, trans. (Chaukhamba, 2014 reprint), vol. I, pp. 56–57.

The phrase "control over sense organs" is *indriyopakrama*, which is what Patañjali calls *paramā vaśyatā indriyāṇām* at YS 2.55. Same phrase, same concept, same discipline. Caraka treats it as the hinge on which health turns.

Read the sadvṛtta list (Sūtra 8.18–29) against Patañjali's yama-niyama. The overlap is not approximate. It is nearly lexical.

### Ahiṁsā

Patañjali (YS 2.30): *ahiṁsā* — nonviolence.

Caraka (Sū. 8.18, P. V. Sharma): "One should behave like kith and kin to all living beings, pacify the angry, console the frightened, help the poor, be truthful, peaceful, tolerant of other's harsh words, remover of intolerance."

Caraka (Sū. 8.19): "One should not be inclined to enmity ... should not be vicious even to sinner."

Vāgbhaṭa's summary at *Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya* Sū. 2.46 (Śrīkāṇṭha Mūrthy, trans.): "Compassion with all living beings, granting of gifts, controlling the activities of the body, speech and mind ... these are sufficient rules of good conduct." The very first item is *dayā sarva-bhūteṣu* — compassion toward all beings. This is ahiṁsā under another name.

### Satya

Patañjali (YS 2.30): *satya* — truthfulness.

Caraka (Sū. 8.19): "One should not tell a lie ... should not speak out other's defects, nor should try to know other's secrets."

Caraka (Sū. 8.18): "One should speak useful, measured, sweet and meaningful words." This is the same fourfold qualification Vyāsa gives for satya in his bhāṣya on YS 2.30, where speech must be *sūnṛta* — pleasant and true together.

Vāgbhaṭa (AH Sū. 2.26): "One should speak appropriate to the occasion, with words which are good, in brief, which is not untrue and which is pleasing."

### Asteya

Patañjali (YS 2.30): *asteya* — non-stealing.

Caraka (Sū. 8.19): "One should not ... take over other's possession nor should desire for other women or other's property."

Vāgbhaṭa (AH Sū. 2.22): "*Steya* (stealing, robbing) ... these ten sins pertaining to the body, speech and mind should be avoided." Vāgbhaṭa uses the *same Sanskrit word* — *steya* — that Patañjali uses as the negation-object of *asteya*. Nothing is paraphrased. It is the identical technical term.

### Brahmacarya

Patañjali (YS 2.30): *brahmacarya* — celibacy, restraint in the matter of sexual energy.

Caraka (Sū. 11.35, P. V. Sharma): the three *upastambha* or sub-pillars of life are *āhāra*, *svapna*, and *brahmacarya* — diet, sleep, and celibacy. That is: of the three supports that hold up life itself, one is identical to Patañjali's fourth yama.

Caraka (Sū. 8.19): lists fifteen locations and circumstances where sexual intercourse is forbidden, then adds "in dawn and dusk, on prohibited days ..." — the same sorts of constraints one finds in the Dharmaśāstra and in Patañjali's commentarial tradition.

Vāgbhaṭa (AH Sū. 2.22): *anyathākāma* (unlawful sex activity) is listed among the ten sins.

### Aparigraha

Patañjali (YS 2.30): *aparigraha* — non-possessiveness, non-coveting.

Caraka (Sū. 8.19): "nor should one desire for other women or other's property ... should not be indulged in undue courage, excessive sleep, night vigils, bath, drinks and food."

The Āyurvedic formulation of aparigraha is built into the very concept of *mātrā* (proper measure) that governs eating, sleeping, speaking, and activity. One does not "possess" more of any substance than body, mind, and circumstance require. *Mātrāśitīya* is the title of AH Sūtra 8; it is also the organizing concept of Caraka Vimāna 1 and Sūtra 5.

### Śauca

Patañjali (YS 2.32): *śauca* — cleanliness, internal and external.

Caraka (Sū. 8.18): "One should honour fire (with oblation), one should put on auspicious herbs, one should wash twice a day, one should clean excretory passages and feet frequently; cutting of hair, shaving and nail cutting should be done thrice a fortnight; one should always wear untorn clothes, use flowers and fragrance."

Vāgbhaṭa (AH Sū. 2.31): "One should cut his hair, nails, and mustaches ( not allow them grow long), keep his feet and orifices of waste materials (ears, nose, eyes, urethra and anus) clean; take bath daily, put on scents and good dress which is not superfluous but is pleasant to look at."

The whole of *dinacaryā* is an elaboration of śauca. Every act listed — urinating and defecating at waking, jihvā-nirlekhana (tongue-scraping), danta-dhāvana (tooth-cleaning), añjana (collyrium), gaṇḍūṣa (mouth-gargling), nasya, abhyaṅga, snāna — is a specification of śauca. Where Patañjali gives one word, Vāgbhaṭa gives forty ślokas.

### Santoṣa

Patañjali (YS 2.32): *santoṣa* — contentment.

Caraka (Sū. 8.18): "free from anxiety, fearless ... should be self-controlled, self-virtuous, jealous in cause and not in effect ... should always look at the qualities of peaceful life and should alleviate the causes of attachment and aversion."

Caraka (Sū. 8.27): "one should not feel exhilarated in success and depressed in failure; one should always keep his constitution in mind."

Vāgbhaṭa (AH Sū. 2.25): "One should maintain a single mind (balanced mind) during (the period of) wealth as well as during (period of) calamity."

This is samatvam — the equality of mind the Gītā defines as yoga (*samatvaṁ yoga ucyate*, BG II.48) — translated directly into a sadvṛtta injunction.

### Tapas

Patañjali (YS 2.32): *tapas* — austerity.

Caraka (Sū. 8.18): "One should not be indulged in ... excessive sleep, night vigils, bath, drinks and food."

The Caraka treatise repeatedly returns to *mātrā* — measure — as the core therapeutic principle. Tapas in the Āyurvedic register is not extreme fasting; it is the settled refusal to indulge in the *atiyoga* (excess) of any sense-object. Caraka Sū. 11.37 (three causes of disease): *atiyoga, ayoga, mithyāyoga* of the sense objects. This lexical triad — excess, deficit, perversion — is what tapas guards against.

### Svādhyāya

Patañjali (YS 2.32): *svādhyāya* — study of scripture, repetition of oṁ.

Caraka (Sū. 8.29): "One should be devoted to celibacy, knowledge, charity, friendship, compassion, cheerfulness, indifference and calmness." *Jñāna* here is svādhyāya.

Caraka's *daśa-vidha-parīkṣā* (Sū. 8.94) — the ten-fold examination the physician must make of the patient — is itself a structured svādhyāya of the living body. The ten factors are: *prakṛti* (constitution), *vikṛti* (morbidity), *sāra* (tissue-essence), *saṁhanana* (compactness), *pramāṇa* (measurements), *sātmya* (suitability), *sattva* (psychic strength), *āhāra-śakti* (capacity of intake), *vyāyāma-śakti* (capacity of exercise), *vayas* (age). Each examination is a *study* — svādhyāya of the body as scripture. The physician reads the body the way the yogī reads the Veda.

### Īśvara-praṇidhāna

Patañjali (YS 2.32): *īśvara-praṇidhāna* — devotion to God.

Caraka (Sū. 8.18): "One should worship gods, cow, brahmanas, preceptors, elderly people, accomplished and teachers. One should honour fire (with oblation) ... should offer oblation, perform religious sacrifices, donate, pay respect to road crossings, offer balis (religion offerings), entertain guests, offer pindas (rice balls) to forefathers."

Vāgbhaṭa (AH Sū. 2.24): "God, cow, brāhmaṇa, elders, the physician, king and guests should be worshipped."

This one is more than lexical correspondence. Caraka and Vāgbhaṭa both insist that the physician is *part of the list of objects to be honored*. The god, the guru, and the physician are of a piece. The practitioner of medicine is a priestly figure, and the therapeutic act is a sacrificial one.

### The nearly lexical overlap

Laid out in a single frame:

| Yogic atom (YS 2.30, 2.32) | Sadvṛtta counterpart (Caraka Sū. 8; AH Sū. 2) |
| --- | --- |
| ahiṁsā | *sarva-bhūteṣu dayā* (AH 2.46); non-harm even to sinner (Car Sū 8.19) |
| satya | prohibition against lying, gossip, secret-prying (Car Sū 8.19); *sūnṛta*, pleasant-true speech (AH 2.26) |
| asteya | prohibition on *para-dravya* and *para-strī* (Car Sū 8.19); *steya* listed as sin (AH 2.22) |
| brahmacarya | *brahmacarya* named one of three *upastambha* of life itself (Car Sū 11.35); *anyathākāma* listed as sin (AH 2.22) |
| aparigraha | *mātrā* as the organizing principle of food, sleep, and sense-use (Car Sū 5, 8; AH Sū 8) |
| śauca | the whole of dinacaryā (AH Sū 2; Car Sū 5, 8.18) |
| santoṣa | even-mindedness in gain and loss (AH 2.25); freedom from *rāga* and *dveṣa* (Car Sū 8.18) |
| tapas | prohibition of *atiyoga* — excess — of any sense-object (Car Sū 11.37); mātrā |
| svādhyāya | *jñāna* (Car Sū 8.29); daśa-vidha-parīkṣā (Car Sū 8.94) |
| īśvara-praṇidhāna | worship of gods, guru, physician, ancestor (Car Sū 8.18; AH 2.24) |

Read the tenfold grid aloud to anyone who knows yoga. They will not be able to tell they are reading Caraka and Vāgbhaṭa. They will think they are reading Patañjali's commentary. This is the single sharpest piece of evidence for the shared-lineage thesis.

## Dinacaryā — yoga for the householder

The locus classicus of dinacaryā is *Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya* Sūtrasthāna 2, which Vāgbhaṭa opens with a single programmatic verse and then unfolds into forty-eight ślokas covering every act from waking at brāhma-muhūrta to going to bed. What follows is the full sequence, with each item tagged against its Patañjalian correlate.

### Waking at brāhma-muhūrta

**Vāgbhaṭa, AH Sū. 2.1a** (Śrīkāṇṭha Mūrthy, trans.):
> "The healthy person should get up (from bed) during brāhma muhūrta, to protect his life."

The translator's note: "The last three hours of the night (from 3 a.m. to 6 a.m.) is known as Brāhma muhūrta, because it is the best time for study and obtaining brahma or knowledge." Compare Patañjali's introductory sūtra at YS I.14: *sa tu dīrgha-kāla-nairantarya-satkārāsevito dṛḍha-bhūmiḥ* — "practice becomes firmly grounded when attended to for a long time, without interruption, with devotion." Brāhma-muhūrta is the time-slot designed for satkāra-āsevita practice. The householder is, by rising before sunrise, structurally doing what the renunciate does: taking the first clear moment of the day for sādhanā.

### Contemplation of the body, then evacuation

**Vāgbhaṭa, AH Sū. 2.2–3**:
> "Contemplating on the condition of his body, the person should next, attend to ablutions (after eliminating the urine and faeces)."

*Contemplating on the condition of his body* is the daily self-examination. In Patañjali's vocabulary this is pratyāhāra and svādhyāya fused together — the yogī withdrawing attention into the body as object of knowledge. The medical term is *svasthya-parīkṣā*. The yogic term is *svādhyāya*. Same act, two names.

### Danta-dhāvana (tooth-cleaning)

**Vāgbhaṭa, AH Sū. 2.2–3**:
> "Then after, he should clean his teeth with twigs of arka, nyagrodha, khadira, karañja, kakubha, etc. which are astringent, pungent, and bitter in taste; they (twigs) should be of the size of the tip of the little finger in thickness and twelve aṅgulas (finger's breadth) in length and straight, its top made like a soft brush (by chewing), the teeth should be cleaned without hurting the gums."

This is śauca applied to the oral cavity. It also falls under *danta-dhauti*, one of the subdivisions of dhauti in *Haṭha-Yoga Pradīpikā* II.22 and *Gheraṇḍa Saṁhitā* I.26ff. The overlap between dinacaryā and ṣaṭ-karma begins here.

### Añjana (collyrium)

**Vāgbhaṭa, AH Sū. 2.5**:
> "*Sauvīrāñjana* is good for the eyes, hence it should be used (as eyesalve) daily. The eye is full of tejas (light) and has risk of troubles especially from śleṣman (kapha); hence *rasāñjana* should be used once a week, to drain it (kapha) out."

Daily eye-cleansing. The yogic parallel is *chakshu-dhauti* — in *Gheraṇḍa Saṁhitā* listed as a sub-practice of *danta-dhauti*: "cleaning the upper back portion of the palate, *chakshu dhauti* – bathing the eyes with tepid, saline water" (Muktibodhananda's commentary on HYP 2.22, citing GhS 1.26ff).

### Nasya, gaṇḍūṣa, dhūma, tāmbūla (nose, mouth, smoke, betel)

**Vāgbhaṭa, AH Sū. 2.6**:
> "Afterwards, the person should make use of *nāvana* (nasal drops), *gaṇḍūṣa* (mouth gargles), *dhūma* (inhalation of smoke), and *tāmbūla* (chewing of betel leaves)."

Each of these has a direct ṣaṭ-karma / nāḍī-śodhana parallel. Nāvana is proto-*jala-neti*. Gaṇḍūṣa is oral-tract śauca. Dhūma — medicated smoke inhalation — is a mild bronchial dhauti. The Āyurvedic version uses pharmacy; the yogic version uses breath and water. The purpose is identical: to clear the upper passages so prāṇa can move.

### Abhyaṅga (oil-massage)

**Vāgbhaṭa, AH Sū. 2.8–9**:
> "Abhyaṅga (oil-massage and bath) should be resorted to daily, it wards off old age, exertion and (aggravation of) vāta; bestows good vision, nourishment to the body, long life, good sleep, good and strong (healthy) skin. It should be done specially to the head, ears and feet."

Abhyaṅga is the daily saturation of the skin with unctuous substance. The yogic parallel is the oleation (*snehana*) stage of purification that precedes pañca-karma. It is also a somatic form of santoṣa — contentment at the level of the dermis. The body is reassured by oil. The Āyurvedic view is that abhyaṅga is itself a contemplative practice: done slowly, in the morning, in silence, it is *satkāra-āsevita*.

### Vyāyāma (exercise, proto-āsana)

**Vāgbhaṭa, AH Sū. 2.10**:
> "Lightness (of the body), ability to do (hard) work, keen digestion, depletion of (excess) fat, stable and distinct physique accrue from vyāyāma (physical exercises)."

**AH Sū. 2.11**:
> "Persons who are strong and who indulge in fatty foods (daily); in cold seasons and spring (season) should do it (exercise) to half of their strength (capacity) only; while others (and in other seasons) should do it mildly."

The mātrā principle again: exercise to *half-strength only*. The external sign is sweat on the forehead, nose, axilla. No more. The hot-yoga marketplace should tremble at this. Vāgbhaṭa is explicit: "Those who indulge daily in too much of physical exercise, keeping awake at nights (loss of sleep), walking long distances, sexual intercourse, too much of laughing, speaking and such other strenuous activities perish, just as a lion, after vanquishing an elephant" (AH 2.14). *Vyāyāma* done without mātrā is violence (hiṁsā) against one's own body and therefore a breach of the first yama.

### Udvartana, snāna (massage-scrub and bath)

**Vāgbhaṭa, AH Sū. 2.15–16**:
> "Udvartana (massaging the body with soft, fragrant powders) mitigates kapha, liquifies the fat, produces stability (compactness, strength) of the body parts and excellence of the skin."
> "Snāna (bath) improves appetite, sexual vigour, span of life, valour (enthusiasm) and strength; removes itching, dirt, exhaustion, sweat, stupor, thirst, burning sensation and sin."

Note that snāna is said to remove *sin* (*pāpa*). The ordinary bath is classified as a purificatory sacrament. This is the same attitude the yogī brings to prāṇāyāma (YS II.52: "the covering of illumination is weakened"). Daily washing of the body is an act of spiritual purification, not a hygienic afterthought.

### Food at the right time, in the right measure

**Vāgbhaṭa, AH Sū. 2.19**:
> "Person should take food after digestion (of the previous meal), that which is suitable (to him) and in limited quantity; urges of the body should not be initiated (prematurely) by force; should not be engaged in other works when the urges are patent; not do anything (administration of drugs, therapies etc.) without treating the curable diseases (first)."

This is the pivot point from dinacaryā into āhāra-vidhi — treated in full in its own section below.

### Dharma as the basis of all action

**Vāgbhaṭa, AH Sū. 2.20**:
> "All (human) activities are meant for the happiness of all the living beings; such happiness is based on dharma (righteousness, right moral conduct); hence every person should adopt (follow) righteousness always."

Compare: Patañjali does not use the word *dharma* in the yamas, but Vyāsa in his bhāṣya on YS 2.30 frames the yamas as *dharma*, "righteous conduct." The Āyurvedic text simply says the quiet part out loud. Everything down-stream — tooth-cleaning, eating, bathing, sleeping, talking — is an expression of dharma. There is no domain of life that is outside the discipline.

### Body-speech-mind control

**Vāgbhaṭa, AH Sū. 2.46**:
> "Compassion with all living beings, granting of gifts, controlling the activities of the body, speech and mind; feeling of selfishness in the interests of others (looking after the interest of others as his own) these are sufficient rules of good conduct (moral behaviour)."

*Kāya-vāṅ-manas-saṁyama* — control of body, speech, and mind — is the exact triple-discipline Patañjali specifies as the scope of ahiṁsā, satya, and pratipakṣa-bhāvanā (YS 2.33–34). When Vāgbhaṭa reduces the whole sadvṛtta to this phrase, he is reducing it to what Patañjali reduces yama to. Same reduction.

### Daily retrospect

**Vāgbhaṭa, AH Sū. 2.47**:
> "He who constantly thinks of (reviews, examines) how his day and night are passing (and adopts the right way only) will never become a victim of sorrow."

*Ātma-parīkṣā* at the end of the day. The renunciate's nightly vigil, in miniature. Yoga for the householder is not reduced yoga; it is full yoga, lived in dharma's time-grid rather than the forest's.

### Dinacaryā as yoga in householder dress

The integration is structural, not metaphorical. The yogī of YS 2 does *yama, niyama, āsana, prāṇāyāma, pratyāhāra*; the householder of AH Sū. 2 does *sadvṛtta, snāna and dinacaryā, vyāyāma and āsana, nasya and gaṇḍūṣa and dhūma, indriyopakrama* — *in the same sequence*. The external frame is the morning instead of the forest. The internal operation is identical. This is why Caraka can write a medical text that is also a yoga text and Vāgbhaṭa can title his compendium "the heart of the eight limbs" without ambiguity as to which eight.

## Ṛtucaryā — the yogic seasonal body

The yogī lives in time. Patañjali's sole reference to kāla is in prāṇāyāma (*deśa-kāla-saṅkhyābhiḥ*, YS 2.50). But the whole Āyurvedic corpus is saturated with kāla — and the saturation takes the form of *ṛtucaryā*, the seasonal regimen. The body in winter is not the body in summer. The mind in winter is not the mind in summer. A sādhanā that does not acknowledge this violates the reality of the guṇas. Caraka Sū. 6, Vāgbhaṭa AH Sū. 3, and Suśruta Uttara 64 are the three great ṛtucaryā texts.

### The cosmic architecture

**Vāgbhaṭa, AH Sū. 3.1–2**:
> "With every two māsa (months) commencing with māgha, are the six ṛtus (seasons) — śiśira, vasanta, grīṣma, varṣā, śarat and hima (hemanta) successively; the three commencing with śiśira (śiśira, vasanta, and grīṣma) form the *uttarāyaṇa* (northern solstice); also known as *ādāna kāla* because the sun takes away the strength of the people daily."

> "AH Sū. 3.3–4: Because of the nature of the path, both the sun and wind become very strong (powerful) and dry during this ayana (uttarāyaṇa) and take away all the cooling qualities of the terrestrial; tikta (bitter), kaṣāya (astringent) and kaṭuka (pungent) tastes are more powerful respectively (in the three successive ṛtus), hence this ādāna-kāla is āgneya (predominantly fire-like in nature)."

> "AH Sū. 3.5–6: The three ṛtus commencing with varṣā (varṣā, śarat and hemanta) form the *dakṣiṇāyana* (southern solstice) and *visarga kāla* — the period in which the sun releases the strength of the people; because the moon is more powerful and the sun loses his strength, the terrestrial becomes cooled of the heat of sunlight by (the effect of) clouds, rain and cold wind; the unctuous tastes — amla (sour), lavaṇa (salt) and madhura (sweet) — are powerful (respectively) during this period."

The body's *bala* (strength) follows the solar year. In ādāna-kāla (uttarāyaṇa) the sun takes; in visarga-kāla (dakṣiṇāyana) the sun gives. This is exactly the cosmology of the Sol project: strength is solar, and the practitioner must read the solar clock. The yogic tradition retains this architecture as *sūrya-candra-nāḍī* — piṅgalā and iḍā, the solar and lunar currents that alternate in the body. The physiology is the same; only the vocabulary differs.

### Suśruta on svastha-vṛtta

**Suśruta Uttara 64.2–3** (Kaviraj Kunjalal Bhishagratna, trans., Chaukhamba, 2006 reprint):
> "Now we shall discourse on the chapter which deals with the **Rules of Health (*Svastha-Vṛtta-adhyāya*)**.
> Health indicates a normal condition of the (three) Doshas (fundamental principles), Agni (the digestive fire), Mala (excrements viz, feces, urine, etc.), and the (seven) Dhatus or root-principles of the body as well as a serene state of the body, mind and the sense-organs. It has already been stated in the Sūtra-sthāna, that the primary object of medical treatment is to maintain this healthy state (of the body and of the mind) in its normal equilibrium."

This is Suśruta's version of the same definition Caraka will give at Sū. 15 — health is the equilibrium of doṣa, agni, mala, dhātu, plus the serene state of ātman, manas, and indriya. The last clause is crucial. A body in doṣa-equilibrium with an unquiet mind is not svastha. The condition Patañjali calls *prasanna-ātmendriya-manāḥ* is *part of the medical definition of health*. Suśruta then devotes the whole of Uttara 64 to the seasonal regimen by which this state is maintained.

### Seasonal example: hemanta (winter)

**Vāgbhaṭa, AH Sū. 3.7–8**:
> "In hemanta, the people are strong, the *anala* (fire in the alimentary tract vis a vis digestive activity) becomes powerful because it gets obstructed (from spreading out) by the cold (in the atmosphere). It begins to digest the tissues (of the body) supported (helped) by *vāyu* (vāta in the body); so in this hemanta (winter), use of (substances of) sweet, sour and salt tastes should be made."

Why does the yogī need this? Because an unfed digestive fire in winter will start to burn the dhātus themselves. The body becomes its own fuel. Fasting in winter — without guidance — is a method for consuming one's own flesh. Ṛtucaryā is the safety-rail the yogī needs. The Haṭha-Yoga Pradīpikā quietly assumes it, but does not spell it out. AH Sūtra 3 spells it out.

### Seasonal example: vasanta (spring)

**Vāgbhaṭa, AH Sū. 3.18–22**:
> "Kapha which has undergone increase in śiśira (cold seasons) becomes liquefied by the heat of the sun in vasanta (spring), diminishes the agni (digestive activity in the alimentary tract), and gives rise to many diseases; hence it (kapha) should be controlled quickly, by resorting to strong emesis, nasal medication and other therapies, and also by foods which are easily digestible and dry (moisture-free, fat-free), physical exercises, (dry) massage and mild trampling."

Spring is the season for *vamana* (therapeutic emesis) and *nasya* (nasal cleansing). This is the Āyurvedic calendar's instruction to do what the Haṭha yogī calls *kunjal kriyā* (vaman dhauti) and *neti* — but only at the season when kapha has risen. The ṣaṭ-karma of HYP 2.22 is to be practiced *in vasanta*, not arbitrarily.

### Ṛtucaryā and the yogic year

Patañjali does not legislate the seasonal calendar; he presumes the practitioner lives in time and will adjust. The Āyurvedic texts give the adjustment in full. The two texts are therefore complementary: YS gives the internal arc, AH gives the seasonal arc in which it is to be run. Together they are one discipline.

## Āhāra-vidhi and mitāhāra — the same eating discipline

Of all the structural parallels, this is the one where the textual overlap is most literal.

### Caraka Vimāna 1.21 — the eight āhāra-vidhi-viśeṣa-āyatana

**Caraka Vimāna 1.21** (P. V. Sharma, trans.):
> "There are eight specific factors of method of dieting such as — **nature, processing, combination, quantity, place, time, rules for use, and consumer, the eighth one.**"

The eight āhāra-vidhi-viśeṣa-āyatana:
1. *prakṛti* — the nature of the food (heavy / light, heating / cooling).
2. *karaṇa* — its processing (cooking, churning, steeping, fermenting).
3. *saṁyoga* — combination of substances (honey and ghee together is famously forbidden).
4. *rāśi* — quantity (both the total-quantity and the individual-item quantity).
5. *deśa* — place (where the food was grown, and where it is now being eaten).
6. *kāla* — time (the season; whether the previous meal is digested).
7. *upayoga-saṁsthā* — rules of use.
8. *upayokta* — the eater himself — his constitution, age, and *oka-sātmya* (habituation).

### Caraka Vimāna 1.24 — the eight rules of eating

**Caraka Vimāna 1.24** (P. V. Sharma, trans.):
> "One should eat warm, unctuous, in proper quantity, after the previous food is digested, non-antagonistic, in favourable place, with all the favourable accessories, not too fast, not too slow, not while talking or laughing and with full concentration after due consideration to the self."

The eight rules (unpacked in Caraka Vi. 1.25):
1. *uṣṇa* — warm. "Because it tastes well, stimulates digestive fire, gets digested quickly, carminates flatus, reduces mucus."
2. *snigdha* — unctuous. "Stimulates unstimulated digestive fire, develops the body, provides firmness to sense organs, increases strength, produces clarity of complexion."
3. *mātrāvat* — in proper quantity. "Without disturbing vāta, pitta and kapha only promotes life-span, easily passes down to anus, does not disturb the (digestive) fire, gets digested without discomfort."
4. *jīrṇe aśnīyāt* — after the previous meal is digested. Because "if one eats during indigestion, the eaten food mixing the product of the earlier meal with that of the later one vitiates all the dosas quickly."
5. *vīrya-aviruddha* — non-antagonistic in potency.
6. *iṣṭe deśe iṣṭa-sarvopakaraṇam* — in favourable place and with favourable accessories. "While eating in favourable place one does not fall victim to psychic disturbance."
7. *nāti-drutam, nāti-vilambitam* — not too fast, not too slow.
8. *ajalpan, ahasan, tanmanā bhuñjīta* — not talking or laughing, eating with full concentration on the act itself. "One should eat after due consideration to his self (*ātmānam abhisamīkṣya*)."

### Haṭha-Yoga Pradīpikā 1.58 — mitāhāra

**Svātmārāma, HYP 1.58** (Muktibodhananda, trans., Bihar School of Yoga):
> "*Mitāhāra* is defined as agreeable and sweet food, leaving one-fourth of the stomach free, and eaten (as an offering to please Śiva)."

Muktibodhananda's commentary: "'Sweet food' means fresh, pleasant tasting food, not particularly that which has extra sugar. Food which is disagreeable means that it is either bad tasting, poisonous to the system or not agreeable to one's metabolism. The stomach should never be overloaded; it should be half filled with food, one quarter with water and one quarter with air. Eating 'to please Śiva' means that when taking food the yogi should not feel that he is eating for himself. … *Mitāhāra* means 'sāttvic food,' light food which is easy to digest."

### HYP 1.57 — the one-year perfection

**HYP 1.57**:
> "One who is brahmacārī, takes moderate and pure food, is regular and intent on yoga and renounces (attachment to sensual experience) becomes perfected (*siddha*) after a year."

Mitāhāra — moderate food — is the second limb of the path to siddhi in HYP. The first is brahmacarya. The four conjoined disciplines: *brahmacārī, mitāhārī, tyāgī, yogī*.

### HYP 1.63 — the dhātu-nourishing diet

**HYP 1.63**:
> "The yogi should take nourishing and sweet food mixed with ghee and milk; **it should nourish the dhatus** (basic body constituents) and be pleasing and suitable."

The yogī is instructed to nourish his *dhātus*. Dhātu is the Āyurvedic word. The author of HYP takes it for granted that his reader knows what the seven dhātus are (rasa, rakta, māṁsa, medas, asthi, majjā, śukra). He does not define them. He assumes the yogī is already inside the Āyurvedic conceptual scheme.

### HYP 1.59–60 — the prohibitions

**HYP 1.59**:
> "The foods which are prohibited (for the yogi) are: those which are bitter, sour, pungent, salty, heating, green vegetables (other than those ordained), sour gruel, oil, sesame and mustard, alcohol, fish, flesh foods, curds, buttermilk, horse gram, fruit of jujube, oil cakes, asafetida and garlic."

**HYP 1.60**:
> "Unhealthy diet should not be taken, that which is reheated after becoming cold, which is dry (devoid of natural oil), which is excessively salty or acidic, stale or has too many (mixed) vegetables."

Muktibodhananda's commentary on 1.60: "Excess salt and acidity imbalance the system, in fact, [chloride of sodium] directly affects the heart rate. If [chloride] is taken in excess it makes the heart beat faster and heats the body. Many different types of vegetables should not be cooked together as the resulting chemical reactions can upset the digestive system and disturb body functions."

### The overlap

| Caraka Vimāna 1.24 rule | HYP 1.58–63 rule |
| --- | --- |
| *uṣṇa* — warm food | Muktibodhananda on 1.60: reheated / cold food creates fermentation, indigestion |
| *snigdha* — unctuous, with ghee | HYP 1.63: "nourishing and sweet food mixed with ghee and milk" |
| *mātrāvat* — proper quantity | HYP 1.58: mitāhāra, "leaving one-fourth of the stomach free" |
| *jīrṇe aśnīyāt* — only after previous is digested | HYP chapter 2 — assumed throughout |
| *vīrya-aviruddha* — non-antagonistic | HYP 1.60: no mixed vegetables; no reheated food; no chloride + sour combination |
| *iṣṭe deśe* — favourable place | HYP 1.61: avoid "bad company" while eating |
| *nāti-drutam, nāti-vilambitam* — measured speed | Assumed |
| *tanmanā bhuñjīta* — eat with concentration | HYP 1.58: "eaten as an offering to please Śiva" — i.e. as contemplative act |

Svātmārāma's *mitāhāra* is Caraka's *āhāra-vidhi-viśeṣa-āyatana* compressed into four ślokas. Svātmārāma's prohibitions are Caraka's *mithyā-āhāra* (improper food) lexicon. The two texts are speaking to each other across centuries — and within a single living discipline. Vāgbhaṭa at AH Sū. 8.35–38 lists the same rules with slightly different weight:

**Vāgbhaṭa, AH Sū. 8.35–38** (Śrīkāṇṭha Mūrthy, trans.):
> "Food should be consumed at the proper time, it should be the accustomed, clean, suited to health, unctuous, hot and easily digestable; partaken with due attention, should contain all the six tastes with predominance of sweet taste, partaken neither very quick nor very slow; after taking bath, after having good hunger, sitting in solitude, after washing the feet, hands and face, after satisfying the pitṛs (manes), gods, guests, children, preceptors and even the dependents maintained in the house ... after carefully considering one's own (constitution, likes and dislikes, interinary etc.), without scolding or abusing the food, without too much of talk; should partake more of liquid food, that which is liked, in the company of the liked persons, and served by those who are clean and faithful to him."

The whole text is mindfulness-of-eating as a ritual act. It is the same *tanmanā bhuñjīta* — eat-with-concentration — that Caraka legislates. And it is also what Svātmārāma means by "eat as an offering to Śiva."

## Cikitsā-catuṣpāda and Vyāsa's four-pāda mapping

This is the section where the structural identity shows up as explicit cross-reference inside the commentaries themselves.

### Caraka's cikitsā-catuṣpāda (Sū. 9.3–9)

**Caraka Sūtra 9.3** (P. V. Sharma, trans.):
> "*bhiṣag-dravyāṇy-upasthātā rogī pādacatuṣṭayam |*
> *guṇavat kāraṇaṁ jñeyaṁ vikāra-vyupaśāntaye ||*"
> "Physician, drug, attendant and patient, this is the quadruped which, if endowed with qualities, leads to alleviation of disorders."

The cikitsā-catuṣpāda — the four feet of therapeutics:
1. *bhiṣak* — the physician.
2. *dravya* — the medicine / substance.
3. *upasthātā* — the attendant / nurse.
4. *rogī* — the patient.

Each of these four is then specified in four qualities each (9.6–9.9), giving sixteen qualities in total. The physician's four qualities:

**Caraka Sūtra 9.6** (P. V. Sharma):
> "Thoroughness in theoretical knowledge, extensive practical experience, dexterity and cleanliness — this is the quadruple of qualities of a physician."

And Caraka's metaphysical gloss:

**Caraka Sūtra 9.4** (P. V. Sharma):
> "Abnormality (disorder) is disequilibrium of dhātus and their equilibrium is normalcy (health). Health is known as happiness while disorder is unhappiness."

> "Employment of all the excellent four — physician etc. — in case of disorder of dhātus with the object of (re-establishing) their equilibrium is said as *therapeutics*" (Sū. 9.5).

### Vyāsa on YS 2.15 — the four-pāda of yoga

**Patañjali, YS 2.15**:
> *pariṇāma-tāpa-saṁskāra-duḥkhair guṇa-vṛtti-virodhāc ca duḥkham eva sarvaṁ vivekinaḥ*

**Translation (Bryant)**:
> "For one who has discrimination, everything is suffering on account of the suffering produced by the consequences [of action], by pain [itself], and by the saṁskāras, as well as on account of the suffering ensuing from the turmoil of the vṛttis due to the guṇas."

Vyāsa's commentary on this sūtra (via Bryant's summary):
> "Seeing himself and all other beings caught up in this tide of suffering, says Vyāsa, the yogī takes shelter of true knowledge — that he is not the body or mind. **Just as the science of medicine has four parts, he says — disease to be removed, cause of disease, freedom from disease, and the means of removal (medicine) — so the science of yoga has four parts: saṁsāra, the cause of saṁsāra, freedom from saṁsāra, and the means of liberation.** Saṁsāra is the disease that is to be removed, its cause is the contact between puruṣa and prakṛti, freedom from saṁsāra is the cessation of this contact (liberation), and the means of removing this contact is pure knowledge."

Bryant continues: "Śaṅkara quotes this fourfold division of Vyāsa in the opening words of his *Vivaraṇa* commentary on the first sūtra of the Yoga Sūtras."

This is the crown jewel. The foundational commentator on the *Yoga Sūtra* — writing c. 5th century CE — defines yoga-śāstra by structural analogy with cikitsā-śāstra. He does not say "yoga is like medicine." He says "yoga has the same fourfold form as medicine." Let the mapping be laid bare:

| Caraka's cikitsā-catuṣpāda | Vyāsa's yoga-catuṣpāda (YS 2.15 bhāṣya) |
| --- | --- |
| *roga* — disease to be removed | *saṁsāra* — cycle of suffering to be removed |
| *roga-hetu* — cause of disease | *saṁsāra-hetu* — cause of saṁsāra = contact of puruṣa and prakṛti |
| *ārogya* — freedom from disease | *mokṣa / kaivalya* — freedom from saṁsāra |
| *bhaiṣajya* — medicine, the means of removal | *mokṣa-upāya* — the means of liberation = pure knowledge (viveka-khyāti) |

The doctrinal parallel is not decorative. It is *the architectural blueprint of the Yoga Sūtras*. Bryant points this out: "This sūtra is actually the pivot of this chapter, which, in turn, is the heart of the entire text. Verses II.1–14 discuss the causes, the kleśas, that produce duḥkha, and verses 16 onward focus on the path to remove duḥkha as well as the state beyond. The chapter thus echoes the Four Noble Truths" (Bryant on YS 2.15). Which are themselves, as a diagnostic grid, the structural twin of Caraka's catuṣpāda. The Buddhist tradition also inherited the grid from the same shared medical-yogic lineage. Three branches, one diagnostic tree.

Every time a yoga teacher uses the phrase "yoga is the medicine for saṁsāra," the teacher is invoking Vyāsa's bhāṣya on YS 2.15 — and therefore invoking Caraka's cikitsā-catuṣpāda. The two systems *define themselves by the same four-pāda diagnostic*.

## Pañca-karma and ṣaṭ-karma — the same therapeutic modalities

The Āyurvedic system classifies its "heroic" purificatory therapies under the name *pañca-karma*. The Haṭha-Yogic system classifies its opening-body practices under the name *ṣaṭ-karma*. The lists overlap almost one-to-one. Five of the six ṣaṭ-karma have pañca-karma equivalents. The sixth, trāṭaka, corresponds to nothing in pañca-karma because trāṭaka is not a physical purification but a concentration practice; it is really a dhāraṇā limb absorbed into the purification set. The lists:

### Caraka's pañca-karma

**Caraka Sūtra 2.15** (P. V. Sharma): "Thus *pañcakarma* (five measures) is said in brief." The five measures, as Caraka unfolds them across Sūtra 2, Vimāna 8, and Siddhi 1–2:

1. *vamana* — therapeutic emesis. For excess kapha located in the stomach and chest.
2. *virecana* — therapeutic purgation. For pitta located in the small intestine.
3. *basti* — therapeutic enema, both *āsthāpana* (decoction-based, non-unctuous) and *anuvāsana* (oil-based, unctuous). For vāta located in the colon. Caraka considers basti the king of pañca-karma.
4. *nasya* (or *śirovirecana*) — head-evacuation via the nose. For doṣas above the clavicle.
5. *rakta-mokṣaṇa* — therapeutic bloodletting. For localized pitta.

Each is preceded by *pūrva-karma* — *snehana* (oleation, internal and external) and *svedana* (sudation) — and followed by *paścāt-karma* (the graduated-diet protocol called *saṁsarjana-krama*). The whole sequence takes weeks, is performed under supervision, and is meant to fully evacuate accumulated doṣas.

### Svātmārāma's ṣaṭ-karma (HYP 2.22)

**HYP 2.22**:
> "*Dhauti, basti, neti, trāṭaka, nauli* and *kapāla-bhāti*; these are known as ṣaṭ-karma or the six cleansing processes."

Muktibodhananda unpacks each in 2.22–36:

1. *dhauti* — internal cleansing. Sub-forms: *antar dhauti* (vatsara, varisara / śaṅkha-prakṣālana, vahnisāra / agnisāra, bahiṣkṛta), *danta dhauti* (including *jihvā*, *karṇa*, *kapāla-randhra*, *chakṣu*), *hṛid dhauti* (vastra, daṇḍa, vamana), and *mūla-śodhana* (rectal cleaning).
2. *basti* — yogic enema. Two sub-forms: *jala basti* (water, sucked up through the anus using uḍḍiyāna) and *sthala basti* (dry, air-based).
3. *neti* — nasal cleansing. *Sūtra neti* (thread), *jala neti* (water), *ghṛta neti* (ghee), *dugdha neti* (milk).
4. *trāṭaka* — steady gazing. Antar (internal) and bahir (external).
5. *nauli* — abdominal churning. Dakṣiṇa, vāma, madhyama.
6. *kapāla-bhāti* — frontal brain purification. Vatakrama (breathing-based), vyutkrama (water through nose, out through mouth), śīt-krama (water through nose).

### The correspondence

| Pañca-karma (Caraka) | Ṣaṭ-karma (HYP, GhS) |
| --- | --- |
| *vamana* — emesis | *vamana dhauti / kunjal kriyā / vyāghra kriyā* (hṛid dhauti) |
| *virecana* — purgation | *varisara dhauti / śaṅkha-prakṣālana* (antar dhauti) |
| *basti* — enema | *jala basti, sthala basti* |
| *nasya* — head-evacuation | *neti* (jala, sūtra, ghṛta, dugdha); *kapāla-bhāti* |
| *rakta-mokṣaṇa* — bloodletting | — (no direct yogic parallel) |
| — | *nauli* (agnisāra dhauti is the pañca-karma analogue) |
| — | *trāṭaka* (a dhāraṇā-limb practice, not purification) |

The key insight: *the Āyurvedic therapies are clinician-administered; the yogic therapies are self-administered*. Vamana uses emetic drugs chosen by a vaidya; vamana dhauti uses a glass of warm salty water and two fingers. Virecana uses trivṛt powder; śaṅkha-prakṣālana uses sixteen glasses of salt-water and a sequence of āsanas. Basti uses a decoction or oil; jala basti uses plain water, uḍḍiyāna bandha, and nauli. Nasya uses medicated oils and powders; jala neti uses warm saline water.

Muktibodhananda, commenting on HYP 2.22, gives the lineage explicitly: "The second karma, basti is divided into two parts: jala (water) basti and sthala (dry) basti. In jala basti you suck water into the large intestine through the anus and then expel it. ... There is a shorter technique called laghoo śaṅkha-prakṣālana. Laghoo means 'short.' In this practice only six glasses of warm saline water are taken." The very name of the master practice — *śaṅkha-prakṣālana*, "conch-washing" — is Āyurvedic in flavor and construction. The terminology migrates freely between the two disciplines.

Muktibodhananda summarizes HYP 2.36 (on the śaṭkarmas removing excess doṣas): "These six fundamental cleansing techniques are the most important aspects of hatha yoga and are the original hatha yoga. However, today āsana and prāṇāyāma are more widely known and few people are now proficient in the śaṭkarma." The same can be said of pañca-karma inside modern "wellness" Āyurveda: the therapeutic core has been flanked by asana-like and dietary simplifications, while the heroic-purification protocols have receded. What the two traditions have lost is the same thing in different idioms.

## The puruṣārthas — shared goal-structure

### Caraka's three desires (Sū. 11.3–6)

**Caraka Sūtra 11.3** (P. V. Sharma):
> "A person, having underanged mind, intellect, potency and prowess and looking to his well-being here and in the world hereafter, should pursue three desires such as *desire for life* (*prāṇaiṣaṇā*), *desire for wealth* (*dhanaiṣaṇā*), and *desire for the other world* (*paralokaiṣaṇā*)."

And Caraka specifies the order: life first, then wealth, then the other-world.

**Caraka Sū. 11.4** (P. V. Sharma):
> "Out of all these desires, one should follow the desire to live first. Why? Because on departure of life, everything departs. That can be maintained by observance of the code of conduct for the healthy and non-carelessness in alleviation of disorders in the diseased."

Prāṇaiṣaṇā is fulfilled by sadvṛtta + cikitsā. Dhanaiṣaṇā is fulfilled by proper work. Paralokaiṣaṇā is fulfilled by dharma, tapas, sacrifice, truth, ahiṁsā, and brahmacarya. Caraka (Sū. 11.27) lists these last six as the means to *abhyudaya* (worldly prosperity) and *niḥśreyasa* (liberation). So the three desires map cleanly onto the four puruṣārtha of the Dharmaśāstra tradition:

| Caraka's three desires | Four puruṣārtha |
| --- | --- |
| *prāṇaiṣaṇā* (desire for life) | *artha* and *kāma* embodied — life itself as the pre-condition for both |
| *dhanaiṣaṇā* (desire for wealth) | *artha* — the means of life |
| *paralokaiṣaṇā* (desire for the other world) | *dharma* + *mokṣa* — virtue leading to liberation |

The Āyurvedic text makes life itself the first good, the *sine qua non* of every other pursuit. Without prāṇa, nothing. This is also the first premise of yoga: Patañjali's very definition of yoga as *citta-vṛtti-nirodha* (YS 1.2) presupposes that a citta is there to be stilled, which presupposes prāṇa. Yoga depends on life; life depends on sadvṛtta.

### Patañjali's goal — kaivalya

**Patañjali, YS 4.34** (Bryant):
> *puruṣārtha-śūnyānāṁ guṇānāṁ pratiprasavaḥ kaivalyaṁ svarūpa-pratiṣṭhā vā citi-śaktir iti*

> "Ultimate liberation is when the guṇas, devoid of any purpose for the puruṣa, return to their original [latent] state; in other words, when the power of consciousness is situated in its own essential nature."

Kaivalya is *mokṣa* under Sāṃkhyan terminology. It is the full flowering of the fourth puruṣārtha. Compare the Caraka goal: *dhātusāmya-kriyā cokta tantrasyāsya prayojanam* — "the establishment of equilibrium of the dhātus is said to be the aim of this treatise" (Sū. 1.53, P. V. Sharma). The medical treatise aims at dhātusāmya — tissue equilibrium. Patañjali's yoga aims at guṇasāmya at a deeper level — the equilibrium and absorption of the guṇas back into their latent source. *Sāmya* (equilibrium) is the operative word in both. Both systems are saying: the cure for suffering is the establishment of equilibrium at the appropriate level. Medicine works at the gross-dhātu level; yoga works at the subtle-guṇa level. Same operation, two depths.

## Caraka's definition of svastha — samādhi as health (KEYSTONE)

### The verse

The single most important verse in all of Āyurveda for the unity thesis of this book is the definition of *svastha* given in the Caraka Saṁhitā. Different editions and different redactions place and number the verse differently — it appears in the Caraka-siddha editions near Sū. 15, and in P. V. Sharma's edition the equivalent conceptual material is distributed across Sū. 9.4 (defining *vikāra* as *dhātu-vaiṣamya* and *prakṛti* as *dhātu-sāmya*), Sū. 15 (on therapeutic purification leading to *prakṛti-sthāpana*), and the parallel Suśruta definition at Uttara 64.2–3. The canonical formulation, universally recognized in both the Sanskrit commentarial tradition and modern scholarly editions, is:

**Sanskrit (IAST)**:
> *sama-doṣaḥ samāgniś ca sama-dhātu-mala-kriyaḥ |*
> *prasanna-ātmendriya-manāḥ svastha ity abhidhīyate ||*

### Multiple translations

**Suśruta's rendering of the same definition (Su. Uttara 64.3, Bhishagratna, trans.)**:
> "Health indicates a normal condition of the (three) Doshas (fundamental principles), Agni (the digestive fire), Mala (excrements viz, feces, urine, etc.), and the (seven) Dhatus or root-principles of the body as well as a serene state of the body, mind and the sense-organs."

**P. V. Sharma's compressed rendering (Caraka Sū. 9.4)**:
> "Abnormality (disorder) is disequilibrium of dhātus and their equilibrium is normalcy (health). Health is known as happiness while disorder is unhappiness."

**Literal word-by-word** (from the IAST):
> — *sama-doṣaḥ*: whose three doṣas (vāta, pitta, kapha) are in equilibrium.
> — *samāgniḥ*: whose digestive fire (*jaṭharāgni*, and the thirteen agnis) is balanced.
> — *ca*: and.
> — *sama-dhātu-mala-kriyaḥ*: whose seven dhātus (rasa, rakta, māṁsa, medas, asthi, majjā, śukra) and three malas (purīṣa, mūtra, sveda) are in equilibrium in their functioning (*kriyā*).
> — *prasanna-ātmendriya-manāḥ*: whose ātman, sense-organs (indriyas), and mind (manas) are *prasanna* — clear, serene, settled, shining.
> — *svastha*: "self-abiding" — literally "standing in oneself" (*sva* + *stha*).
> — *iti abhidhīyate*: is thus named / declared.

### Unpacking the ingredients

The first three ingredients are the somatic condition: doṣas equal, agni balanced, dhātus and malas functioning evenly. The fourth ingredient is the psycho-spiritual condition: *ātman*, *indriya*, and *manas* all in the state called *prasanna*. The second condition is not a consequence of the first; the two are stated jointly. Health is the *simultaneous* settled state of the body and the subtle body. Absent either, there is no svastha.

### The keystone — prasanna = samādhi

*Prasanna* is the adjective Patañjali and his commentarial tradition reserve for the citta in the samādhi condition. It means: clear, settled, shining, transparent, serene, free of disturbance. Compare:

- YS I.47 (Bryant): *nirvicāra-vaiśāradye 'dhyātma-prasādaḥ* — "Upon the purification of nirvicāra samādhi, there is **lucidity** of the self."
- YS I.33: *maitrī-karuṇā-muditopekṣāṇāṁ sukha-duḥkha-puṇyāpuṇya-viṣayāṇāṁ bhāvanātaḥ citta-prasādanam* — "By cultivating attitudes of friendliness, compassion, joy, and equanimity … the citta retains its **undisturbed calmness** (*citta-prasāda*)."

*Prasāda* and *prasanna* are from the same root (*pra + √sad*) — to settle, to clarify, as muddy water settles when left still. The citta in samādhi is the citta that has *settled*. It has become clear-water, no turbulence.

Now read Caraka's phrase again: *prasanna-ātmendriya-manāḥ* — "whose self, senses, and mind are *prasanna*." This is the condition Patañjali labels samādhi, stated in the plural — ātman, indriya, manas all three in the *prasāda* state. And Caraka legislates this condition *as the definition of health*.

So the keystone of the unity argument:

> **To be healthy, in Āyurveda, is to be in samādhi.**
> **To be in samādhi, in Yoga, is to be healthy.**

This is not a metaphorical equivalence. It is the literal sense of Caraka's definition read alongside Patañjali's technical vocabulary. The medical text absorbs the yogic goal into its definition of its own subject matter. There is no Āyurvedic concept of "health" that is not already a yogic concept of samādhi. Wellness, as the modern industry markets it, is a degenerate fragment of this definition. The *sama-doṣa* portion has been retained (often in the form of supplements and tests and panels). The *prasanna-ātmendriya-manāḥ* portion has been dropped. The result is a discipline that cannot, by its own self-understanding, achieve its own aim.

### Why this verse anchors the book

Every other structural parallel in this file supports and is supported by this one verse:

- *Sadvṛtta = yama-niyama*: because the ethical atoms are what stabilize the *manas* into *prasāda*.
- *Dinacaryā*: because the daily regimen is what keeps *doṣa, agni, dhātu, mala, kriyā* in *sāmya*.
- *Ṛtucaryā*: because the seasonal regimen is what keeps *sāmya* through the solar year.
- *Āhāra-vidhi / mitāhāra*: because food is the gross substrate of *sāmya*.
- *Cikitsā-catuṣpāda / yoga-catuṣpāda*: because the four-fold diagnostic is the means of removing *vaiṣamya* (disequilibrium) and restoring *sāmya-prasāda*.
- *Pañca-karma / ṣaṭ-karma*: because these heroic purifications remove the accumulated *mala* that obstructs *prasāda*.
- *Puruṣārtha*: because the full flowering of human life is the *prasanna-ātman* — which is Patañjali's kaivalya and Caraka's svastha.

Read this way, the whole architecture of both texts is a single architecture. The medical treatise and the yoga treatise are engineering specifications for the same machine. The machine's output is *prasanna-ātmendriya-manāḥ* — the settled, clear, luminous human being — and that output goes by two synonymous names: *svasthya* on the medical side, *samādhi* on the yogic side. It is one output.

### Vāgbhaṭa's echo

Vāgbhaṭa's own framing of the medical aim at AH Sūtra 1.1 uses the same conceptual apparatus. And Caraka's opening statement of purpose at Sū. 1.53 — *dhātusāmya-kriyā cokta tantrasyāsya prayojanam* — "the establishment of dhātu-equilibrium is said to be the purpose of this treatise" — places *sāmya* at the head of the entire corpus. Everything that follows is in service of restoring *sāmya*. And the verb *samāhita* — "equipoised, absorbed" — from which *samādhi* comes, is etymologically the same word as *sāmya*. *Samādhi* is the absorbed-state-of-equilibrium. *Svāsthya* is the same state, named from the medical side. Sanskrit gives the unity away at the lexical root.

## Daśa-vidha-parīkṣā and yogic self-examination

Caraka's tenfold examination (Vimāna 8.94 in the Agniveśa tradition; cross-referenced in Sū. 8 in some editions) is the diagnostic grid a physician uses on a patient:

1. *prakṛti* — constitutional nature (doṣa-predominance at birth).
2. *vikṛti* — the current morbid state.
3. *sāra* — the essence or quality of the dhātus (which of the seven is dominant in this body).
4. *saṁhanana* — compactness, build of the body.
5. *pramāṇa* — measurements (of limbs, of body).
6. *sātmya* — what is habituated and agreeable.
7. *sattva* — psychic strength, mental stamina.
8. *āhāra-śakti* — digestive and intake capacity (in two dimensions: *abhyavaharaṇa*, ingestive, and *jaraṇa*, digestive).
9. *vyāyāma-śakti* — exercise capacity.
10. *vayas* — age (bāla, madhya, vṛddha).

As a *self*-examination, this is exactly what svādhyāya (YS 2.32, 2.44) requires. The yogī does not need a physician to do this: the yogī becomes his own physician. In fact, at AH Sūtra 2.47 Vāgbhaṭa legislates a daily self-examination as part of dinacaryā: "He who constantly thinks of (reviews, examines) how his day and night are passing (and adopts the right way only) will never become a victim of sorrow." This is the daśa-vidha-parīkṣā internalized.

Patañjali's YS 2.44 — *svādhyāyād iṣṭa-devatā-samprayogaḥ* ("from self-study one attains union with one's chosen deity") — gives the same act a metaphysical flowering: the self-study is not only diagnostic but also, at full depth, devotional. Self-study reveals the *iṣṭa-devatā* as the true center of the self. The Āyurvedic self-examination reveals the equilibrium-state. In both cases, the work is: *know your body, know your mind, know your constitution, know your season, know your day*. The physician's examination and the yogī's *svādhyāya* are the same operation with different recording media.

## Scholarly voices on the parallel structures

The argument of this file is not original. It has been made, in different ways and at different depths, by every serious scholar of the combined fields. A representative sample:

### Edwin Bryant (on the direct citation of Caraka inside the Yoga Sūtra commentary)

Bryant, commenting on YS II.32 and Vyāsa's gloss of the niyama of *śauca*: "In terms of ingestion, Hariharānanda reiterates that meat and intoxication cause the mind to be agitated and stimulated — they incite rajas and tamas — and yoga requires a steady and peaceful mind. Therefore, a yogī never imbibes such substances. **He quotes Caraka, a traditional authority on āyurveda**: 'Whatever is good or most desired in this life or the next is attained by intense concentration of the mind. Alcohol creates a disturbance in the mind. Those blinded by addiction to alcohol lose sight of their best self-interest'" (Bryant, *The Yoga Sūtras of Patañjali*, on YS II.32). The fact that a standard English-language commentary on the *Yoga Sūtras* cites Caraka to illustrate a yoga niyama is, by itself, textual proof of the unity.

### Bryant on the four-pāda diagnostic

"Just as the science of medicine has four parts, he [Vyāsa] says — disease to be removed, cause of disease, freedom from disease, and the means of removal (medicine) — so the science of yoga has four parts: saṁsāra, the cause of saṁsāra, freedom from saṁsāra, and the means of liberation" (Bryant on YS 2.15). Bryant continues: "Śaṅkara quotes this fourfold division of Vyāsa in the opening words of his *Vivaraṇa* commentary on the first sūtra of the Yoga Sūtras." Two of the three foundational commentators on the YS — Vyāsa and Śaṅkara — open their exegesis by identifying yoga-śāstra with cikitsā-śāstra.

### Mallinson (on the migration of the purification vocabulary)

James Mallinson, in his translator's introduction to the *Gheraṇḍa Saṁhitā*, notes that the *ghaṭa-yoga* of Gheraṇḍa is built around a sevenfold path where the first limb is *śodhana* (purification) accomplished via the six cleansing techniques — dhauti, basti, neti, etc. — and goes on to show that the same six practices are known from earlier and concurrent tantric texts. The Āyurvedic pañca-karma vocabulary leaks into the Haṭha-yoga manuals from the beginning of the genre. The two corpora are cross-referencing each other for centuries. See *Gheraṇḍa Saṁhitā* I.9: "Purification, strength, steadiness, calmness, lightness, realization, and abstraction are the seven means of perfecting the body. The six cleansing techniques bring about purification and āsanas bring about strength; mudrās bring about steadiness and pratyāhāra brings about calmness. From prāṇāyāma, lightness arises and from dhyāna, realization of the self. Through samādhi arises abstraction and liberation itself; in this there is no doubt" (Mallinson, trans.).

Gheraṇḍa's seven limbs are not a contradiction of Patañjali's eight; they are a reorganization that foregrounds the *śodhana* stage (which Patañjali compresses into śauca, the first niyama). In Āyurvedic terms, Gheraṇḍa is saying: before any yama-niyama work, the body must first undergo purification — which is a pre-pañca-karma prescription. The yoga tradition itself here absorbs the Āyurvedic insight that therapy precedes discipline.

### Muktibodhananda Swami (Bihar School commentary on HYP)

Muktibodhananda, commenting on HYP 1.57: "Two others specifications are also given here: moderate diet and contemplation on ātmā. A pure and moderate diet helps establish equilibrium and creates conditions conducive for higher experiences. **In fact, the basic cause of disease can be attributed to faulty diet.**"

And on HYP 1.58: "Mitāhāra means 'sāttvic food,' light food which is easy to digest. As Swami Sivānanda of Rishikesh points out, 'Heavy food leads to a tāmasic state and induces sleep only. There is a general misapprehension that a large quantity of food is necessary for health and strength. Much depends upon the power of assimilation and absorption. Generally, in the vast majority of cases, most of the food passes away undigested along with feces... Almost all diseases are due to irregularity of meals, overeating and unwholesome food.' His maxim is that through overeating you become a *rogī* [diseased one], through sāttvic diet a *yogī*."

The rogī-yogī distinction is the unity thesis in one word-pair. A rogī is a not-yet-yogī. A yogī is a not-rogī. The difference is not ontological; it is methodological. It is a difference of *āhāra*.

### Muktibodhananda on the ṣaṭ-karma and kośa

"According to yoga, human existence extends through five layers or sheaths which are called *kośas*. The physical body and its elements comprise the first layer, *annamaya kośa*. *Anna* is 'food,' *maya* means 'comprised of.' The ṣaṭ-karma directly influence this *kośa* and penetrate the next layer, *prāṇamaya kośa*, as they allow free flow of prāṇa. The third layer, *manomaya kośa*, the 'mental sheath,' is indirectly affected through prāṇāyāma. Purification of these sheaths opens the fourth *kośa*, *vijñānamaya*" (Muktibodhananda on HYP 2.27–28).

The annamaya-kośa vocabulary is Upanishadic (Taittirīya II). It is the same hierarchy used by Caraka when he speaks of *rasa* (the first nutritive essence of food) being the first of seven dhātus. Food-sheath becomes rasa-dhātu becomes rakta becomes māṁsa becomes medas becomes asthi becomes majjā becomes śukra — the sevenfold bodily transformation of the annamaya-kośa into the densest tissues and finest essence (ojas). The yogic kośa-model and the Āyurvedic dhātu-model are two projections of the same underlying architecture.

### Svātmārāma's own framing (HYP 2.1)

**HYP 2.1**:
> "Thus being established in āsana and having control (of the body), taking a balanced diet; prāṇāyāma should be practiced according to the instructions of the guru."

Muktibodhananda's gloss: "Only when the body is regulated by āsana and moderate diet should a sādhaka begin the next stage of haṭha yoga, i.e. prāṇāyāma ... Eating is a direct means of prāṇic absorption, which affects the body, mind and prāṇic vibrations."

Svātmārāma refuses to move from āsana to prāṇāyāma without first specifying *diet*. This is not a stray interpolation. It is the structural claim that the yogic limbs *cannot be done* on an ill-fed body. Which is the claim of the whole Āyurvedic tradition: health is the precondition of all higher attainment. Without *svastha*, no samādhi. Without *samādhi*, no *svasthya* in the full sense.

### The convergence

Bryant's Western exegesis, Mallinson's philology of Haṭha-yoga, Muktibodhananda's living-lineage commentary, Svātmārāma's own text, Vāgbhaṭa's compendium, Caraka's root treatise, and Patañjali's aphorisms all converge on the same proposition: Yoga and Āyurveda are two faces of one lineage, and the face you see depends on the angle from which you read. Look at them as cosmological / soteriological texts and they look like yoga. Look at them as clinical / diagnostic texts and they look like medicine. Look at them from above, and they look like one text.

The keystone remains the one verse. Write it on the title page of the book:

> *sama-doṣaḥ samāgniś ca sama-dhātu-mala-kriyaḥ |*
> *prasanna-ātmendriya-manāḥ svastha ity abhidhīyate ||*

Whoever's doṣas are in equilibrium, whose agni is balanced, whose dhātus and malas function in equilibrium, whose self, senses, and mind are settled and luminous — *that one is called svastha*. That one is in samādhi. That one has arrived at the goal of both disciplines. There is no two; there is only one.
