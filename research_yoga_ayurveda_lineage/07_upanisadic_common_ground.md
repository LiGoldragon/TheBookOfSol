# Upaniṣadic Common Ground

*Angle*: The Upaniṣads are the shared soil from which both Yoga and Āyurveda
grew. Root concepts — ātman, prāṇa, anna, rasa, amṛta, pañca-kośa, pañca-prāṇa,
the four states of consciousness, and the first technical use of the word
*yoga* itself — were given their classical formulation in the Upaniṣads, and
both Yoga and Āyurveda draw their vocabulary from this single well. Kaṭha
2.3.10–11 is where *yoga* first receives its classical definition. Śvetāśvatara
2.8–15 is the oldest surviving explicit description of yoga-practice. Praśna
3.4–10 is the canonical pañca-prāṇa teaching. Taittirīya 2 and 3 are the
pañca-kośa / Bhṛgu-vallī. These four receive full treatment below.

## Taittirīya — pañca-kośa and "annaṁ brahma"

The Taittirīya Upaniṣad (Taittirīya Āraṇyaka 7–9, Black Yajurveda) is the
locus classicus for the *pañca-kośa* anthropology that became, under different
names, the bodymap of both haṭha-yoga and Āyurveda: a human being is an onion
of five sheaths — food (annamaya), breath (prāṇamaya), mind (manomaya),
understanding (vijñānamaya), bliss (ānandamaya) — each successive sheath
"other than and within" (anyo 'ntara) the previous, each rebuilt from the
materials of the previous, each the body-of the next. The whole Āyurvedic
thesis that a human being is what he eats because he is *digested food at
every level* is already stated here, in a text centuries older than Caraka.

TU 2.1 (Hume trans., with IAST restored from the Sanskrit pāṭha):

> *satyam jñānam anantam brahma / yo veda nihitaṁ guhāyāṁ parame vyoman / so
> 'śnute sarvān kāmān saha brahmaṇā vipaścitā.*
>
> "He who knows Brahma as the real (satya), as knowledge (jñāna), as the
> infinite (ananta), set down in the secret place [of the heart] and in the
> highest heaven (parame vyoman), — he obtains all desires, together with the
> intelligent (vipaścit) Brahma."
> — TU 2.1 (Hume 1921, p. 283)

Then comes the great evolution-of-substance formula that every later
Āyurvedic cosmology inherits:

> *tasmād vā etasmād ātmana ākāśaḥ sambhūtaḥ / ākāśād vāyuḥ / vāyor agniḥ /
> agner āpaḥ / adbhyaḥ pṛthivī / pṛthivyā oṣadhayaḥ / oṣadhībhyo 'nnam / annāt
> puruṣaḥ.*
>
> "From this Soul (ātman), verily, space (ākāśa) arose; from space, wind
> (vāyu); from wind, fire; from fire, water; from water, the earth; from the
> earth, herbs (oṣadhi); from herbs, food (anna); from food, semen; from
> semen, the person (puruṣa)."
> — TU 2.1 (Hume p. 283)

This is the precise chain that Caraka and Suśruta quote when they explain
*dhātu-poṣaṇa* — the transformation of food through rasa-rakta-māṁsa-medas
into ojas. The Upaniṣad has already given the ladder; the Saṁhitās just
refine the rungs. *Oṣadhi* (herb) is both the botanical raw material of food
and the pharmacopoeia of medicine; the same word does both jobs because in
the Upaniṣadic frame food and medicine are not two categories.

The famous food-gāthā of TU 2.2 is the foundational Indian statement that
existence is a food-chain:

> *annād vai prajāḥ prajāyante yāḥ kāś ca pṛthivīṁ śritāḥ / atho annenaiva
> jīvanti athainad apiyanty antataḥ. annaṁ hi bhūtānāṁ jyeṣṭham tasmāt
> sarvauṣadham ucyate.*
>
> "From food, verily, creatures are produced, whatsoever creatures dwell on
> the earth. Moreover by food, in truth, they live. Moreover into it also they
> finally pass. For truly, food is the chief of beings; therefore it is called
> a panacea (*sarvauṣadha*)."
> — TU 2.2 (Hume p. 284)

*Sarvauṣadha* — "consisting of all herbs / the whole medicine" — is the
sūtra of Āyurveda four centuries before the word *Āyurveda* is coined.

> *annād bhavanti bhūtāni / jātāny annena vardhante / adyate atti ca bhūtāni
> tasmād annaṁ tad ucyate.*
>
> "From food created things are born. By food, when born, do they grow up.
> It both is eaten and eats things. Because of that it is called food (anna,
> from the root *ad-*)."
> — TU 2.2 (Hume p. 284)

Food is what eats and is eaten; the body is the middle term. The same
verse appears verbatim at Maitrī 6.11.

Then the fivefold body is laid out. TU 2.2 (Hume p. 284): "The in-breath
(prāṇa) is its head; the diffused breath (vyāna), the right wing; the
out-breath (apāna), the left wing; space, the body (ātman); the earth, the
lower part, the foundation." TU 2.3: "The gods do breathe along with breath
(prāṇa), as also men and beasts. For truly, breath is the life (āyus) of
beings; therefore it is called the life-of-all (*sarvāyuṣa*)." TU 2.4 names
the *manomaya* sheath: "Verily, other than and within that one that consists
of breath is a self that consists of mind (mano-maya)." TU 2.4 continues:
"The Yajur-Veda is its head; the Ṛg-Veda, the right side; the Sāma-Veda, the
left side; teaching, the body (ātman); the Hymns of the Atharvans and
Aṅgirases, the lower part." The sheath of mind is textual: mind is made of
mantra.

TU 2.4–5 (the vijñānamaya kośa):

> "Verily, other than and within that one that consists of mind is a self
> that consists of understanding (vijñāna-maya). … Faith (śraddhā) is its
> head; the right (ṛta), the right side; the true (satya), the left side;
> *yoga*, the body (ātman); might (mahas), the lower part, the foundation."
> — TU 2.4 (Hume p. 285)

In the sheath of discernment, *yoga* is itself the *ātman* — the first
Upaniṣadic use of the word anatomically, foreshadowing Kaṭha.

TU 2.5 names the innermost sheath as *ānanda-maya*: "Pleasure (priya) is
its head; delight (moda), the right side; great delight (pra-moda), the
left side; bliss (ānanda), the body (ātman); Brahma, the lower part, the
foundation." The innermost sheath is not *nothing* but *bliss* — ānanda
as substance, not absence. This underwrites the Āyurvedic teaching that
*ojas* is a substance of delight, not a metabolic surplus.

TU 2.7 grounds the whole cosmos on *rasa*:

> *raso vai saḥ / rasaṁ hy evāyaṁ labdhvā ānandī bhavati / ko hy evānyāt
> kaḥ prāṇyāt yad eṣa ākāśa ānando na syāt.*
>
> "Verily, what that well-done is—that, verily, is the essence (*rasa*) [of
> existence]. For truly, on getting the essence, one becomes blissful. For
> who indeed would breathe, who would live, if there were not this bliss in
> space! For truly, this (essence) causes bliss."
> — TU 2.7 (Hume pp. 286–287)

Rasa — the very word Āyurveda takes for its first *dhātu*, the essence of
digested food from which all the other tissues derive — is declared by the
Taittirīya to be identical with brahman. This is why the Caraka Saṁhitā
(Sūtrasthāna 25.40) can say that rasa is *prāṇa*: the identification was
already authorized in the Upaniṣad the Caraka school recited in morning
prayer.

### TU 3.1–6 — the Bhṛgu-vallī: progressive discovery by *tapas*

Where the Brahmānanda-vallī (TU 2) gives the kośas from outside in, the
Bhṛgu-vallī (TU 3) gives them as a *progressive discovery* — a sādhanā,
not a doctrine. Young Bhṛgu asks his father Varuṇa for the knowledge of
Brahma; Varuṇa's answer is not a lecture but a method: *tapasā brahma
vijijñāsasva / tapo brahmeti* — "desire to understand Brahma by austerity.
Austerity is Brahma."

Each successive answer is obtained only after *tapas*. Varuṇa gives five
recursive insights, each the same form:

> "He performed austerity. Having performed austerity, he understood that
> Brahma is **food** (anna) / **breath** (prāṇa) / **mind** (manas) /
> **understanding** (vijñāna) / **bliss** (ānanda). For truly, indeed,
> beings here are born from [it], when born they live by [it], on
> deceasing they enter into [it]."
> — TU 3.2–6 (Hume p. 290)

The insight is not *given*; it is *cooked* (*paktvā*) out of the seeker by
tapas. This is the template: every level of realization must be earned by
the same fire that cooks the food the body lives on. Yoga's later insistence
that *jñāna* without *tapas* is empty — and Āyurveda's insistence that
*agni* (digestive fire) is the condition of every other substance in the
body — both trace back to this narrative. *Tapas* is the single root of
yogic discipline and Āyurvedic agni; the Bhṛgu-vallī is where the two
become a single image.

TU 3.10 seals the whole vallī with the *mahā-vākya* of food-yoga:

> "I am food! I am food! I am food! I am a food-eater! I am a food-eater!
> I am a food-eater! … I, who am food, eat the eater of food! I have overcome
> the whole world!"
> — TU 3.10.6 (Hume p. 293)

This is not poetry. It is the cosmological-somatic identity statement that
the self's supremacy lies in its ability to remain both what is eaten and
what eats. The Āyurvedic teaching that the skilled eater *becomes* his food
— that one *is* what one digests — follows directly.

## Chāndogya — the threefold transformation of food

The Chāndogya Upaniṣad (the Sāma-Vedic counterpart to the Bṛhadāraṇyaka)
contains, in its sixth book, the anna-vidyā that Āyurveda cites as the
original source of its own doctrine of metabolism. Uddālaka Āruṇi, teaching
his son Śvetaketu, begins with the most famous cosmogonic declaration in
Indian thought:

> *sad eva somyedam agra āsīd ekam evādvitīyam.*
>
> "In the beginning, my dear, this world was just Being (sat), one only,
> without a second (ekam evādvitīyam)."
> — ChU 6.2.1 (Hume p. 241)

From *sat* emerge *tejas* (heat), *āp* (water), and *anna* (food). ChU 6.3
gives the threefold evolution: *sat* enters these three divinities "with
this living ātman" (*anena jīvenātmanā*) and separates out *nāma-rūpa*.
Already the ātman is the agent that takes in the three elemental
constituents and produces name-and-form — the exact role that ātman plays
in Caraka's *śārīra* where consciousness is the cause of the continual
remaking of body from food.

ChU 6.5 is the direct source of Āyurveda's *tri-bhāga* doctrine — that
everything eaten divides into three parts:

> "**Food**, when eaten, becomes divided into three parts. That which is its
> coarsest constituent (*sthaviṣṭha*) becomes the feces (mala); that which
> is medium (*madhyama*), the flesh (māṁsa); that which is finest
> (*aṇiṣṭha*), the mind (manas).
>
> **Water**, when drunk, becomes divided into three parts. That which is its
> coarsest constituent becomes the urine; that which is medium, the blood
> (lohita); that which is finest, the breath (prāṇa).
>
> **Heat** (tejas), when eaten [assimilated], becomes divided into three
> parts. That which is its coarsest constituent becomes bone (asthi); that
> which is medium, the marrow (majjan); that which is finest, the voice
> (vāk).
>
> For, my dear, the mind consists of food; the breath consists of water;
> the voice consists of heat (*annamayaṁ hi somya manaḥ, āpomayaḥ prāṇaḥ,
> tejomayī vāk*)."
> — ChU 6.5.1–4 (Hume p. 243)

This is the charter of the Āyurvedic *dhātu-poṣaṇa* sequence (rasa-rakta-
māṁsa-medas-asthi-majjā-śukra): the Upaniṣad has already said that food
becomes mala, māṁsa, manas — waste, flesh, and mind — with mind as the
*aṇiṣṭha* (subtlest) residue. Caraka's refinement adds more dhātus between
flesh and marrow; the logic is identical.

The great mantra — that mind is made of food — is then the lever on which
the whole ascetic-therapeutic tradition rests:

> *annamayaṁ hi somya manaḥ.*
>
> "For mind, dear boy, consists of food."
> — ChU 6.5.4 (Hume p. 243; cited also at Maitrī 6.11 and TU 3)

If mind *consists* of food, then what one eats is a direct determinant of
what one thinks — the premise of every sāttvika-āhāra prescription in the
Bhagavad-Gītā, the Gheraṇḍa-Saṁhitā, and the Caraka Saṁhitā alike. The
entire yogic discipline of *mitāhāra* (measured eating), and the entire
Āyurvedic discipline of *ahāra-vidhi* (rules of eating), are practical
applications of this single ChU 6.5.4 sentence.

ChU 6.6 then gives the churning-butter image of the finest essence rising:

> "Of coagulated milk, my dear, when churned, that which is the finest
> essence (*aṇimā*) all moves upward; it becomes butter. Even so, verily,
> my dear, of food, when eaten, that which is the finest essence all moves
> upward; it becomes the mind. Of water, my dear, when drunk, that which
> is the finest essence all moves upward; it becomes the breath. Of heat,
> my dear, when eaten, that which is the finest essence all moves upward;
> it becomes the voice."
> — ChU 6.6.1–4 (Hume p. 243)

This is the ōjas-doctrine of Āyurveda in nucleus: from every dhātu, a
finest portion (*aṇiṣṭha*) rises and becomes the substrate of the next,
culminating in the subtle substance that is the ground of mind and voice.
Caraka's *ojas*, Suśruta's *dhātu-prasāda*, Haṭha's *amarolī* — they all
flow from this churning image.

ChU 6.7 then conducts Śvetaketu's own pañca-kośa experiment: fifteen days
without food, only water; at the end he cannot recite the Veda; he eats
and the mind returns. The experiment *proves* that mind is made of food,
and equally proves that prāṇa (which does not fail while the boy drinks
water) is made of water. The yogic practice of fasting and the Āyurvedic
practice of *laṅghana* (therapeutic lightening) have their empirical
charter here.

From ChU 6.8 onward the refrain *tat tvam asi Śvetaketo* — "that art thou,
O Śvetaketu" — repeats nine times, driving home that the subtle essence
(aṇimā) which pervades everything is the very self of the hearer:

> "That which is the finest essence (*aṇimā*) — this whole world has that as
> its soul. That is Reality. That is ātman. That art thou (*tat tvam asi*),
> Śvetaketu."
> — ChU 6.8.7 (Hume p. 246; repeated at 6.9.4, 6.10.3, 6.11.3, 6.12.3,
>   6.13.3, 6.14.3, 6.15.3, 6.16.3)

The *same subtle essence* that food leaves as its finest residue is
identified with the ātman. The yogic goal and the Āyurvedic goal are thus
described in a single idiom: to realize the ātman is to realize the finest
residue of everything eaten. This is why the yogic and Āyurvedic
literatures are untranslatably welded together in the later tradition.

## Bṛhadāraṇyaka — *amṛtaṁ vai prāṇāḥ*

The Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad (Śukla-Yajurveda), the oldest and longest of
the corpus, supplies four load-bearing teachings that both Yoga and
Āyurveda quote as source material.

### BU 1.3 (Kāṇva) / 6.1 (Mādhyandina) — the contest of the vital functions

The most-cited passage of the whole Upaniṣadic corpus for the *supremacy
of prāṇa* is the prāṇa-saṁvāda: the vital functions dispute which of them
is the best. Each in turn departs for a year, and each time the body
survives in an impaired form; but when Breath prepares to leave, it
threatens to pull all the others up "as a large fine horse of the Indus
country might pull up the pegs of his foot-tethers together." The other
functions beg it to stay.

> "These vital breaths (prāṇa), disputing among themselves on
> self-superiority, went to Brahma. Then they said: 'Which of us is the most
> excellent?' … Speech went off. Having remained away a year, it came back
> and said: 'How have you been able to live without me?' They said: 'As the
> dumb, not speaking with speech, but breathing with breath, seeing with the
> eye, hearing with the ear, knowing with the mind, procreating with semen.
> Thus have we lived.' Speech entered in. … Then Breath was about to go off.
> As a large fine horse of the Indus-land might pull up the pegs of his
> foot-tethers together, thus indeed did it pull up those vital breaths
> together. They said: 'Sir, go not off! Verily, we shall not be able to live
> without you!'"
> — BU 6.1.7–13 (Hume pp. 158–159) / BU 1.3.2–7 (Olivelle 1998, pp. 45–47)

The conclusion: breath is not *one* faculty among many but the substrate
of all. Every subsequent prāṇāyāma in the yoga literature, every
*vāta-prakopa* diagnostic in Āyurveda, is a practical consequence of this
verdict. In *Patañjali-Yogasūtra* 1.34 "*pracchardana-vidhāraṇābhyāṁ vā
prāṇasya*" and in *Caraka Sūtra* 12 ("*vāyur tantra-yantra-dharaḥ* — vāyu
holds the body's mechanism") the authors are simply applying the BU
charter.

### BU 1.5.3 — manas, vāk, prāṇa

> "There are three worlds: this [earth] world, the intermediate world,
> and the world beyond. This [earth] world is speech (vāk); the intermediate
> world, the mind (manas); the world beyond, the breath (prāṇa). …
> These are indeed the three Vedas: speech is the Ṛgveda, mind the
> Yajurveda, breath the Sāmaveda."
> — BU 1.5.3–5 (Olivelle 1998, p. 55)

The triad speech–mind–breath maps cleanly onto the three *doṣas* (vāta
being linked to prāṇa, pitta to mental heat, kapha to bodily substance in
the later reception) and equally onto the three *guṇas* of the yoga-
darśana. One Upaniṣadic triad; two downstream applications.

### BU 2.4 — Yājñavalkya and Maitreyī

Yājñavalkya tells Maitreyī before entering the forest that wealth cannot
make one immortal, and then delivers the most important statement in all
Indian literature about the object of desire:

> "Lo, verily, not for love of the husband is a husband dear, but for love
> of the Soul (ātmanas tu kāmāya) a husband is dear. Lo, verily, not for
> love of the wife is a wife dear, but for love of the Soul a wife is dear.
> … Lo, verily, not for love of all is all dear, but for love of the Soul
> all is dear. Lo, verily, it is the Soul (Ātman) that should be seen, that
> should be hearkened to, that should be thought on, that should be pondered
> on, O Maitreyī. Lo, verily, with the seeing of, with the hearkening to,
> with the thinking of, and with the understanding of the Soul, this
> world-all is known (*ātmani khalv are dṛṣṭe śrute mate vijñāta idaṁ
> sarvaṁ viditam*)."
> — BU 2.4.5 (Hume pp. 98–99)

The fourfold hermeneutic — *śravaṇa, manana, nididhyāsana, sākṣātkāra*
(seeing, hearing, reflecting, realizing) — is the first schematic
description of the yogic interior method, later formalized as *śravaṇa-
manana-nididhyāsana* in Vedānta and as the *saṁyama* of Patañjali YS 3.4.

And the lump-of-salt-in-water image (BU 2.4.12) is the direct source of
the Advaitic and Āyurvedic image of the body as a solution in which a
single essence is pervasive but not locally seizable:

> "It is — as a lump of salt cast in water would dissolve right into the
> water; there would not be any of it to seize forth, as it were, but
> wherever one may take, it is salty indeed — so, lo, verily, this great
> Being (bhūta), infinite, limitless, is just a mass of knowledge
> (*vijñāna-ghana*)."
> — BU 2.4.12 (Hume p. 101)

### BU 3.9.26 — *neti neti*

The apophatic mahā-vākya. Yājñavalkya tells Śākalya:

> *sa eṣa neti nety ātmā gṛhyo na hi gṛhyate / aśīryo na hi śīryate /
> asaṅgo na hi sajyate / asito na vyathate / na riṣyati.*
>
> "That Soul (Ātman) is not this, it is not that (neti, neti). It is
> unseizable, for it is not seized. It is indestructible, for it is not
> destroyed. It is unattached, for it does not attach itself. It is
> unbound. It does not tremble. It is not injured."
> — BU 3.9.26 (Hume p. 135)

*Neti neti* becomes the technical procedure of apophatic meditation in the
*Vedānta* and, transposed, the *nirodha* of PYS 1.2 — the progressive
elimination of every determinable modification of citta. Both yogas, the
contemplative and the dietary, advance by subtracting.

### BU 4.4.7 — the mokṣa-sūtra

> "When are liberated all the desires that lodge in one's heart, then a
> mortal becomes immortal! Therein he reaches Brahma! … When are cut all
> the knots of the heart (*hṛdayasya granthayaḥ*) here on earth, then a
> mortal becomes immortal — thus far is the teaching."
> — BU 4.4.7 (Hume p. 141; cf. KaU 6.15, MuU 3.2.9)

The "knots of the heart" (*hṛdaya-granthi*) are literally named — three of
them are canonically called *brahma-granthi, viṣṇu-granthi, rudra-granthi*
in the later haṭha-yoga literature (Haṭha-Yoga-Pradīpikā 3.1, 3.5), and
the piercing of these granthis is the explicit goal of kuṇḍalinī practice.
The same word *granthi* in Āyurveda designates a pathological swelling
(Suśruta Nidāna 11); the difference is that Yoga takes it as the object
of *sādhanā* and Āyurveda as the object of *cikitsā*. Both use the
Upaniṣadic word; both apply it to the same anatomical-energetic site.

### BU 2.1 — *amṛtaṁ vai prāṇāḥ*

> "The breaths (*prāṇāḥ*), verily, are the immortal. Truth is the clothing
> of the immortal."
> — BU 2.1.20 / 2.4 (cf. Olivelle p. 67; Hume p. 96)

*Amṛtaṁ vai prāṇāḥ* — "breaths are indeed the immortal" — is the verse
that licenses both the Yoga-tradition's claim that prāṇāyāma confers
*amṛtatva* and the Āyurveda-tradition's claim that prāṇa is the medium
in which *ojas* (the subtle immortal essence) is carried.

## Kaṭha — the first definition of yoga

The Kaṭha Upaniṣad (Kṛṣṇa-Yajurveda) narrates young Naciketas' encounter
with Yama (Death), from whom he demands the knowledge of what survives.
The Kaṭha is where the word *yoga* first receives its classical Indian
definition, and where the *ratha-saṁjñaka* (chariot simile) — later used
in the Bhagavad-Gītā 6 and in Caraka Śārīra 1 — is first given.

### KU 1.3.3–9 — the chariot simile

> *ātmānaṁ rathinaṁ viddhi śarīraṁ ratham eva ca / buddhiṁ tu sārathiṁ
> viddhi manaḥ pragraham eva ca. indriyāṇi hayān āhur viṣayāṁs teṣu
> gocarān / ātmendriya-mano-yuktaṁ bhoktety āhur manīṣiṇaḥ.*
>
> "Know the self as a rider in a chariot, and the body, as simply the
> chariot. Know the intellect (buddhi) as the charioteer, and the mind
> (manas), as simply the reins. The senses (indriyāṇi), they say, are the
> horses, and sense objects are the paths around them; he who is linked to
> the body, senses, and mind, the wise proclaim as the one who enjoys.
>
> When a man lacks understanding, and his mind is never controlled, his
> senses do not obey him, as bad horses, a charioteer. But when a man has
> understanding, and his mind is ever controlled, his senses do obey him,
> as good horses, a charioteer.
>
> When a man lacks understanding, is unmindful and always impure, he does
> not reach that final step, but gets on the round of rebirth. But when a
> man has understanding, is mindful and always pure, he does reach that
> final step, from which he is not reborn again.
>
> When a man's mind is his reins, intellect, his charioteer, he reaches the
> end of the road, that highest step of Viṣṇu."
> — KU 1.3.3–9 (Olivelle 1998, pp. 388–389)

This is the *first* appearance in Indian literature of the psychological
hierarchy that Patañjali will crystallize as *citta, buddhi, manas,
indriyāṇi* and that Caraka will crystallize as *ātmā, manas, indriya,
buddhi* (Caraka Sūtra 8.4; Śārīra 1.20, which cites Kaṭha). Both the
Yoga-darśana and the Āyurveda-darśana inherit the hierarchy from here.

### KU 1.3.10–11 — *parāvara-krama*

> "Higher than the senses are their objects; higher than sense objects is
> the mind; higher than the mind is the intellect; higher than the intellect
> is the immense self (mahān ātmā). Higher than the immense self is the
> unmanifest (avyakta); higher than the unmanifest is the person (puruṣa).
> Higher than the person there's nothing at all. That is the goal, that's
> the highest state."
> — KU 1.3.10–11 (Olivelle p. 389)

The sequence *indriya < artha < manas < buddhi < mahān < avyakta <
puruṣa* is the first articulation of what will become the Sāṁkhya
*tattva*-ladder. Sāṁkhya is the shared metaphysics of both Yoga and
Āyurveda (Caraka Sūtra 1.17 calls *Sāṁkhya-pāribhāṣā* his authority for
all ontology). The ladder is already here in the Kaṭha.

### KU 2.3.10–11 — *tāṁ yogam iti manyante*

This is the single most important passage in the whole Upaniṣadic corpus
for the history of yoga — the *first technical, classical definition* of
yoga, the verse that Patañjali and every later commentator cite:

> *yadā pañcāvatiṣṭhante jñānāni manasā saha / buddhiś ca na viceṣṭati
> tām āhuḥ paramāṁ gatim. tāṁ yogam iti manyante sthirām indriya-dhāraṇām
> / apramattas tadā bhavati yogo hi prabhavāpyayau.*
>
> "When the five perceptions are stilled, together with the mind, and not
> even reason (buddhi) bestirs itself; they call it the highest state.
>
> When senses are firmly reined in (*sthirām indriya-dhāraṇām*), that is
> Yoga, so people think (*tāṁ yogam iti manyante*). From distractions a man
> is then free, for Yoga is the coming-into-being, as well as the
> ceasing-to-be (*yogo hi prabhavāpyayau*)."
> — KU 2.3.10–11 (Olivelle p. 401)

Four points deserve underlining.

*First*, *sthirām indriya-dhāraṇām* — "firm holding of the senses". The
word *dhāraṇā* is introduced here in its technical sense; Patañjali's
*dhāraṇā* (YS 3.1 "deśa-bandhaś cittasya dhāraṇā") is a refinement of
this Kaṭha usage.

*Second*, the stilling of the five *jñānas* (perceptions) together with
*manas* and *buddhi* is the exact hierarchy that Patañjali inherits as
the progressive cessation of *vṛtti* at PYS 1.2 (*yogaś citta-vṛtti-
nirodhaḥ*). The Kaṭha and the Yoga-sūtra are teaching the same thing in
near-identical vocabulary, separated by perhaps five to seven centuries.

*Third*, the word *yoga* in KU 2.3.11 is given a *double-valued*
definition: *yogo hi prabhavāpyayau* — yoga is both origination (*prabhava*)
and dissolution (*apyaya*). To practice yoga is to be vigilant at the
edge where the citta arises and dissolves. This is precisely the focus
of the later *nirodha-samāpatti* of PYS 1.51 and of the *udaya-vyaya*
attention of the Buddhist satipaṭṭhāna; the Kaṭha is prior to both.

*Fourth*, the phrase *apramattas tadā bhavati* ("then he becomes
un-heedless") introduces *apramāda* as the *condition* of yoga. This is
the exact word that Caraka uses in Vimāna 3.38 and elsewhere for the
attention that the physician must bring to pulse, to agni, to symptom;
and it is the exact word the Dhammapada (II.21–24, *appamāda-vagga*) uses
as the pith of the Buddhist path. One Upaniṣadic term fathers three
disciplines.

### KU 2.3.14–18 — the knots of the heart and the 101 nāḍīs

The Kaṭha ends with the anatomical-esoteric teaching that directly feeds
both haṭha-yoga and Āyurvedic *nāḍī*-mapping:

> "When the knots are all cut, that bind one's heart on earth; then a
> mortal becomes immortal — for such is the teaching. *One hundred and one,
> the veins of the heart* (*śataṁ caikā ca hṛdayasya nāḍyaḥ*). One of them
> runs up to the crown of the head. Going up by it, he reaches the immortal.
> The rest, in their ascent, spread out in all directions."
> — KU 2.3.15–16 (Olivelle pp. 401–402)

The count of 101 nāḍīs and the singling-out of the central channel
(*suṣumnā* in the later literature) to the *brahma-randhra* at the crown
is literally here. Haṭha-Yoga-Pradīpikā 4.16 quotes this Kaṭha verse.
Caraka Śārīra 7 and Suśruta Śārīra 5 both list nāḍīs in the same
language. The yogic *cakra*-map and the Āyurvedic *marman*-map both
branch from this single Upaniṣadic root.

KU 2.3.18 closes with the explicit word *yoga-vidhi* — "the rules of yoga":

> "Then, after Naciketas received this body of knowledge, and the entire
> set of *yogic rules* taught by Death, he attained brahman; he became
> free from aging and death (*jarā-maraṇa-nirmuktaḥ*); so will others who
> know this teaching about the self."
> — KU 2.3.18 (Olivelle p. 402)

*Yoga-vidhi* — the procedural, rule-governed discipline of yoga — is
introduced as a known category by the author of the Kaṭha. It is not yet
the eightfold path of Patañjali, but it is already not a vague mysticism;
it is a rule-book. And the reward — *jarā-maraṇa-nirmukti*, freedom from
aging and death — is the exact language that Caraka Sūtra 1.39–41 and
Vāgbhaṭa Sūtra 1.2 will use for *rasāyana* (Āyurvedic rejuvenation).

## Praśna — the five prāṇas

The Praśna Upaniṣad (Atharvaveda) is organized as six questions to the
sage Pippalāda. The third question — asked by Kauśalya Āśvalāyana — is
the canonical pañca-prāṇa teaching, the verse that every later yoga and
Āyurveda text quotes when it lists the five breaths.

> "Then Kauśalya Āśvalāyana asked him: 'Lord, from what does this
> lifebreath arise? How does it enter this body? How does it divide itself
> and settle down? By what path does it set off? How is it designated
> outside the body (ātman) and how within the body?'
>
> He told Kauśalya: 'You ask too many questions! But since you are a very
> eminent Brahmin, I will answer you.
>
> The lifebreath here arises from the self (ātman). As this shadow here,
> upon a man, so this mind is stretched upon lifebreath; and it enters by
> a path created by the mind."
> — PU 3.1–3 (Olivelle 1998, pp. 465)

Then the fivefold distribution:

> "As only a sovereign king appoints administrators, telling them, 'You
> govern these villages, and you govern these other villages,' in just the
> same way the lifebreath here assigns the other breaths to their
> respective places.
>
> The in-breath (apāna) settles in the anus and the sexual organ, while
> the lifebreath (prāṇa) itself settles in sight and hearing through the
> mouth and nostrils. The link-breath (samāna), on the other hand, settles
> in the midregion, for it makes the food that has been offered alike
> (sama). And from it arise these seven flames here.
>
> The self (ātman) resides within the very heart. There are a hundred and
> one veins here in the body. Each of them branch into a hundred more, and
> each of these branches into seventy-two thousand more. Along these veins
> travels the link-breath.
>
> Now, the up-breath (udāna) rises up along one of these and conducts a
> person to a good world if he has done good deeds, to a bad world if he
> has done bad deeds, and to the world of men if he has done both.
>
> Now, the sun rises as the external lifebreath, for this conforms to the
> lifebreath residing within sight. The deity in the earth — that deity is
> there on account of the in-breath of a person. The space that is in
> between is the link-breath, and the wind is the inter-breath (vyāna).
>
> The up-breath is fire. Therefore, when one's fire is extinguished, one
> returns again to the lifebreath with the faculties uniting in the mind
> and with whatever thought one then has. United with heat, then, the
> lifebreath, together with the self, leads him to the world that accords
> with his conception.
>
> When a man knows this and thus understands the lifebreath, his line of
> progeny will not be cut off, and he will become immortal. On this there
> is this verse:
>
>     *The origin, entrance, position,
>        the fivefold expansion of lifebreath;
>        how it relates to what's in the body —
>     A man who knows this becomes immortal.*"
> — PU 3.4–12 (Olivelle pp. 465–466)

This is the *single verbatim source* for the pañca-vāyu doctrine of:

— Caraka Cikitsā 28.6: *prāṇo 'pānaḥ samāno 'nyau vyāna udāna eva ca* —
"prāṇa, apāna, samāna, and the two others, vyāna and udāna" — five
vāyus with the same placements as Praśna 3.

— Suśruta Nidāna 1.13, Vāgbhaṭa Sūtra 12.4–9, and every later Āyurvedic
*vāyu-viveka*: each cites or paraphrases Praśna 3.

— Patañjali's PYS 2.49–51 (prāṇāyāma) and especially the Vyāsa-bhāṣya
on it, which names the five breaths with the placements of PU 3.5.

— Haṭha-Yoga-Pradīpikā 3.72–74 and Gheraṇḍa-Saṁhitā 5 on the five vāyus.

In Praśna 3 the breath-doctrine is already anatomically specific (apāna
*low*, prāṇa at mouth-nose, samāna at midregion, udāna rising, vyāna
pervading) and cosmologically specific (breath = solar, samāna =
atmospheric space, vyāna = wind). The *one text* is the parent of both
the yogic and the medical treatments. Any attempt to separate them into
distinct lineages fails at Praśna 3.

Praśna 4 then gives the four-states doctrine of sleep and dreaming, which
is the parent of Māṇḍūkya's turīya and of Caraka's *nidrā-prakaraṇa*
(Sūtra 21). Praśna 5 is the meditation on OṂ. Praśna 6 names the sixteen
*kalā* (parts) of the puruṣa — *prāṇa*, *śraddhā*, *ākāśa*, *vāyu*,
*jyotis*, *āpa*, *pṛthivī*, *indriya*, *manas*, *anna*, *vīrya*, *tapas*,
*mantra*, *karma*, *loka*, *nāman* — the earliest explicit list of the
constituents of a human being, which Caraka Śārīra 3 takes over in
modified form.

## Śvetāśvatara — the oldest explicit yoga instructions

The Śvetāśvatara Upaniṣad (also Kṛṣṇa-Yajurveda) is the key bridging
text: it belongs to the late-Upaniṣadic layer, is strongly theistic
(Rudra-Śiva as Īśvara), and contains in its second *adhyāya* the oldest
detailed description of yoga-practice we possess. The Śvetāśvatara is
also saturated with medical-anthropological language — *dhātu, agni,
nāḍī, jarā, vyādhi, vāyu, rasa* — and is therefore the single clearest
demonstration that yoga in its classical form arose in a matrix that
was also the matrix of Āyurveda.

### SU 2.1–7 — the yoking of mind

The opening of Adhyāya 2 is a ṛṣika re-yoking of old Savitṛ hymns to
serve a yogic intent:

> "Yoking first his mind, and extending then his thoughts, Savitṛ, having
> recognized the fire as the light, brought it here from the earth. With
> minds yoked, we [make the offering] under the stimulus of the god Savitṛ
> for a heavenly abode, for strength. Yoking the gods, as they go to heaven
> with their mind and to the firmament with their thought, may Savitṛ
> stimulate them to create the lofty light. They yoke their minds, they
> yoke their thoughts, those inspired poets of the lofty poet. … I yoke
> with adorations the ancient formulation (brahman) of you two."
> — SU 2.1–5 (Olivelle 1998, pp. 417)

The ritual vocabulary of "yoking" (*yuj-*) — used in the old Vedic hymns
for the harnessing of horses to the sacrificial chariot — is here
explicitly transposed to the yoking of mind to meditation. This is the
semantic step by which the word *yoga* acquired its inner meaning.

### SU 2.8–15 — the posture, breath, place, and signs of yoga

The eight verses that follow are the oldest surviving how-to of yoga:

> *trir unnataṁ sthāpya samaṁ śarīraṁ hṛdīndriyāṇi manasā saṁniveśya /
> brahmoḍupena pratareta vidvān srotāṁsi sarvāṇi bhayāvahāni.*
>
> "**When he keeps his body straight, with the three sections erect
> (trir-unnataṁ śarīraṁ), and draws the senses together with the mind into
> his heart, a wise man shall cross all the frightful rivers with the boat
> consisting of that formulation (brahman).**"
> — SU 2.8 (Olivelle p. 418)

The *trir-unnata* posture — three erect parts (commentators list them as
chest, neck, head; or trunk, neck, head) — is the seed of the later
*āsana* tradition. Patañjali YS 2.46–48 (*sthira-sukham āsanam*) is the
refinement; the Gītā 6.13 (*samaṁ kāya-śiro-grīvaṁ*) is the direct heir.
The instruction "draw the senses into the heart with the mind"
(*hṛdīndriyāṇi manasā saṁniveśya*) is the seed of *pratyāhāra* (PYS 2.54).

> "Compressing his breaths in here and curbing his movements, a man should
> exhale through one nostril when his breath is exhausted. A wise man
> should keep his mind vigilantly under control, just as he would that
> wagon yoked to unruly horses."
> — SU 2.9 (Olivelle p. 418)

This is the first surviving description of *nāḍī-śodhana* — alternate-
nostril breathing. The procedure is *prāṇāyāma* in all but name.

> "Level and clean; free of gravel, fire, and sand; near noiseless running
> waters and the like; pleasing to the mind but not offensive to the eye;
> provided with a cave or a nook sheltered from the wind — in such a spot
> should one engage in yogic practice (*yoga-prayoga*)."
> — SU 2.10 (Olivelle p. 418)

The environment instruction is Āyurvedic in character: Suśruta Cikitsā
28 on *rasāyana* prescribes almost identical environmental conditions
for the rejuvenation retreat (*kūṭī-praveśika*). Gheraṇḍa-Saṁhitā 5.2–3
(on *deśa*) and Haṭha-Yoga-Pradīpikā 1.12–14 (on *maṭha*) are direct
descendants.

> *nīhāra-dhūmārkānilānalānāṁ khadyota-vidyut-sphaṭika-śaśīnām / etāni
> rūpāṇi puraḥsarāṇi brahmaṇy abhivyaktikarāṇi yoge.*
>
> "**Mist, smoke, sun, wind, fire, fireflies, lightning, crystal, moon —
> these are the apparitions that, within yogic practice, precede and pave
> the way to the full manifestation in brahman.**"
> — SU 2.11 (Olivelle p. 418)

The *nimitta* (precursor-sign) doctrine — that inner visions of certain
qualities (mist, fire, lightning) are stage-markers of meditative
progress — begins here. Every later yoga text that lists *yoga-nimittas*
(Amṛta-bindu, Nāda-bindu, Dhyāna-bindu, Yoga-tattva) is glossing SU 2.11.

> "When earth, water, fire, air, and ether have arisen together, and the
> body made up of these five becomes equipped with the attribute of yoga
> (*yogāgni-mayaṁ śarīraṁ*), that man, obtaining a body tempered by the
> fire of yoga, will no longer experience sickness, old age, or suffering.
> Lightness (laghimā), health (ārogyā), the absence of greed, a bright
> complexion, a pleasant voice, a sweet smell, and very little feces and
> urine (*alpa-mūtra-purīṣatā*) — that, they say, is the first working of
> yogic practice (*yoga-pravṛttiḥ*)."
> — SU 2.12–13 (Olivelle pp. 418–419)

The list of *yoga-siddhi* signs — lightness, health, clear complexion,
sweet voice, sweet smell, reduction of excreta — is *medical vocabulary*.
*Alpa-mūtra-purīṣatā* is what a physician would note on examination;
*ārogyā* is the direct Āyurvedic noun for health (Caraka Sūtra 1.15).
The Śvetāśvatara author understood yoga-practice as *simultaneously* a
therapeutic intervention. There is no separation of domains. The same
person who wrote SU 2 would have recognized in Caraka's *rasāyana-siddhi*
(Cikitsā 1) his own signs.

> "Just as a disk smeared with clay, once it is cleaned well, shines
> brightly, so also an embodied person, once he has perceived the true
> nature of the self, becomes solitary, his goal attained and free from
> sorrow. When, by means of the true nature of the self, which resembles
> a lamp, a man practicing yogic restraint sees here the true nature of
> brahman, he is freed from all fetters."
> — SU 2.14–15 (Olivelle p. 419)

The Śvetāśvatara's yoga-section ends on the *dīpa-upamā* (lamp simile)
that is the exact image Patañjali picks up at YS 3.5 (*tajjayāt prajñā-
lokaḥ*) and that Āyurveda adopts for *buddhi-agni* (the fire of
discernment, Caraka Vimāna 8.84). Same image, same text.

Earlier in SU 1.4–5 the Upaniṣad gives a cosmology in terms of a
five-streamed wheel, with an explicit reference to the *dhātus* (elements
/ bodily tissues) that Āyurveda takes over:

> "We understand him as a wheel with a single felly, triple-rimmed,
> sixteen-tipped, fifty-spoked, twenty-counter-spoked, and six-eighths; …
> a river of five streams, with five fierce and crooked currents, the
> waves of which are the five vital breaths (pañca-prāṇa), whose first
> spring is fivefold perception."
> — SU 1.4–5 (paraphrase, cf. Olivelle pp. 414–415)

The *pañca-prāṇa* here are equally the Āyurvedic vāyus and the yogic
breaths. The parentage is unitary.

## Māṇḍūkya — the four states of consciousness

The Māṇḍūkya, though only twelve verses, is the canonical teaching on
the four states (*catuṣpād ātmā*) that Caraka absorbs for his *nidrā-
prakaraṇa* (Caraka Sūtra 21) and that every later yogic *turīya-vāda*
takes as its substrate.

> "OṂ — this whole world is that syllable! … The past, the present, and the
> future — all that is simply OṂ; and whatever else that is beyond the three
> times, that also is simply OṂ — for this brahman is the Whole. Brahman
> is this self (ātman); that [brahman] is this self consisting of four
> quarters (*catuṣpāt*).
>
> **The first quarter is Vaiśvānara — the Universal One — situated in the
> waking state (jāgrat-sthānaḥ), perceiving what is outside, possessing
> seven limbs and nineteen mouths, and enjoying gross things.**
>
> **The second quarter is Taijasa — the Brilliant One — situated in the
> state of dream (svapna-sthānaḥ), perceiving what is inside, possessing
> seven limbs and nineteen mouths, and enjoying refined things.**
>
> **The third quarter is Prājña — the Intelligent One — situated in the
> state of deep sleep (suṣupta-sthānaḥ) — deep sleep is when a sleeping
> man entertains no desires or sees no dreams — become one, and thus being
> a single mass of perception; consisting of bliss, and thus enjoying
> bliss; and having thought as his mouth. He is the Lord of all; he is
> the knower of all; he is the inner controller; he is the womb of all —
> for he is the origin and the dissolution of beings.**
>
> **They consider the fourth quarter (caturthaḥ) as perceiving neither
> what is inside nor what is outside, nor even both together; not as a
> mass of perception, neither as perceiving nor as not perceiving; as
> unseen; as beyond the reach of ordinary transaction; as ungraspable;
> as without distinguishing marks; as unthinkable; as indescribable; as
> one whose essence is the perception of itself alone; as the cessation
> of the visible world; as tranquil; as auspicious; as without a second.
> That is the self (ātman), and it is that which should be perceived.**"
> — MaU 1–7 (Olivelle pp. 474–475)

The fourth quarter — *turīya* in the later tradition — is the irreducible
ground on which yogic *samādhi* (PYS 1.17–18; *saṁprajñāta*, *asaṁprajñāta*)
and Āyurvedic *nidrā-nidāna* (Caraka Sūtra 21.35: "sleep and waking are
the modes of the ātman, not of the body") both locate themselves. The
Māṇḍūkya gives the categories; the downstream disciplines inherit them.

MaU 8–12 identifies the three states with the three *mātrās* of OṂ — *a,
u, m* — and *turīya* with the *amātra* (without-measure) silence after
the syllable. The entire later yogic cultivation of *nāda* — from the
Nāda-bindu Upaniṣad through Haṭha-Yoga-Pradīpikā 4.66–102 — is a gloss
on MaU 8–12.

## Maitrī — six-limbed yoga before Patañjali

The Maitrī (Maitrāyaṇī) Upaniṣad is a composite late-Upaniṣadic text that
contains, in its sixth *prapāṭhaka*, the *six-limbed yoga* (*ṣaḍaṅga-yoga*)
that predates Patañjali's eight-limbed scheme and that uses openly
Āyurvedic vocabulary throughout.

> "Verily, in the beginning this world was Brahma, the limitless One …
> His is that shining form which gives heat in yonder sun and which is the
> brilliant light in a smokeless fire, as also the fire in the stomach
> which cooks food. For thus has it been said: 'He who is in the fire, and
> he who is here in the heart, and he who is yonder in the sun — he is
> one.'"
> — MaitU 6.17 (Hume pp. 434–435)

*The fire in the stomach which cooks food* — *jāṭharāgni*, the digestive
fire of Āyurveda — is here explicitly identified with the sun and with
brahman. This is not metaphor; in both traditions, *agni* is one
substance with three loci. Caraka Vimāna 1 makes this identity; Maitrī
6.17 is the Upaniṣadic authorization.

The ṣaḍaṅga-yoga itself is MaitU 6.18:

> *prāṇāyāmaḥ pratyāhāro dhyānaṁ dhāraṇā tarkaḥ samādhiḥ ṣaḍaṅga ity ucyate
> yogaḥ.*
>
> "**The precept for effecting this [unity] is this: restraint of the
> breath (prāṇāyāma), withdrawal of the senses (pratyāhāra), meditation
> (dhyāna), concentration (dhāraṇā), contemplation (tarka), absorption
> (samādhi). Such is said to be the sixfold Yoga (ṣaḍaṅga yoga).**"
> — MaitU 6.18 (Hume p. 435)

The six limbs of the Maitrī differ from Patañjali's eight (no *yama,
niyama, āsana*; *tarka* in place of Patañjali's *āsana*) and are in fact
closer to the later Bhagavad-Gītā 6 and to the *Yoga Upaniṣads* proper
(which often follow the ṣaḍaṅga scheme). The doctrine that *yoga* is a
structured ladder of limbs begins here.

MaitU 6.19 continues with the classic *laya*-formula:

> "Verily, when a knower has restrained his mind from the external, and
> the breathing spirit (prāṇa) has put to rest objects of sense, thereupon
> let him continue void of conceptions. Since the living individual (jīva)
> who is named 'breathing spirit' has arisen here from what is not
> breathing spirit, therefore, verily, let the breathing spirit restrain
> his breathing spirit in what is called the fourth condition (turya)."
> — MaitU 6.19 (Hume p. 436)

The fourth state, named *turya*, is the object of the yoga-discipline, and
it is reached by a prāṇa-based method. The practice proper is MaitU 6.20:

> "By pressing the tip of his tongue against the palate (*jihvāgram
> avadhāya tālu-madhye*), by restraining voice, mind, and breath, one sees
> Brahma through contemplation."
> — MaitU 6.20 (Hume p. 436)

The practice of pressing the tongue against the palate — later *nabho-mudrā*
/ *khecarī-mudrā* in the Haṭha corpus (Haṭha-Yoga-Pradīpikā 3.32–54,
Gheraṇḍa 3.25–28) — is already here. There is a direct line from MaitU
6.20 to khecarī. The medical-yogic overlap could not be more explicit:
what the Āyurvedic physician notes as an anatomical position of the
tongue, the yogin practices as a gateway to turya.

MaitU 6.21 speaks of the *suṣumnā-nāḍī* by that very name:

> "There is a channel (*nāḍī*) called Suṣumnā, leading upward, conveying
> the breath, piercing through the palate. Through it, by joining the
> breath, the syllable OṂ, and the mind, one may go aloft."
> — MaitU 6.21 (paraphrase following Hume p. 437)

This is the first explicit occurrence of *suṣumnā* in the Upaniṣadic
corpus, linked to the *tāluka* (palate) and *brahma-randhra* — the
anatomical ensemble that will dominate the entire haṭha-yogic literature.

MaitU 6.30 on *deśa* (place of practice) reads exactly like Suśruta's
prescription for the *rasāyana-śālā*:

> "There is a place, sheltered from wind, level, free of pebbles, fire,
> sand, rodents and noise, pleasing to the eye, not offensive to the ear …"
> — MaitU 6.30 (paraphrase; cf. Hume p. 443)

The direct parallel with SU 2.10 and with Suśruta Cikitsā 28 (on the
rejuvenation hut) is evidence of a shared technical literature.

## Muṇḍaka — epistemology and guru

The Muṇḍaka (Atharva) is short but contains four foundational statements.

### MuU 1.1.3–5 — the two knowledges

> "Two types of knowledge a man should learn — those who know brahman tell
> us — the higher and the lower (parā cāparā ca). The lower of the two
> consists of the Ṛgveda, the Yajurveda, the Sāmaveda, the Atharvaveda,
> phonetics, the ritual science, grammar, etymology, metrics, and astronomy;
> whereas the higher is that by which one grasps the imperishable."
> — MuU 1.1.4–5 (Olivelle pp. 437)

The *parā / aparā* distinction authorizes the Upaniṣadic literature's
treatment of the classical Veda as *aparā* (preliminary) and its own
teaching as *parā* (ultimate). Āyurveda's own self-description as *pañcama-
veda* ("the fifth Veda," Caraka Sūtra 1.7) takes for granted the Muṇḍaka
framework.

### MuU 1.2.12 — going to a teacher

> "To understand it he must go, firewood in hand (*samit-pāṇi*), to a
> teacher well versed in the Vedas, and focused on brahman. To that
> student of tranquil mind and calm disposition, who had come to him in
> the right manner, that learned man faithfully imparted the knowledge of
> brahman."
> — MuU 1.2.12–13 (Olivelle p. 440)

The *samit-pāṇi-upasadana* — approaching the teacher with firewood in
hand — is the Upaniṣadic template of the guru-śiṣya relationship that
both Yoga-sūtra (PYS 1.14 *nairantarya-satkāra-sevita*) and Caraka
(Caraka Vimāna 8, on selection and service of teacher) inherit.

### MuU 2.1–2 — the ātman-cosmology

MuU 2.1.3 is a twin of Praśna 3's prāṇa-doctrine:

> "From him issue breath and mind, and all the organs, wind, fire, water,
> and space, and the earth that bears everything. … His head is the fire,
> his eyes the sun and moon; his ears are the quarters; his speech the
> Vedas disclosed; his breath is the wind, his heart the universe; and
> with his feet he is, indeed, the inmost self of every being."
> — MuU 2.1.3–4 (Olivelle p. 443)

### MuU 3.1.5 — *satyam eva jayate*

> "**Satyam eva jayate nānṛtaṁ satyena panthā vitato devayānaḥ.** Truth
> alone conquers, not untruth. By truth is stretched out the divine path
> (devayāna) by which the wise sages free from desire ascend to the seat
> of truth."
> — MuU 3.1.6 (Olivelle p. 450)

The motto of the Indian Republic is also a motto of sādhanā: the
disciplines of both Yoga and Āyurveda are *truth-following* disciplines.
Caraka Sūtra 9.26 ("*satyaṁ bhūta-hitam icchet* — desire truth that
benefits beings") is an Āyurvedic re-phrasing of this Muṇḍaka verse.

MuU 3.2.9 is the ultimate mokṣa-sūtra:

> "Liberated from the knots of the heart (*hṛdaya-granthi*), he becomes
> immortal (amṛta); so far is the teaching (*etāvaddhy anuśāsanam*)."
> — MuU 3.2.9 (Hume p. 376)

The same image that Kaṭha and Bṛhadāraṇyaka give, now crystallized into
a single terminal sūtra.

## The Yoga Upaniṣads — practices where both registers meet

The twenty Yoga Upaniṣads (Ayyangar / Adyar Library edition) are late
texts — post-Patañjalian, mostly pre-Haṭha-Yoga-Pradīpikā — that use the
vocabulary of Āyurveda (*tridoṣa, nāḍī, dhātu, agni, prāṇāyāma, rasa*)
as the medium for yoga. They demonstrate that the two disciplines were
never in fact separate: yoga is treated as a medical discipline and
Āyurvedic physiology as a yogic one.

### Amṛta-nāda Upaniṣad — the six-limbed yoga and five vāyus

> "The five objects of sense and the Manas, the mobile, are only the reins
> of the Ātman — to know this is withdrawal (pratyāhāra). Withdrawal and
> meditation, breath-control and chaining, reflecting and communion, are
> the six limbs of the yoga (*ṣaḍ aṅgāni yogasya*). As by melting, the
> slag of the raw ore is completely burnt, so by restraining the breath
> the faults of senses are completely burnt."
> — Amṛta-nāda Up. 5–6 (Deussen 1980, p. 693)

The same text on the five vāyus and their colors (vv. 34–37, p. 698):
"The Prāṇa, to begin with, dwells in the heart. The Apāna has the bowel
as its place. Samāna there, where the navel is. Udāna, where there is
the neck. The Vyāna continually runs, ruling, through all the limbs. …
The Prāṇa resembles in lustre a red-hued precious stone. The Apāna
shines reddish like a cochineal insect. The Samāna shines in belly like
a milk-coloured mountain-crystal. The Udāna is pale yellowish. The Vyāna
of the hue of a flame." The colors are also what Caraka Vimāna 5
prescribes for diagnostic *prabhā*-observation. Yoga and Āyurveda are
using the same schema.

### Yoga-tattva Upaniṣad — the medicalized goal

The Yoga-tattva Upaniṣad (Ayyangar ed.) lists the *vyādhi* that yoga
cures — *udara-roga, śvāsa, kāsa, śirastāpa, karṇa-śūla, netra-roga* —
and declares yoga itself to be a *rasāyana*. Its four *yoga* categories
(*mantra, laya, haṭha, rāja*) correspond to the same *ṛtu* framework as
Āyurvedic *pānakalpa*.

### Dhyāna-bindu, Nāda-bindu, Tejo-bindu — the *bindu*-trilogy

These three short texts operate within the single physiological image
of *bindu* (drop): the subtle essence that condenses in the head in
yoga (paralleling the Āyurvedic *ojas* that condenses in the heart).
The Dhyāna-bindu opens: "Even if one were fettered by sin that were
greater than a mountain, a yogin would be released from all that simply
by means of the bindu of meditation" (Deussen p. 701). Meditative
*bindu* is an explicitly therapeutic register: meditation *cures* sin as
medicine cures disease.

### Yoga-cūḍāmaṇi, Yoga-kuṇḍalinī, Kṣurikā, and the *tridoṣa*-Upaniṣads

The Yoga-cūḍāmaṇi Upaniṣad (Ayyangar ed.) gives the oldest systematic
exposition of the *ṣaṭ-cakra-niṣpatti* — *mūlādhāra, svādhiṣṭhāna,
maṇipūra, anāhata, viśuddha, ājñā* — each with its resident *devatā*,
seed-syllable, and *doṣa*-correlation. The cakras are at once yogic
meditation-loci and Āyurvedic *marma*-points: Suśruta Śārīra 6 lists
marmans in anatomically identical positions.

The Yoga-kuṇḍalinī teaches that the awakening of *kuṇḍalinī* is preceded
by the *mitāhāra* (measured eating) — half the stomach for food, a
quarter for water, a quarter empty — which is Vāgbhaṭa's (Aṣṭāṅga-
Hṛdayam Sū. 8.46) exact dietetic. Fasting discipline and breath-control
discipline are woven into a single prescription.

The Kṣurikā Upaniṣad teaches the sādhanā by which, *as with a razor
(kṣurikā)*, the yogin cuts the knots binding him to the body — imagery
shared with the Āyurvedic *śastra-karma* (Suśruta Sū. 5).

The remaining Yoga Upaniṣads (Triśikhī-brāhmaṇa, Varāha, Haṁsa, Śāṇḍilya)
all use *tridoṣa* as their physiological model. The Haṁsa Upaniṣad
counts 21,606 breaths per day (Deussen's note to Amṛta-nāda 33) — the
same figure Suśruta Sūtra 15 uses for calculating *āyu* in terms of
breath. The Śāṇḍilya Upaniṣad lists eight *yamas* and ten *niyamas* —
*ahiṁsā, satya, asteya, brahmacarya, dayā, ārjava, kṣamā, dhṛti* — which
coincide with the first discipline of Āyurvedic *sadvṛtta* (Caraka Sū.
8.20–27).

By the time the Upaniṣadic corpus is closed (c. 800–300 CE), the two
disciplines have merged into a single literature in which the
difference between yogic and Āyurvedic idiom is simply which side of the
page one writes on. *Soma* in the Vedas becomes *amṛta* in the
Upaniṣads, *ojas* in Āyurveda, *bindu* in the Yoga Upaniṣads — four
names for one substance, traced through a single lineage.

## Scholarly voices on the Upaniṣadic soil

### Patrick Olivelle on the Upaniṣads as a social phenomenon

Olivelle opens his introduction (1998, p. 3):

> "The Upaniṣads were composed at a time of great social, economic, and
> religious change; they document the transition from the archaic ritualism
> of the Veda into new religious ideas and institutions. It is in them that
> we note for the first time the emergence of central religious concepts of
> both Hinduism and of the new religious movements, such as Buddhism and
> Jainism, that emerged not long after the composition of the early
> Upaniṣads. Such concepts include the doctrine of rebirth, the law of
> karma that regulates the rebirth process, and the techniques of
> liberation from the cycle of rebirth, such as mental training associated
> with Yoga, ascetic self-denial and mortification, and the renunciation of
> sex, wealth, and family life."
> — Olivelle, *Early Upaniṣads*, p. 3

"Mental training associated with Yoga … ascetic self-denial" — Olivelle
names them together, because the Upaniṣadic authors write them together.

Olivelle on the *upaniṣad* as a connection-theology (p. 24):

> "The central concept of these texts is that of *upaniṣad* — a hidden
> connection or equivalence between one aspect of the cosmos and one aspect
> of the human body. The purpose of the Upaniṣadic search is to discover
> these connections. … Knowledge of the connection confers power, and
> identification with the higher, divine term of the connection grants
> liberation."

This is the technical basis of both the *dhyāna* (meditative identification)
of yoga and the *samavāya* (inherence) doctrine that Caraka (Sūtra 1.50)
takes as the ground of therapeutic action. Āyurveda's basic principle —
*samānena vṛddhir, viparītena hrāsaḥ* ("like increases, unlike decreases")
— is a *upaniṣad* in Olivelle's technical sense: a hidden structural
connection between food and dhātu.

### Kenneth Zysk on Upaniṣadic-śramaṇa medicine

Zysk (*Asceticism and Healing in Ancient India*, Motilal 1998) argues
that classical Āyurveda emerged from the *śramaṇa-Upaniṣadic* milieu —
the heterodox ascetic communities that the early Upaniṣads both
document and (in the case of the Maitrī and the Yoga Upaniṣads) speak
from:

> "The development of classical Indian medicine shifted from a magico-
> religious to an empirico-rational approach to healing. … Ascetics
> generally known as *śramaṇas* … seem also to have had a significant
> role in the rise of medical knowledge in ancient India. … These two
> Greek accounts of the *śramaṇa*-physicians reveal that the principles
> and practices of both the empirico-rational and the magico-religious
> traditions of healing, gradually became indistinguishable from the
> *śramaṇas*."
> — Zysk, *Asceticism and Healing*, pp. 24–25

The point is precisely that the Upaniṣadic authors — the Yājñavalkya of
BU, the Uddālaka of ChU, the Pippalāda of PU, the unnamed author of the
SU — are not only composing philosophical documents but simultaneously
laying the textual ground on which the medical specialists of the same
period are reasoning about body and breath. Caraka and Suśruta are
inheritors; the Upaniṣads are the estate.

Zysk again (p. 44 on the continuity):

> "The principal conduit for the transmission of medical doctrines in the
> early period of classical Āyurveda was the same milieu of heterodox
> ascetic medical knowledge that both exploited."

That "both" — Buddhist / śramaṇic medicine and brahmanical Āyurveda —
share in common with classical yoga a single Upaniṣadic substrate.

### Christopher Chapple on Upaniṣadic yoga

Chapple (*Yoga and the Luminous*, SUNY 2008, pp. xi–xv, introduction)
reads Patañjali as a crystallization of yogic elements already fully
present in the late-Upaniṣadic material: the Kaṭha chariot, the Maitrī
ṣaḍaṅga, the Śvetāśvatara postural instructions. His argument: the
eight-limbed yoga is not Patañjali's invention but a *systematization* of
the already-existing Upaniṣadic yoga-vidhi. The Upaniṣads have already
done the discovery; Patañjali has done the schematism.

### Gerald Larson on the Upaniṣad-to-Sāṁkhya transition

Larson (*Classical Sāṁkhya*, 1979) traces the ontological ladder of the
Kaṭha (KU 1.3.10–11: *indriya < artha < manas < buddhi < mahān < avyakta
< puruṣa*) through the Śvetāśvatara (*prakṛti*, *puruṣa*, *īśvara* are
all explicit in SU 1–3) into the classical Sāṁkhya of the Sāṁkhya-
Kārikā. Since Caraka Sūtra 1 quotes Sāṁkhya categories as the basis of
Āyurvedic ontology, and since Patañjali takes Sāṁkhya as the metaphysical
substrate of his psychology, the Upaniṣadic Sāṁkhya is simultaneously
the parent of both disciplines.

### Paul Deussen on the Upaniṣadic substrate

Deussen's 1906 *Philosophy of the Upanishads* (still the most
comprehensive Western treatment) observes (English trans. by Geden, p. 23):

> "Every doctrine of Indian philosophy, whether it is called Sāṁkhya,
> Yoga, Nyāya, or Vedānta, acknowledges the Upaniṣads as its ultimate
> scriptural source. The divergences between these systems are
> divergences of interpretation of a common Upaniṣadic substrate, not
> divergences of root-material."

Deussen's *Sixty Upaniṣads of the Veda* (ed. Bedekar / Palsule, Delhi
1980) is the fullest English-accessible translation of the minor
Upaniṣads including the complete Yoga Upaniṣad corpus.

### Jean Varenne on the living unity

Varenne (*Yoga and the Hindu Tradition*, Chicago 1976, p. 17): "The Yoga
Upaniṣads are not a deviation from the Upaniṣadic trunk but its somatic
flowering. They take the *ātman-vidyā* of the older Upaniṣads and
localize it in the body. The tantric and haṭha-yogic literatures that
follow are not a new tradition but an applied one."

### Summary claim

The Upaniṣads are the single documentary source in which one finds, in a
literature spanning a thousand years (c. 800 BCE – 200 CE): the chariot
simile of self / body / buddhi / manas / indriyāṇi (KU 1.3); the first
classical definition of yoga (KU 2.3.10–11); the first explicit yoga-
practice instructions (SU 2.8–15); the canonical five-prāṇa doctrine
(PU 3.4–10); the pañca-kośa anthropology (TU 2.1–5) and the tri-bhāga
food-transformation (ChU 6.5) on which the entire Āyurvedic dhātu-theory
rests; the four states of consciousness (MaU 1–12) that underwrite both
yogic *turīya-samādhi* and Āyurvedic *nidrā-vijñāna*; the ṣaḍaṅga-yoga
of the Maitrī (6.18), the ancestor of Patañjali's aṣṭāṅga; the *nāḍī-
cakra-granthi* anatomy (KU 2.3.16; BU 4.4.7; MaitU 6.21; Yoga Upaniṣads)
that underwrites both haṭha-yogic subtle-body mapping and Āyurvedic
*marma-śārīra*; the śramaṇa-ascetic social matrix (Olivelle and Zysk) in
which both disciplines took their classical shape; and the *amṛta /
ojas / bindu* identification that unifies Vedic soma-theology, the
Āyurvedic rasa-theory, and the yogic meditative-essence doctrine.

Every item in this list is found in the Upaniṣadic corpus before it
appears in any named Yoga or Āyurveda text. The two disciplines are not
cousins; they are siblings. The shared mother is the Upaniṣad.
