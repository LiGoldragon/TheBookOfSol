# Apathya and Mitāhāra — Primary-Source Extracts

A thematic digest of *direct passages* from the *bṛhat-trayī* (Caraka, Suśruta, Vāgbhaṭa), the Yogic corpus (*Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā*, *Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā*, *Śiva Saṃhitā*), the *Bhagavad Gītā*, and the *Upaniṣads*, on what the śāstra identifies as unwholesome food (*apathya*) and how it defines the measured discipline of eating (*mitāhāra*). This is a working compilation for the article [*Apathya*](../Apathya.md) and any future work on the modern misuse of Ayurvedic vocabulary.

Format follows the project's canonical quote-block: Sanskrit IAST on top (bold), blank blockquote line, English translation in quotes, em-dash attribution line. Where no clean Sanskrit IAST is available in the working corpus, the passage appears as an English-only block quote with the attribution on its own break.

## I. The goal — svastha

### Caraka Sū. 1.41 — the definition of Āyurveda

> "Āyurveda is that which deals with good, bad, happy and unhappy life,\
> its promoters and non-promoters, measure and nature."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 1.41, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

Discernment — *hitāhita, sukha-duḥkha, āyuṣya-anāyuṣya* — placed at the root of the discipline.

### Caraka Sū. 15.48 — the definition of health

> **sama-doṣaḥ samāgniś ca sama-dhātu-mala-kriyaḥ |**\
> **prasanna-ātmendriya-manāḥ svastha ity abhidhīyate ||**
>
> "Even in doṣas, even in agni, even in the functions of dhātus and malas,\
> with a serene self, a serenity of the senses and of the mind —\
> this is called *svastha*, the healthy one."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 15.48

The plate that produces *svastha* is the plate that does not provoke, does not strain, does not scatter.

### Caraka Sū. 25.31 and 25.33 — wholesome vs. unwholesome food

> "Only the use of wholesome food promotes the growth of person\
> and that of unwholesome one is the cause of disorders."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 25.31, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

> "The food which maintains the balanced dhātus in normalcy\
> and restores the equilibrium in mal-balanced ones,\
> should be taken as wholesome; otherwise unwholesome."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 25.33, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

## II. The discipline — mitāhāra

### Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā 1.15 — no yoga without mitāhāra

> **mitāhāraṁ vinā yastu yogārambhaṁ tu kārayet |**\
> **nānā-rogo bhaved tasya kiñcid yogo na sidhyati ||**
>
> "Without moderate diet, whoever begins yoga practice\
> will acquire various diseases\
> and will not succeed in yoga."\
> — *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā* 1.15

### Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā 1.57 — what the yogī must eat

> **hitaṁ mitaṁ ca bhoktavyaṁ annaṁ yogārthinā sadā |**
>
> "The yogin should always eat wholesome anna in measured quantity."\
> — *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā* 1.57

The word is *anna* — cooked grain, the staple of the śāstraic diet — not *phala* (fruit), not *śāka* (vegetable).

### Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā 1.58 — the positive definition

> **susnigdha-madhurāhāraś caturthāṃśa-vivarjitaḥ |**\
> **bhujyate śiva-samprītyai mitāhāraḥ sa ucyate ||**
>
> "Eating food that is unctuous (*snigdha*) and sweet (*madhura*),\
> leaving a fourth of the stomach empty,\
> offered to please Śiva —\
> this is called measured eating."\
> — *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā* 1.58

The two-word positive register of the yogic meal: *snigdha-madhura*. Everything outside these two adjectives is either allowed marginally or excluded.

### Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya Sū. 8 — food that kindles agni

> **uṣṇaṁ snigdhaṁ mātrāvad ajīrṇe'nupasevitam |**\
> **agniṁ dīpayati āhāraḥ samyak tu bala-vardhanaḥ ||**
>
> "Food taken warm, unctuous, in proper measure,\
> and not during indigestion, kindles agni\
> and nourishes strength."\
> — *Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya*, Sūtrasthāna 8 (on āhāra-vidhi)

## III. The prohibition list — apathya

### Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā 1.59 — the canonical apathya list

> **kaṭvamla-tīkṣṇa-lavaṇoṣṇa-harita-śāka-sauvīra-taila-tila-sarṣapa-madya-matsyān |**\
> **ajādi-māṃsa-dadhi-takra-kulattha-kola-piṇyāka-hiṅgu-laśunādyam apathyam āhuḥ ||**
>
> "The pungent, the sour, the sharp, the salty, the hot,\
> green vegetables, sour gruel, oil, sesame, mustard, alcohol, fish,\
> goat and other flesh, curds, buttermilk, horse-gram, jujube,\
> oil-cake, asafoetida, garlic and the like —\
> these they call unwholesome."\
> — *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā* 1.59

The list is comprehensive. Five tastes (*kaṭu, amla, tīkṣṇa, lavaṇa, uṣṇa*) plus raw greens, plus a named set of substances. The verse is the single most-cited yogic statement on *apathya*.

### Bhagavad Gītā 17.9 — the dharmic parallel (rājasic food)

> **kaṭv-amla-lavaṇāty-uṣṇa-tīkṣṇa-rūkṣa-vidāhinaḥ |**\
> **āhārā rājasasyeṣṭā duḥkha-śokāmaya-pradāḥ ||**
>
> "Foods that are pungent, sour, salty, excessively hot, sharp, dry, and burning\
> are dear to those of agitated nature.\
> Such foods bring forth sorrow, grief, and disease."\
> — *Bhagavad Gītā* 17.9

The Gītā reproduces HYP 1.59's list of excluded tastes almost verbatim and places it under the heading of *rajoguṇa*. The agreement between the dharmic and the yogic corpus is not coincidental; both are reading the same classical pharmacology of taste.

### Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā 5.21 — the yogic prohibition, restated

> "Food that is pungent, sour, salty, bitter, or parched;\
> curd, buttermilk, an excess of leafy greens, alcohol,\
> palm nut, jackfruit, horse gram, masur and white beans,\
> white pumpkin, the stalks of leafy greens, bottle gourd, jujube,\
> elephant apple, thorny bel, dhak, kadamba, lemon, bimba, breadfruit,\
> garlic, lotus stalk, wild turmeric, chironji, asafoetida,\
> the fruit of the silk-cotton tree, and taro root:\
> these should be avoided by the yogi at the beginning of his Yoga practice."\
> — *Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā* 5.21, trans. Mallinson

### Śiva Saṃhitā 3.33 — the third yogic witness

> "Sour, astringent, pungent, salty, mustard, and bitter flavors... —\
> the yogi should definitely give up these."\
> — *Śiva Saṃhitā* 3.33

## IV. The doctrine of rasa-atiyoga

### Caraka Sū. 26.40 — the six rasas

> "The six rasas in the mouth are: *madhura, amla, lavaṇa, kaṭu, tikta and kaṣāya*.\
> These are produced from earth and water but differ in potencies\
> because of the predominance of remaining elements."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 26.40, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

Six rasas admitted in the physiology; none of them privileged for daily food without qualification. The text then itemizes what happens when each is consumed "singly and excessively."

### Caraka Sū. 26.43(2) — amla rasa in excess

> "Amla rasa... though endowed with so many qualities,\
> if used singly and excessively,\
> sensitises teeth, causes thirst,\
> makes the eye close, raises the body hairs,\
> liquifies kapha, aggravates pitta,\
> affects blood morbidity,\
> causes heat in muscles and laxity in body,\
> produces swelling in wasted, injured, emaciated and debilitated persons,\
> because of its āgneya nature causes suppuration\
> in wounds, injuries, bites, burns, fractures, swellings,\
> falling, poisoned spots due to urination and contact of insects,\
> compressed, excised, dislocated, punctured and crushed...\
> and causes burning sensation in throat, chest and cardiac region."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 26.43(2), trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Caraka Sū. 26.43(3) — lavaṇa rasa in excess

> **lavaṇasyātiyogāt puṁstvopaghāta\
> indriyopaghāto vali-palita-khālityaṃ ca bhavati ||**
>
> "From excess of the salty taste comes loss of virility,\
> impairment of the senses,\
> and the production of wrinkles, greying, and baldness."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 26.43(3)

The full doctrinal elaboration of the same passage:

> "Lavaṇa rasa... if used singly and excessively,\
> vitiates pitta, aggravates rakta, causes thirst, fainting, heat,\
> tearing, sloughing, increases leprosy and other skin diseases,\
> aggravates poisons, makes the inflamed part burst and teeth fall down;\
> damages potency, hinders sense organs,\
> gives rise to wrinkles, grey hair and baldness;\
> and also produces disorders like internal haemorrhage,\
> acid gastritis, erysipelas, vātarakta, eczema, alopecia."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 26.43(3), trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Caraka Sū. 26.43(4) — kaṭuka rasa in excess

> "Kaṭuka rasa... if used singly and excessively,\
> damages sexual potency due to the effect of vipāka,\
> causes mental confusion, malaise, depression, emaciation, fainting,\
> bending, feeling of darkness, giddiness, burning in throat, body-heat,\
> loss of strength and thirst due to the effect of rasa, vīrya and prabhāva;\
> over and above, due to abundance of vāyu and agni,\
> it produces vātika disorders in feet, hands, sides, back etc.,\
> particularly with symptoms like movements, burning pain, tremors,\
> piercing and tearing pains."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 26.43(4), trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Caraka Sū. 26.44 — proper use yields benefit; excess becomes harm

> "These six rasas, when used in proper quantity,\
> are beneficial for the living beings, otherwise they become harmful.\
> Hence the wise should use these properly in proper quantity\
> in order to derive benefit (from them)."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 26.44, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya Sū. 10.12–13 — lavaṇa in excess (Vāgbhaṭa)

> **atisevitas tu asra-pavana-kopano\
> dehasya dauḥśīlya-vali-palita-khālitya-\
> tṛṣṇā-kuṣṭha-viṣa-visarpa-balakṣayān karoti ||**
>
> "Used in excess, salt causes increase of blood and wind;\
> it produces coarseness of the body, wrinkles of the skin,\
> greying of the hair, baldness, thirst,\
> leprosy and other skin diseases, the action of poisons, herpes,\
> and the diminution of bodily strength."\
> — *Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya*, Sūtrasthāna 10.12–13, trans. K. R. Srikantha Murthy

### Suśruta Uttara 44 — salt among the causes of pāṇḍu-roga

> "The deranged bodily *doṣas* of a person addicted to sexual excesses,\
> or to eating clay or salts, or articles of keen potency\
> and of acid or saline taste,\
> or to strong liquors in excessive quantities,\
> or to sleeping in the day,\
> contaminate the blood and produce a yellowish (*pāṇḍu*) colour of the skin.\
> This is known as *pāṇḍu-roga*."\
> — *Suśruta Saṃhitā*, Uttaratantra 44, trans. Kaviraj Kunjalal Bhishagratna

### Suśruta Uttara 45 — salt among the causes of rakta-pitta

> "Excessive indulgence in grief, fright or anger,\
> excessive physical labour, exposure to the sun and fire,\
> constant use of pungent, acid, saline, and alkaline food,\
> or of articles of fare which are keen or heat-making in potency,\
> or incompatible in their combination,\
> or are followed by deficient gastric or intestinal digestion —\
> these are the factors which tend to produce *rakta-pitta*."\
> — *Suśruta Saṃhitā*, Uttaratantra 45, trans. Bhishagratna

### Suśruta Uttara 48 — salt among the causes of pathological thirst

> "The thirst which is produced by using extremely fatty and saline articles of fare,\
> as well as those which are hard to digest,\
> is due to errors in diet — and is known as *annaja* thirst."\
> — *Suśruta Saṃhitā*, Uttaratantra 48, trans. Bhishagratna

## V. The doctrine of viruddha-anna

### Caraka Sū. 26.84 — fruits antagonistic to milk (with nārikela)

> **āmrāmrātaka-mātuluṅga-nikuca-karamarda-moca-dantaśāṭha-badara-**\
> **kośāmra-bhavya-jambū-nārikela-dāḍima-āmalakāni ...**\
> **payasā saha viruddham ||**
>
> "Mango, wild mango, citron, nikuca, karamarda, plantain,\
> dantaśāṭha, jujube, kośāmra, bhavya, java-plum, coconut,\
> pomegranate, āmalaka — these fruits and similar other substances,\
> all sour liquids or non-liquids, are antagonistic to milk."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 26.84, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Caraka Sū. 26.86–87 — the general definition of viruddha

> **yaccāpi deśakālāgni-mātrā-sātmyānilādibhiḥ |**\
> **saṁskārato vīryataśca koṣṭhāvasthā-kramair api ||**\
> **parihāropacārābhyāṁ pākāt saṁyogato'pi ca |**\
> **viruddhaṁ tacca na hitaṁ hṛt-sampad-vidhibhiśca yat ||**
>
> "That which is incompatible by place, time, digestive capacity, quantity,\
> habituation, doṣa, processing, potency, bowel, condition, sequence,\
> treatment, cooking, combination, palatability, quality, or method —\
> all such food is unwholesome."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 26.86–87, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Caraka Sū. 26.90 — honey and ghṛta in equal quantity

> **madhu-sarpiḥ samadhṛtaṁ mātrayā tad virudhyate |**
>
> "Honey and ghee held in equal measure —\
> by quantity, that becomes incompatible."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 26.90, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Caraka Sū. 26.92 — saṃskāra viruddha (processing antagonism)

> **saṁskārato viruddhaṁ tad yad bhojyaṁ viṣavad bhavet |**
>
> "That which, by processing, becomes poisonous —\
> that is *saṁskāra viruddha*."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 26.92, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Caraka Sū. 26.86 (combination-clause) — sour things with milk

> "Sour things taken with milk is antagonism in combination."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 26.86 (saṃyoga-viruddha clause), trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Caraka Sū. 26.102–103 — consequences of antagonistic food

> "Antagonistic food is the cause of impotency, blindness, erysipelas,\
> ascites, pustules, insanity, fistula-in-ano, fainting, narcosis,\
> tympanitis, spasm in throat, anaemia, *āma-viṣa*, leucoderma,\
> leprosy, *grahaṇī-roga*, oedema, acid gastritis, fever, rhinitis,\
> genetic disorders and even death."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 26.102–103, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

## VI. On salt — the eight varieties and the *saindhava* exception

### Caraka Sū. (on the five salts)

> "*Sauvarcala* (obtained from plants like suvarcalā etc.),\
> *saindhava* (rock salt), *viḍa* (obtained from excreta),\
> *audbhida* (obtained from earth) along with *sāmudra* (obtained from sea water)\
> are the five salts. They are unctuous, hot, sharp,\
> best promoters of digestion..."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna, on the *lavaṇa-varga*, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya — the saindhava exception

> "Generally, salts are bad for the eyes (vision), except *saindhava*."\
> — *Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya*, Sūtrasthāna, on the *rasa-dravya-guṇa* classification, trans. Srikantha Murthy

### Caraka Sū. 5.12 — salt admitted to the daily diet is saindhava

> **ṣaṣṭikāḥ śāli-mudgāś ca saindhavaṁ āmalakāni ca |**\
> **yavā dhānyaṁ ca kṣīraṁ ca sarpir madhu ca...**
>
> "One should take as daily routine *ṣaṣṭika* and *śāli* (rice), *mudga* (pulse),\
> *saindhava* (rock salt), *āmalaka* (fruits),\
> barley, rain water, milk, ghee and honey."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 5.12, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

The single salt Caraka admits by name into the daily routine is *saindhava* — not *sāmudra* (sea salt, the table salt of modern kitchens).

## VII. On the gourd family (trapusa, ervāruka, alābu, nārikela)

### Caraka Sū. 27 — cucumber-gourd family classification

> "*Trapusa* and *ervāruka*: sweet, heavy, distending,\
> cold, relishing and rough.\
> *Trapusa* is excessively diuretic."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

> "*Alābu* (bottle gourd): purgative, rough, cold and heavy."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Caraka Sū. 27 — ripe coconut (nārikela)

> "The ripe fruits of *tāla* and *nārikela* (coconut)\
> are bulk-promoting, unctuous, cold, sweet,\
> strength-promoting, heavy..."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Caraka Sū. 27 — the preparation rule for heavy vegetables

> "All legumes, *phañjī*, *cillī*, *kuṭumbaka*, all types of *ālūka* with leaves...\
> these vegetables are heavy, rough and often digest with distension.\
> They are *madhura*, *śīta vīrya* and purgative.\
> **They should be boiled, the juice be expressed out,\
> and then added with some fat before intake.**"\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.88–124, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

The classical preparation is explicit: boil, press out the juice, add fat. The modern recipe that "dices and simmers" contradicts the instruction on three counts at once.

## VIII. On ghṛta — the first among fats

### Caraka Sū. 27.231–233 — ghṛta praise

> "Cow ghee promotes memory, intelligence, agni, semen, ojas, kapha and medas;\
> alleviates vāta, pitta, poison, insanity, phthisis,\
> inauspiciousness and fever.\
> **It is the best of all fats**, is cold, *madhura rasa*, *madhura vipāka*,\
> has thousand potentialities, and so, if used properly\
> according to prescribed methods,\
> exerts thousand types of action."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.231–233, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Caraka Sū. 27.234 — the ghṛta beneficiary list

> "Ghee is the best one for those desirous of intelligence,\
> lustre of complexion, good voice, longevity, semen, happiness,\
> tenderness of body and strength."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.234, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā 1.63 — the yogi's fat and milk

> "The yogi should take nourishing and sweet food\
> mixed with ghee and milk;\
> it should nourish the dhatus and be pleasing and suitable."\
> — *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā* 1.63, trans. Muktibodhananda

The fat is *ghṛta*. Not *taila* (oil). The Yogic and the Ayurvedic corpora converge on the same single substance at the center of the cooking medium.

## IX. On medicine versus food

### Caraka Sū. 1.124–125 — drug as poison, drug as nectar

> "A drug, if unknown, is fatal like poison, weapon, fire and thunderbolt;\
> while, if known, is like nectar."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 1.124–125, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Caraka Sū. 1.126 — poison becomes drug, drug becomes poison

> "A sharp poison also becomes the best drug by proper administration;\
> on the contrary, even the best drug is reduced to sharp poison,\
> if administered badly."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 1.126, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Caraka Sū. 26.12 — every substance admits pharmacological use

> "There is no substance in the universe which cannot be used as drug,\
> on the condition that they are used rationally\
> and with a definite objective."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 26.12, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

The distinction between *auṣadha* (medicine) and *āhāra* (food) is not that different substances inhabit the two categories; it is that the same substance, given with indication and dose, is *auṣadha*, and given without them is *āhāra* — or, when wrongly used, is harm. Daily "blooming" of kaṭu-tīkṣṇa spices at every meal is the administration of *auṣadha* without indication — which this verse already classifies as poisoning by misuse.

### Caraka Sū. 4.8(6) — the ten appetisers (*dīpanīya*)

> "*Pippalī*, *pippalī-mūla*, *cavya*, *citraka*, *śuṇṭhī*, *amlavetasa*,\
> *marica*, *ajamodā*, *bhallātaka-aśthi* and *hiṅgu-niryāsa* —\
> these ten are appetisers (*dīpanīya*)."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 4.8(6), trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

The appetisers are classified — and therefore circumscribed. They are drugs, with names, with doses, with indications. They are not seasoning.

## X. On agni — the first receiver of food

### Caraka Sū. 27.342 — agni and food

> "On agni depend strength, health, life-span and vital breath.\
> Agni burns with the fuel of food and drinks;\
> otherwise it is impaired."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.342, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya Sū. 12 — the proverbial form

> "All diseases arise where agni is weak."\
> — proverbial formulation, after *Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya*, Sūtrasthāna 12 (*rogāḥ sarve'pi mandāgnau*)

## XI. On anna — food as the basis of prāṇa

### Taittirīya Upaniṣad 2.2.1 — food as origin

> **oṣadhībhyo'nnam. annāt puruṣaḥ.**
>
> "From plants arises *anna*. From *anna* arises the human being."\
> — *Taittirīya Upaniṣad* 2.2.1

### Taittirīya Upaniṣad 2.2.4 — food as Brahman

> **annaṁ brahmeti vyajānāt.**
>
> "He knew *anna* as Brahman."\
> — *Taittirīya Upaniṣad* 2.2.4

### Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad — prāṇa as amṛta

> **amṛtaṁ vai prāṇāḥ**
>
> "Prāṇa itself is amṛta."\
> — *Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad*

Food, *anna*, is the material base of the subtle body. Prāṇa is what the eater makes of *anna* when the classical discipline is respected. When the discipline is abandoned — when the plate is *apathya, viruddha*, loaded with excluded tastes — *anna* does not give rise to prāṇa but to *āma*, undigested residue, the material basis of disease. The entire prohibition-list of the classical corpus is, in the end, a protection of the pathway from *anna* to prāṇa.
