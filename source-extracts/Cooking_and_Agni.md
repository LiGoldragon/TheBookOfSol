# Cooking and Agni — Primary-Source Extracts

A thematic digest of *direct passages* from the Ayurvedic (*bṛhat-trayī* — Caraka, Suśruta, Vāgbhaṭa), the Yogic (*Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā*, *Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā*, *Bhagavad Gītā*), and the Vedic / Upaniṣadic corpora, bearing on the position that **cooking does not kill *prāṇa* in food** — that *prāṇa* is built from *anna* by *agni*, that the body is itself a cooking vessel whose digestive fire continues the work the kitchen begins, that uncooked input does not yield *rasa* (the first nutritive juice from which all *dhātus* are built), and that the canonical staple of the human plate is grain cooked soft in water and finished with *ghṛta*.

This compilation supports [*Cooking and Spices*](../ayurveda/Cooking_and_Spices.md), [*Apathya*](../ayurveda/Apathya.md), [*Yogic Food*](../diet/Yogic_Food.md), [*Penultimate Sāttvic Food*](../diet/Penultimate_Sāttvic_Food.md), and any future writing on the doctrine of *agni*, the *anna*-*rasa*-*prāṇa* chain, or the modern raw-food ideology. The general *apathya* / *mitāhāra* doctrine and the canonical prohibition lists live in [*Apathya* and *Mitāhāra* — primary-source extracts](./Apathya.md); the AH water / *udakavaha-srotas* material lives in [*Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya — water, thirst, and the *ambuvaha-srotas*](./Astanga_Hrdaya/water-and-srotas.md). This file gathers the *cooking-and-agni* argument across all three corpora and refutes — through the classical texts in their own words — the modern claim that cooking destroys *prāṇa*, *life force*, *enzymes*, or *vitality*.

Format follows the project's canonical quote-block: bold IAST Sanskrit on top, blank `>` line, English translation in straight quotes, em-dash attribution on the final line. Where the working corpus lacks clean IAST, the passage appears as an English-only blockquote with the attribution underneath.

## I. Prāṇa is what the eater builds from anna

The modern raw-food claim imagines *prāṇa* as a thermal substance hovering inside the raw vegetable, driven out by heat. The Upaniṣads describe *prāṇa* in the opposite direction: not a property the plant carries, but the ordering capacity the eater applies to what he has digested.

### Taittirīya Upaniṣad 2.1.1 — body made of the rasa of anna

> **annaṃ na nindyāt | tad vratam |\
> ... anna-rasa-mayo 'yam ātmā ||**
>
> "One should not despise food. That is the rule.\
> ... This self is made of the essence of *anna*."\
> — *Taittirīya Upaniṣad* 2.1, trans. Olivelle

The body is *anna-rasa-maya* — composed of the *rasa* (the first nutritive juice) of *anna* (cooked grain). The Upaniṣad identifies the substance of the body with the substance of grain that has been cooked, eaten, and digested. The grammar is anatomical.

### Taittirīya Upaniṣad 2.2.1 — anna at the origin

> **oṣadhībhyo'nnam | annāt puruṣaḥ ||**
>
> "From plants arises *anna*. From *anna* arises the human being."\
> — *Taittirīya Upaniṣad* 2.2.1

### Taittirīya Upaniṣad 2.2.4 — anna as Brahman

> **annaṃ brahmeti vyajānāt ||**
>
> "He knew *anna* as Brahman."\
> — *Taittirīya Upaniṣad* 2.2.4

### Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad — prāṇa as amṛta

> **amṛtaṃ vai prāṇāḥ |**
>
> "*Prāṇa* itself is *amṛta*."\
> — *Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad*

*Prāṇa* — the ambrosia within the body — is what the eater makes of *anna* when the classical discipline is respected. *Prāṇa* arises *in the eater*, generated by *agni* from properly digested *anna*.

## II. From uncooked food, rasa cannot form

The single most direct refutation of the raw-food claim is in the *Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya*'s *Śārīrasthāna* chapter on the *agni-grahaṇī*. The verse states the chain explicitly: *agni* → *rasa-dhātu* → all subsequent *dhātus* → *ojas* and *prāṇa*. Without *agni*, the chain does not begin. Without cooking, *agni* cannot complete its work.

### Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya Śā. 3.53–54 — the canonical anchor

> **grahaṇyā balam agnir hi sa cāpi grahaṇī-balaḥ |\
> dūṣite 'gnāv ato duṣṭā grahaṇī roga-kāriṇī ||\
> yad annaṃ deha-dhātv-ojo-bala-varṇādi-poṣaṇam |\
> tatrāgnir hetur āhārān na hy apakvād rasādayaḥ ||**
>
> "The strength of *grahaṇī* is *agni* itself; *agni* in turn draws its strength from *grahaṇī*.\
> When *agni* is corrupted, *grahaṇī* is corrupted too, and disease arises.\
> The food that nourishes the body, the *dhātus*, *ojas*, strength, and complexion —\
> *agni* is the cause of it. **From uncooked food, *rasa* and the rest do not arise.**"\
> — *Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya* Śārīrasthāna 3.53–54

*na hy apakvād rasādayaḥ* — "from uncooked food, *rasa* and the rest do not arise." Six words that close the case. The *rasa-dhātu* (plasma, the first nutritive juice) is built from food that has been cooked. From cooked food *agni* draws *rasa*; from *rasa* it builds *rakta* (blood), *māṃsa* (flesh), *medas* (fat), *asthi* (bone), *majjā* (marrow), *śukra* (semen / generative essence) — and from those, *ojas* (the eight-drop refined essence) and *prāṇa*. Without the cooking that initiates the chain, the chain does not start. The "life force" the raw foodist seeks is the last link in a chain whose first link is *pāka*.

### Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya Śā. 3.55–56 — the body is the cooking vessel

> **annaṃ kāle 'bhyavahṛtaṃ koṣṭhaṃ prāṇānilāhṛtam |\
> dravair vibhinna-saṃghātaṃ nītaṃ snehena mārdavam |\
> saṃdhukṣitaḥ samānena pacaty āmāśaya-sthitam |\
> audaryo 'gnir yathā bāhyaḥ sthālī-sthaṃ toya-taṇḍulam ||**
>
> "Food taken at the proper hour is carried by *prāṇa-vāyu* into the *koṣṭha*,\
> broken up by liquids, softened by *sneha* (oily substance);\
> kindled by *samāna*, it is cooked in the *āmāśaya* by the belly's fire —\
> **as an outer fire cooks the rice and water in the pot.**"\
> — *Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya* Śārīrasthāna 3.55–56

The image is precise: *audaryo 'gnir yathā bāhyaḥ sthālī-sthaṃ toya-taṇḍulam* — "the belly's fire is like the outer fire cooking the rice-and-water in the pot." The *āmāśaya* (the digestive belly) is the inner *sthālī* (cooking pot). The body is a kitchen. The kitchen on the stove is the same operation as the kitchen in the belly — they are continuous, two stages of one transformation.

### The implication

External cooking is the *first stage* of internal cooking. It softens the food, releases the *rasa*, reduces the work the inner *agni* must perform. Skipping the external cooking forces the *jaṭharāgni* to do the entire job — with input it was never designed to receive in that form. What the inner *agni* cannot fully cook becomes *āma* — undigested residue, the material basis of disease.

## III. Agni is the cause of strength, health, and vital breath

*Agni* in Caraka is not a metaphor. It is the operative principle of life — the digestive fire, present in thirteen forms in the body (*jaṭharāgni* in the belly; *bhūtāgnis* in the elemental processings; *dhātvāgnis* in each tissue's metabolism). The classical corpus places *agni* at the centre of every clinical question.

### Caraka Sū. 27.342 — agni and life

> **balam ārogyam āyuś ca prāṇāś cāgnau pratiṣṭhitāḥ |\
> annapānendhanāś cāgnir... ||**
>
> "On *agni* depend strength, health, life-span and vital breath.\
> *Agni* burns with the fuel of food and drinks; otherwise it is impaired."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.342, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

Strength, health, life, *prāṇa* — all four rest on *agni*. *Agni* needs fuel — *anna* and *pāna*. The fuel of the body's fire is cooked food.

### Caraka Sū. 27.343 — every meal is an antaragni offering

> "One should offer regularly the oblations of wholesome food and drinks\
> to *antaragni* (internal fire) with due consideration to quantity and time."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.343, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

Caraka frames eating as an *antaragni*-yajña — an offering to the internal fire. The grammar is the *agnihotra* grammar, internalized. The eater is a priest at his own digestive fire; the food is *havis* (oblation); the *agni* receives, transforms, and elevates. Cooking is the preparation of the offering. Raw input would be unfit *havis*: the same way no priest offers raw rice to the external fire, no eater offers uncooked input to the internal one.

### Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya Sū. 12 — the proverbial form

> **rogāḥ sarve'pi mandāgnau ||**
>
> "All diseases arise where *agni* is weak."\
> — proverbial formulation, after *Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya* Sūtrasthāna 12

The two-word diagnosis the classical tradition repeats across the *bṛhat-trayī*. Weak *agni* is the upstream of every disease. Cooking is the principal way to *not* burden the *agni* with input it cannot process.

### Caraka Sū. 15.48 — agni in the definition of svastha

> **sama-doṣaḥ samāgniś ca sama-dhātu-mala-kriyaḥ |\
> prasanna-ātmendriya-manāḥ svastha ity abhidhīyate ||**
>
> "Even in *doṣas*, even in *agni*, even in the functions of *dhātus* and *malas*,\
> with a serene self, a serenity of the senses and of the mind —\
> this is called *svastha*, the healthy one."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 15.48

*Samāgni* — even / balanced *agni* — is named in the very definition of health. The healthy one is the one whose digestive fire is neither sluggish nor flaring. A diet that constantly burdens *agni* with raw input cannot maintain *samāgni*; the fire is forced into overwork to break down what cooking should already have softened.

## IV. Cooking transforms substance — the saṃskāra doctrine

The classical pharmacology recognizes that heat is a *saṃskāra* (processing operation) that *transforms* the *guṇas* (qualities) of substances. Heavy substances become light; light become heavy; sweet becomes pungent; medicinal becomes nutritive; poisonous becomes therapeutic. The transformation is named, classified, used.

### Caraka Sū. 27.339 — processing changes guru/laghu

> "By processing, the heavy ones become light and vice versa.\
> Such as *vrīhi* (heavy) becomes light when transformed into *lājā* after frying;\
> on the other hand, the flour of roasted grains becomes heavy when processed into cooked bolus."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.339, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

*Vrīhi* — paddy rice — is *guru* (heavy) in its raw grain form. Frying it into *lājā* (parched grain, popcorn) makes it *laghu* (light). Heat makes the heavy substance digestible. The same grain in two forms — one harder for the *agni*, one easier. The text states the rule directly: *saṃskārād-guru-laghutvāntaraṃ syāt* — "by processing, the difference of heaviness and lightness arises."

### Caraka Sū. 27.275 — overcooking makes hard food strength-promoting

> "The edibles which are bulky and hard\
> are heavy if mildly cooked\
> and **nourishing and strength-promoting if overcooked.**"\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.275, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

The verse is the inverse of the modern raw-food claim. Mildly cooked = heavy = poorly digestible. Thoroughly cooked = nourishing = strength-promoting. The longer the cooking, the more the substance gives up its *rasa* to the *agni*. The *yavāgū* / *peya* / *vilēpī* register — grain cooked into water for hours until it dissolves — is the maximally cooked form, and accordingly the maximally nourishing.

### Caraka Sū. 27.257–259 — well-cooked rice is light; poorly cooked is heavy

> "*Odana* (boiled rice), prepared of well-cleaned grains,\
> devoid of water, **well-cooked and warm is light**.\
> ... *Odana* prepared of uncleaned grains, not well-filtered,\
> **not well-cooked and cold is heavy.**"\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.257–259, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

The same rice — light when well-cooked, heavy when poorly cooked. The verse refutes any claim that cooked food is intrinsically heavier than its uncooked form. Cooking *lightens* the rice; cooking it warm and serving it warm keeps it light. Cold and undercooked is the heaviness.

### Caraka Sū. 1.126 — substance is transformed by administration

> **yogād api viṣaṃ tīkṣṇam uttamaṃ bheṣajaṃ bhavet |\
> bheṣajaṃ cāpi duryuktaṃ tīkṣṇaṃ saṃpadyate viṣam ||**
>
> "A sharp poison also becomes the best drug by proper administration;\
> on the contrary, even the best drug is reduced to sharp poison\
> if administered badly."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 1.126, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

The principle generalizes: heat is one of the principal *saṃskāras* by which a poison becomes medicine. *Bhallātaka* (the marking nut) is poisonous raw; cooked in milk in the *Cikitsāsthāna*-prescribed way it becomes a *rasāyana*. Mango is *raktapitta*-causing when young; ripe and cooked in proper preparation it builds tissue. The *saṃskāra* of heat is the operator that transforms the *guṇa*. Removing it is removing the transformation.

## V. The classical staple is cooked grain finished with ghṛta

The recommended foods of the classical corpus — Caraka's daily-routine list, the HYP's *mitāhāra* register, Caraka's *yavāgū* / *peya* / *vilēpī* / *odana* preparations — are uniformly cooked. Raw items appear only by exception (a piece of fruit by indication, a salad herb by *prabhāva*, raw honey by name). The staple is grain dissolved in water and finished with ghee.

### Caraka Sū. 5.12 — the daily routine

> **ṣaṣṭikāḥ śāli-mudgāś ca saindhavaṃ āmalakāni ca |\
> yavā dhānyaṃ ca kṣīraṃ ca sarpir madhu ca ...||**
>
> "One should take as daily routine *ṣaṣṭika* and *śāli* (rice), *mudga* (pulse),\
> *saindhava* (rock salt), *āmalaka* (fruits),\
> barley, rain water, milk, ghee and honey."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 5.12, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

Of the items: *ṣaṣṭika*, *śāli*, *mudga*, *yava*, *dhānya* — five grains, all cooked before eating; *kṣīra* (milk) — boiled to be safe and to bind kapha; *sarpis* (ghee) — itself the product of multiple heat treatments (boiling milk → curd → butter → clarified ghee); *madhu* — the one substance the daily-routine list takes raw, with the strict prohibition that it must not be heated. The structure is explicit: cooked grain at the centre, heat-processed dairy and fats, one named raw exception. *Āmalaka* is the *rasāyana* fruit. The plate is built around *pāka*.

### Caraka Cikitsā 1 — yavāgū as universal pathya

> **yavāgūḥ sarva-rogeṣu pathyā |**
>
> "Grain gruel is wholesome in all conditions."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Cikitsāsthāna 1 (proverbial formulation, after the *Rasāyana-pāda*)

*Yavāgū* — barley or rice grain dissolved in water by prolonged cooking — is the food the system always returns to when clarity, digestion, and stability are required. The verse names it *sarva-rogeṣu pathyā* — wholesome in all diseases. A maximally cooked food, named as the most universally indicated.

### Caraka Sū. 27.252 — vilepikā

> "*Vilepikā* (paste-like gruel) is saturating, constipating, light and cordial."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.252, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

*Vilēpī* — grain cooked even longer than *yavāgū*, into a paste — is light. The cooking does not weigh it down; the cooking *lightens* it.

### Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā 1.57 — the yogin's anna

> **hitaṃ mitaṃ ca bhoktavyaṃ annaṃ yogārthinā sadā |**
>
> "The yogin should always eat wholesome *anna* in measured quantity."\
> — *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā* 1.57

*Anna* — cooked grain, the *bhakta* of the daily plate. The Yogic register and the Ayurvedic register converge on the same staple at the same word.

### Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā 1.58 — snigdha-madhura

> **susnigdha-madhurāhāraś caturthāṃśa-vivarjitaḥ |\
> bhujyate śiva-samprītyai mitāhāraḥ sa ucyate ||**
>
> "Eating food that is unctuous (*snigdha*) and sweet (*madhura*),\
> leaving a fourth of the stomach empty,\
> offered to please Śiva —\
> this is called measured eating."\
> — *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā* 1.58

*Snigdha* — unctuous, oily, lubricated. *Madhura* — sweet, grounding, building. The two-word positive register of the yogic plate. Raw vegetables are rarely *snigdha*. Cooked grain finished with ghee is the canonical *snigdha-madhura* preparation.

### Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā 1.63 — ghee, milk, anna

> "The yogi should take nourishing and sweet food\
> mixed with ghee and milk;\
> it should nourish the *dhātus* and be pleasing and suitable."\
> — *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā* 1.63, trans. Muktibodhananda

The food that nourishes the *dhātus* is *anna* + *ghṛta* + *kṣīra*. All three are heat-processed. The classical dhātu-nourisher is the canonical cooked combination.

### Bhagavad Gītā 17.8 — sāttvic food is snigdha-sthira

> **āyuḥ-sattva-balārogya-sukha-prīti-vivardhanāḥ |\
> rasyāḥ snigdhāḥ sthirā hṛdyā āhārāḥ sāttvika-priyāḥ ||**
>
> "Foods that increase life, clarity, strength, health, happiness, and satisfaction —\
> that are juicy, unctuous, substantial, and agreeable —\
> are dear to those in *sattva*."\
> — *Bhagavad Gītā* 17.8

The four sāttvic adjectives — *rasya, snigdha, sthira, hṛdya*. *Snigdha* is the signature of cooking with ghee. *Sthira* is the signature of grain that has been cooked long enough to settle into the body. Raw food is rarely *snigdha* and almost never *sthira*. The *Gītā*'s definition of sāttvic food, applied honestly, returns a cooked plate.

## VI. The classical apathya lists name raw greens specifically

The Yogic prohibition lists are precise about *harita-śāka* — fresh / raw greens. The dharmic register places dry-burning *rūkṣa-vidāhin* foods in the rajasic camp. Both registers exclude the raw-vegetable mode of eating from the recommended diet.

### Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā 1.59 — apathya, with raw greens prohibited

> **kaṭvamla-tīkṣṇa-lavaṇoṣṇa-harita-śāka-sauvīra-taila-tila-sarṣapa-madya-matsyān |\
> ajādi-māṃsa-dadhi-takra-kulattha-kola-piṇyāka-hiṅgu-laśunādyam apathyam āhuḥ ||**
>
> "The pungent, the sour, the sharp, the salty, the hot,\
> **green vegetables**, sour gruel, oil, sesame, mustard, alcohol, fish,\
> goat and other flesh, curds, buttermilk, horse-gram, jujube,\
> oil-cake, asafoetida, garlic and the like —\
> these they call unwholesome."\
> — *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā* 1.59

*Harita-śāka* — raw / fresh leafy greens — is named in the apathya list. Not all greens forever; the verse continues elsewhere with permitted vegetables prepared by cooking. But raw greens as a daily staple are excluded from the yogic plate by name.

### Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā 5.21 — the second yogic witness

> "Food that is pungent, sour, salty, bitter, or parched;\
> curd, buttermilk, **an excess of leafy greens**, alcohol, ...\
> these should be avoided by the yogi at the beginning of his Yoga practice."\
> — *Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā* 5.21, trans. Mallinson

### Bhagavad Gītā 17.9 — rajasic register

> **kaṭv-amla-lavaṇāty-uṣṇa-tīkṣṇa-rūkṣa-vidāhinaḥ |\
> āhārā rājasasyeṣṭā duḥkha-śokāmaya-pradāḥ ||**
>
> "Foods that are pungent, sour, salty, excessively hot, sharp, **dry**, and burning\
> are dear to those of agitated nature.\
> Such foods bring forth sorrow, grief, and disease."\
> — *Bhagavad Gītā* 17.9

*Rūkṣa* — dry, rough, lacking the unctuous moisture that cooking-with-ghee provides — is in the rajasic register. The raw vegetable, especially the dry / fibrous one, is *rūkṣa* by classification.

## VII. Uncooked input is the chief external cause of āma

*Āma* is the central pathology of Ayurvedic clinical theory — undigested food residue, the material basis of disease. The classical etiology of *āma* names raw / uncooked / cold input among its principal causes, alongside excessive eating, indigestion-while-eating, and antagonistic combinations.

### Caraka Sū. 25.40 — the etiology list

> "**Excessive eating among the causes of *āma-doṣa*,**\
> intake of food according to power of digestion among those stimulating *agni*,\
> ... **eating during indigestion** and during the period when previous meal is not digested\
> among those damaging the *grahaṇī*,\
> **irregular meals** among those causing irregularity of digestion,\
> intake of food consisting of antagonistic items among those producing despicable diseases."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 25.40, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Aṣṭāṅga Saṅgraha Śā. 6.16 — vitiation of the udakavaha-srotas

> "Of the channels that carry water, the root is the palate (*tālu*) and the *kloma*.\
> They become vitiated by *āma* (undigested residue), fear, excessive drinking,\
> hot food, **dry food (*śuṣka-anna*)**, astringents, and the suppression of thirst."\
> — *Aṣṭāṅga Saṅgraha* Śārīrasthāna 6.16

*Śuṣka-anna* — dry food — is named as a principal vitiator of the water-channels of the body. Dehydrated, raw, fibrous input is *śuṣka* by definition. The classical cure for the dry-food pathology is *snigdha* food — unctuous, warm, cooked. The *Cooking_and_Spices.md* essay's remark holds: *"Cooking is the first stage by which prāṇa is made available."*

### Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya Sū. 8 — what kindles agni

> **uṣṇaṃ snigdhaṃ mātrāvad ajīrṇe'nupasēvitam |\
> agniṃ dīpayati āhāraḥ samyak tu bala-vardhanaḥ ||**
>
> "Food taken **warm**, **unctuous**, in proper measure, and not during indigestion\
> kindles *agni* and nourishes strength."\
> — *Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya*, Sūtrasthāna 8

The four conditions of agni-kindling food: *uṣṇa* (warm), *snigdha* (unctuous), *mātrāvat* (in proper measure), *ajīrṇe'nupasēvitam* (not during indigestion). Raw food fails the first two conditions structurally — it is *śīta* (cold) and *rūkṣa* (dry) by default, the inverse of the kindling conditions. The verse is the positive specification of what cooking achieves: warm, unctuous food kindles the fire that builds the body.

## VIII. The Vedic frame — agni as transformer, not destroyer

Above and behind the Ayurvedic and Yogic corpora stands the Vedic doctrine of *agni*. The first hymn of the Ṛg-veda is to Agni. The whole of Vedic ritual is a cooking — *havis* (oblations) of grain, ghee, milk, and Soma are heated in fire and become offerings fit for the gods. The yajña is the cosmic kitchen. Caraka 27.343 internalizes this frame explicitly: every meal is an *antaragni* offering. Fire is the first recipient and elevator of food — the operator that raises substance from the merely living into the assimilable.

### Taittirīya Upaniṣad 2.2 — the chain from plant to puruṣa

> **oṣadhībhyo'nnam | annāt puruṣaḥ ||**
>
> "From plants arises *anna*. From *anna* arises the human being."\
> — *Taittirīya Upaniṣad* 2.2.1

The Upaniṣad does not say "from plants the human being arises directly." It names *anna* as the intermediate. *Anna* is the cooked-grain transformation of plant into human-edible substance. The transformation is not optional; it is what permits the next link in the chain.

### Caraka Sū. 27.343 — internalized yajña

> "One should offer regularly the oblations of wholesome food and drinks\
> to *antaragni* (internal fire) with due consideration to quantity and time."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.343, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

The Vedic yajña, internalized in the body. The eater offers *havis* to the inner *agni* in the form of properly prepared food. The same way the Vedic priest does not offer raw rice to the external fire, the Caraka eater does not offer uncooked input to the internal one. The eating itself is a sacrificial act; cooking is the preparation of the offering.

### The Vedic implication

The Ṛg-veda begins *agnim īḷe* — "I praise Agni." The Vedic civilization placed fire at the head of its theology because fire is what makes substance tractable to life. The yajña frame is the ancestor of the *anna-rasa-prāṇa* doctrine. Heat as transformer, not destroyer. The raw foodist's claim — that fire steals the life from food — inverts the entire Vedic position.

## IX. The synthesis — cooking builds rasa; rasa builds prāṇa

The classical position is consistent across all three corpora and stated explicitly:

- *Prāṇa* is what the eater builds from properly digested *anna* (Taittirīya 2.2; Bṛhad. *amṛtaṃ vai prāṇāḥ*).
- *Rasa-dhātu* — the first nutritive juice from which all subsequent tissues are built — does not arise from uncooked food (AH Śā. 3.54: *na hy apakvād rasādayaḥ*).
- The body is itself a cooking vessel; external cooking is the first stage of internal cooking (AH Śā. 3.55–56: *audaryo 'gnir yathā bāhyaḥ sthālī-sthaṃ toya-taṇḍulam*).
- *Agni* — the digestive fire — is the cause of strength, health, life-span, and *prāṇa* (Caraka Sū. 27.342).
- Eating is an offering to the internal *agni* (Caraka Sū. 27.343). The yajña frame is internalized.
- Cooking is a *saṃskāra* that transforms *guṇa* — heavy becomes light (Caraka Sū. 27.339: *vrīhi* → *lājā*); hard substances become strength-promoting only when overcooked (Sū. 27.275); the same rice is light when well-cooked and heavy when not (Sū. 27.257–259).
- The classical staple is cooked grain finished with ghee — *yavāgū*, *peya*, *vilēpī*, *odana*, *anna* with *ghṛta* (Caraka Cik. 1; HYP 1.57–58; BhG 17.8).
- The classical apathya lists exclude raw greens by name (HYP 1.59 *harita-śāka*; GhS 5.21 *excess of leafy greens*).
- The dry / *rūkṣa* / *śuṣka-anna* register is named as a principal cause of *āma* and as a vitiator of the body's water-carrying channels (AS Śā. 6.16).

Cooking is what makes food into body. Raw input gives rise to *āma*, not *rasa*. The life force the raw foodist seeks is what the eater *becomes* when *anna* has been properly cooked, properly digested, and properly assimilated. Fire is the elevator of substance into life. To cook is to begin the offering.
