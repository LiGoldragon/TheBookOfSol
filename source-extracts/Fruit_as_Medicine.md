# Fruit as Medicine — Primary-Source Extracts

A thematic digest of *direct passages* from the Ayurvedic (*bṛhat-trayī* — Caraka, Suśruta, Vāgbhaṭa), the Yogic (*Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā*, *Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā*, *Śiva Saṃhitā*, *Bhagavad Gītā*), and the Vedic / Upaniṣadic corpora, bearing on the position that **fruit (*phala*) belongs to medicine (*auṣadha*) before it belongs to food (*āhāra*)** — that the Caraka Saṃhitā classifies fruit by the same parameters it uses for drugs (*rasa, vīrya, vipāka, prabhāva,* indication, contraindication), that the same fruit which heals in measured indication depletes in chronic excess, and that the staple sustenance of the human body is *anna* — cooked grain — with fruit appearing in the daily routine only in its medicinal capacity (*āmalakī* as *rasāyana*; *saindhava* as the one daily salt).

This compilation supports [*Yogis Don't Eat Fruit*](../diet/Yogis_Dont_Eat_Fruit.md), [*Fruitarianism*](../diet/Fruitarianism.md), [*Fruits From India Are Different*](../diet/Fruits_From_India_Are_Different.md), and [*Dehydrated Fruit Mixed with Coconut Butter and Honey*](../diet/Dehydrated_Fruit_Coconut_Honey.md). The general *apathya* / *mitāhāra* doctrine and the canonical prohibition lists (HYP 1.59, GhS 5.21, ŚS 3.33, BhG 17.9) live in [*Apathya* and *Mitāhāra* — primary-source extracts](./Apathya.md); the per-fruit Caraka warnings live in [`caraka-samhita/fruit-vegetable-warnings.md`](https://github.com/LiGoldragon/caraka-samhita/blob/main/fruit-vegetable-warnings.md). This file gathers the *fruit-as-medicine* argument across all three corpora.

Format follows the project's canonical quote-block: bold IAST Sanskrit on top, blank `>` line, English translation in straight quotes, em-dash attribution on the final line. Where the working corpus lacks clean IAST, the passage appears as an English-only blockquote with the attribution underneath.

## I. The doctrinal frame — every substance is potentially auṣadha or viṣa

The Caraka Saṃhitā opens its theory of substance with a doctrine that does not separate medicine from food at all. What separates them is the user's discrimination — *yogavittva*, the knowledge of administration.

### Caraka Sū. 1.124–125 — drug as poison, drug as nectar

> **yathā viṣaṃ yathā śastraṃ yathāgnir aśanir yathā |\
> tathauṣadham avijñātaṃ vijñātaṃ tv amṛtaṃ yathā ||**
>
> "A drug, if unknown, is fatal like poison, weapon, fire and thunderbolt;\
> while, if known, is like nectar."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 1.124–125, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Caraka Sū. 1.126 — poison becomes drug, drug becomes poison

> **yogād api viṣaṃ tīkṣṇam uttamaṃ bheṣajaṃ bhavet |\
> bheṣajaṃ cāpi duryuktaṃ tīkṣṇaṃ saṃpadyate viṣam ||**
>
> "A sharp poison also becomes the best drug by proper administration;\
> on the contrary, even the best drug is reduced to sharp poison\
> if administered badly."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 1.126, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Caraka Sū. 1.134 — the proper-administration test

> "That very medicine may be taken as properly administered\
> which is capable to bring about disease-free condition,\
> and that very physician as the best one\
> who could relieve (the patient) of the disorders."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 1.134, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Caraka Sū. 26.12 — every substance admits pharmacological use

> "There is no substance in the universe which cannot be used as drug,\
> on the condition that they are used rationally\
> and with a definite objective."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 26.12, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

The distinction between *auṣadha* (medicine) and *āhāra* (food) is not that different substances inhabit the two categories. The same substance, given with indication and dose, is *auṣadha*; given without them is *āhāra*; given in excess or in error becomes *viṣa*. Daily fruit eaten in volume sits in the third place, not the first.

## II. The Caraka fruit-list reads as a materia medica

Sūtrasthāna chapter 27 (*Annapānavidhi* — "the methods of food and drink") classifies all foods by *rasa* (taste), *vīrya* (potency), *vipāka* (post-digestive effect), *prabhāva* (specific action), *guru/laghu* (heavy/light), *śīta/uṣṇa* (cold/hot), and doṣic effect. The fruit-group runs from verse 27.125 to 27.165. Every fruit is given an indication and a contraindication. The structure is the structure of the materia medica — the fruit catalogue is built from the same parameters as the drug catalogue.

### Caraka Sū. 27.4 — taste-by-doṣa, fruit named only by its medicinal exception

> "Sour substance is mostly *pitta*-aggravating except pomegranates and *āmalaka*.\
> Sweet is mostly *kapha*-aggravating except honey\
> and old *śāli*, *ṣaṣṭika* (rice), barley and wheat.\
> Bitter is mostly *vāta*-aggravating and non-aphrodisiac\
> except the tip of *vetra*, *guḍūcī* and *paṭola* leaves.\
> Pungent is mostly *vāta*-aggravating and non-aphrodisiac\
> except long pepper and ginger."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.4, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

The fruits that escape the default doṣic-aggravation rule are exactly the fruits classed as *medicines*: *dāḍima* and *āmalaka*. The exceptions are not nutritional outliers; they are pharmacological agents.

### Caraka Sū. 27.5–7 — fruit listed in the same parameters as drugs

> "Hereafter (I) will describe the food substances according to groups such as\
> *śūkadhānya* (awned grains), *śamīdhānya* (legumes), *māṃsa* (meat),\
> *śāka* (vegetables), *phala* (fruit), *harita* (salads), *madya* (alcoholic drinks),\
> *jala* (water), *gorasa* (milk and its products),\
> *ikṣu* (sugarcane and its products), *kṛtānna* (dietary preparations)\
> and *āhārayogī* (condiments) — in terms of *rasa, vīrya, vipāka and prabhāva*."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.5–7, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

*Rasa, vīrya, vipāka, prabhāva* — the same four parameters under which Caraka classifies every drug in his pharmacology. *Phala* is admitted to the food groups but is described under the drug parameters. The grammar is the diagnosis.

### Caraka Sū. 27.143 — *drākṣā* (grapes), the indication list reads as a prescription

> "Grapes alleviate thirst, burning sensation, fever, dyspnoea, internal haemorrhage,\
> wound, wasting, *vāta-pitta*, *udāvarta*, hoarseness of voice, alcoholism,\
> bitterness in mouth, dryness of mouth and cough\
> because of being sweet, aphrodisiac, sweet, unctuous and cold."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.143, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

A clinical-indication list. The passage could be lifted into a *cikitsā* chapter without altering its grammar. *Drākṣā* is a drug with an indication panel.

### Caraka Sū. 27.146 — *āmra* (mango), three pharmacological forms

> "The young fruits of *āmra* (mango) cause internal haemorrhage,\
> the mature ones aggravate *pitta*\
> while the ripe ones alleviate *vāyu* and promote muscles, semen and strength."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.146, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

A single fruit is staged across three developmental forms, each with its own indication and contraindication. Young: *raktapitta*-causing. Mature: *pitta*-aggravating. Ripe: *vāta*-alleviating, tissue-building. Mango is dosed by ripeness as a tincture is dosed by dilution.

### Caraka Sū. 27.156 — *āmalaka*, all rasas except saline

> "In *āmalaka*, there are all the *rasas* except *lavaṇa* (saline).\
> It is rough, sweet, astringent, sour\
> and excellent alleviator of *kapha* and *pitta*."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.156, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

The fruit which carries five of six tastes simultaneously is the *rasāyana* fruit — the one Caraka admits to the daily routine (Sū. 5.12) and names elsewhere as best among age-stabilisers (Sū. 25.40). Its admission to the daily plate is its medicinal credential, not its nutritional one.

### Caraka Sū. 27.157 — *dāḍima* (pomegranate), three varieties, three clinical profiles

> "*Dāḍima* (pomegranate), which is sour and astringent-sweet,\
> alleviates *vāta*, is anti-diarrhoeal, appetiser,\
> unctuous, hot, cordial and non-conflicting with *kapha* and *pitta*.\
> That which is only sour and rough aggravates *pitta* and *vāta*;\
> while only sweet alleviates *pitta*.\
> Out of these (three varieties) the first one (sour-sweet) is the best one."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.157, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

Three cultivars, three pharmacological signatures. The same logic that governs the dispensing of a multi-form drug governs the eating of a pomegranate.

### Caraka Sū. 27.158 — six fruits that "alleviate three doṣas and poison"

> "*Nīpa, śatāhvaka, pīlu, ketakī, vikaṅkata* and *prācīnāmalaka* —\
> these alleviate (three) *doṣas* and poison."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.158, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

The fruits named here are described in exactly the language Caraka uses for *tridoṣaghna* and *viṣaghna* drugs. The verbs are interchangeable with those used in the materia medica chapters (Sū. 1, Sū. 4) — *alleviate doṣas, neutralise poison*. These fruits are pharmacology, named at the moment of description.

### Caraka Sū. 27.158b — *bibhītaka*, by tissue and disease

> "*Bibhītaka* removes the disorders produced in *rasa, rakta, māṃsa* and *medas*,\
> and alleviates hoarseness of voice, excessive secretion of sputum,\
> and *paittika* disorders."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.158b, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

A fruit indexed by *dhātu* (tissue) and by named disease. The structure is the structure of a clinical entry.

## III. Fruits explicitly named in the best-of-class medicinal list

Sūtrasthāna chapter 25 (*Yajjaḥ-Puruṣīya* — on the origin of person and disease) contains the longest single passage in the Caraka where each substance is named as the best of its category. Most categories are medicinal — *dīpanīya* (appetiser), *grāhī* (constipating), *rakta-pitta-śamana* (haemostatic), *rasāyana* (rejuvenative), *tridoṣaghna* (alleviator of all three doṣas). When fruits appear in this list, they appear in the medicinal categories.

### Caraka Sū. 25.40 — āmalakī as the best rasāyana

> "*āmalakī* fruits among age-stabilisers,\
> *harītakī* among those wholesome for channels,\
> *kāśmarya* (fruit) among haemostatics and pacifiers of internal haemorrhage,\
> *bilwa* among astringents, appetisers and pacifiers of *kapha* and *pitta*."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 25.40, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

*Vayaḥsthāpana* — "age-stabilising," the technical term Caraka uses for *rasāyana*. The fruit on the daily-routine list earns its place by virtue of being the canonical rejuvenative. The same passage names *harītakī* — also a fruit — as the best *srotas*-cleanser. *Kāśmarya* fruit as the best *raktapitta*-pacifier. The structure of the verse is consistent: each fruit appears under a medicinal heading, never under a food heading.

### Caraka Sū. 25.40 — the principle that decides daily eating

> "Regular intake of all the *rasas* among the strength-promoting ones,\
> regular use of one *rasa* among the debilitating ones."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 25.40, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

The aphoristic anchor for the entire fruit-as-medicine doctrine. Eat all six *rasas* daily — the meal a six-rasa meal — and the body grows strong. Eat any one *rasa* daily — including *madhura*, even when the source is a fruit — and the body weakens. A fruit-only diet is the consumption of one *rasa*, mostly sweet, in chronic excess. By this verse, the diet is debilitating before any specific fruit is named.

### Caraka Sū. 25.38 — grapes as the best wholesome among fruits

> "Red *śāli* rice is the best wholesome among the awned cereals,\
> green gram among the legumes ... *jīvantī* among the pot-herbs ...\
> ginger among the tubers, **grapes among fruits**, sugar among the products of sugarcane.\
> Thus the substances of diet which are naturally wholesome\
> are explained according to importance."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 25.38, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

The structure of the verse pairs each food category with its best-of-class. Most categories produce *anna*-class staples — rice, legumes, ginger, sugar. The fruit category produces *drākṣā* — and *drākṣā*'s indication list (Sū. 27.143 above) is a clinical-prescription list. The "best fruit" is the most reliably medicinal one.

### Caraka Sū. 25.39 — *lakuca* as the worst unwholesome among fruits

> "*Yavaka* is the most unwholesome among the awned cereals,\
> black gram among the legumes ...\
> **lakuca among fruits**, *śluka* among tubers,\
> *phāṇita* among the sugarcane products."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 25.39, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

A category in which the worst entry is named, by name, as worst. The category is being managed.

## IV. Rasa-atiyoga — every taste, used singly and in excess, harms

Caraka Sū. 26 establishes the central doctrinal pivot: even the most prized *rasa*, used alone and in excess, generates disease. *Madhura* — the *rasa* of fruit — is no exception.

### Caraka Sū. 26.43 — madhura rasa, used singly and excessively

> **sa evaṅguṇo'py eka evātyartham upayujyamānaḥ\
> sthaulyaṃ mārdavam ālasyam atisvapnaṃ gauravam\
> anannābhilāṣam agner daurbalyam\
> ... kaphajān vikārān upajanayati ||**
>
> "This *rasa* (madhura), though having many qualities,\
> if used singly and excessively, causes obesity, softness, laziness,\
> excessive sleep, heaviness, loss of appetite, weakness of *agni* ...\
> and generates diseases of *kapha*."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 26.43, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

The fruit is sweet. The fruitarian eats sweet alone. The verse is the diagnosis of the diet before the diet is named.

### Caraka Sū. 26.44 — proper measure beneficial; otherwise harmful

> "These six *rasas*, when used in proper quantity,\
> are beneficial for the living beings, otherwise they become harmful.\
> Hence the wise should use these properly in proper quantity\
> in order to derive benefit (from them)."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 26.44, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

The criterion is *mātrā*, not category. A fruit eaten in measure is medicine; the same fruit eaten in volume is harm. The classical test is whether the substance is digesting *as anticipated* — neither escaping the system through *atiyoga* nor accumulating through *hīna-yoga*.

## V. Mātrā — the measure that decides whether ahāra heals or harms

Caraka chapter 5 (*Mātrāśitīya* — "on quantity of food etc.") and *Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya* 8 establish the same rule: every food, light or heavy, depends on quantity matched to *agnibala* (digestive power). The rule binds fruit as it binds every other food.

### Caraka Sū. 5.3 — mātrā depends on agnibala

> **mātrāśī syāt; āhāramātrā punar agnibalāpekṣiṇī ||**
>
> "One should take food in (proper) quantity.\
> This quantity of food depends on the power of digestion."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 5.3, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Caraka Sū. 5.4 — the mātrā is what digests in time

> "Whatever quantity of food taken gets digested in time without disturbing the normalcy\
> should be regarded as the measure of (proper) food."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 5.4, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Caraka Sū. 5.6 — light articles bound to mātrā as much as heavy ones

> "By this, it should not be confused that lightness and heaviness of (food) articles are quite inert.\
> ... Light articles, being stimulant of digestion,\
> even taken up to the saturation point, produce little derangement.\
> Heavy articles, because of dissimilarity, are not stimulant of digestion by nature\
> and thus cause considerable derangement if taken up to the saturation point ...\
> Hence the quantity (of food) is related to *agnibala* (power of digestion)."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 5.6, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

The Caraka catalogue (27.125–165) classifies most fruits as *guru* (heavy), *ādhmāna* (distending), or doṣa-aggravating. Caraka's rule: heavy articles taken to saturation cause considerable derangement. Fruits eaten in volume meet exactly this rule. Even the lighter fruits, by 5.7, exceed the agni when over-saturated:

### Caraka Sū. 5.7 — even light articles must respect mātrā

> "It is not so that the article itself has no importance in this respect.\
> Considering the (property of the) article,\
> the intake of heavy ones is advised as one-third or half of the saturation point\
> and not excessive saturation even in case of light articles\
> in order to maintain the proper (strength of) *agni*."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 5.7, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

### Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya Sū. 8.1–2 — measure kindles agni

> **mātrāśī sarva-kālaṃ syān mātrā hy agneḥ pravartikā |\
> mātrāṃ dravyāṇy apekṣante gurūṇy api laghūny api ||\
> gurūṇām ardha-sauhityaṃ laghūnāṃ nāti-tṛpta-tā |\
> mātrā-pramāṇaṃ nirdiṣṭaṃ sukhaṃ yāvad vijīryati ||**
>
> "One should always eat in measure — for measure is what kindles *agni*.\
> All substances depend on measure, whether heavy or light.\
> Of heavy foods, half-fullness; of light foods, not over-fullness:\
> this is the standard, eating only as much as digests with ease."\
> — *Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya*, Sūtrasthāna 8.1–2

### Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā 1.15 — without mitāhāra, no yoga

> **mitāhāraṃ vinā yas tu yogārambhaṃ tu kārayet |\
> nānā-rogo bhaved tasya kiñcid yogo na sidhyati ||**
>
> "Without moderate diet, whoever begins yoga practice\
> will acquire various diseases\
> and will not succeed in yoga."\
> — *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā* 1.15

The *Pradīpikā* makes the absence of *mātrā* the disqualifier of practice — *kiñcid yogo na sidhyati*, "no yoga succeeds." A diet that exceeds *mātrā* — fruit eaten as food, in volume, daily — disqualifies the practitioner before practice begins.

## VI. The classical daily routine — anna at the centre, fruit at the medicinal edge

The classical daily-routine verse names the substances admitted to the everyday plate. Most are grain. One salt is admitted. One fruit is admitted, and the fruit is admitted in its medicinal capacity.

### Caraka Sū. 5.12 — the daily-routine list

> **ṣaṣṭikāḥ śāli-mudgāś ca saindhavaṃ āmalakāni ca |\
> yavā dhānyaṃ ca kṣīraṃ ca sarpir madhu ca ...||**
>
> "One should take as daily routine *ṣaṣṭika* and *śāli* (rice), *mudga* (pulse),\
> *saindhava* (rock salt), *āmalaka* (fruits),\
> barley, rain water, milk, ghee and honey."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 5.12, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

The list is the entire Caraka theory of daily eating in one verse. *Ṣaṣṭika, śāli, mudga, yava, dhānya* — five grains. *Kṣīra, sarpis, madhu* — milk, ghee, honey. *Saindhava* — the one salt. *Āmalaka* — the one fruit. Each item earns its place by indication: *saindhava* because it is the only salt that does not damage the eye (AH); *āmalaka* because it is the *rasāyana* (Sū. 25.40, 27.156). The daily plate is grain plus rasāyana plus medicinal salt. Fruit appears once, as the medicinal exception.

### Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā 1.57 — the yogin's food

> **hitaṃ mitaṃ ca bhoktavyaṃ annaṃ yogārthinā sadā |**
>
> "The yogin should always eat wholesome *anna* in measured quantity."\
> — *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā* 1.57

The word is *anna* — cooked grain — not *phala* (fruit), not *śāka* (vegetable). The Yogic corpus and the Ayurvedic corpus converge on the same staple at the same word.

### Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā 1.58 — the positive quality of the yogic plate

> **susnigdha-madhurāhāraś caturthāṃśa-vivarjitaḥ |\
> bhujyate śiva-samprītyai mitāhāraḥ sa ucyate ||**
>
> "Eating food that is unctuous (*snigdha*) and sweet (*madhura*),\
> leaving a fourth of the stomach empty,\
> offered to please Śiva —\
> this is called measured eating."\
> — *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā* 1.58

The two adjectives that describe the yogic meal are *snigdha* and *madhura*. Fruit is *madhura* but rarely *snigdha* — it carries water, not unctuosity. The yogic meal is grain cooked with ghee. The fruit's sweetness is admitted as a flavour-axis; the fruit itself is not the body of the meal.

### Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā 1.63 — the yogi's milk-and-ghee plate

> "The yogi should take nourishing and sweet food\
> mixed with ghee and milk;\
> it should nourish the *dhātus* and be pleasing and suitable."\
> — *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā* 1.63, trans. Muktibodhananda

The food that nourishes the *dhātus* is *anna* finished with *ghṛta*. Fruit clears; *anna* + *ghṛta* binds.

### Bhagavad Gītā 17.8 — sāttvic food is *snigdha-sthira*

> **āyuḥ-sattva-balārogya-sukha-prīti-vivardhanāḥ |\
> rasyāḥ snigdhāḥ sthirā hṛdyā āhārāḥ sāttvika-priyāḥ ||**
>
> "Foods that increase life, clarity, strength, health, happiness, and satisfaction —\
> that are juicy, unctuous, substantial, and agreeable —\
> are dear to those in *sattva*."\
> — *Bhagavad Gītā* 17.8

The two words that decide the dharmic-yogic *sāttvika āhāra*: *snigdha* (unctuous) and *sthira* (stable). Fruit is *rasya* but light, watery, and dispersing — *cala*, not *sthira*. It does not match the second adjective. The *Gītā*'s definition of sāttvic food, applied to fruit, returns the verdict: not the body of the plate.

## VII. The Yogic apathya lists — fruit named only by exception

The canonical yogic *apathya* verses prohibit the rough tastes, raw greens, sour gruel, alcohol, fish, flesh, and a long list of specific items. Fruit, as a *category*, is not in the prohibition; specific fruits are. The *Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā* names *kola* (jujube), *bimba*, *udumbara*, *nikuca*, and the silk-cotton fruit among substances the yogi must avoid. Other named fruits — plantain, pomegranate, grapes, *āmalaka*, dates — are admitted as *accessory items*, taken seasonally and in measure.

### Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā 5.21 — the yogic prohibition

> "Food that is pungent, sour, salty, bitter, or parched;\
> curd, buttermilk, an excess of leafy greens, alcohol,\
> palm nut, jackfruit, horse gram, masur and white beans,\
> white pumpkin, the stalks of leafy greens, bottle gourd, jujube,\
> elephant apple, thorny bel, dhak, kadamba, lemon, bimba, breadfruit,\
> garlic, lotus stalk, wild turmeric, chironji, asafoetida,\
> the fruit of the silk-cotton tree, and taro root:\
> these should be avoided by the yogi at the beginning of his Yoga practice."\
> — *Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā* 5.21, trans. Mallinson

### Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā 5.22–24 — what *is* permitted (the seasonal-fruit register)

The text follows immediately with a list of permitted items: rice, barley, wheat, milk, ghee, sugar, *mudga*; and the named fruits: plantain, pomegranate, grapes, *āmalaka*, dates. The structure is the structure of an apothecary's permitted-list: each named, none generalised. Fruit, even when admitted, is admitted item-by-item, the way a medicine is admitted by indication.

### Bhagavad Gītā 17.9 — the rajasic register

> **kaṭv-amla-lavaṇāty-uṣṇa-tīkṣṇa-rūkṣa-vidāhinaḥ |\
> āhārā rājasasyeṣṭā duḥkha-śokāmaya-pradāḥ ||**
>
> "Foods that are pungent, sour, salty, excessively hot, sharp, dry, and burning\
> are dear to those of agitated nature.\
> Such foods bring forth sorrow, grief, and disease."\
> — *Bhagavad Gītā* 17.9

The *Gītā* keeps fruit out of both lists — it appears neither in the sāttvic register (17.8) nor in the rajasic prohibition (17.9). Where medicines sit, fruit sits: somewhere by specific indication, nowhere by category.

## VIII. The Vedic / Upaniṣadic foundation — anna at the origin of the embodied being

Above and behind the Ayurvedic and Yogic corpora stands the Vedic frame. The *Taittirīya Upaniṣad* traces the human being to *anna*. The *Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad* names *prāṇa* as *amṛta*. Neither names *phala* at the foundation.

### Taittirīya Upaniṣad 2.1.1 — body as anna-rasa-mayaḥ

> **annaṃ na nindyāt | tad vratam |\
> ... anna-rasa-mayo 'yam ātmā ||**
>
> "One should not despise food. That is the rule.\
> ... This self is made of the essence of *anna*."\
> — *Taittirīya Upaniṣad* 2.1, trans. Olivelle

The body is *anna-rasa-maya* — composed of the *rasa* of *anna*. The Upaniṣad identifies the substance of the body with the substance of grain. The grammar is anatomical. From cooked grain the *rasa-dhātu* is built (AH Śā. 3.53–54); from *rasa-dhātu* the remaining *dhātus*; from those the *ojas* and *prāṇa*. The chain is grain → plasma → tissue → vitality. The chain does not start at fruit.

### Taittirīya Upaniṣad 2.2.1 — anna at the origin

> **oṣadhībhyo'nnam | annāt puruṣaḥ ||**
>
> "From plants arises *anna*. From *anna* arises the human being."\
> — *Taittirīya Upaniṣad* 2.2.1

### Taittirīya Upaniṣad 2.2.4 — anna as Brahman

> **annaṃ brahmeti vyajānāt ||**
>
> "He knew *anna* as Brahman."\
> — *Taittirīya Upaniṣad* 2.2.4

The metaphysical equation rests on *anna*, not *phala*. The Upaniṣad's most-cited line on the food-foundation of being identifies that foundation as the same substance HYP 1.57 names as the yogin's daily food.

### Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad — prāṇa as amṛta

> **amṛtaṃ vai prāṇāḥ |**
>
> "*Prāṇa* itself is *amṛta*."\
> — *Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad*

*Prāṇa* — the ambrosia within the body — is what the eater makes of *anna* when the classical discipline is respected. Fruit feeds neither the rasa-dhātu (AH Śā. 3.54: "from uncooked food, *rasa* and the rest do not arise") nor *prāṇa* directly. It clears, refines, sweeps. *Anna* builds. *Ghṛta* binds.

## IX. The synthesis — phala is auṣadha; phala is not anna

Two doctrines hold across the Ayurvedic, Yogic, and Vedic corpora. The substance is not inherently food or medicine; the user's discrimination decides into which category it falls (Caraka 1.124–126, 26.12). And the staple of the human body is *anna* — cooked grain — to which fruit is admitted by specific indication, in measure, in season (Caraka 5.12; HYP 1.57; Taittirīya Up. 2.2).

The Caraka Saṃhitā classifies fruit pharmacologically. Each fruit carries a *rasa* signature, a *vīrya*, a *vipāka*, a *prabhāva*, an indication, a contraindication. *Āmalakī* is the best *rasāyana*. *Harītakī* is the best *srotas*-cleanser. *Drākṣā* alleviates thirst, fever, burning, internal haemorrhage, *vāta-pitta*, *udāvarta*, hoarseness, alcoholism, dryness of mouth, and cough — a list of clinical indications. *Dāḍima* exists in three varieties with three distinct doṣic signatures. Mango is dosed by ripeness. Six fruits — *nīpa, śatāhvaka, pīlu, ketakī, vikaṅkata, prācīnāmalaka* — are *tridoṣaghna* and *viṣaghna*, the same language Caraka uses for the most prized of his drugs.

The same chapter that names these fruits as medicines names the criterion for daily eating: *all six rasas*, in measure, are strength-promoting; *one rasa*, taken alone and chronically, is debilitating. A fruit-only diet is the consumption of one *rasa* — *madhura* — in chronic excess. Caraka has classified that mode of eating as a debilitator before any specific fruit is named.

The position is the position of the entire classical corpus. Fruit is *auṣadha*. Eaten by indication, in measure, in season, it heals — *āmalakī* steadies age, *dāḍima* alleviates *vāta*, *drākṣā* abates fever and *raktapitta*. Eaten as food — daily, in volume, without indication — it depletes *ojas*, dries the channels, and disqualifies the practitioner from yoga (HYP 1.15). The classical corpus does not call fruit *anna*. The classical corpus calls *anna* *anna*.
