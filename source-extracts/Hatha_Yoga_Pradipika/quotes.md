# Hatha Yoga Pradīpikā — Selected Quotes

Extracted from *Hatha Yoga Pradīpikā* with commentary by Swami
Muktibodhananda (Bihar School of Yoga).


## The Purpose and Nature of Hatha Yoga

> "Salutations to the glorious primal guru, Sri Adinath, who instructed the
> knowledge of hatha yoga which shines forth as a stairway for those who wish
> to ascend to the highest stage of yoga, raja yoga."
> — HYP 1.1

> "Hatha yoga is the greatest secret of the yogis who wish to attain perfection
> (siddhi). Indeed, to be fruitful, it must be kept secret; revealed it
> becomes powerless."
> — HYP 1.11

> "All the processes of hatha and laya yoga are but the means to attain raja
> yoga. One who attains raja yoga is victorious over time (death)."
> — HYP 4.103

> "Tattwa is the seed, hatha is the soil, total desirelessness (vairagya) is
> the water. By these three the kalpa vriksha — the wish-fulfilling tree —
> which is the unmani avastha immediately sprouts forth."
> — HYP 4.104


## Prāṇa and Life

> "When prana moves, chitta moves. When prana is without movement, chitta is
> without movement. By this steadiness of prana the yogi attains steadiness
> and should thus restrain the vayu."
> — HYP 2.2

> "As long as the vayu remains in the body, that is called life. Death is when
> it leaves the body. Therefore, retain vayu."
> — HYP 2.3

> "Mind is the ruler of the senses, prana is the ruler of the mind. Dissolution
> is the lord of the prana and that dissolution is the basis of nada."
> — HYP 4.29

> "How can there be inner knowledge in the mind, as long as prana is alive and
> mind is not dead? As long as the twofold nature of mind and prana can be
> quiescent, liberation is attained."
> — HYP 4.15

> "The sun and moon divide time into day and night. Sushumna is the consumer of
> time. This is the conveyed secret."
> — HYP 4.17

> "When the prana flows in the sushumna this state of manonmani is established.
> Therefore, other forced practices are just laborious to the yogi."
> — HYP 4.20

> "While the prana does not flow in the middle passage of sushumna, while the
> bindu is not steadied by restraining the prana, while mind does not reflect
> spontaneous meditation, then those who speak of spiritual knowledge are only
> indulging in boastful and false tales."
> — HYP 4.114


## Kuṇḍalinī

> "As the serpent upholds the earth and its mountains and woods, so kundalini is
> the support of all the yoga practices."
> — HYP 3.1

> "Indeed, by guru's grace this sleeping kundalini is awakened, then all the
> lotuses and knots are opened."
> — HYP 3.2

> "Then indeed, sushumna becomes the pathway of prana, mind is free of all
> connections and death is averted."
> — HYP 3.3


## Āsana

> "Of all the eighty-four asanas, siddhasana should always be practiced. It
> purifies the 72,000 nadis."
> — HYP 1.39

> "Just as moderate diet is the most important of the yamas, and non-violence,
> of the niyamas, so the siddhas know that siddhasana is the most important of
> the asanas."
> — HYP 1.38

> "When perfection is attainable through siddhasana, what is the use of
> practicing many other asanas? When the flow of prana is stabilized, the
> breath stops spontaneously and a mindless state arises by itself."
> — HYP 1.41

> "There is no asana like siddhasana, no kumbhaka like kevala, no mudra like
> khechari and no laya or dissolution of mind like nada, the inner sound."
> — HYP 1.43


## Diet (Mitāhāra)

> "Mitahara is defined as agreeable and sweet food, leaving one fourth of the
> stomach free, and eaten as an offering to please Shiva."
> — HYP 1.58

> "The yogi should take nourishing and sweet food mixed with ghee and milk; it
> should nourish the dhatus and be pleasing and suitable."
> — HYP 1.63


## Mind and Prāṇa

> "Chitta has two causes, vasana and prana. When one of the two is destroyed or
> inactivated the other also will become immobile."
> — HYP 4.22

> "Where mind is stilled, then the prana is suspended there, and where prana is
> suspended, there the mind is still. Mind and prana are mixed like milk and
> water."
> — HYP 4.23–24

> "Mercury and mind are unstable by nature. By stabilizing mercury and mind,
> what cannot be perfected?"
> — HYP 4.26


## Mudrā and Bandha

> "Maha mudra, maha bandha, maha vedha, khechari, uddiyana, moola bandha and
> jalandhara bandha — verily, these are the ten mudras which destroy old age
> and death."
> — HYP 3.6–7


## Bindu, Ojas, and Vitality

> "As long as the bindu is steady in the body, then where is the fear of death?
> The yogi's body smells pleasant by conserving the bindu."
> — HYP 3.88

> "Preservation of the bindu is life."
> — HYP 3.89

> "A man's semen can be controlled by the mind and control of semen is
> life-giving. Therefore, his semen and mind should be controlled and
> conserved."
> — HYP 3.90


## Vajrolī, Sahajolī, and Amarolī

> "Drinking the cool midstream of urine — the first part of the urine is left
> as it contains bile, and the last part is left as it does not contain
> goodness."
> — HYP 3.96–97 (Amarolī)

> "Verily this yoga is perfected by virtuous and well-conducted men who have
> seen the truth and not those who are selfish."
> — HYP 3.95


## Nāda — The Inner Sound

> "Nada is like the net which snares the deer inside; it is also like the
> hunter who slays the deer."
> — HYP 4.94

> "Just as liquid mercury is solidified by sulphur, so mind is bound by nada."
> — HYP 4.96

> "As fire burns wood and both subside together, so the mind which moves with
> nada is absorbed in it."
> — HYP 4.97

> "Just as a deer attracted by the sound of bells is easily killed by an expert
> archer, so is the mind silenced by an adept in nada yoga."
> — HYP 4.99

> "Whatever nada the mind initially adheres to, it becomes perfectly still in
> that and dissolves with it."
> — HYP 4.89

> "Just as a bee drinking honey is unconcerned about the fragrance, so the mind
> engaged in nada is not craving for sensual objects."
> — HYP 4.90

> "One hears the sound of the unstruck resonance; the quintessence of that
> sound is the supreme object — consciousness. The mind becomes one with that
> object of knowledge and it dissolves therein. That is the supreme state of
> Vishnu."
> — HYP 4.100

> "Whatever is heard of the nature of the mystical nada is indeed Shakti. That
> in which all the elements find dissolution, that is the formless being, the
> supreme lord."
> — HYP 4.101–102


## Samādhi

> "As salt merges in the sea, likewise the mind and atma are considered united
> in samadhi."
> — HYP 4.5

> "When the movement of prana is completely annihilated, then mind is reabsorbed
> and then samadhi is considered attained."
> — HYP 4.6

> "As camphor dissolves in fire, and salt dissolves in the sea, in samadhi mind
> dissolves into Thatness."
> — HYP 4.59

> "All that is in this world, animate and inanimate, is the appearance of mind.
> When mind attains unmani, duality is lost."
> — HYP 4.61

> "The yogi who has gone beyond all the states of consciousness, who is freed
> of thought, who appears dead — impervious to stimulus — is liberated
> without doubt."
> — HYP 4.107

> "In samadhi a yogi is neither consumed by the processes of time nor is he
> affected by action nor affected by any influence."
> — HYP 4.108

> "He who seems asleep in the waking state, who is without breathing yet is
> perfectly healthy, is verily liberated."
> — HYP 4.112


## Cautions, Warnings, and Aphorisms

> "Overeating, exertion, talkativeness, adhering to rules, being in the company
> of common people and unsteadiness — these are the six causes which destroy
> yoga."
> — HYP 1.15

> "Enthusiasm, perseverance, discrimination, unshakeable faith, courage,
> avoiding the company of common people — these are the six causes which bring
> success in yoga."
> — HYP 1.16

> "Perfection results from practical application. Without practicing how can it
> happen? Just by reading the shastras perfection in yoga will never be
> attained."
> — HYP 1.65

> "Neither by wearing the garb of a siddha, nor by talking about it is
> perfection attained. Only through practical application does one become a
> siddha. This is the truth without a doubt."
> — HYP 1.66

> "Without the compassion of the true guru, renunciation is impossible,
> perception of the truth inaccessible and sahaja samadhi unobtainable."
> — HYP 4.8–9
