# Death and the Immortal Essence — Extracts from the Hermetica

Primary-source extractions from the *Corpus Hermeticum* and the *Asclepius* on three linked theses: that physical immortality is impossible, that the body's dissolution is structurally necessary, and that incarnate life is the unique window in which a human being may compose — out of mind and the shedding of the twelve vices — a body of powers that survives the body of flesh.

Translations: Brian P. Copenhaver, *Hermetica: The Greek Corpus Hermeticum and the Latin Asclepius* (Cambridge, 1992). Supplementary references from Clement Salaman et al., *The Way of Hermes* (Inner Traditions, 2000), especially the *Definitions of Hermes Trismegistus to Asclepius*.

## I. Man is twofold: mortal in body, immortal in the essential man

The Poimandres discourse (*C.H.* I) opens the entire Hermetic teaching by placing the anthrōpos between the cosmic framework above and the watery matter below. The body is a joining of the two that must one day come apart.

> "Because of this, unlike any other living thing on earth, mankind is twofold — in the body mortal but immortal in the essential man. Even though he is immortal and has authority over all things, mankind is affected by mortality because he is subject to fate; thus, although man is above the cosmic framework, he became a slave within it."\
> — *Corpus Hermeticum* I.15, trans. Copenhaver

The fall into gendered embodiment is the fall into mortality: desire, not sin, is the cause.

> "'Increase in increasing and multiply in multitude, all you creatures and craftworks, and let him who is mindful recognize that he is immortal, that desire is the cause of death, and let him recognize all that exists.'"\
> — *Corpus Hermeticum* I.18, trans. Copenhaver

> "The one who recognized himself attained the chosen good, but the one who loved the body that came from the error of desire goes on in darkness, errant, suffering sensibly the effects of death."\
> — *Corpus Hermeticum* I.19, trans. Copenhaver

## II. Dissolution is the law of composite bodies

*C.H.* VIII is the most compact Hermetic statement of the thesis that nothing real is destroyed, but everything composite must dissolve. Heavenly bodies recur without dissolution; earthly bodies recur *through* dissolution. This distinction — not a denial of death, but a relocation of what in us is undying — is load-bearing for the whole corpus.

> "He wanted to adorn what comes after him with every quality, to surround the whole body with immortality so that even matter, tending to separate from the composition of this body, would not dissolve into its typical disorder. … Especially here below, matter has the property of increase and decrease that humans call death."\
> — *Corpus Hermeticum* VIII.3, trans. Copenhaver

> "But this disorder arises among things that live on earth; the bodies of heavenly beings have a single order that they got from the father in the beginning. And this order is kept undissolved by the recurrence of each of them. The recurrence of earthly bodies, by contrast, is the dissolution of their composition, and this dissolution causes them to recur as undissolved bodies — immortal, in other words. Thus arises a loss of awareness but not a destruction of bodies."\
> — *Corpus Hermeticum* VIII.4, trans. Copenhaver

The same teaching is repeated in *C.H.* XI, where death is explicitly redefined as a passage rather than an ending for what is in us:

> "Death is not the destruction of things that have been combined but the dissolution of their union. They say that change is death because the body is dissolved and life passes on to the unseen."\
> — *Corpus Hermeticum* XI.15, trans. Copenhaver

The *Asclepius* repeats it once more, this time in the direct voice of counsel:

> "Death results from the disintegration of a body worn out with work, after the time has passed when the body's members fit into a single mechanism with vital functions. The body dies, in fact, when it can no longer support a person's vital processes. This is death, then: the body's disintegration and the extinction of bodily consciousness. Worrying about it is pointless. But there is another problem worth worrying about, though people disregard it out of ignorance or disbelief."\
> — *Asclepius* 27, trans. Copenhaver

The problem worth worrying about is what happens to the *other* part — not the part that will certainly dissolve, but the part whose fate in the dissolution is decided by how the life was used.

## III. The mixing bowl: life as the window in which to receive mind

*C.H.* IV is the Hermetic doctrine of vocation. God shared reason (*logos*) with all people but not mind (*nous*); mind was put in a mixing bowl and sent down as a prize for souls to contest. Those who "immerse themselves" receive it and become immortal rather than mortal; those who do not remain rational animals. Incarnate life is the window in which this is possible, because it is only from the body that the bowl can be reached.

> "'He filled a great mixing bowl with it and sent it below, appointing a herald whom he commanded to make the following proclamation to human hearts: Immerse yourself in the mixing bowl if your heart has the strength, if it believes you will rise up again to the one who sent the mixing bowl below, if it recognizes the purpose of your coming to be.' All those who heeded the proclamation and immersed themselves in mind participated in knowledge and became perfect people because they received mind. But those who missed the point of the proclamation are people of reason because they did not receive the gift of mind as well and do not know the purpose or the agents of their coming to be."\
> — *Corpus Hermeticum* IV.4, trans. Copenhaver

> "But those who participate in the gift that comes from god, O Tat, are immortal rather than mortal if one compares their deeds, for in a mind of their own they have comprehended all things on earth, things in heaven and even what lies beyond heaven."\
> — *Corpus Hermeticum* IV.5, trans. Copenhaver

The choice is structural, and it cannot be made twice:

> "My child, it is impossible to be engaged in both realms, the mortal and the divine. Since there are two kinds of entities, corporeal and incorporeal, corresponding to mortal and divine, one is left to choose one or the other, if choice is desired. … Choosing the stronger, then, not only has splendid consequences for the one who chooses — in that it makes the human into a god — but it also shows reverence toward god. On the other hand, choosing the lesser has been mankind's destruction."\
> — *Corpus Hermeticum* IV.6–7, trans. Copenhaver

## IV. The way up: shedding the energies of the seven zones

At the close of the Poimandres, Hermes is shown the mechanism of the ascent. The material body is released; the shape vanishes; the temperament passes to the demon; the body's senses "rise up and flow back to their particular sources." Then the human being ascends through the seven planetary zones, surrendering at each one an acquired energy — the very energies that had composed the condition of being alive in a body. What remains at the ogdoad is no longer the man who was born and died; it is the bare *proper power*, joined to the powers, hymning the father.

> "'First, in releasing the material body you give the body itself over to alteration, and the form that you used to have vanishes. To the demon you give over your temperament, now inactive. The body's senses rise up and flow back to their particular sources, becoming separate parts and mingling again with the energies. And feeling and longing go on toward irrational nature. Thence the human being rushes up through the cosmic framework, at the first zone surrendering the energy of increase and decrease; at the second evil machination, a device now inactive; at the third the illusion of longing, now inactive; at the fourth the ruler's arrogance, now freed of excess; at the fifth unholy presumption and daring recklessness; at the sixth the evil impulses that come from wealth, now inactive; and at the seventh zone the deceit that lies in ambush. And then, stripped of the effects of the cosmic framework, the human enters the region of the ogdoad; he has his own proper power, and along with the blessed he hymns the father. … They rise up to the father in order and surrender themselves to the powers, and, having become powers, they enter into god. This is the final good for those who have received knowledge: to be made god.'"\
> — *Corpus Hermeticum* I.24–26, trans. Copenhaver

Poimandres is explicit that most are not on this trajectory at all. The sleep of the body is a sleep of ignorance from which the majority do not wake. The call he makes is directly to the incarnate living:

> "'Why have you surrendered yourselves to death, earthborn men, since you have the right to share in immortality? You who have journeyed with error, who have partnered with ignorance, think again: escape the shadowy light; leave corruption behind and take a share in immortality.'"\
> — *Corpus Hermeticum* I.28, trans. Copenhaver

## V. Rebirth: the twelve punishments repulsed by the ten powers

*C.H.* XIII is the Hermetic ritual-discourse on "being born again." The new body is not a physical body: it is a body of the ten powers which replaces the twelve punishments of the zodiacal body. The old body is dissoluble; the new body "cannot succumb to dissolution."

> "'This ignorance, my child, is the first torment; the second is grief; the third is incontinence; the fourth, lust; the fifth, injustice; the sixth, greed; the seventh, deceit; the eighth, envy; the ninth, treachery; the tenth, anger; the eleventh, recklessness; the twelfth, malice. These are twelve in number, but under them are many more besides, my child, and they use the prison of the body to torture the inward person with the sufferings of sense. Yet they withdraw — if not all at once — from one to whom god has shown mercy, and this is the basis of rebirth, the means and method.'"\
> — *Corpus Hermeticum* XIII.7, trans. Copenhaver

> "'My child, you have come to know the means of rebirth. The arrival of the decad sets in order a birth of mind that expels the twelve; we have been divinized by this birth. Therefore, whoever through mercy has attained this godly birth and has forsaken bodily sensation recognizes himself as constituted of the intelligibles and rejoices.'"\
> — *Corpus Hermeticum* XIII.10, trans. Copenhaver

Tat then asks the only question that matters for our thesis: does this new body also dissolve? Hermes reacts as if to a blasphemy. The composite body that was born of flesh *must* dissolve; the body constituted of powers *cannot*.

> "'Tell me, father, does this body constituted of powers ever succumb to dissolution?' 'Hold your tongue; do not give voice to the impossible! Else you will do wrong, and your mind's eye will be profaned. The sensible body of nature is far removed from essential generation. One can be dissolved, but the other is indissoluble; one is mortal, the other immortal. Do you not know that you have been born a god and a child of the one, as I, too, have?'"\
> — *Corpus Hermeticum* XIII.14, trans. Copenhaver

## VI. The Asclepius on man the great miracle and the judgment of souls

The *Asclepius* gives the most famous single sentence of the Hermetic tradition — that man is *magnum miraculum*, the great miracle — and explains why: because he alone of created things stands in the junction where he can ascend to god or sink below the beasts. The window is life; the stake is what happens to the soul afterward.

> "In short, god made mankind good and capable of immortality through his two natures, divine and mortal, and so god willed the arrangement whereby mankind was ordained to be better than the gods, who were formed only from the immortal nature, and better than all other mortals as well."\
> — *Asclepius* 22, trans. Copenhaver

The judgment passage describes the two outcomes: the upright soul is "conceived in confidence of immortality to come," while the soul that has not purified itself while in the body is tumbled down into the storms of matter, where its *own* eternity becomes its bane.

> "When soul withdraws from the body, it passes to the jurisdiction of the chief demon who weighs and judges its merit, and if he finds it faithful and upright, he lets it stay in places suitable to it. But if he sees the soul smeared with the stains of wrongdoing and dirtied with vice, he sends it tumbling down from on high to the depths below and consigns it to the storms and whirlpools of air, fire and water in their ceaseless clashing — its endless punishment to be swept back and forth between heaven and earth in the streams of matter. Then the soul's bane is its own eternity, for an undying sentence oppresses it with eternal torment."\
> — *Asclepius* 28, trans. Copenhaver

> "When he has seen the light of reason as if with his eyes, every good person is enlightened by fidelity, reverence, wisdom, worship and respect for god, and the confidence of his belief puts him as far from humanity as the sun outshines the stars."\
> — *Asclepius* 29, trans. Copenhaver

## VII. "Consider yourself immortal": the incarnate exercise

*C.H.* XI is a single sustained instruction to Hermes on how to understand god. It contains what reads as an imperative practice — the expansion of awareness beyond body, place, and time, during life — not to abolish the coming death of the body but to compose, before the dissolution, the thing in us that does not dissolve.

> "Unless you make yourself equal to god, you cannot understand god; like is understood by like. Make yourself grow to immeasurable immensity, outleap all body, outstrip all time, become eternity and you will understand god. Having conceived that nothing is impossible to you, consider yourself immortal and able to understand everything, all art, all learning, the temper of every living thing. Go higher than every height and lower than every depth. Collect in yourself all the sensations of what has been made, of fire and water, dry and wet; be everywhere at once, on land, in the sea, in heaven; be not yet born, be in the womb, be young, old, dead, beyond death. And when you have understood all these at once — times, places, things, qualities, quantities — then you can understand god."\
> — *Corpus Hermeticum* XI.20, trans. Copenhaver

> "But if you shut your soul up in the body and abase it and say, 'I understand nothing, I can do nothing; I fear the sea, I cannot go up to heaven; I do not know what I was, I do not know what I will be,' then what have you to do with god?"\
> — *Corpus Hermeticum* XI.21, trans. Copenhaver

## VIII. *C.H.* XII: mortal gods and immortal men

Hermes gives the Agathos Daimon's formula — gods are immortal humans, humans are mortal gods — not as paradox but as a statement of identity-in-degree. The kinship is real; the mortality is real; the passage from one to the other is what the whole discipline is for.

> "In humans this mind is god; among humans, therefore, some are gods and their humanity is near to divinity. For the good demon has said that gods are immortal humans and humans are mortal gods."\
> — *Corpus Hermeticum* XII.1, trans. Copenhaver

## IX. Salaman's *Definitions*: the measure between mortal and immortal

The *Definitions of Hermes Trismegistus to Asclepius* are ten groups of aphorisms preserved only in Armenian and translated into English for the first time in Salaman's volume (Mahé's translation). Several of them compress the whole doctrine into a single line each: man is the measure; only the man who has mind is truly man; the body is the coffin of the soul while the soul lives badly, and its vehicle while it lives well.

> "Man is a divine being; he is not to be compared to the other living beings upon the earth, but to those said to be gods in the heavens. Rather — if one must speak the truth boldly — the man who is truly man is even above these, or at least they are entirely of equal power. For none of the heavenly gods will come down to earth, leaving behind the limits of heaven; but man climbs up even to heaven and measures it, and he knows what things are aloft, and what below."\
> — *Definitions of Hermes Trismegistus to Asclepius* (after Mahé in Salaman, *The Way of Hermes*)

The standing claim is that the measure runs through a living human body. Outside the body one cannot receive mind, because reception happens in the krater and the krater is reached from below. Above there is no more choosing: there is only what the living once chose.
