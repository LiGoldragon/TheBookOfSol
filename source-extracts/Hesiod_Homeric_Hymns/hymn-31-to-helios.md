# Homeric Hymn XXXI — To Helios

Translation: Hugh G. Evelyn-White, *Hesiod, the Homeric Hymns, and Homerica* (Loeb / Heinemann, 1920), pp. 457–459.

> "And now, O Muse Calliope, daughter of Zeus, begin to sing of glowing Helios whom mild-eyed Euryphaessa, the far-shining one, bare to the Son of Earth and starry Heaven. For Hyperion wedded glorious Euryphaessa, his own sister, who bare him lovely children, rosy-armed Eos and rich-tressed Selene and tireless Helios who is like the deathless gods."\
> — *Homeric Hymn* 31.1–7, trans. Evelyn-White

> "As he rides in his chariot, he shines upon men and deathless gods, and piercingly he gazes with his eyes from his golden helmet. Bright rays beam dazzlingly from him, and his bright locks streaming from the temples of his head gracefully enclose his far-seen face: a rich, fine-spun garment glows upon his body and flutters in the wind: and stallions carry him. Then, when he has stayed his golden-yoked chariot and horses, he rests there upon the highest point of heaven, until he marvellously drives them down again through heaven to Ocean."\
> — *Homeric Hymn* 31.8–17, trans. Evelyn-White

> "Hail to you, lord! Freely bestow on me substance that cheers the heart. And now that I have begun with you, I will celebrate the race of mortal men half-divine whose deeds the Muses have showed to mankind."\
> — *Homeric Hymn* 31.17–19, trans. Evelyn-White

## Notes for The Book of Sol

- **Genealogy.** Helios, Selene, Eos share one mother (Euryphaessa = Theia under a cult-title) and one father (Hyperion — "the one who goes above"). Sun, Moon, and Dawn are siblings of a single generation, not hierarchy; they are the three luminaries descended from Earth (Gaia) and starry Heaven (Ouranos). The archaic Greek cosmogony binds Luna and Sol together as co-children of the same light-bearing pair. Compare *Theogony* 371–374 (next file).
- **Helios's visual intensity.** The hymn's Helios is not a reflected or receiving light; he *pierces*, *beams*, *dazzles*, *shines upon*, and his face is "far-seen." Every verb is active emission. This is the archaic Greek solar signature — light that originates, that sees, that irradiates. Useful for Sol chapter Yang-register material.
- **The chariot and Ocean.** Helios rides from east to highest heaven and then back down through heaven to Ocean — the daily solar arc. The Homeric cosmography already contains the solar return, the descent into the great encircling water, the renewal from below. Relates to *Aqua Vitae* and the solar/oceanic exchange.
- **Haplography note.** Evelyn-White restores line 15–15a from manuscript corruption; the Loeb text marks this.
