# The Raw-Vegan Staples — Classical Warnings

The raw-vegan plate is built from a short list of staples: raw fruit, raw vegetables, raw nuts, raw seeds, and sprouted grains and legumes, usually bound with cold-pressed oil. The classical corpus weighs each of these foods, and the verdict is consistent — every load-bearing food of the raw plate is classed as *guru* (heavy, slow to digest), as *āma*-producing when taken uncooked, or as *doṣa*-aggravating when taken in volume. None is admitted as a raw daily staple; each is conditioned by indication, season, measure, and above all *saṃskāra* (processing — soaking, cooking, roasting).

The fruit and vegetable warnings are gathered already: in [*Fruit as Medicine*](./Fruit_as_Medicine.md), in [*Raw Vegan Salad and Juice*](./Raw_Vegan_Salad_and_Juice.md), in [*Apathya* and *Mitāhāra*](./Apathya.md), and in the [caraka-samhita fruit-vegetable-warnings digest](https://github.com/LiGoldragon/caraka-samhita/blob/main/fruit-vegetable-warnings.md); the modern clinical record is in [*Raw Veganism and Fruitarianism — The Harm in the Clinical Record*](./Raw_Vegan_Fruitarian_Harm.md). This file gathers the warnings those files leave uncovered — on **nuts, seeds, and sprouts** — and then states the corpus's verdict on the raw plate as a whole.

Citations follow Priya Vrat Sharma's translation of the *Caraka Saṃhitā* (Chaukhambha, 2014); where the working digest carries no clean Sanskrit IAST, the passage appears as an English-only block, per the project convention. Caraka chapter-and-verse numbers follow Sharma and are treated as provisional against the GRETIL critical edition (see [poet report 85](https://github.com/LiGoldragon/TheBookOfSol) and the caraka-samhita philology notes).

## I. Nuts — nourishing, but heavy and kapha-pitta-aggravating

The raw-vegan plate leans on raw nuts and nut "cheeses," "milks," and oils for its fat and bulk. The *Caraka Saṃhitā* names the nut group in one verse, and the classification is exact.

> "*Vātāma* (almond), *abhiṣuka* (pistachio), *akṣota* (walnut), *mukūlaka*, *nikocaka*, and *urumāṇa* are heavy (*guru*), hot, unctuous, sweet, tonic, vāta-alleviating, bulk-promoting, and aphrodisiac, and they aggravate *kapha* and *pitta*. *Priyāla* (chironji) is similar to them in properties, except in hotness."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27, the nut group of the *phala-varga* (between the pomegranate verses, GRETIL 27.149–150, and *śleṣmātaka*, GRETIL 27.159), trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

The nut is genuinely nourishing — *bṛṃhaṇa* (bulk-building) and *vṛṣya* (restorative), and it pacifies *vāta*. That is exactly why the classical kitchen uses it, and why it cannot be the body of the plate. It is *guru*: heavy, and slow to leave the stomach. It is *uṣṇa* and *kapha-pitta*-aggravating: in volume it heats the blood and thickens the channels. And it carries its weight raw. The classical handling of a heavy, unctuous food is *saṃskāra* — the almond soaked and peeled, the harder nuts roasted — the same logic by which heavy vegetables are "boiled, the juice expressed out, and added with fat before intake" (Sū. 27.88–124). The raw-vegan plate inverts the rule: it takes the heaviest unctuous food in the materia medica and eats it raw, daily, by the handful. *Guru* taken to saturation, Caraka warns, "causes considerable derangement" (Sū. 5.6).

## II. Seeds — tila and the oily seeds

Sesame, sunflower, pumpkin, flax, and chia carry the same load as the nuts in a smaller body, and the raw-vegan plate uses them the same way — raw, ground into "butters," milked, sprinkled by the bowlful. *Tila* (sesame) is the seed the corpus classifies, and the verdict travels to the rest.

> "*Tila* (sesame) is unctuous, hot, sweet, bitter, astringent, and pungent; beneficial for skin, hair, and strength; it alleviates *vāta* and aggravates *kapha* and *pitta*."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27, the *śamī-dhānya* (legume-and-seed) group, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

Taken as a paste — the raw-vegan "tahini," the seed-butter — Caraka marks it for a specific harm:

> "The vegetable preparation of *tila* (sesame paste) causes malaise (*glāni*)."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.4, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

And among the foods the yogin must avoid, the *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā*'s *apathya* verse names *tila* and *sarṣapa* (sesame and mustard seed) by name (HYP 1.59; the full block is in [*Apathya*](./Apathya.md) §III). The seed is a medicine and a strengthener in measure; eaten raw in volume it heats *pitta*, thickens *kapha*, and, as paste, dulls the body.

## III. Sprouted grains and legumes — guru, strength-reducing, not for the weak

The raw-vegan answer to the prohibition on raw grain is to sprout it — sprouted wheat, sprouted mung, sprouted lentil — eaten raw on the theory that germination "pre-digests" the seed. The corpus classes germinated grain in the opposite register.

> "Germinated grains, fried grains, *śaṣkulī*, *madhukroda*, *piṇḍaka*, *pūpa*, *pūpalikā*, and the like are heavy (*guru*); the preparations of rice-flour are the heaviest of all."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27.265–267, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

Germination does not lighten the grain; the text files it among the *guru* preparations. The raw legume fares no better, and Caraka gives the rule for how it must be taken:

> "Various legumes are sweet, cold, heavy (*guru*), strength-reducing, and roughening (*rūkṣa*). They are fit to be taken along with some fatty substance, by strong persons."\
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 27, the *śamī-dhānya* group, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

Two conditions sit on the legume: it must be taken *with fat*, and it suits *the strong*. The raw-vegan sprout-bowl meets neither — raw, oil-or-ghee-poor by doctrine, and offered as a staple to everyone, including the depleted whom the text specifically excludes. Heavy, *rūkṣa*, and strength-reducing, the raw sprout is the *vāta*-aggravating signature again, in the one food group the raw-vegan believed it had rescued by sprouting.

## IV. The raw principle — uncooked input yields āma, not rasa

Beneath every item sits one rule that condemns the mode itself, not the food. The body builds its tissues from *rasa* (the first nutritive juice), and *rasa* is the product of cooking — first in the kitchen, then in the belly.

> **na hy apakvād rasādayaḥ**
>
> "For from uncooked food, *rasa* and the rest [of the tissues] do not arise."\
> — *Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya*, Śārīrasthāna 3.54

The full account — *agni* as transformer, the body as a cooking vessel, raw input as the producer of *āma* (the undigested residue that is the material basis of disease) — is gathered in [*Cooking and Agni*](./Cooking_and_Agni.md). The raw-vegan plate is, by this single verse, a plate that cannot complete the chain from food to tissue: it delivers raw nuts, raw seeds, raw sprouts, and raw greens to a fire asked to do in the belly the cooking that the kitchen refused, and what the fire cannot finish becomes *āma*.

The verse condemns the raw *staple*, not every raw mouthful. The corpus keeps its own raw exceptions and guards them: honey, which Caraka forbids heating on pain of poisoning it (Sū. 27.243–246); ripe fruit in season, eaten raw by indication; fresh buttermilk, curd, and butter. None of these is a daily staple, and none is what the raw-vegan plate is built from.

## V. Fruit and vegetables — the verdict, in brief

The other two staples are documented in full elsewhere; the verdict, in one line each:

- **Fruit (*phala*)** is *auṣadha* (medicine) before it is *āhāra* (food): classed by *rasa*, *vīrya*, *vipāka*, and *prabhāva* like a drug, admitted by indication, in measure, in season. A diet of one *rasa* — mostly *madhura* (sweet) — eaten daily in volume is debilitating by Caraka's own rule before any single fruit is named (Sū. 25.40, 26.43–44). Full account: [*Fruit as Medicine*](./Fruit_as_Medicine.md).
- **Raw vegetables (*śāka*)** are *rūkṣa* (dry), *śīta* (cold), and often *guru* or channel-blocking; the text's instruction for the heavy ones is "boil, press out the juice, add fat," and the yogic *apathya* lists name raw greens (*harita-śāka*) by name. The cold, dry, scouring quality dries the *dhātus* and deranges *vāta* (Sū. 26.43; 27.88–124). Full account: [*Raw Vegan Salad and Juice*](./Raw_Vegan_Salad_and_Juice.md).

## VI. The synthesis — the raw-vegan plate, food by food

Set the plate down and read it against the corpus, item by item:

- raw fruit — one *rasa* in chronic excess, debilitating (Sū. 25.40);
- raw vegetables — *rūkṣa*, *śīta*, drying the tissues, raising *vāta* (Sū. 26.43);
- raw nuts — *guru*, *uṣṇa*, kapha-pitta-aggravating, meant to be soaked or roasted (Sū. 27, nut group);
- raw seeds — kapha-pitta-aggravating; the paste causes malaise (Sū. 27.4);
- sprouted grains and legumes — *guru*, strength-reducing, fit only with fat and only for the strong (Sū. 27.265–267);
- and the whole plate raw — *āma*-producing, because *na hy apakvād rasādayaḥ* (AH Śā. 3.54).

There is no staple on the raw-vegan plate that the classical corpus admits as a raw daily food. Every one is a medicine, a seasonal accessory, or a heavy substance requiring *saṃskāra* and fat. The sanctioned staple the corpus does name is the one the raw plate excludes: *anna* — grain cooked soft in water and finished with *ghṛta* (Caraka Sū. 5.12; the yogic plate in [*Apathya*](./Apathya.md) §II). The raw-vegan plate is not a plate the tradition forgot to praise; it is a plate the tradition examined, food by food, and conditioned out of the daily diet.
