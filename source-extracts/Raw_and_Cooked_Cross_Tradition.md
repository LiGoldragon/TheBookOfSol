# Raw and Cooked — Cross-Tradition Witnesses

A thematic digest of *direct passages* from the Greek (Hippocratic, Galenic), the Persian-Islamic (Avicenna), the Chinese (Huang Di Nei Jing, Li Dong-yuan), the structuralist-anthropological (Lévi-Strauss), and the modern-empirical (Wrangham) corpora, bearing on the position that **digestion is cooking by the body's innate heat, that the staple of the human plate is cooked food, and that prolonged raw-food eating depletes the body and disrupts its functions** — the same doctrine that the Ayurvedic, Yogic, and Vedic corpora name *agni* + *pakva* + *uṣṇa-snigdha*.

The Sanskrit witness is in [`Cooking_and_Agni.md`](./Cooking_and_Agni.md). This file gathers the position as it appears, *independently arrived at*, in five other traditions. The convergence is not borrowing but recognition: each tradition observed the same physiology and reached the same conclusion.

Format follows the project's canonical quote-block, adapted for non-Sanskrit sources: where original-language text is available it appears on top (italicised where Latin/Greek transliteration; native script where supported); blank `>` line; English translation in straight quotes; em-dash attribution on the final line.

## I. The Greek frame — cooking is the origin of medicine

The Hippocratic position, articulated in the late fifth century BCE, identifies cooking as the founding medical discovery — the human invention that transformed the raw, brutish, disease-prone life of pre-civilised humankind into a life that could be sustained. Galen, five centuries later, gives the same doctrine a precise physiological form: digestion (*pepsis*) is cooking by the body's innate heat. The Greek conclusion is the same conclusion as the Sanskrit, reached on different soil.

### Hippocrates, *Ancient Medicine* 3 — cooking as the origin of medicine

> "*From the strong and brutish living when they partook of raw foods, uncompounded and possessing strong potencies — the same things in fact as humans would suffer if they used them today, falling into violent pains and diseases quickly followed by death.*\
> *...For this reason the ancients, too, seem to me to have looked for nourishment that harmonized with the human constitution, and to have discovered the diet we use now. So from wheat, after steeping, winnowing, grinding and sifting it, kneading and baking, they made bread, and from barley they made cake. Working on food in many other ways, they boiled it or baked it...*\
> *To this discovery and research what more accurate or appropriate name could be given than 'medicine,' seeing that it was discovered with a view to the health, salvation and nourishment of the human being, in the place of a mode of living from which resulted pains, diseases, and deaths?*"\
> — *Hippocrates*, *Ancient Medicine* 3, trans. Paul Potter (Loeb 147)

Cooking is medicine. The act of preparing food *is* the founding clinical operation. The Greek doctrine names the same thing the Caraka tradition names by calling the eater's meal an *antaragni-yajña* (Sū. 27.343) — an oblation to the inner fire. Where Hippocrates speaks of raw food causing "violent pains and diseases quickly followed by death," Caraka speaks of *āma* — undigested residue, the material basis of disease. The two corpora describe the same physiology.

### Hippocrates, *Ancient Medicine* 13 — the clinical experiment

> "*Take a person whose constitution is not strong, but rather of the weaker kind; have him eat wheat straight from the threshing floor, untoasted and unworked, and raw meat, and drink water. The employment of this diet will assuredly cause him much severe suffering; he will experience pains and weakness of his body, his cavity will be disturbed, and most likely he will be unable to survive for very long.*\
> *Well, what remedy should be prepared for a man in this condition...? The most reliable and most obvious medicine would be for the patient to abandon the regimen he was using, and to give him bread instead of wheat, meat boiled instead of raw, and after that some wine to drink. These changes could not but restore him to health, unless indeed his health had been entirely ruined by long continuance of the diet.*"\
> — *Hippocrates*, *Ancient Medicine* 13, trans. Paul Potter (Loeb 147)

The argument is structured as a clinical thought-experiment. Raw wheat plus raw meat — pain, weakness, disturbance of the cavity, short life. The remedy: substitute bread (cooked wheat) for raw wheat; substitute boiled meat for raw meat. *These changes could not but restore him to health.* The verb is in the necessary mood — the recovery is structural. Caraka's *na hy apakvād rasādayaḥ* (AH Śā. 3.54) is the same observation at the level of physiology: *rasa* does not arise from uncooked food. Hippocrates' is the same observation at the level of clinical outcome: bread plus boiled meat restore the body that raw wheat plus raw meat destroyed.

### Hippocrates, *Regimen in Health* — the seasonal rule

> "*The layman ought to order his regimen in the following way. In winter eat as much as possible and drink as little as possible; drink should be wine as undiluted as possible, and food should be bread, with all meats roasted; during this season take as few vegetables as possible, for so will the body be most dry and hot. When spring comes... diminish meats, taking them all boiled instead of roasted, and eating when spring comes a few vegetables, in order that a man may be prepared for summer by taking all foods soft, meats boiled, and vegetables raw or boiled. ...In summer the barley-cake to be soft, the drink diluted and copious, and the meats in all cases boiled.*"\
> — *Hippocrates*, *Regimen in Health* 1, trans. W. H. S. Jones (Loeb 150)

The structure of the Hippocratic year is the structure of cooked food. Bread is the constant. Meat is roasted in winter, boiled in spring through summer. Vegetables appear only briefly, mostly cooked, occasionally raw in the warm seasons by exception. The annual diet is a year-long argument that cooking is the default and rawness the exception.

### Hippocrates, *Nutriment* 21 — food only nourishes when it actually nourishes

> "*Nutriment is not nutriment if it has no potency; what is not nutriment becomes nutriment if it can nourish. Nutriment in name, but not in deed; nutriment in deed, but not in name.*"\
> — *Hippocrates*, *Nutriment* 21, trans. Paul Potter (Loeb 147)

The aphoristic core of the Hippocratic position. Substance only nourishes when nourishment actually occurs. Raw food that fails to be assimilated is "nutriment in name, but not in deed." Caraka's *yad annaṃ deha-dhātv-ojo-bala-varṇādi-poṣaṇam, tatrāgnir hetur* (AH Śā. 3.53–54) names the same condition: the food that nourishes the body — *agni* is the cause of it. Without *agni*, the food is named food but does not nourish.

### Galen, *On the Properties of Foodstuffs* — digestion is cooking by innate heat

Galen's *De alimentorum facultatibus* is the most extensive classification of foods in antiquity. Throughout the work, Galen uses the verb *pepsis* — the same verb the Greeks use for boiling and ripening — to describe what the stomach does to food. The doctrine is identical to Caraka's *audaryo 'gnir yathā bāhyaḥ sthālī-sthaṃ toya-taṇḍulam* (AH Śā. 3.55–56). The belly is a cooking pot; the digestive fire is the same kind of fire as the kitchen fire.

> "*Pepsis was thought of in much the same light as cooking, and resulted from the action of the body's innate heat. For this reason it is translated here as concoction, rather than the digestion of some translators... He envisaged the food, under the influence of the innate heat of the stomach, undergoing a process much like cooking, and hence the translation as 'concoction'.*"\
> — John Wilkins, foreword to Owen Powell's *Galen: On the Properties of Foodstuffs* (Cambridge 2003), summarising Galen's physiology

### Galen — the boiled-wheat anecdote

> "*One of them put the wheat into the pot all at once and boiled it. Then they seasoned it with a moderate amount of salt and asked us to eat it. Reasonably enough, since we had been walking and were famished, we set to with a will. We ate it with gusto, and felt a heaviness in the stomach, as though clay seemed to be pressing upon it. Throughout the next day we had no appetite because of indigestion, so that we could eat nothing, were full of wind and suffered from headaches and blurred vision...*\
> *Certainly wheat eaten this way has great potential if it has been concocted, nourishing the body very much and imparting notable strength to those taking it.*"\
> — *Galen*, *On the Properties of Foodstuffs* I, trans. Owen Powell (Cambridge 2003)

The anecdote stages the rule. The same wheat, undercooked, weighs in the stomach like clay; concocted thoroughly, it nourishes the body strongly. Caraka Sū. 27.257–259 states the rule in the abstract: *odana* (boiled rice) well-cooked and warm is light; the same rice not well-cooked and cold is heavy. Galen states it in the anecdotal: he and his companions ate the boiled-pot wheat hungry, paid the price for a day, and were taught by their own bodies what the doctrine names — that cooking has to go far enough.

### Galen — what the kitchen does

> "*[The healthiest bread] is that with the most leaven and the most salt, which has been kneaded by the baker to the greatest extent until it has been prepared for baking, and has been baked in a moderately hot pan in the way I spoke of previously.*"\
> — *Galen*, *On the Properties of Foodstuffs* I (On bread), trans. Owen Powell (Cambridge 2003)

Leaven, salt, kneading, baking. The kitchen operations are the *saṃskāras* of Caraka Sū. 27.339 — processing operations that change the *guṇa* of a substance. Caraka names *vrīhi* → *lājā* (paddy → parched grain) as the case-example: the same grain in two forms, one heavy, the other light. Galen names *unleavened, unsalted, unkneaded, raw wheat* → *bread* by the same transformation.

## II. The Persian-Islamic frame — Avicenna and the cooking-of-the-humours

Avicenna's *Canon of Medicine* (11th century CE) inherits the Greek physiology through Galen and extends it. The cooking-as-digestion doctrine is no longer metaphor but a working theory of disease: humours are products of the body's cooking of food, with the "raw" humour (*khilṭ* in the Arabic, the *phlegm* of the Galenic translators) being the food that has not been brought to maturity by the body's heat.

### Avicenna, *Canon* I — the body's cooking

> "*The food is cooked, and like all cooking there is a foam and there is a sediment, and there may be some burning when cooking is prolonged or rawness [uncooked] when shortened. The foam is the yellow bile, the sediment is the black bile, and both are normal. The light portion of the burned substance is waste yellow bile, and the thick portion is bad black bile. The raw, uncooked portion is the phlegm. The remaining mature substance is the blood, which after passing through the liver is lighter than it should be due to the excessive water that was needed initially.*"\
> — *Avicenna*, *Canon of Medicine* Book I, trans. Mones Abu-Asab, Hakima Amri & Marc S. Micozzi (Inner Traditions 2013)

The humours are cooking-products. Phlegm is *the raw, uncooked portion* — the material the body did not bring to maturity. The doctrine is identical to Caraka's *āma*: undigested residue from food the *jaṭharāgni* could not finish cooking. The Persian-Arabic terminology preserves the equation directly — *raw* = phlegm = uncompleted concoction. The four humours are four grades of bodily cooking.

### Avicenna, *Canon* I — the principle of selection

> "*If the innate heat is strong it will enable nature to mediate its action on moistures by maturation, digestion, and preservation of health, therefore, the moistures act in the proper functions and do not follow the influence of abnormal heat, thus produce no putrefaction.*"\
> — *Avicenna*, *Canon of Medicine* Book I, trans. Mones Abu-Asab et al. (Inner Traditions 2013)

*Innate heat* is the precise calque of Caraka's *agni*. *Maturation* is the precise calque of *pakti* (the action of *pāka*). The Greek-Arabic medical tradition uses the same physiological grammar as the Sanskrit. The clinical recommendation that follows in Avicenna is the inverse formulation of Caraka Sū. 27.342 (*on agni depend strength, health, life-span and vital breath*): when the inner cooking is strong, the body remains in health; when it is weak, the input remains raw and the body remains sick.

### Avicenna, *Canon* I — the prescription

> "*Within the humoral context, it is obvious that healthy living requires a proper method of food preparation and cooking; it also points out what to eat and what to reject. One should favor well-cooked food whose components have been cooked through, turned into light texture, and contain a minimal amount of burn (no black sediment), and reject food (or at least minimize its consumption) that is lightly cooked, dense, or overcooked with black sediment.*"\
> — *Avicenna*, *Canon of Medicine* Book I (Abu-Asab et al. translators' summary of Avicenna's regimen doctrine)

The Avicennan rule is the same rule as Caraka Sū. 27.275: hard, bulky food is heavy if mildly cooked; nourishing and strength-promoting if overcooked. The Arabic register names *well-cooked food whose components have been cooked through, turned into light texture*. The Sanskrit register names *uṣṇa, snigdha, mātrāvat* (AH Sū. 8). Two languages, one operation.

## III. The Chinese frame — five grains, warmth as rule

The Chinese medical tradition arrives at the same doctrine through the cosmology of the five phases. Grain — cooked grain — sits at the centre of the nutritional system. Tree-fruits augment. Vegetables complete. The structure is the structure of Caraka Sū. 5.12 in another language. And the rule of food temperature — neither chillingly cold nor burning hot, warm in measure — is the precise Chinese-medical equivalent of *uṣṇa-snigdha*.

### Huang Di Nei Jing Suwen ch. 22 — the fivefold food doctrine

> "*The five grains, which act as nourishment. They are wheat, glutinous millet, millet, rice, and beans.*\
> *The five tree-fruits, which serve to augment the nourishment. They are peaches, plums, apricots, chestnuts, and dates.*\
> *The five domestic animals, which contribute additional nutritional benefit. They are fowl, sheep, beef, horses and pigs.*\
> *The five vegetables, which complete nourishment. They are mallows, coarse greens, scallions, onions, and leeks.*"\
> — *Huang Di Nei Jing Suwen* ch. 22, trans. Ilza Veith (University of California Press 1972)

Five grains at the centre. The Sanskrit canonical daily-routine list (Caraka Sū. 5.12) names *ṣaṣṭika, śāli, mudga, yava, dhānya* — five grains. Two corpora, two cosmologies, the same plate. Fruit augments. Vegetables complete. The grammar of "primary nourishment vs. secondary augmenter" sits identically in both systems. And in both, the grain is cooked; the fruit is medicinal exception; the vegetable enters in measure.

### Huang Di Nei Jing Suwen ch. 22 — rice gruel is medicine

> "*One must use paddy rice and steam it. The stalks of the rice serve as fire wood. When the steaming of the rice is completed, the rice (gruel) is very strong.*"\
> — *Huang Di Nei Jing Suwen* ch. 22, trans. Ilza Veith (University of California Press 1972), on the preparation of medicinal rice gruel

Rice gruel — the most thoroughly cooked grain preparation — appears in the *Nei Jing* as a *medicine*. This is the precise Chinese parallel to Caraka's *yavāgūḥ sarva-rogeṣu pathyā* — *yavāgū* (long-cooked grain gruel) is wholesome in all diseases (Caraka Cikitsāsthāna 1). Maximally cooked grain is maximally medicinal in both systems.

### Li Dong-yuan, *Pi Wei Lun* 3.15 — the temperature rule

> "*If food and drink are never burning hot nor chillingly cold but are moderately and appropriately cold or warm, the qi will be sustained and evils will not result in aggregation. [However, if] eating and drinking are unregulated and cold and warm [foods] are not suitable, this will engender disease, there will be diarrhea without limit, or there will be glomus and oppression below the heart, abdominal and rib-side drum distention, loss of enrichment and flavor in the mouth, and encumbrance and fatigue of the four limbs.*"\
> — *Li Dong-yuan*, *Treatise on the Spleen & Stomach* (*Pi Wei Lun*) ch. 3.15, trans. Bob Flaws (Blue Poppy Press 2004), citing the *Shi Si Nan*

The clinical formulation. Cold food (the raw register) is named at the head of the prohibition list. The TCM frame is temperature-explicit where the Caraka frame is *vīrya*-explicit (potency, hot/cold). Both arrive at the same plate: warm food, in measure, neither burning nor chilling — Sanskrit *uṣṇa*, Chinese 溫 (*wēn*). And the symptoms of the wrong eating — distension, fatigue, loss of taste, diarrhea — are the standard signs of *grahaṇī-doṣa* and *āma* in the Sanskrit corpus.

### Li Dong-yuan, *Pi Wei Lun* — the spleen-stomach pivot

> "*Damage [due to] drinking is a damage done by formless qi. [Therefore,] it should [be treated] by effusing sweat and disinhibiting urination in order to abduct dampness. Damage [due to] eating is a damage [due to] a substance that has form. A mild [case] calls for dispersion and transformation, though reducing grains is the wondrous [or best way].*"\
> — *Li Dong-yuan*, *Treatise on the Spleen & Stomach* ch. 3.13 (*Drinking & Eating Damaging the Spleen*), trans. Bob Flaws (Blue Poppy Press 2004)

The spleen-stomach pair in TCM is the precise functional equivalent of Caraka's *jaṭharāgni* + *grahaṇī*. The clinical lesson is the same in both systems: weak digestive capacity is the upstream of disease; the rule is *eat what your fire can cook*, and the daily plate is what supports that fire rather than overburdening it. Where Caraka names *mandāgni* (weak fire), Li Dong-yuan names *pi xu* (脾虛, spleen vacuity).

## IV. The structural-anthropological witness — Lévi-Strauss

Claude Lévi-Strauss's *Le Cru et le Cuit* (1964) is the structuralist analysis of South American indigenous mythology that takes the raw/cooked opposition as the founding axis of the human-symbolic system. The South American myths he documents arrive at the same conclusion the Greek, Persian, Chinese, and Sanskrit medical corpora arrive at: cooking is what made humans human. The pre-cultural state is the raw-food state.

### Lévi-Strauss, *The Raw and the Cooked* — the founding opposition

> "*The aim of this book is to show how empirical categories — such as the categories of the raw and the cooked, the fresh and the decayed, the moistened and the burned, etc., which can only be accurately defined by ethnographic observation and, in each instance, by adopting the standpoint of a particular culture — can nonetheless be used as conceptual tools with which to elaborate abstract ideas and combine them in the form of propositions.*"\
> — *Claude Lévi-Strauss*, *The Raw and the Cooked* (Mythologiques I), Overture, trans. John & Doreen Weightman (Harper Colophon 1969)

The raw/cooked pair sits at the foundation of the cultural-symbolic system. Across hundreds of South American myths from the Bororo, Ge, Tupi, Apinayé, Sherente, and dozens of other peoples, the same axis appears: the raw is what the human was before fire; the cooked is what the human becomes after the gift of fire from the jaguar or the vultures or the heroes Pud and Pudlere. The myths Lévi-Strauss documents are the indigenous-South-American version of the same insight Hippocrates names at the founding of Greek medicine.

### Eastern Timbira (Kraho) — what humans were before fire

> "*Formerly men were unacquainted with fire and dried their meat by laying it out in the sun on a flat stone, so that it was not completely raw.*"\
> — *Mio. Eastern Timbira myth*, in Lévi-Strauss, *The Raw and the Cooked* I.1, citing Nimuendaju

> "*When the heroes [Pud and Pudlere] went away, they took the fire with them, and men were reduced to eating their meat raw, sun-dried, and accompanied by pau puba [rotten wood].*"\
> — *Mii. Eastern Timbira (Kraho group) myth*, in Lévi-Strauss, *The Raw and the Cooked* I.1

The pre-cultural condition: raw meat, sun-dried, accompanied by rotten wood. The condition the Hippocratic corpus names as "violent pains and diseases quickly followed by death." The condition the Caraka corpus names as the production of *āma*. Three traditions, three idioms, one diagnosis.

### Lévi-Strauss on the transition from nature to culture

> "*The Ge tribes have two strictly parallel mythological series to account for the transition from nature to culture. In one series, culture begins with the theft of fire from the jaguar; in the other, with the introduction of cultivated plants. But in all contexts the origin of man's loss of immortality is linked with the advent of civilized life, which is thought of as culture whenever the question at issue is the origin of fire... (the conquest of "the jaguar's belongings"; fire for cooking purposes, bows and arrows...).*"\
> — *Claude Lévi-Strauss*, *The Raw and the Cooked* (Mythologiques I), trans. Weightman

The acquisition of fire is the founding cultural act. *Fire for cooking purposes* is itemised first in the inventory of cultural goods. In the indigenous mythological frame, to be human is to be a cooking animal. The myth-system is the South American formulation of what Boswell (cited by Wrangham below) names as the definition of man.

## V. The modern empirical witness — Wrangham

Richard Wrangham's *Catching Fire* (2009) is the contemporary biological argument. The cooking-as-humanising thesis, formerly symbolic in Lévi-Strauss, becomes nutritional and energetic. Wrangham gathers the empirical data that the ancient traditions predicted: prolonged raw-food eating wastes the body; reproductive function fails; cooking and the control of fire are *human universals*, present in every documented culture.

### Wrangham — the definition of man

> "*'My definition of Man is, a "Cooking Animal". The beasts have memory, judgement, and all the faculties and passions of our mind, in a certain degree; but no beast is a cook... Man alone can dress a good dish.'*"\
> — James Boswell, *Journal of a Tour to the Hebrides* (1773), cited as epigraph to Richard Wrangham, *Catching Fire: How Cooking Made Us Human*, ch. 1

### Wrangham — cooking is a human universal

> "*The control of fire and the practice of cooking are human universals.*"\
> — *Richard Wrangham*, *Catching Fire*, ch. 1

The empirical, anthropological observation. There is no recorded human culture that does not cook. Even the Inuit, whose diet contains more raw animal products than other societies, eat their main meal of the day cooked. Even hunter-gatherer groups eating a tiny fraction of their total calories raw still build the structure of the day around the evening cooked meal. The biological-anthropological convergence with the indigenous-mythological data Lévi-Strauss documented: cooking is the definition of the human.

### Wrangham — the Giessen Raw Food study

> "*The most extensive [study] is the Giessen Raw Food study, conducted by nutritionist Corinna Koebnick and her colleagues in Germany, which used questionnaires to study 513 raw-foodists who ate from 70 percent to 100 percent of their diet raw... As the proportion of food eaten raw rose, BMI fell. The average weight loss when people changed to a raw diet was 9 kilograms (almost twenty pounds) for women and 10 kilograms for men. Among those eating a purely raw diet (31 percent), the body weights of almost a third indicated chronic energy deficiency. The scientists' conclusion was unambiguous: 'a strict raw food diet cannot guarantee an adequate energy supply.'*"\
> — *Richard Wrangham*, *Catching Fire*, ch. 1, citing Koebnick et al. 1999

### Wrangham — what raw-food eating does to reproductive function

> "*Among women eating totally raw diets, about 50 percent entirely ceased to menstruate. A further proportion, about 10 percent, suffered irregular menstrual cycles that left them unlikely to conceive. These figures are far higher than for women eating cooked food. Healthy women on cooked diets rarely fail to menstruate, whether or not they are vegetarian. But ovarian function predictably declines in women suffering from extreme energy depletion, such as marathoners and anorexics... Reduced reproductive function means that in our evolutionary past, raw-foodism would have been much less successful than the habit of eating cooked food. A rate of infertility greater than 50 percent, such as was found in the Giessen Raw Food study, would be devastating in a natural population of foragers.*"\
> — *Richard Wrangham*, *Catching Fire*, ch. 1

The empirical confirmation. Half of all-raw-diet women cease to menstruate. The classical Sanskrit register names the prediction as *śukra-kṣaya* — depletion of generative essence — and *vandhyatva* — sterility. The Hippocratic register predicts wasting, weakness, and "inability to survive for very long" (Ancient Medicine 13). The 21st-century clinical study returns the same numbers.

### Wrangham — the energy theory of cooking

> "*Cooked food does many familiar things. It makes our food safer, creates rich and delicious tastes, and reduces spoilage. Heating can allow us to open, cut, or mash tough foods. But none of these advantages is as important as a little-appreciated aspect: cooking increases the amount of energy our bodies obtain from our food.*"\
> — *Richard Wrangham*, *Catching Fire*, ch. 1

The biological mechanism. Cooking pre-digests starch, breaks down protein structure, ruptures cell walls, and makes nutrients accessible that raw food locks away. The Sanskrit doctrine names the same operation as *saṃskāra* — the processing operation that transforms *guṇa* and shifts the *guru-laghu* balance in favour of the eater (Caraka Sū. 27.339). Two languages, three thousand years apart, the same physiology.

## VI. Convergence

The position is the position of every documented medical and anthropological corpus that has considered the question:

1. **Digestion is cooking.** The body's inner heat (Greek *innate heat*, Arabic *gharīza al-ḥarāra*, Chinese 脾陽 *pi yang* / 胃火 *wei huo*, Sanskrit *jaṭharāgni*) is the same kind of fire as the kitchen fire; the belly is a cooking vessel; the cooking that begins in the kitchen continues in the body. Galen, Avicenna, Li Dong-yuan, and Vāgbhaṭa state this in identical physiological language.

2. **Raw food is the upstream of disease.** Hippocrates names the condition as "violent pains and diseases quickly followed by death." Avicenna names the residue as the *raw, uncooked portion* — phlegm, the first humoral pathology. Caraka and Vāgbhaṭa name it *āma* — undigested residue, the material basis of disease. Wrangham documents the modern clinical version: amenorrhea, chronic energy deficiency, body wasting.

3. **The staple of the human plate is cooked grain.** The HDNJ names five grains as primary nourishment. Caraka Sū. 5.12 names five grains. The yogic *mitāhāra* maxim names *anna*. Hippocrates names bread. Galen calls it *the food we use continually*. The same staple, in five idioms.

4. **Vegetables and fruits enter by indication and preparation.** Hippocrates limits raw vegetables to spring and summer, by exception. Galen recommends boiling. Caraka classifies fruits as *auṣadha* before *āhāra*. Li Dong-yuan warns against cold foods damaging the spleen. The dispersal of these substances into category-level prescriptions is itself the structure of the materia medica.

5. **Cooking is what made humans human.** Lévi-Strauss documents this as the symbolic foundation of indigenous-South-American mythology. Wrangham documents it as the biological foundation of *Homo*. Hippocrates documents it as the founding act of medicine. The convergence across structural-anthropological, evolutionary-biological, and clinical-medical frames suggests this is not a cultural artefact but an observation about the species.

The four-thousand-year cross-tradition agreement on the cooking-and-agni doctrine does not require any single tradition's authority. It is the natural conclusion of any sustained, attentive look at the body and the food that enters it.
