# Ṛgveda — Ghṛta as Amṛta: the Vedic Witness for the Lunar Pillar

A thematic digest of *direct passages* from the *Ṛgveda* bearing on the position that **the Vedic corpus names clarified butter (*ghṛta*) as *amṛta* — the deathless, the navel of immortality — and places it, with *anna* (grain), *kṣīra* (milk), and *soma*, at the centre of the substances fit to nourish and to offer.** *Phala* (fruit) is not a Vedic food-category; the Veda's nourishing vocabulary is grain, milk, ghee, and the pressed Soma. This is the oldest layer of the same doctrine the Yogic and Ayurvedic corpora carry forward.

This compilation supports [*Yogis Don't Eat Fruit*](../../diet/Yogis_Dont_Eat_Fruit.md) (the lunar / *ghṛta* pillar), [*The Two Pillars of Nourishment*](../../ayurveda/The_Two_Pillars_of_Nourishment.md), and [*Cooking and Agni*](../Cooking_and_Agni.md) (the Vedic *agni* frame). The companion lunar-nectar material is in [`soma-and-the-moon.md`](./soma-and-the-moon.md); the *anna* foundation is in [`../Upanisads/`](../Upanisads/) and [`../Cooking_and_Agni.md`](../Cooking_and_Agni.md).

Format follows the project's canonical quote-block, adapted for the Vedic register: where a clean IAST half-line is verified it appears in bold on top; blank `>` line; the Griffith English in straight quotes; em-dash attribution on the final line. ## I. Ghṛta is amṛta — the secret name

The Ṛgveda's hymn to *ghṛta* (RV 4.58, the *Ghṛta-sūkta*) is the densest Vedic statement on clarified butter. It is not a recipe; it is a metaphysics. *Ghṛta* rises from the cosmic ocean as the wave of sweetness, turns to *amṛta* (the deathless), and is named the tongue of the gods and the navel of the immortal.

### Ṛgveda 4.58.1 — the wave of sweetness, the navel of amṛta

> **ghṛtasya nāma guhyaṁ yad asti jihvā devānām amṛtasya nābhiḥ**
>
> "Forth from the ocean sprang the wave of sweetness; together with the stalk it turned to Amṛta —\
> that which is the secret name of *ghṛta*: but the gods' tongue is truly the navel of the immortal."\
> — *Ṛgveda* 4.58.1, trans. Griffith (Sanskrit half-line verified per the standard accented text)

The verse equates *ghṛta* with *amṛta* — the deathless nectar. Where the *Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad* names *prāṇa* as *amṛta* and the *Khecarīvidyā* hunts the lunar nectar inside the skull, the Ṛgveda names the terrestrial form the body can actually eat: clarified butter. *Ghṛta* is the descending, binding, building substance — the lunar pillar in its concentrated edible form. The fruitarian, who refuses the lunar fat, refuses the one *amṛta* the Veda places on the plate.

### Ṛgveda 4.58.5–6 — the streams of ghee

> "I look upon the streams of oil descending, and lo! the Golden Reed is there among them.\
> From inmost reservoir in countless channels flow down these rivers which the foe beholds not.\
> The streams of holy oil pour swiftly downward like the wild beasts that fly before the bowman."\
> — *Ṛgveda* 4.58.5–6, trans. Griffith

The image is descent — *ghṛta* flowing downward in rivers from an inmost reservoir. The direction is the lunar direction: down, in, gathering. It is the precise inverse of the fruit's solar movement — up, out, dispersing. The hymn sees in ghee the quality the Ayurvedic corpus will later name *guru* (heavy), *snigdha* (unctuous), and *sthira* (stable): the substance that descends and settles and stays.

### Ṛgveda 4.58.10 — ghee as the fit offering

> "Bear to the gods the sacrifice we offer; the streams of oil flow pure and full of sweetness."\
> — *Ṛgveda* 4.58.10, trans. Griffith

*Ghṛta* is the *havis* — the oblation poured into the fire. The Vedic ritual is a cooking, and ghee is what feeds the flame. Caraka internalises the frame at Sūtrasthāna 27.343, calling every meal an *antaragni-yajña*, an offering to the inner fire; ghee is the offering at both altars, the outer and the inner.

## II. The Vedic plate is grain, milk, ghee, Soma — not fruit

The Veda's nourishing vocabulary is consistent. *Anna* and *yava* (grain, barley); *kṣīra*, *dadhi*, *ghṛta* (milk, curds, ghee); *soma* (the pressed lunar draught). These are the substances praised, offered, and named as the food of gods and men. *Phala* (fruit) does not appear among them as a staple. The argument from the Vedic corpus is not that the Veda forbids fruit — it is that fruit is simply not in the Vedic food-vocabulary at the level grain and ghee occupy. The plate the Veda assembles is the plate the Yogic and Ayurvedic corpora inherit.

### Taittirīya Upaniṣad 2.2.1 — anna at the origin (cross-reference)

> **oṣadhībhyo'nnam | annāt puruṣaḥ ||**
>
> "From plants arises *anna*. From *anna* arises the human being."\
> — *Taittirīya Upaniṣad* 2.2.1, trans. Olivelle

The Upaniṣad does not name *phala* at the origin of the human being. It names *anna* — cooked grain. The full *anna* compilation lives in [`../Cooking_and_Agni.md`](../Cooking_and_Agni.md) §I and [`../Apathya.md`](../Apathya.md) §XI; it is recalled here only to mark the continuity: the Vedic-Upaniṣadic foundation, the Yogic *mitāhāra* register, and the Ayurvedic *dinacaryā* all name the same staple at the same word.

## III. Synthesis

The Vedic witness, taken on its own terms:

1. **Ghṛta is amṛta.** RV 4.58.1 names clarified butter the secret name of the deathless, the tongue of the gods, the navel of the immortal. The lunar pillar of the two-pillar diet has its root in the oldest hymn-layer.
2. **Ghee descends.** RV 4.58.5–6 sees *ghṛta* as rivers flowing downward — the gathering, binding, settling direction, the inverse of the fruit's upward solar dispersal.
3. **Ghee is the fit offering.** RV 4.58.10 makes *ghṛta* the *havis*. The Vedic yajña is a cooking; Caraka internalises it (Sū. 27.343); ghee feeds both fires.
4. **The Vedic staple is grain, milk, ghee, Soma.** *Phala* is not a Vedic food-category. The plate the Veda assembles — *anna*, *kṣīra*, *ghṛta* — is the plate the whole later tradition keeps.

The fruitarian holds the solar half of a diet the Veda built from two. The lunar half — the descending, deathless, binding *ghṛta* — is the half the Veda names first and names *amṛta*.
