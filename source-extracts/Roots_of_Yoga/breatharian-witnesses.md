# Roots of Yoga — Breatharian Witnesses, Lunar Nectar, and the Body That Eats Itself

Direct passages from Mallinson & Singleton, *Roots of Yoga* (Penguin Classics, 2017) — a critical-source anthology of the Indian yogic textual tradition — on the doctrines that underwrite the article *The Body Does Not Want Water*: the body sustained without external food and drink, the consumption of the body's own dhātus by Kuṇḍalinī, and the historical witnesses to yogins who lived for months without intake.

Editions cited inside Mallinson & Singleton (M&S): *Amaraughaprabodha* (haṭha text), *Jñāneśvarī* (13th-c. Marathi commentary on the *Bhagavad Gītā* by Jñāneśvar), Ibn Baṭṭūṭa's *Riḥla* (14th-c. travel account), the *Tashrīḥ al-Aqvām* (early-19th-c. Persian ethnography of Indian sects), and the 1837 CE Sir Claude Wade account of the fakir Hari Das's samādhi-burial.

This extract supports the article *The Body Does Not Want Water* alongside the [Khecarīvidyā extract](../Khecarividya/lunar-nectar-and-breath.md) and the [Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya extract on water and srotāṃsi](../Astanga_Hrdaya/water-and-srotas.md).

## I. The body sustained by its own lunar nectar

### Amaraughaprabodha 22–26 — bindu, breath, and the lunar nectar

> "(22) Three are present as one in the body; it is when the breath is mastered that they are all certain to attain perfection. (23) When the breath stops through contact with the middle of the Goddess of the Centre, then *bindu* and the mind stop together with the breath. (24) All these elements dwell in the body. They move when the breath moves and they stop when the breath is stopped. (25) **Death comes about through the fall of *bindu*, life from its preservation.** When the great jewel *bindu* is perfected, then everything is perfected. (26) [When] the lunar nectar of immortality goes down, [there is] death for all embodied beings. He whose *bindu* is one with the indivisible is an adept in a diamond body."\
> — *Amaraughaprabodha* 22–26, trans. Mallinson & Singleton (*Roots of Yoga* §5.5.1)

This is the metaphysics that the *Khecarīvidyā* practice operationalizes: life is the retention of the lunar nectar; death is its descent. The body that is leaking *bindu* downward is the body that thirsts. The body in which the breath has been arrested at the middle channel is the body whose nectar no longer falls.

### Jñāneśvarī 6.247–270 — the body filled with moon-nectar

> "(247) Then the tank of moon-nectar pours from above [and], turning over, merges into the mouth of the goddess (Śakti). (248) Through that tube the fluid fills [and] is held in the whole body, where it merges with the *prāṇa* wind. (249) Just as in a heated mould the wax disappears [and] then the filled [mould] remains with the solidified liquid, (250) so that *digit* (*kalā*) [of the nectar of immortality] becomes manifest in the shape of the body, [and] is covered on top with a screen of skin. […]\
>
> "(258) as if the disc of the moon [had] blossomed with autumnal moisture, or solid light [were] seated on a seat: (259) **like that becomes the body when Kuṇḍalinī drinks the moon.** Then death fears the form of the body. (260) Then old age turns back, the knot of youth is untied, the lost state of childhood [re]appears."\
> — *Jñāneśvarī* 6.247–270, trans. Mallinson & Singleton (*Roots of Yoga* §5.5.3)

The 13th-century Marathi text describes the post-Kuṇḍalinī body as one in which the lunar nectar has *physically taken on the shape of the body* — a body composed of the substance the practice has been drawing down. External food is, after this, not a moral excess but a category error — the body is no longer made of food.

## II. The body consuming its own constituents

### Jñāneśvarī 6.228–240 — Kuṇḍalinī eats the dhātus

> "(228) Naturally hungry for many days, then awakened — that is how it starts. Then she forcefully spreads out her mouth straight upward. (229) There, Kirīṭi, she embraces all the wind that has filled [the region] below the space of the heart. (230) With the fire of her mouth she reaches up and down [and] **begins to eat morsels of flesh**.\
>
> "(231) Whatever place is endowed with flesh, there she takes a small bite. […] (233) Although she does not leave the [Base] support, she also draws out the essence [from] the nails, [and] after cleaning the skin unites with the skeleton. (234) [When] she scrapes the hollows of the bones [and] rubs the fibres of the head, then the outside sprout of hair-seed gets scorched.\
>
> "(235) **Then the thirsty one takes a gulp in the ocean of the seven bodily constituents (*dhātus*)** and immediately produces dry heat. (236) Holding back the neck, the wind that goes twelve fingers' breadth out of the cavity of the nose pours inside. […] (239) Listen, **consuming the whole element belonging to the earth, she does not leave anything and then cleans away the water.** (240) [When] she eats both elements in that way, then she is completely satisfied. Then, mollified, she remains near Suṣumnā."\
> — *Jñāneśvarī* 6.228–240, trans. Mallinson & Singleton (*Roots of Yoga* §5.6.1)

The 13th-century text states the metabolic basis of breath-only sustenance with no euphemism: the awakened serpent *consumes the body's own seven dhātus*, including the water (*ap*), and is satisfied. The body has become its own food and drink. The "thirsty one" of verse 235 is Kuṇḍalinī herself — drinking from the body's own ocean of constituents — not the embodied person seeking external water.

This is the doctrinal anchor for the article's central claim: the body that has set up the internal circuit *does not require external water* because the channel that was drinking water is now drinking the body's own refined fluid, which is now being supplied by the body's own digestion of itself.

## III. Witnesses — yogins who lived for months without food and drink

### Ibn Baṭṭūṭa, *Riḥla* (14th c.) — the samādhi of the yogins

> "These people work wonders. For instance, **one of them remains for months without food and drink**; many of them dig a pit under the earth which is closed over them, leaving therein no opening except one through which the air might enter. There one remains for months and I have heard that some *jogis* hold out in this manner for a year."\
> — Ibn Baṭṭūṭa, *Riḥla*, trans. in Mallinson & Singleton, *Roots of Yoga* §9.1.20

The Moroccan traveller's first-hand 14th-century report does not philosophize. It records what was visible to him: yogins remained in earth-pits for months, sometimes a year, without food and water. The doctrinal claim of the *Khecarīvidyā* — that the body of a practiced yogin is sustained from within by the lunar nectar — is corroborated by the empirical observation of an outsider with no Sanskritic stake in the matter.

### *Tashrīḥ al-Aqvām* — the samādhi of the saṃnyāsīs

> "Some take *samādhi* when they are still alive and of these there are two types. […] Those who take *samādhi* by the power of their ritual worship and ascetic practices, whatever they say to anybody will come to pass. And these people can appear in another city 500 or even 1,000 leagues (*kos*) distant in their own shape and form, appearing from under the ground."\
> — *Tashrīḥ al-Aqvām*, trans. in Mallinson & Singleton, *Roots of Yoga* §9.1.21

### Sir Claude Wade — the 1837 CE samādhi-burial of Hari Das

> [Wade describes accompanying Mahārāja Ranjit Singh to a sealed, air-tight, guarded building in Lahore where the fakir Hari Das had been buried. Hari Das was successfully revived after 40 days of burial, attended by witnesses including Wade himself.]\
> — Mallinson & Singleton, *Roots of Yoga* §9.1.22 (extract)

The 1837 CE incident at the Lahore court of Ranjit Singh is among the most thoroughly attested cases. Wade was the British political agent at Lahore; his account names the building, the seals, the witnesses, and the revival procedure. The fakir was sustained for 40 days inside an airtight, guarded room — the basic premise of the article: the body that does not want water is, at the limit of the discipline, a body that does not require it.

The *Bṛhatkhecarīprakāśa*'s commentary on *Khecarīvidyā* 3.27–30 (M&S §9.1.23) describes the precise revival procedure used to bring such yogins back. The technical infrastructure for this state was developed and transmitted as a discipline.

## IV. The interpretive lens

The body sustained by its own lunar nectar (KV 1.65–72), the body in which Kuṇḍalinī has consumed the seven dhātus and the water-element (Jñāneśvarī 6.228–240), the body whose retained *bindu* is what life is (Amaraughaprabodha 22–26), and the body that withstands months of burial without external intake (Ibn Baṭṭūṭa, Hari Das) — these are not four claims. They are one claim, stated at four different registers: doctrinal, metabolic, metaphysical, and empirical.

The article's working thesis — that the body does not want water but a plasma it can retain, and that the diet of *anna + ghṛta* (with *amarolī* / distilled plasma closing the loop externally) is what permits this — sits inside this larger frame. The *external* practice of distilling plasma is the early-stage analogue of the *internal* practice of cranial amṛta-drinking. Each operates by the same principle: the body fed at depth from its own refined output ceases to require what it was reaching for outside.
