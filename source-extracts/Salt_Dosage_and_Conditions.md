# Salt — Dosage, Frequency, and Conditions

What the guidelines actually specify for salt — quantity, frequency, and the conditions that indicate or forbid it — pinned across three layers that do not agree: the classical Āyurvedic texts (qualitative, no grams), contemporary Āyurvedic clinical practice (which assigns gram figures), and the Book of Sol's own chloride position (stricter than either). The salt at issue is *saindhava* (rock salt), the only one classical Āyurveda defends; *sāmudra* (sea/table salt) is the condemned common ingestant. Note throughout: saindhava is itself ~95–99% sodium chloride (see [*Witnesses Against Salt — Āyurveda*](../chloride/witnesses/Witnesses_Against_Salt_Ayurveda.md)), so the project's anion-is-the-toxin logic implicates even saindhava — which is why the Book of Sol's standing rule goes beyond the classical allowance.

## I. Dosage

**Classical texts — no quantity, only measure.** No Āyurvedic source gives a gram figure for salt. The rule is qualitative: take it *in measure*, sparingly, and only the least-burning salt (saindhava). Excess (*atiyoga*) is catalogued as harm, not bounded by a number — loss of virility, sensory decline, wrinkles, greying, baldness, blood vitiation (Caraka Sū. 26.43); coarseness, skin disease, thirst, poison-action, loss of strength (Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya Sū. 10.13–14).

**Contemporary Āyurvedic practice — gram figures (not from the classical texts).** Modern clinical Āyurveda translates the qualitative rule into doses for *saindhava*:

- ordinary/therapeutic daily use: about **1.5–2 g/day**;
- **above ~4 g/day**: raises blood pressure, brings early greying, body heat, and burning — note this is exactly Caraka's *lavaṇa-atiyoga* signature (Sū. 26.43), recovered as a modern dose-threshold;
- declared hypertensive (if permitted at all): **≤ 500 mg/day**;
- edema / ascites: **≤ 1 g/day**.

These figures are contemporary Āyurvedic-practice standards, consistent across sources but not present verbatim in Caraka, Suśruta, or Vāgbhaṭa.

**Physiology / Book of Sol — none added.** A whole-food, salt-free diet already supplies roughly **0.9–2.7 g of chloride per day from food itself** (Hulet 1955, via EFSA; [`Chloride_In_Produce_Evidence_Table.md`](../chloride/Chloride_In_Produce_Evidence_Table.md)). The body's small chloride requirement is met without any added salt, which is the basis of the project's operational rule, *"no salt should ever be used on anything"* ([`Vegan.md`](../diet/Vegan.md)).

## II. Frequency and season

**Daily, small, admitted classically.** Saindhava is named in Caraka's daily-routine verse (Sū. 5.12) and is counted among the *nitya-sevanīya* substances (those fit for daily use) — a small daily quantity, not a free condiment.

**Seasonally modulated, not constant (Caraka Sū. 6, *Tasyāśitīya*).** The salty taste belongs to the strength-promoting *visarga* (cooler) half of the year and is dialed up and down by season:

- **Increased** in the cold-wet *vāta* seasons, to pacify *vāta*: the rains (*varṣā*) — "diet having predominance of sour, salted and fatty articles should be taken for pacification of *vāyu*" (Sū. 6.33–40); and winter (*hemanta* / *śiśira*) — "one should use the unctuous, sour and salted…" (Sū. 6.9–21). The general rule: *amla, lavaṇa, madhura* increase strength in *varṣā, śarad, hemanta* (Sū. 6.7).
- **Reduced or avoided** in the hot and *pitta*/*kapha* seasons: summer (*grīṣma*) — "salted, sour, pungent and hot food… should be avoided" (Sū. 6.27–32); autumn (*śarad*, the *pitta* season) — "sweet, light, cold, slightly bitter, *pitta*-pacifying food… avoid alkali, fat, curd" (Sū. 6.41–48); spring (*vasanta*) — the *kapha*-clearing regimen, heavy/sour/sweet avoided (Sū. 6.18–26).

So even classically there is no "universal daily amount": salt rises in the rains and winter and falls in summer, spring, and autumn.

**Therapeutic / occasional.** Beyond the daily pinch, saindhava appears by indication in formulations and external applications (below), not as a standing food.

## III. Conditions that indicate salt

- **Vāta pacification** — salt is *vāta-ghna*; indicated in *vāta* disorders and the *vāta* seasons.
- **Digestion** — *dīpana–pācana* (appetiser–digestive); saindhava is "digestive, sharp, palatable, dear to the heart, *tri-doṣa*-balancing" (AH Sū. 6.143–144).
- **External and therapeutic** — eye preparations (the saindhava eye-exception, AH Sū. 10.34cd, is a *therapeutic application to the eye-tissues*), gargles, baths, medicated enema (*basti*), and classical powders (e.g. *hiṅgvaṣṭaka*, *lavaṇa-bhāskara* churṇa) where saindhava is a dosed ingredient, not free seasoning.

## IV. Conditions that forbid salt

- **Any excess (*atiyoga*)** — the wrinkle / greying / baldness / impotence / blood-vitiation catalogue (Caraka Sū. 26.43); coarseness, skin disease, thirst, loss of strength (AH Sū. 10.13–14).
- **The eyes** — all salt is unwholesome for the eyes *except* saindhava (AH Sū. 10.34cd); and since saindhava is itself NaCl, the project's chloride frame withholds even that exemption.
- **Named disease etiologies (salt to be avoided):** *rakta-pitta* — the bleeding disorders (Suśruta Uttara 45); *pāṇḍu-roga* — anemia / jaundice / wasting (Suśruta Uttara 44); *tṛṣṇā* — pathological thirst (Suśruta Uttara 48); and blood disorders generally (the cross-tradition witnesses say the same — the Chinese: "one suffering from blood disorders should not eat much salt").
- **Modern correlates:** hypertension (≤ 500 mg or none), edema / ascites (≤ 1 g).
- **Hot and pitta/kapha seasons** — summer, spring, and (moderated) autumn, per §II.

## V. Which salt is meant

Only *saindhava* (rock salt) is ever defended; *sāmudra* (sea / table salt) is the condemned common ingestant, and the *lavaṇa-gaṇa* as a category even includes non-chloride members — *kṣāra* (alkali / carbonate) and *sīsa* (lead) among them (AH Sū. 10.27). But the eye-good, daily-admitted saindhava is chemically ~NaCl, the same primary compound as the sea salt the texts condemn; the classical preference for it rests on *guṇa* (it is classed as the least burning, least *pitta*-aggravating, *tri-doṣa*-balancing salt), not on a lower chloride load. By the project's chloride toxicology, that *guṇa*-based exemption does not hold, and the consistent position is the stricter one: none added, the body's chloride supplied by whole food.
