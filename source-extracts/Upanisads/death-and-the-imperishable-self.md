# Death and the Imperishable Self — Extracts from the Upaniṣads and the Ṛgveda

Primary-source extractions from the Vedic corpus — Ṛgveda 10.90 (*Puruṣa-sūkta*), the *Kaṭha Upaniṣad* (the Naciketas-Yama dialogue), and the *Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad* — on the three-part thesis this file supports: physical immortality is impossible and the pursuit of it through rites is the specific trap the Vedic tradition warns against; bodily death is a necessity built into the very grammar of mortality; and incarnate life is the unique occasion in which the *ātman* may be identified and composed as that in us which "is not killed when the body is killed."

Translations: Patrick Olivelle, *The Early Upaniṣads: Annotated Text and Translation* (Oxford, 1998), for the Upaniṣadic passages; Ralph T. H. Griffith, *The Hymns of the Ṛgveda* (complete, reprinted), for the Ṛgveda passages.

## I. Naciketas rejects the offer of long life

The Kaṭha Upaniṣad opens with Naciketas, a boy, sent to the house of Yama — Death himself — and offered three boons. He uses the first two on smaller matters; his third wish is for the teaching about what happens after death. Yama tries every tactic to deflect him: "As to this even the gods of old had doubts, for it's hard to understand, it's a subtle doctrine. Make, Naciketas, another wish." When Yama offers him sons, grandsons, livestock, gold, land, "lovely girls, with chariots and lutes," and "as many autumns as you wish," Naciketas refuses on a single ground: a long life is still not an immortal life, and the thing worth asking for is not the extension of the mortal but the knowledge of what transcends it.

> "Since the passing days of a mortal, O Death,\
> sap here the energy of all the senses;\
> And even a full life is but a trifle;\
> so keep your horses, your songs and dances!"\
> — *Kaṭha Upaniṣad* 1.1.26, trans. Olivelle

> "With wealth you cannot make a man content;\
> Will we get to keep wealth, when we have seen you?\
> And we get to live only as long as you will allow!\
> So, this alone is the wish that I'd like to choose."\
> — *Kaṭha Upaniṣad* 1.1.27, trans. Olivelle

> "What mortal man with insight,\
> who has met those that do not die or grow old,\
> himself growing old in this wretched and lowly place,\
> looking at its beauties, its pleasures and joys,\
> would delight in a long life?"\
> — *Kaṭha Upaniṣad* 1.1.28, trans. Olivelle

The image at the centre of the text — that of man as grain, ripening and replanted — is the Upaniṣadic acknowledgment that physical life is given on the condition of return.

> "A mortal man ripens like grain,\
> And like grain he is born again."\
> — *Kaṭha Upaniṣad* 1.1.6, trans. Olivelle

## II. The self that is not killed when the body is killed

What Yama finally teaches, once Naciketas has refused every less-than-immortal substitute, is that the self (*ātman*) is unborn and undying and is not slain even when the body is slain. This is the central Vedic answer to the question "can one be made physically immortal?" — no, and that is not the question. The question is whether one identifies with the body that must die or with the self that never was born.

> "The wise one—\
> he is not born, he does not die;\
> he has not come from anywhere;\
> he has not become anyone.\
> He is unborn and eternal, primeval and everlasting.\
> And he is not killed, when the body is killed."\
> — *Kaṭha Upaniṣad* 2.1.18, trans. Olivelle

> "If the killer thinks that he kills;\
> If the killed thinks that he is killed;\
> Both of them fail to understand.\
> He neither kills, nor is he killed."\
> — *Kaṭha Upaniṣad* 2.1.19, trans. Olivelle

> "Finer than the finest, larger than the largest,\
> is the self (ātman) that lies here hidden\
> in the heart of a living being.\
> Without desires and free from sorrow,\
> a man perceives by the creator's grace\
> the grandeur of the self."\
> — *Kaṭha Upaniṣad* 2.1.20, trans. Olivelle

Yama's warning about what it takes to grasp this is uncompromising: it is not available to a man who has not purified his conduct, and it is not available by mere intelligence. The window for identifying it is the body that will die — and that window is not wide.

> "This self cannot be grasped,\
> by teachings or by intelligence,\
> or even by great learning.\
> Only the man he chooses can grasp him,\
> whose body this self chooses as his own."\
> — *Kaṭha Upaniṣad* 2.1.23, trans. Olivelle

> "Not a man who has not quit his evil ways;\
> Nor a man who is not calm or composed;\
> Nor even a man who is without a tranquil mind;\
> Could ever secure it by his mere wit."\
> — *Kaṭha Upaniṣad* 2.1.24, trans. Olivelle

## III. The razor's edge: life as the difficult crossing

The third *vallī* of the Kaṭha gives the chariot simile and the one-line imperative that has been the epitaph of every Upaniṣadic teaching on the death-window since: *uttiṣṭhata jāgrata* — arise, awake! — the life-long window is narrow and the path is hard.

> "Arise! Awake! Pay attention,\
> when you've obtained your wishes!\
> A razor's sharp edge is hard to cross—\
> that, poets say, is the difficulty of the path."\
> — *Kaṭha Upaniṣad* 1.3.14, trans. Olivelle

> "It has no sound or touch,\
> no appearance, taste, or smell;\
> It is without beginning or end,\
> undecaying and eternal;\
> When a man perceives it,\
> fixed and beyond the immense,\
> He is freed from the jaws of death."\
> — *Kaṭha Upaniṣad* 1.3.15, trans. Olivelle

## IV. Rites are unsafe boats: the Muṇḍaka refusal of ritual immortality

The Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad mounts the most direct Vedic critique of the idea that sacrifice alone can produce an immortal result. The eighteen forms of sacrifice are called "floating unanchored," "frail boats," and those who imagine themselves to have obtained the imperishable result through rites alone "return once more to old age and death." The teaching is precise: rites yield heavenly enjoyment, but heavenly enjoyment is finite, and its exhaustion returns one to the wheel. For the imperishable result, one must turn from what is made to what cannot be made.

> "Surely, they are floating unanchored,\
> these eighteen forms of the sacrifice,\
> the rites within which are called inferior.\
> The fools who hail that as the best,\
> return once more to old age and death."\
> — *Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad* 1.2.7, trans. Olivelle

> "Wallowing in ignorance, but calling themselves wise,\
> thinking they are learned, the fools go around,\
> Hurting themselves badly, like a group of blind men,\
> led by a man who is himself blind."\
> — *Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad* 1.2.8, trans. Olivelle

> "Wallowing in ignorance time and again,\
> the fools imagine, 'We have reached our aim!'\
> Because of their passion, they do not understand,\
> these people who are given to rites.\
> Therefore, they fall, wretched and forlorn,\
> when their heavenly stay comes to a close."\
> — *Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad* 1.2.9, trans. Olivelle

> "Deeming sacrifices and gifts as the best,\
> the imbeciles know nothing better.\
> When they have enjoyed their good work,\
> atop the firmament,\
> They return again to this abject world."\
> — *Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad* 1.2.10, trans. Olivelle

> "But those in the wilderness, calm and wise,\
> who live a life of penance and faith,\
> as they beg their food;\
> Through the sun's door they go, spotless,\
> to where that immortal Person is,\
> that immutable self."\
> — *Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad* 1.2.11, trans. Olivelle

The key line — "What's made can't make what is unmade" — is the Upaniṣadic formulation of the thesis that no fabricated body, however cunningly fabricated, can produce the unfabricated essence.

> "When he perceives the worlds as built with rites,\
> A Brahmin should acquire a sense of disgust—\
> 'What's made\
> can't make\
> what is unmade!'"\
> — *Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad* 1.2.12, trans. Olivelle

The positive side of the Muṇḍaka teaching — the way the imperishable is in fact reached — turns on direct perception of the Person (*puruṣa*) who is "both within and without," and on the stilling of the senses:

> "Knowing him, a man is freed,\
> and attains immortality."\
> — *Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad* 3.2.8, trans. Olivelle

> "His appearance is beyond the range of sight;\
> no one can see him with his sight;\
> With the heart, with insight, with thought,\
> has he been contemplated—\
> Those who know this become immortal."\
> — *Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad* 3.2.9, trans. Olivelle

## V. The Ṛgvedic *Puruṣa-sūkta*: three-fourths of Puruṣa are above

Ṛgveda 10.90, the *Puruṣa-sūkta*, supplies the cosmological ground for the later Upaniṣadic teaching: the incarnate world is one-fourth of the Puruṣa; three-fourths of him are above, "eternal life in heaven." The embodied being is a quarter-presence of an essence that has always been fuller than the body.

> "A thousand heads hath Puruṣa, a thousand eyes, a thousand feet.\
> On every side pervading earth he fills a space ten fingers wide."\
> — *Ṛgveda* 10.90.1, trans. Griffith

> "This Puruṣa is all that yet hath been and all that is to be;\
> The Lord of Immortality which waxes greater still by food."\
> — *Ṛgveda* 10.90.2, trans. Griffith

> "So mighty is his greatness; yea, greater than this is Puruṣa.\
> All creatures are one-fourth of him, three-fourths eternal life in heaven."\
> — *Ṛgveda* 10.90.3, trans. Griffith

> "With three-fourths Puruṣa went up: one-fourth of him again was here.\
> Thence he strode out to every side over what eats not and what eats."\
> — *Ṛgveda* 10.90.4, trans. Griffith

The hymn states plainly that the mortal quarter — the part that "eats" — is bounded; the three-quarters above are "eternal life." The body is what eats. What is eternal is not.

## VI. Cross-reference note

The Muṇḍaka's "what's made can't make what is unmade" is the exact Vedic formulation of the Hermetic principle in *C.H.* XIII.14 — "the sensible body of nature is far removed from essential generation; one can be dissolved, but the other is indissoluble" — and of the Castanedan principle in *The Fire from Within* that the old seers' attempt to carry the *physical* body into freedom was precisely the error that cost them their freedom. Three traditions, separated by millennia and oceans, converge on the same proposition: the fabricated body cannot be immortalized. Only the essence composed *while* the body is alive — called *ātman* in the Upaniṣads, the body of ten powers in the *Corpus Hermeticum*, and total freedom or the definitive journey in the don-Juan teachings — can survive the dissolution that is structurally built into what was born.
