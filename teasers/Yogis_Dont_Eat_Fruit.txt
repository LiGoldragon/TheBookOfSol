Somewhere in the last half-century, a myth settled into Western yoga: that the yogi lives on fruit. That the higher the practice, the lighter the food, and that lightness means raw, sweet, cold, and uncooked. That the endpoint of spiritual refinement is a bowl of mango.

Every classical text says otherwise. Every single one.

The Hatha Yoga Pradipika states: "The yogin should always eat wholesome anna in measured quantity." The word is anna. Not phala. Not saka. Anna -- grain cooked in water. The text then lists the foods suitable for yogis: wheat, rice, barley, milk, ghee, mung dal. Fruit appears nowhere in the recommended list. Jujube is explicitly prohibited.

The Bhagavad Gita describes sattvic food as snigdha (unctuous) and sthira (stable). Fruit is neither. It is light, watery, dispersing. Ghee is snigdha. Grain cooked with ghee is sthira. A fruit diet fails the Gita's own definition.

The first weeks of fruit-only eating produce exhilaration -- lightness, sharp perception, clarity. This is real. It is the experience of clearing. The error is in mistaking this relief for the permanent state. Clearing is not sustaining. A room swept clean is not a room that needs no floor.

Within months, the dispersion that clarified now destabilizes. The colon dries. Vata rises. Ojas depletes. The organism, having expelled its burdens, begins to expel its reserves.

No major yoga teacher -- not Sivananda, not Vivekananda, not Vishnudevananda -- has ever advocated fruitarianism. The smoothie bowl replaced the vilepi. The acai replaced the yavagu. Instagram replaced the Pradipika.

The yogic food is anna. Grain cooked soft in ample water, finished with ghee. Warm, unctuous, stable, quiet. It sits in the belly like a warm hand and lets the practitioner forget about food entirely, which is the whole point.

The floor is built before the room is swept. Anna is the floor. Fruit is the broom. A broom is useful. But no one lives on a broom.
